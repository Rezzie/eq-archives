Collection of interface tips

English only - Last updated: 22 August 2001

Please E-mail any comment or addition to daniel.ravon@wanadoo.fr

Everquest's interface is powerful, but takes some time to master. Here are some disorderly tips which I didn't see on other sites (or not too often), mostly about the interface and some specific aspects of magic.

1) MUNDANE TIPS

"Tomb Raider" playing view

To watch your group and your environment properly, I advise to play a Wizard in "Tomb Raider" view instead of "Quake" view (Quake view is for fighters getting their kick out feeling themselves in the heat o battle; they call that "realism"; as a result, they have no idea of where you are when you need help, and they don't see the trains coming). Tomb Raider view has the inconvenience of making mouse-targeting more difficult (you will often target yourself), but this is hugely offset by the benefit of wider vision (and of seeing yourself casting the pretty spells). To get into Tomb Raider view: press F9 twice (you see your own back); press Home to lock the camera focus on you; use Insert and Delete to adjust camera distance; use Alt-Uparrow and Alt-Downarrow to adjust camera height (if you didn't press Home, this gives you a periscopic effect); use Alt-Pageup and Alt-Pagedown to unlock the camera focus off you and look up or down; when you have your back to a wall, your body will tend to become transparent so you can still see in front of you; in dungeons, your body will tend to obstruct the screen, so you may want to switch to Quake view, but I still use Tomb Raider view 99% of the time - it is manageable, and it also tends to reveal secret passages.

Changing money at the bank

You are not obliged to abandon large quantities of small change on the ground or bear their burden. Whenever you are at the bank, take your coppers and put them in your silver box, then take your silvers and put them in your gold box, then take your gold coins and put them in the platinum box, et voilà ! You are lightweight again and you have platinum for trade. This works with the coin boxes of your purse as well as with the coin boxes of your bank account, as long as you are at the bank.

The command /loc

You should program a social button with that command. For giving appointments, for noting where your body will be (when you are about to die), and to mark other places of interest to you, you need /loc. Learn to interpret the figures: the higher the first figure, the further North you are in the zone. The higher the second figure, the further West you are in the zone. The point zero zero is not necessary in the center of the zone (it can be on an edge). Let's assume that the command gives you a result of 1085.14, -824.58 and you want to tell a friend where you are: you will tell your friend "plus1100 minus800 " and this is enough to find you. About rounding: you can round to the nearest 50 (at least outdoors), because each 15 of distance is only about the width of a dungeon corridor. About writing "plus" and "minus" in full letters (or "pos" and "neg"): the "+" and "-" signs most often don't pass through the system, and the other player won't get them or will have a doubt. About leaving a few spaces after the last digits: sometimes the last letter of a message disappears, and you don't want a zero to disappear, or the location is wrong by up to a few thousands.

Finding a group member with /follow

It is useful to program a social button with /follow for travelling under guidance. But this button can be useful in combat too. When a group member is in trouble and you can't see him or her, you may target him or her with PF key, and hit the /follow button, and, if he or she is not too far away, you will automatically run to him or her, so you can check what's happening.

Repeating sentences with Shift-Uparrow

Pressing Shift-Uparrow will make you type again the sentence or command which you last typed (and you can modify it before validating it with Enter). By pressing repeatedly, you can scroll back up through the last 19 sentences or commands you have typed.

Selecting an item from a stack with Ctrl-Leftclick

If you left-click on a stack of items in your inventory, a box with a cursor appears to let you select how many you want to move. To get only one item without the box appearing, you have to keep Ctrl pressed while you click on the stack. This is particularly useful when practicing trade skills. Note that, for ANY combination of ingredients in a container, if the combination involves two or more similar and stackable items, you must put each item in a different box of the container, NOT all of them stacked in one box of the container, or the combination will fail.

Jumping while running

This is a classical tip, but you might not know it if you are a newbie. When you are chased, jumping allows you to gain some ground over the creature chasing you. You lose ground each time you take a turn, so you should jump once or twice after taking a turn. Your Stamina will not allow you to jump forever, though.

Spending practice points

For some time when I was a newbie, I had the weird idea that spending practice points in a skill would increase the skill cap (maximum score you can reach at your level). Not so; practice points only get you started in a skill. I recommend to spend 3 points before starting a new weapon (spend nothing in the five basic magical skills). If you seem unable to get your Sense Heading skill started, you can consider spending a few points in it.

Creatures calling for help (the mystery of lazy guards unveiled)

When a creature is angry at you (whether fighting you or fleeing from you), you are inscribed on its "hate list". If this creature is within the listening range (or "aggro range", it's the same) of another creature, which is friendly to the first creature, the second creature will attack you (if it dares), even if it is friendly to you too (creature friendship is biaised in favor of other creatures). Who attacked first has no importance. When you are under attack, nearby creatures friendly to you (especially guards and merchants) will intervene in your favor if, and only if, they are programmed to hate your opponent; if they don't care about your opponent, they will "warmly" look at you dying.

Creature limitations versus players

I wrote this as humor ("Top 16 reasons why EverQuest is unfair to mobs"; an earlier version was published on The Cackling Klaknak), but there is some basis behind in terms of creature behavior. Think of these limitations, before complaining that creatures can do impossible things such as: see you through house walls, find you anywhere within their track range once you caught their attention, stack (many of them clustering in a space where only one player would fit), get past any number of bodyguards to come and hit you whenever they feel like it, hit you from beneath through the floor when they are unable to find the way up to reach you, have a pet surviving the death of its master, have several pets including charmed players, have backstab in addition to attributes of other classes, etc.

By the way, "mob" means "mean old bastard"; it is the same as "NPC" ("non-player character"), but the term is more combat-oriented.

1) Neutral or friendly mobs can't "consider" us to check if we love them; they can only hope that we will pass by.

2) Most aggressive mobs don't really attack us "on sight", or they are as blind as moles (we can remain just outside their reaction radius, showing them our butt and eating our lunch unpunished until we are ready to fight, while they stand there scowling at us and trying to look dignified, and we can cast our first spell at them comfortably).

3) Mobs can't perceive players as a group: they "consider" you to know your level when making combat decisions, but they seemingly forget to take into account your friends standing next to you (would you go thinking "great, there is one blue gnoll next to these five red gnolls, I can attack it safely" ?)

4) Mobs can't camp us at our home point (unless we had real bad inspiration when choosing our binding place. Imagine the mobs sitting at your home point for hours and chatting: "Do you think he'll spawn soon ? - Wait, /who Dumbo all... He's on Plane of Fear, first spawn should be soon, and respawn time is very short after first spawn").

5) Mobs don't have Harmony to pull us one-by-one.

6) Mobs can't kite us (mob would go: "yeah, Druid, run panting after me while your life is eaten away by DOT").

7) Mobs can't check the Internet for spoilers and tactics (they could have arguments on mob discussion forums).

8) Mobs don't have long-distance communication, such as group chat and guild chat (they cry for help in a ridiculously small radius around them, and they can tell their story over a long distance only by giving us bad faction when they die. Imagine the Blackburrow gnolls with a chat channel to coordinate their resistance against us intruders, with the gnoll commander managing his troops dungeon-wide).

9) Nobody shouts "train" before six of us suddenly fall pounding at a single mob.

10) Mobs can't taunt us away from our opponent.

11) Mobs can't zone when in danger (leaving us exhausted, with no exp, walking back to our spot, and having forgotten about them even if they zone back in).

12) Mobs can't loot our bodies (did you say "Ultima Online" ? <shriiieeek>)

13) Mobs don't gain exp and levels when killing players (with any luck, a gnoll pup could survive in Blackburrow several months, and grow big enough to kill higher level players on their way to Permafrost, then eventually become a mega-death level 54 gnoll equal to Vox or Nagafen - possibly giant size but still with "a gnoll pup" name tag, or maybe it would have been allowed to choose a second name at level 20, such as "a gnoll pup Spot").

14) Mobs can't plan elaborate large-scale expeditions into a city to kill a level 50 player rumored to drop rare and valuable loots.

15) Mobs can't use cyber-flirt emotes ("a burly gnoll stares dreamily into a gnoll pup's eyes", "an elite gnoll guard massages a gnoll commander's shoulders"), get married in the Arena, get drunk, and duel.

Item availabilty and prices according to server maturity

On a new server with many low level players and few higher level players, item prices will be high, and players will basically live with the equipment they can conquer. On a mature server, there are many intermediate and high level players, and items conquered by the player community tend to accumulate (even if some of them are absorbed by the high level players creating "twinked" newbies to try out other character classes), so prices fall, and some useful items are even given out to newbies. Conclusion: if you want Norrath to be yours to take, go to a new server; if crowding doesn't bother you and you want nice equipment quicker, go to an old server.

2) MAGIC TIPS

Casting a spell after running

If you stop running and start casting a spell immediately, it is almost certain that your spell will be interrupted. This is probably the effect of routine lag. You should wait almost one second before starting to cast, even if you are in dire need. Creatures do not have to wait: they can stop running, cast their spell, and resume running, without losing a fraction of a second. I reached level 16 before figuring this out...

Interrupting your own spells

Wrong spell ? Or you think the creature will be dead before you finish casting ? Press D for "duck", and you spend no mana (press D again to cancel ducking). Since a 2001 patch, the casting is interrupted immediately upon ducking.

Virtues of the Staff of Temperate Flux

This Staff is Wizard-only, and probably one of the most useful items a Wizard can have, because it does something nothing else does. Note that the "Staff of Ro" needed for the quest is obtained from another NPC in the Temple, who calls it "Darkwood Staff". You don't need to equip the Staff of Temperate Flux to use its power, and you can use it while running.

- Basically the Staff of Temperate Flux gives you your own small free instant debuff spell against fire and cold.

- Before casting a long spell (in group play), if you are unsure that the creature is in your line of sight ("you can't see your target"), check with the Staff first.

- The Staff's effect has the same range as your direct damage. If you kite a creature with Bonds of Force, you can use the Staff repeatedly while running, and, when it tells you that the creature is out of range, you know it's time to stop and cast. As Bonds of Force has a short duration, this is a valuable help in managing your timing.

- The Staff can be used repeatedly and has good taunting power. It won't do good against a creature already mad at someone else, but, if the creature is hesitating, you can keep it aggressive against you indefinitely with the Staff without spending mana. This is interesting especially if the creature is Snared (or Bonds-of-Forced) so you can escape from it, kiting it around while the group finishes it off or takes a moment to breathe.

A failed theory about high resistance to "magic"

When I was level 20, I thought I suddenly understood that cold and heat-based spells would be reliable and almost always do some damage, while "magic"-based spells would often be fully resisted because creatures would have, on the average, a resistance to "magic" higher than their resistance to heat or cold. Well, at high level it is common to meet creatures with high resistance to "magic", but not so at intermediate levels: Root, Tishan's Clash, Force Shock, etc, use "magic" energy, but are highly resisted essentially because they are designed as "all-or-nothing" (although one could argue that Root may have "partial" effect through shorter duration, and becomes more reliable at high level with the Immobilize upgrade). Other spells such as Shock of Lightning, Cast Force, etc, are also "magic"-based and are not "all-or-nothing", so they have regular resistance. By the way, lightning is included in "magic", not "heat".

Immunity of gigantic creatures to stun

From level 20 on, you will regularly use stun spells (Tishan's Clash and its upgrades), because they are nice spells for the early stages of a fight (they will not annoy creatures too much, and fighters like to be able to hit at creatures during a few seconds without retaliation). However, on gigantic creatures such as giants of all kinds, cyclops, most dragons and gods, the stun is inefficient. It's not a matter of resistance: the creature will not necessarily resist your spell (you may get a system message "creature is stunned"), but will take only the "damage" part of the effect, and will be stunned during zero seconds only. The system of immunities to specific spell lines is getting more complex since 2001, though.

Virtues of skin spells (Shieldskin, Leatherskin, etc)

Skin spells make you munch costly gems (I carry a basic stock of 40 peridots), but they are well worth it.

1) Basically, the skin spells give you those precious extra seconds to finish something important, both in a group (when the time comes to chain-cast and expose yourself, a mere few seconds of additional survival time allow you to get your share of the victory) and in solo (in the intermediate levels, you can solo with Root and Shock most non-Root-resistant blue creatures, if and only if you buy the time for extra Root attempts with your skin spell - when the creature is Rooted and your skin spell is gone, refresh the skin spell before blasting).

2) Skin spells are a diplomatic asset in a group, and allow you to be brave (hence the expression "skin tanking"). Most groups will consider that you should even avoid attracting the creatures' attention on you, and, especially, the group's healers will not like having to watch over you, unless the situation is exceptionally hot and the group needs you to chain-cast spells. However, isn't it boring ? And being attacked is a proof that you contribute. Skin spells are the perfect solution, acting as preventive self-heals, allowing you to be reasonably active in battle, while making you routinely independent from healers. Skillful use of blast spells and skin spells will show your group that you are able to manage effectively your offense and your defense, so you may get away with other things (using AE spells, for instance).

3) The skin spell, acting as an alarm signal, allows you not to care much for your life bar. As long as you get the "your magical skin absorbs the blow" messages, you don't care at all for your life bar. As soon as you start taking damage, it's time to put more emphasis on evasive action. Without this signal, you need to watch your life bar closely to decide when you are safe and when you are in danger (the bar moves fast), whereas you want to concentrate on what you're doing.

4) Even after you stop using weapons, you still need to melee on a frictional basis, to improve your Defense and Dodge skills (until they max out, unfortunately). Wearing your skin spell in group play, you can afford to take blows for a short time, to improve your defensive skills, without losing noticeable life.

5) Skin spells will absorb some forms of magical damage in addition to melee damage, including the Shadowknights' Harm Touch, but they will not absorb, for instance, falling damage, or damage-over-time (once a DOT spell sticks on you, you start losing life while your skin spell remains in place). As long as your skin holds, melee hits will not poison you (skin spells make you even more brave against venomous creatures). But the skin will not protect you from stunning hits.

6) Beware that refreshing your skin spell in battle (once your opponent has been taunted away from you) has a huge taunting effect (the creature is likely to change its mind and come back for you).

7) The skin spell reduces your chances of being interrupted by hits. The spell interruptions are of three kinds: stun (skin spell doesn't help), red message with immediate interruption (I don't remember if the skin spell helps), and pushback (movement) causing interruption at end of casting with blue message (the skin spell helps because it reduces pushback, and the chance of interruption depends on movement amplitude).

8) Remember that, to cast some of their spells, other spellcasters need the same gems than you do (for instance, Clerics need them for their symbol spells); so you can give or lend them some if needed, as well as borrow some from them.

The level 20 Enstill is not an upgrade to the level 4 Root

If you're still a teenage Wizard, don't wait for Enstill with high hopes. Enstill is superior to Root only in duration, and is less versatile for breaking free from melee because of a longer cast time; the true upgrade to Root is the level 39 Immobilize.
