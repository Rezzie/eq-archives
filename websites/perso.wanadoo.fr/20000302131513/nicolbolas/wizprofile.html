Wizards: profile and misconceptions

English only - Last updated: 13 February 2000

Please E-mail any comment or addition to daniel.ravon@wanadoo.fr

 

This text is mostly meant to complement my other editorial (Wizard AE spell guide), to underline the importance and place of Area-Effect spells in the Wizard's profile. I also thought that beginning players should be aware of four common misconceptions about Wizards. Wizards have the most extreme powers and drawbacks, and are either very pleasant to play or very frustrating according to player temper. Read this before investing much time in your Wizard !

1) "Wizards are weak at low level and powerful at high level".

Yes and no. "Low level" is to be understood as level 1-7 only. As a true newbie Wizard (i.e. without the good mastery of game commands and deluxe equipment which a false newbie has), you will have a terrible time dying and getting lost repeatedly (my own piece of advice: spend immediately 3 practice points in Piercing instead of magical skills; buy a Giant Snake Fang from another player for 6 gold; and program the /loc command as a social button and learn how to use it).

At levels 8-11, you have enough mana to start mastering the Root-Shock routine, and you can afford better gear (provided that you refrain from buying immediately all the level 8 spells, which are quite expensive), so you improve rapidly.

At level 12, you learn the Shock of Lightning spell, and you reach your full battle potential. This is the brightest direct damage spell you ever get, considering that, at higher levels, you meet the added challenge of swaying through the creatures' resistances. Also, later, your active battle time tends to shrink (see paragraph 3).

Levels 20-23 are a trial, because the level 20 direct damage spell is not so good (Force Shock is big but unreliable; it will be more useful to you later - see paragraph III in 3rd section). Also, until your late teens, you have melee capabilities to complement your magical damage, but not so afterwards (your melee performance becomes ridiculous as creatures grow tougher, and you will want to meditate when not casting, to reduce downtime. It took me much time to accept that; I thought I would continue to melee at higher levels part of the time, wielding a super magical staff and sweating with my fighter pallies unlike those lazy casters with pets, but... no). Getting your first Teleports is a kind of consolation. At this stage, levelling up is much slower.

At level 24, you're back at the peak of your power. The level 24 direct damage spell is good (a bit smaller than Force Shock, but reliable), and you've got all your commodity spells (except the taxi-driving, group-saving Evacuation spells).

At later levels, there are reports of Wizards feeling somewhat more powerful when they gain access to level 29 or 34 spells, but nothing really awe-inspiring.

Conclusion: don't look up dreaming to the level 49 Ice Comet spell. If you have a Wizard of level 12 or higher and you're not satisfied with your performance in combat, learn to like it, or quit playing your Wizard, because it won't get better.

2) "The direct damage of Wizards doesn't shine that much".

Yes and no. Many Wizards believe that they should be able to outdamage anybody over the course of a fight; they complain that the Magicians' direct damage is almost as effective, and that all casters with pets outdamage Wizards easily. But the distinctive skill of Wizards is the ability to chain-cast fast and large damage spells over the course of a few seconds; so we were told, and so it is; no more and no less. Wizards will not so often want to chain-cast, because it consumes a lot of mana, and it doesn't always kill creatures quickly enough to prevent deadly retaliation; but they can do it.

Also, relying only on spells after losing their melee abilities, Wizards are the first affected by the "spell resistance cap": no chance (or maybe 5%) of affecting with spells "deep red" creatures which fighters of same level are able to hit (when a large group fights a "deep red" creature, the Wizard, after wasting some mana, sits with flaming cheeks while the others do the job; this is one reason why you should avoid being the junior in your group, unless Verant alleviates the resistance cap; another reason is that the group's juniors have a spontaneous taunting effect on creatures passing by, and you want the tanks to be the taunters, not you).

A different issue regarding resistances is that, as the game reaches higher levels, the creatures' resistances to spells increase so much that scoring full damage becomes infrequent, and many Wizards feel less and less useful to a group, and feel dependent from the help of a Shaman or Magician to lower the resistance of targets to spells. However, this resistance issue is felt mostly on the Planes (Fear, Hate, etc); a useful quest item from Temple of Ro (Staff of Temperate Flux) allows Wizards to diminish somewhat the targets' resistances in the absence of a Shaman or Magician; the patch of 21 January 2000 has lessened this particular resistance issue for higher level Wizards (35 and above); and I have read reports that, even before the patch, Wizards with a higher Intelligence had much less resistance problems (most casters do not care to increase their Intelligence beyond 200, because the additional mana is not worth it; but it is strongly rumored that an Intelligence of 230 allows the spells to be much less resisted, so you should start maximizing your Intelligence as soon as you reach level 20 or so).

Additionally, spells annoy creatures on the basis of potential damage (the angering effect is the same whether the damage is fully scored, partly resisted or fully resisted), whereas weapons annoy creatures on the basis of inflicted damage only; so, you will often get pounded down even though your spells did little or no damage, while the fighters are busy elsewhere or try in vain to shake the creature off you (in this case, your direct damage "shines" the wrong way).

The picture might seem dark, but it is important to remember that Wizards have had a very bad reputation as kill-stealers for a long time (kill-stealing is waiting for someone to take away 45% of the life of a creature, then finish it off with 2 or 3 spells to steal the experience; this behavior is now a reportable offence), and Verant felt the need, throughout 1999, to adjust the game balance in favor of fighting classes. Also, Wizards have inherited most of the spellcasting legend of traditional roleplay (they tend to be thought of as "the real thing" if one wants to play a caster), so I suspect the class is kept slightly under-attractive technically, to prevent a rush and allow the other spellcasters to gain respect.

There is enough matter here to fuel kilometers of arguing on the forums. In spite of their uneven performance, Wizards often amaze their comrades with their swiftness, and are always welcome in a group.

3) "Wizards can't solo".

Yes and no. Wizards are definitely not one of the best classes for solo play ( for that, you need a character with a pet - this includes Shamen at high level - or a Druid or Bard), but there are reports of Wizards being able to solo effectively, with some limitations. I even heard advice to solo through the "hell" levels (30, 35, 40 and 45); it's supposed to get you quicker through, for reasons I'm not sure of.

First, you may solo well with the Root-Shock routine as a teenage Wizard (then, after reaching level 20, this combination becomes less reliable, as creatures get tougher and you can't count on your melee skills to complement your magical damage).

Wizard solo above level 20 is generally thought of as "kiting" as Druids do. That is, you need a borrowed Spirit of Wolf (or your own Journeyman's Boots) to run faster than the creature, gain sufficient distance, stop, cast, then run again. This is possible only outdoors. The level 29 spell Bonds of Force is supposed to slow down the movement of a group of creatures, so you could expect not to need Spirit of Wolf anymore, but it's not as reliable as the Druid spell "snare" (the mana cost is 75 i.e. a bit high, you may affect yourself by mistake with the spell, and the duration is said to be rather short). However, the main limitation of "kiting", for a Wizard, is the large downtime. When a Druid runs kiting a creature, he or she is not wasting time: he or she is allowing the Damage-Over-Time spells to give their best efficiency. When a Wizard runs kiting a creature, he or she is only regaining distance between two Shocks (not scoring any extra damage, and not regaining mana either), so, the downtime of running is simply added to the downtime of meditating (I prefer to cast my spells comfortably on a creature kept busy by fighters, and to meditate between two castings). One can also fear that, for each 9 creatures killed solo, one will have a good chance to kill the Wizard if something goes wrong (mana runs out, creature scores a few stuns) and the experience gained is negated. I had a report of a Wizard using Column spells in solo (see my Wizard AE spell guide ) to kite 3 or 4 creatures at a time and gain a damage/mana bonus reducing downtime (this requires identifying the proper zone and proper creatures to do it without too much danger).

Another way to solo, for a Wizard, is to bully smaller spellcasting creatures. The meleeing creatures are the hardest to kill (many hit points, and they hit hard), whereas the spellcasting creatures are softer (although they may pose problems to fighter players, who can't resist spells as well as Wizards can). Not so many Shocks are needed to kill a spellcasting creature, and, in the meantime, the creature will waste time casting weak spells instead of hitting. Depending on your level, creatures such as Gnoll High Shamen, Evil Eyes, and the like, will be relatively easy solo experience with no need to run.

4) "Playing a Wizard is boring".

Yes and no. The Wizard class is straightforward; it's the magical equivalent of the Warrior class, at least at the lower levels. If some are happy playing a Warrior, some can also be happy playing a Wizard. After losing their melee abilities, Wizards become very mana-dependent, and meditation time is dull; but spellcasting time is intense, and meditation is often an opportunity to socialize or ponder.

At intermediate levels and high levels, Wizards just can't be as active as most other players. If Wizards try to keep up with the others, they run out of mana in less than a minute, and are excluded from group operations for up to eight minutes afterwards (full meditation time). Wizards are meant, apparently, to watch the pulse of the group and act as an insurance: they stay idle 80% of the time when the rest of the group can do without them, and they are active 20% of the time (stepping in without losing a single second), to maintain group cohesion by finishing off fleeing creatures, to curb opposition when the group tends to suffer heavier damage, and to Evacuate the group when it is likely to be overwhelmed.

One can wonder if the experience drained from group operations by higher level Wizards during their idle time is worth their contribution during their active time. Still, without a Wizard, a group needs both a Druid and an Enchanter (or Bard) to have both Evacuation and opposition regulation. Evacuation is very valuable to a group. Enchanters and Bards (i.e. Mesmerizers, see paragraph A.2 in 3nd section) are better than Wizards for opposition regulation, but the Wizard's way of regulating opposition (blasting) is different and complementary. However, both Evacuation and opposition regulation are support functions (such as the abilities of healers, Enchanters and Bards), and Wizards possibly don't want to be support characters (they signed for being magical fighters). So, Wizards may be reluctant to accept their "pulse-watching" role, may not want to be loved for Evacuation, and may even find Evacuation a boring privilege (self-only Teleport makes you free as the wind; not so Evacuation; Evacuation is a Cleric's job). That's the kind of things Wizards must learn to like: becoming more of a support character when growing older (although they still remain part-time magical fighters). I once read that Wizards functioned as "aggressive Clerics"; I couldn't put it better. Actually, Wizards will feel more effective if they start thinking "support" early in their career.

Higher level Wizards may feel a "magical fighter" again sometimes, when they act as the bodyguard of the group's healer, or when they make a constructed use of area-effect spells (see my Wizard AE spell guide), or when they act as "magical tank" against spellcasting creatures (Wizards probably have the best resistances, and, if they increase their resistances with their self-only buffs and other buffs from Clerics and Shamen, they can face without fear spells which would take down a fighter quickly; so, against a casting creature, the Wizard may purposedly lead the fight so that the fighters can hit at the enemy without being burned away).

Of course, a player who wants to keep active as often as possible should choose a fighter, and a player who needs a multi-purpose array of spells to be happy should choose another type of spellcaster. But the diversity of Wizard spells is decent enough to fill the eight memorization slots. Although Shock and Root are the meat and drink of a fighting Wizard, a handful of playable Area-Effect spells add much to combat thrill.

Nicolbolas Whizz (Aargh)
