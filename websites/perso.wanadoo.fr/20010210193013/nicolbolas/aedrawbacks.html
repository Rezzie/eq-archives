AE damage backfire

Wizard AE spell guide menu - English only - Last updated: 25 October 2000

 

Drawbacks in the form of backfire risks are not an absolute nuisance, and can be dealt with. They are much less annoying than the inherently poor stats of some spells.

Aggroing mobs

It is difficult to predict the mobs' reaction to AE damage, less easy than to predict a mob's reaction to DD. Experience gives a feel, but all attempts to lay down precaution guidelines (assuming you want to avoid mob retaliation) have failed. Overall, it's the same as for DD: don't cast too soon in the fight, don't cast from a range, and wear your Skin spell at all times. It has been said that the mobs were truly acting as a pack, as long as all the player fighters were beating at the same ("main") mob, so, nothing wrong would happen to you as long as you don't aggro the main mob; this is false. It has been said that targeting a player tank (instead of one of the mobs) would direct the aggro against the tank, or at least lessen the aggro directed towards you; this is also false. The experimental proofs are hard to produce, but many Wizards have testified against these theories.

The psychology of a pack of mobs is very mixed: each mob is governed all at once by its loyalty to fellow mobs, by its desire for personal revenge, and by the principle of giving some priority to the easiest task; and it is not easy to say when the scales are tipped. It is possible that the lowest-level mobs in the pack think more of themselves (and attack you more easily), maybe because the damage from the AE spell represents a higher proportion of their hit points. Your level and life bar, compared with the level and life bar of other players, also count, because mobs find it easier to bully the lowest-level or near-dead players, unless the higher-level or healthier players are particularly good taunters. Many "intelligent" mobs also seem to over-react to spells being cast at them, damage nonwithstanding. A particular case is when there's a master mob with a pet in the crowd: unless the master mob is your tanks' main target, the AE spell roughly aggroes that mob pair double, because the pet cares only for itself and its master (not for its master allies) and will instantly perceive you as enemy number one; the master mob, in turn, takes the pet's decision into account, and is likely to assist the pet against you (the master hardly ever calls the pet back).

Anyway, being attacked as a consequence of playing an AE spell is not always "backfire". More often than not, I am happy when I am attacked by one mob (or even by several mobs, provided that they are Snared and I can walk them around). I often play a Column early in a fight, so that I can both help the fighters with damage, and pull a mob away from them for Rooting purposes. Even arousing the main mob may be perceived as a success, if you're actively trying to pull all the mobs to give your endangered tank a break. Still, the low predictability of AE spells is a challenge, and one might wish for some split between very-low-aggro spells and rather-high-aggro spells (but I don't want to start guessing how the split should be determined). The aggro from AE damage was apparently lowered twice, in patches, since the game was created (the second time was in early 2000). While lower aggro promotes safer and more popular use of the spells, it would probably not be sound to have the aggro lowered further, because this would diminish the value of the spells as voluntary taunting tools.

I haerd a few Wizards wishing that the aggro from AE spells be officially redirected to the target player, when a party member is the target. This "fix" would have far-reaching harmful effects on gameplay, because, first, it is dubious that you should ever be allowed to generate aggro toward another player (there is a consent issue, even if the player is a party member; see the specific page on scattered spells for dismissing the "damage shield" case), and, second, this would so greatly help your tanks with their basic job of taunting, that the fights would become very predictable and boring (fighters fight mobs, healers over-heal undisturbed, casters over-nuke undisturbed).

Aggroing passers-by

This is very annoying, and happens with Bonds of Force as well as with AE damage spells: a nearby friendly or neutral NPC will be caught in the area of effect and attack you, possibly killing you (some NPCs, such as guards, are tough) or forcing you to flee just because you don't want to bear the negative faction from killing it. With any luck, you will be able to Root the NPC away and continue to assist your group a bit before you have to Gate out. Keep your eyes open !

Breaking Mesmerize

(Mesmerize is a long stun which is broken if the mob takes damage).

When there is an Enchanter in your group, it's all-or-nothing: either he/she uses Mesmerize, and your AE is shut off (breaking Mesmerize is a major backfire severely frowned upon), or he/she uses stun, and this is compatible with your AE. If you're not very motivated, or if the Enchanter is of lesser level, it's best to let him/her stick to Mesmerize as usual. But, if you have a particularly good AE damage spell available, and you feel ready to use it whenever there's more than one mob around, you should try and persuade your group to switch to rotated stun spells instead of Mesmerize (preferably the Enchanter's PB "color" spells) to give you free hands. Enchanters may enjoy the variety too. The knowledge that some Wizard AE damage spells are powerful has spread somewhat, and I believe that groups are not as reluctant as before to give up the "safe" Mesmerize in favor of stun and AE damage. The incompatibility with Mesmerize tends to make AE damage a part-time ability, but, with time, I found out that the opportunities to use AE damage were still many, with or without Enchanter.

Saturating the graphic cards

Many AE spells have heavy particle effects which will create huge temporary lag or even system crash, for the weakest computers. This is worst when several AE spells are cast simultaneously. In any settings where AE spells are heavily used, the particle density should be shut off (even if the spells look unfortunately less pretty).

The "rogue pet" bug

This is different from the widely-known "pet bug" (pets absorbing part of the allowed hits which should go to mobs, a nuisance especially in the case of Rains). If the player master of a pet goes linkdead and you use an AE spell touching the pet, the pet may go rogue and attack you (and you have NO means of withholding the attack, not even Root). This is a second-hand report; I never witnessed that bug. I'm also speaking of carebear play, not PvP.

Hurting yourself

You will hurt yourself with Rain spells, Al'Kabor spells, Gravity spells, and Thunderbold (as well as Snare yourself with Bonds of Force), if you don't cast it from sufficient distance.

Hurting allies in PvP

AE spells are generally considered unplayable in PvP, because they harm your allies in addition to your foes. I don't know any means of getting around this - or, if some means can be devised (such as using an AE spell of an energy type against which your allies have good resistance), the reward will probably not be worth the trouble (most rewards are already problematic on carebear servers).

Z-axis aberrations

This varies from zone to zone depending on zone design, but the use of AE spells in dungeons will sometimes pull mobs from other floors (generally below you), or from behind walls. This is felt especially when you are near a pit, and there are mobs at the bottom of the pit: they are likely to come for you. There is nothing much to do about it, except not do the mistake twice in a given location. A related matter which is not quite "backfire", is that yu can't use AE spells as a way to shoot around a corner. Each of the mobs to be affected has to be in line of sight; mobs which are around a corner will not be hit, even if they are theoretically within the spell's radius.
