Collection of tips for Wizards

English only - Last updated: 31 May 2000

Please E-mail any comment or addition to daniel.ravon@wanadoo.fr

Everquest's interface is powerful, but takes some time to master. Here are some tips which I didn't see on other sites (or not too often), mostly about the interface and some specific aspects of magic. This is all rather disorderly, because I don't want to go encyclopedic about strategy for now.

1) MUNDANE TIPS

"Tomb Raider" playing view

To watch your group and your environment properly, I advise to play a Wizard in "Tomb Raider" view instead of "Quake" view (Quake view is for fighters getting their kick out feeling themselves in the heat o battle; they call that "realism"; as a result, they have no idea of where you are when you need help, and they don't see the trains coming). Tomb Raider view has the inconvenience of making mouse-targeting more difficult (you will often target yourself), but this is hugely offset by the benefit of wider vision (and of seeing yourself casting the pretty spells). To get into Tomb Raider view: press F9 twice (you see your own back); press Home to lock the camera focus on you; use Insert and Delete to adjust camera distance; use Alt-Uparrow and Alt-Downarrow to adjust camera height (if you didn't press Home, this gives you a periscopic effect); use Alt-Pageup and Alt-Pagedown to unlock the camera focus off you and look up or down; when you have your back to a wall, your body will tend to become transparent so you can still see in front of you; in dungeons, your body will tend to obstruct the screen, so you may want to switch to Quake view, but I still use Tomb Raider view 99% of the time - it is manageable, and it also tends to reveal secret passages.

Changing money at the bank

You are not obliged to abandon large quantities of small change on the ground or bear their burden. Whenever you are at the bank, take your coppers and put them in your silver box, then take your silvers and put them in your gold box, then take your gold coins and put them in the platinum box, et voilà ! You are lightweight again and you have platinum for trade. This works with the coin boxes of your purse as well as with the coin boxes of your bank account, as long as you are at the bank.

The command /loc

You should program a social button with that command. For giving appointments, for noting where your body will be (when you are about to die), and to mark other places of interest to you, you need /loc. Learn to interpret the figures: the higher the first figure, the further North you are in the zone. The higher the second figure, the further West you are in the zone. The point zero zero is not necessary in the center of the zone (it can be on an edge). Let's assume that the command gives you a result of 1085.14, -824.58 and you want to tell a friend where you are: you will tell your friend "plus1100 minus800 " and this is enough to find you. About rounding: you can round to the nearest 50 (at least outdoors), because each 15 of distance is only about the width of a dungeon corridor. About writing "plus" and "minus" in full letters (or "pos" and "neg"): the "+" and "-" signs most often don't pass through the system, and the other player won't get them or will have a doubt. About leaving a few spaces after the last digits: sometimes the last letter of a message disappears, and you don't want a zero to disappear, or the location is wrong by up to a few thousands.

Finding a group member with /follow

It is useful to program a social button with /follow for travelling under guidance. But this button can be useful in combat too. When a group member is in trouble and you can't see him or her, you may target him or her with PF key, and hit the /follow button, and, if he or she is not too far away, you will automatically run to him or her, so you can check what's happening.

Repeating sentences with Shift-Uparrow

Pressing Shift-Uparrow will make you type again the sentence or command which you last typed (and you can modify it before validating it with Enter). By pressing repeatedly, you can scroll back up through the last 19 sentences or commands you have typed.

Selecting an item from a stack with Ctrl-Leftclick

If you left-click on a stack of items in your inventory, a box with a cursor appears to let you select how many you want to move. To get only one item without the box appearing, you have to keep Ctrl pressed while you click on the stack. This is particularly useful when practicing trade skills. Note that, for ANY combination of ingredients in a container, if the combination involves two or more similar and stackable items, you must put each item in a different box of the container, NOT all of them stacked in one box of the container, or the combination will fail.

Jumping while running

This is a classical tip, but you might not know it if you are a newbie. When you are chased, jumping allows you to gain some ground over the creature chasing you. You lose ground each time you take a turn, so you should jump once or twice after taking a turn. Your Stamina will not allow you to jump forever, though.

Spending practice points

For some time when I was a newbie, I had the weird idea that spending practice points in a skill would increase the skill cap (maximum score you can reach at your level). Not so; practice points only get you started in a skill. I recommend to spend 3 points before starting a new weapon (spend nothing in the five basic magical skills); if you get into Tailoring, spend 20 points there (you won't even be allowed to spend more). If you seem unable to get your Sense Heading skill started, you can consider spending a few points in it. Save enough points to feed a particular skill which is hard to practice: Research; practicing it with the Concordance of Research book is tedious (when you first gain Research, you are allowed to spend only one point in it, but, a few levels later, you are allowed to start pouring practice points in it, and you will possibly need to spend there all of the 5 practice points you gain per level).

Creatures calling for help (the mystery of lazy guards unveiled)

When a creature is angry at you (whether fighting you or fleeing from you), it constantly broadcasts a cry for help in a certain radius. Any other creature in this radius, friendly to the first creature, will attack you (if it dares), even if is friendly to you too (its friendship is biaised in favor of the first creature). Who attacked first has no importance. When you are under attack, nearby creatures friendly to you (especially guards and merchants) will intervene in your favor if and only if they are programmed to hate your opponent; if they don't care about your opponent, they will "warmly" look at you dying.

Creature limitations versus players

I first wrote this as humor ("Top 16 reasons why EverQuest is unfair to mobs"; an earlier version was published on The Cackling Klaknak), but there is solid basis behind in terms of creature behavior. Think of these limitations, before complaining that creatures can do impossible things such as: see you through house walls, find you anywhere in the zone once you caught their attention, stack (many of them clustering in a space where only one player would fit), get past any number of bodyguards to come and hit you whenever they feel like it, hit you from beneath through the floor when they are unable to find the way up to reach you, have a pet surviving the death of its master, have several pets including charmed players, have backstab in addition to attributes of other classes, etc.

By the way, "mob" means "mean old bastard"; it is the same as "NPC" ("non-player character"), but the term is more combat-oriented.

1) Neutral or friendly mobs can't "consider" us to check if we like them (they can only hope that we will pass by, or be content with doing quest transactions with them). Many had better hide from us or flee without delay.

2) Aggressive mobs don't really attack us "on sight", or they are as blind as moles (we can remain just outside their reaction radius, showing them our butt and eating our lunch unpunished until we are ready to fight, while they stand there scowling at us and trying to look dignified, and we can cast our first spell at them comfortably).

3) Mobs can't perceive players as a group: they "consider" you to know your level before deciding between attacking and staying very quiet (or between retaliating and fleeing, in case you attacked first), but they seemingly forget to take into account your friends standing next to you (would you go thinking "great, there is one blue gnoll next to these five red gnolls, I can attack it safely" ?)

4) Mobs don't routinely go invisible (they could ambush and hunt us nicely; players would shout all the time "plz give me see invis" in addition to "plz give me sow").

5) Mobs can't camp us at our home point (unless we had real bad inspiration when choosing our binding place. Imagine the mobs sitting at your home point for hours and chatting: "Do you think he'll spawn soon ? - Wait, /who Dumbo all... He's on Plane of Fear, first spawn should be soon, and respawn time is very short after first spawn").

6) Mobs don't have Harmony to pull us one-by-one (psst, mob, pull the Druid first for your revenge...)

7) Mobs can't kite us (mob would go: "yeah, Druid, run panting after me while your life is eaten away by DOT").

8) Mobs can't check the Internet for spoilers and tactics (they could have arguments on mob discussion forums, such as "Should one kill the healer or evacuator first ? - Evac's casting time is 9 seconds, interrupt it near the end so the players lose more time - Save your stunning bash for Greater Healing when a player is almost dead", and so on).

9) Mobs don't have long-distance communication, such as group chat and guild chat (they cry for help in a ridiculously small radius around them, and they can tell their story over a long distance only by giving us bad faction when they die. Imagine the Blackburrow gnolls with a "radio gnoll" chat channel to coordinate their resistance against us intruders, with the gnoll commander managing his troops dungeon-wide).

10) A single mob routinely gets six of us pounding at it suddenly, without anybody ever shouting "train" as a warning.

11) Mobs can't taunt us away from our opponent (picture yourself forced to give up your nearly dead mob opponent and face a fresh mob instead...)

12) Mobs can't zone or Gate when in danger (leaving us exhausted, with no exp, walking back to our spot, and having forgotten about them even if they zone back in).

13) Mobs can't loot our bodies (did you say "Ultima Online" ? <shriiieeek>)

14) Mobs don't gain exp and levels when killing players (with any luck, a gnoll pup could survive in Blackburrow several months, and grow big enough to kill higher level players on their way to Permafrost, then eventually become a mega-death level 54 gnoll equal to Vox or Nagafen - possibly giant size but still with "a gnoll pup" name tag, or maybe it would have been allowed to choose a second name at level 20, such as "a gnoll pup Spot").

15) Mobs can't plan elaborate large-scale expeditions into a city to kill a level 50 player rumored to drop rare and valuable loots.

16) Mobs can't use cyber-flirt emotes ("a burly gnoll stares dreamily into a gnoll pup's eyes", "an elite gnoll guard massages a gnoll commander's shoulders"), get married in the Arena, get drunk (with Blackburrow Stout), and duel.

Item availabilty and prices according to server maturity

Most of the items you find will not be useful to you or to any player of your level, but you may sell or give them to lower level players. So, the price of most not-too-rare items depends on the composition of the player population: on a new server with many low level players and few higher level players, item prices will be high, and players will basically live with the equipment they can conquer. On a mature server, there are many intermediate and high level players, and items conquered by the player community tend to accumulate (even if some of them are absorbed by the high level players creating "twinked" newbies to try out other character classes), so prices fall, and some useful items are even given out to newbies. Conclusion: if you want Norrath to be yours to take, go to a new server; if crowding doesn't bother you and you want nice equipment quicker, go to an old server.

Experience distribution in group

There is much rumor and legend about how experience is distributed among group members. I'm still not quite sure, but this is what I think I could derive from several discussions in forums:

1) There is an experience bonus for group play, increasing with group size (the shared experience for each killed creature adds up to more than what the creature would be worth in solo play).

2) The damage inflicted to the creature by each player (or other wrong done indirectly to the creature and making the creature aggressive, such as healing the creature's opponent) does NOT count when distributing experience among the players. Many players believe that inflicting much damage has an importance, but it seems that, on the contrary, the player sitting through the fights gets as much experience as the other group members of the same level (so, being lazy, or away-from-keyboard often, can be frowned upon). The issue is critical to you as a Wizard, for you might want to exhaust your mana supply constantly to keep up with the others, for fear of not getting your share of experience; this is unnecessary. The only things you need to do is have your own fun and keep the group happy with you.

3) The contribution of players is measured by their level, so the higher level players get a higher share of the experience (if the level difference is too large, the lower level players don't get any experience). It is hard to translate this in terms of progression speed of the saffron experience bar, because the amount of experience needed to reach the next level depends much on class, race, and the current level. In other words, when cooperating with a friend of a different level, you may want to know if this causes the level gap between you to grow, diminish, or remain stable, but I can't tell. When the level gap is already large, obviously, playing together makes it grow even larger (because the lower level player tends to get no experience at all). But, if the level gap is small, you might be lucky enough so that playing together makes the gap diminish or remain stable (this would be fair, unless the game aims at separating friends...) You should make a test to be sure: play exclusively together for some time, and then compare how many bubbles were gained by each.

2) MAGIC TIPS

Casting a spell after running

If you stop running and start casting a spell immediately, it is almost certain that your spell will be interrupted. This is probably the effect of routine lag. You should wait almost one second before starting to cast, even if you are in dire need. Creatures do not have to wait: they can stop running, cast their spell, and resume running, without losing a fraction of a second.

Interrupting your own spells

Wrong spell ? Or you think the creature will be dead before you finish casting ? Press D for "duck" and wait until the casting time is over, and you spend no mana (press D again to stand up). If you are low-level, you may also do the same trick by moving slightly, so you don't have to wait till the end of the casting time - but you should still wait almost a second before beginning casting another spell, or your movement will interrupt that one too (you can't use that trick effectively at higher level, because your chance of regaining concentration and casting the spell nevertheless becomes sizeable, so you need to duck).

Virtues of the Staff of Temperate Flux

This Staff is Wizard-only, and probably the best item obtained through a quest in the Temple of Ro, because it does something nothing else does (the mask and cloak give good Intelligence, and you can live without the kilt and wand). Note that the "Staff of Ro" needed for the quest is obtained from another NPC in the Temple, who calls it "Darkwood Staff". You don't need to equip the Staff of Temperate Flux to use its power, and you can use it while running. Now, why is it so good ?

- Only the Staff of Temperate Flux gives you your own small free instant debuff spell against fire and cold. Any additional chance of scoring full damage is good to take.

- The Staff's effect has the same range as Shock. If you kite a creature with Bonds of Force, you can use the Staff repeatedly while running, and when it tells you the creature is out of range you know it's time to stop and cast. As Bonds of Force lasts only 45 seconds, this is a valuable help in managing your timing.

- The Staff can be used repeatedly and has good taunting power. It won't work very well against a creature very mad at someone else, but, if the creature is hesitating a bit, you can keep it aggressive against you indefinitely with the Staff without spending mana. This is interesting, of course, in emergency situations when you are low on mana and your group is low on life, especially if the creature is Snared (or Bonds-of-Forced) so you can escape from it, kiting it around the group while the group finishes it off (the fighters might not understand your tactics; they might think you are running in panic, and call you names, but they're not very intelligent, you must explain to them). You may try to turn this into a way of kiting with a Ranger or Shadowknight friend (you keep the creature indefinitely mad at you with the Staff while the fighter keeps it Snared and follows it hitting it in the back), but I tried it and didn't find it very reliable (at least, don't do it with creatures who have a natural high running speed, even if Snared they will cause you trouble).

Virtues of Bonds of Force

The level 29 Bonds of Force is an ersatz of the Druid's level 1 Snare spell, considerably less good; but it's still very valuable, as the only spell of the kind that Verant gave us, so let's accept it eagerly and say "thank you". Still, imagine if we had Snare instead: it costs 15 mana instead of 75; the casting time is 2 seconds instead of 2.5; it lasts 2 minutes instead of 45 seconds; and it is single-target instead of AE (for kiting, the AE feature of Bonds of Force is actually a drawback: you almost never want to affect several creatures with it, but you Bond yourself with it when you use it in melee, so you're entitled to only one safe shot. In melee against multiple not-too-big opponents in dungeons, though, Bonds of Force might exceptionally save you, provided that all your opponents are affected and you happen to resist it). Wizards would rule the world if they were given the true Snare. If you Bond yourself, your best chances to escape are either short-distance Teleport (outdoors and downhill), or jumping while "running". Being Bonded will not affect much your jumping speed, so, jumping can make you gain quite some distance over your opponents until you are tired, so you can zone or cast whatever single spell you want to save yourself (obviously, this won't work if your opponents resisted the Bonds and only you are Bonded). Now, you have other options, either in addition to or instead of the above. You may take your chances and Gate in spite of interruptions. Or you may try and Root your opponents, so they don't move at all - but, when they break the Root, the Bonds is gone and they run faster than you. Or, as another desperate attempt, you may try to Root yourself: if you resist your own Root, the Bonds remains, but, if the Root affects you, it breaks the Bonds, and the Root has every chance to last a very short time and let you free as the wind afterwards, while your opponents are still Bonded. Don't try it more than once, though, or you will lose too much time. You can also try the trick of Rooting yourself when you suffer from another form of slow-movement spell (Snare, Clinging Darkness), although I'm not sure it works.

Interrupting the spells of others

The best specialist of spell interruption is the Enchanter, not the Wizard, but you have some tools. The spells which you mostly want to interrupt are healing spells, for those pesky healers just never die, whatever blast you pour into them. The healing spells are: Healing (about 80 points healed, 3 seconds casting time, Cleric level 14 or Shaman/Druid level 19, not a big deal); Greater Healing (about 300 points healed, 3.8 seconds casting time, Cleric level 19 or Shaman/Druid level 24, this one is pesky); Superior Healing (about 500 points healed, 4 seconds casting time, Cleric level 34, pesky too); Complete Healing (10 seconds casting time, Cleric level 39, apparently not a big threat, but who knows). Here are 3 spells with a casting time short enough so that you can hope to interrupt healing spells with them:

1) Tishan's Clash (level 20): doesn't do much damage (30 to 40, for 60 mana), has moderate casting time (2.5 seconds), and will be among your regularly memorized spell anyway because of the stunning effect, which lasts about 4 seconds and interrupts spells. Being all-or-nothing, Tishan's Clash is somewhat easily resisted (you can expect roughly a 20% resistance chance on blues, 33% on whites, 50% on yellows, and high resistance on reds), so it is best used preventively, not as a last resort. Having wasted 60 mana is usually not too painful, though. Also, it cannot be chain-cast, so, if you can't kill the healer with a subsequent Shock, you'd better have another spell-interrupting tool ready.

2) Force Shock (level 20): does nice damage (179 maximum), has moderate casting time (2.8 seconds), and interrupts spells consistently thanks to a brief stun, although you get no system message telling you about the stun (no chance for the creature to regain concentration and continue casting). But the spell, being all-or-nothing, is easily resisted (see Tishan's Clash above). Unless you are high level, it is very depressing to spend 120 mana for naught, and to see the creature heal back up instead. Also, you cannot chain-cast this spell.

3) Shock of Lightning (level 12): has a short casting time (2.2 seconds) and is said to interrupt spells by a slight knock-back effect; I believe it is true, but, because of the interference of fighters (!) and lag scrambling the order of appearance of system messages on your screen, there is some room for doubt on this spell's efficiency. Sometimes it seems to work, sometimes not. At least, the creature is allowed a chance to regain concentration and continue casting. However, you are almost sure to score some damage, so I would say that Shock of Lighning, memorized as a secondary damage spell, is worth using against healer creatures just in case.

Anyway, even when using interrupt spells technically shorter than the healing spell, if you wait until you see the creature starting to cast the healing spell before starting to cast yours, it's probably too late. The routine lag plays in the creature's favor, and there is every chance that the healing spell will be completed first. You need to feel the mood of your opponent and anticipate a bit.

A failed theory about high resistance to "magic"

When I was level 20, I thought I suddenly understood that cold and heat-based spells would be reliable and almost always do some damage, while "magic"-based spells would often be fully resisted because creatures would have, on the average, a resistance to "magic" higher than their resistance to heat or cold. Well, not so: Root, Tishan's Clash, Force Shock, etc, use "magic" energy, but are highly resisted because they are designed as "all-or-nothing" (although one could argue that Root may have "partial" effect through shorter duration). Other spells such as Shock of Lightning, Cast Force, etc, are also "magic"-based and are not "all-or-nothing", so they have regular resistance. By the way, lightning is included in "magic", not "heat".

Immunity of gigantic creatures to stun

From level 20 on, you will regularly use stun spells (Tishan's Clash and its upgrades), because they are nice spells for the early stages of a fight (they will not annoy creatures too much, and fighters like to be able to hit at creatures during a few seconds without retaliation). However, on gigantic creatures such as Giants of all kinds (and also Cyclops I believe), Nagafen, Vox, Cazic Thule, Innooruuk, Dracoliche, the stun is inefficient. It's not a matter of resistance: the creature will not necessarily resist your spell, but will take only the "damage" part of the effect, and will not be stunned at all (or the stun duration will be almost null), although you get a system message telling you that it is stunned.

Virtues of skin spells (Shieldskin, Leatherskin, etc)

Skin spells make you munch costly gems (I carry a basic stock of 40), but they are well worth it.

1) Basically, the skin spells give you those precious extra seconds to finish something important, both in a group (when the time comes to chain-cast and expose yourself, a mere few seconds of additional survival time allow you to get your share of the victory) and in solo (in the intermediate levels, you can solo with Root and Shock most non-Root-resistant blue creatures, if and only if you buy the time for extra Root attempts with your skin spell - when the creature is Rooted and your skin spell is gone, refresh the skin spell before blasting).

2) Skin spells are a diplomatic asset in a group and allow you to be brave (hence the expression "skin tanking"). Most groups will consider that you should even avoid attracting the creatures' attention on you, and, especially, the group's healers will not like having to watch over you, unless the situation is exceptionally hot and the group needs you to chain-cast spells. However, the contrary is also true: if no creature ever turns on you and you always stay quietly undisturbed, you might be suspected of being lazy. Being attacked is the proof that you contribute. Skin spells are the perfect solution, acting as preventive self-heals, allowing you to be reasonably active in battle, while making you routinely independent from healers (at least in the intermediate levels; maybe not at high level). Skillful use of blast spells and skin spells will show your group that you are able to manage effectively your offense and your defense, so you may get away with other things (using AE spells, for instance). By the way, here is something for Wizard ears only: with the Wizard-only Staff of Temperate Flux, you can create an illusion of contribution by making a creature mad at you without spending any mana... I'm not telling you to do it ! But you can save a group member with the Staff, because it can be activated repeatedly in no time until the taunting is successful (then, the group can recollect itself while you tank), whereas a direct damage spell takes several seconds and might not suffice, and a Root or stun spell is unreliable.

3) The skin spell, acting as an alarm signal, allows you not to care much for your life bar. As long as you get the "your magical skin absorbs the blow" messages, you don't care at all for your life bar. As soon as you start taking damage, it's time to put more emphasis on evasive action. Without this signal, you need to watch your life bar closely to decide when you are safe and when you are in danger (the bar moves fast), and you don't want to do that: you want to concentrate on what you're doing.

4) Even after you stop using weapons, you still need to melee on a frictional basis, to improve your Defense and Dodge skills. Wearing your skin spell in group play, you can afford to take blows for a short time, to improve your defensive skills, without losing noticeable life.

5) Skin spells will absorb some forms of magical damage in addition to melee damage, including the Shadowknights' Harm Touch, but they will not absorb, for instance, falling damage, or damage-over-time (once a DOT spell sticks on you, you start losing life while your skin spell remains in place). As long as your skin holds, melee hits will not poison you (skin spells make you even more brave against venomous creatures). But the skin will not protect you from stunning hits.

6) Beware that refreshing your skin spell in battle (once your opponent has been taunted away from you) has a huge taunting effect (the creature is likely to change its mind and come back for you).

7) Beware that, although absorbing melee damage, the skin will not prevent your spells from being interrupted by hits. The chances of regaining concentration might be higher thanks to the skin (because it seems that low-damage hits are easier to regain concentration from, but I'm not sure whether the important factor is the low damage or the low level of the attacking creature).

8) Remember that, to cast some of their spells, other spellcasters need the same gems than you do (for instance, Clerics need them for their symbol spells); so you can give or lend them some if needed, as well as borrow some from them.

The level 20 Enstill is not an upgrade to the level 4 Root

If you're still a teenage Wizard, don't wait for Enstill with high hopes. Enstill is superior to Root only in duration, and only if you leave the creature alone: the spell is broken as soon as the creature takes the slightest damage; think of it as a bad Mesmerize (Enchanter spell), with a purpose only if you manage to glue the creature somewhere away from the combat. 99% of the time, Root will do the same job and much more, so you don't have to spend a memorization slot on Enstill. The upgrade to Root is the level 39 Immobilize (the level 49 Paralyzing Earth is an "upgrade" to Enstill).

Concept of downtime reduction through grouping

This is only nice-to-have theory, so I will be short (many factors I won't consider, such as group experience bonus and non-damage crowd control tools). The goal is to enjoy playing and to eliminate the "particularly boring" downtime (for instance, when half the group is ready and the other half is not ready); the name of the game is not to maximize the "experience gained / time played" ratio.

1) When 2 fighters (of the same level) group, they need half as much time as in solo to kill a creature, and they each earn half the experience. But, during the fight, the creature is able to deal only half the damage it would have inflicted in a solo fight, and this damage is (or should) be divided evenly, so each fighter takes 4 times less damage than in solo; the recovery downtime per creature is cut in 4. When 3 fighters group, the experience per creature and per fighter is cut in 3, for a recovery downtime per creature cut in 9; and so on (4 for 16, 5 for 25, 6 for 36). This is a huge downtime saving. Then, the death downtime (the time needed to recover your body after defeat and regain the lost experience) is harder to put in equations, but at least you know that a group can take on yellow or red creatures which would be impossible to solo, and this can be calculated: if a creature can inflict to the solo fighter, before dying, damage equal to 200% of the solo fighter's life (so the solo fighter dies while the creature is still at half life), when facing two fighters, the creature will die 2 times quicker and will have time to inflict only damage equal to 100% of one fighter's life, and the damage will be split, so the fighters will win the fight, and lose only 50% life each. Here, you have an all-or-nothing difference between solo play and group play which is invaluable in terms of downtime reduction.

2) When 2 Wizards (of the same level) group, they need half as much time as in solo to kill a creature, but it doesn't matter - what matters is that each spends half the mana - and they each earn half the experience. So, the recovery downtime per creature is cut in 2 (instead of 4 in the case of a fighter group; recovery for a Wizard is mana recovery, rather than life recovery). When 3 Wizards group, the experience per creature and per Wizard is cut in three, for a recovery downtime per creature cut in 3 (instead of 9 in a fighter group); and so on (4 for 4, 5 for 5, and 6 for 6, instead of 4 for 16, 5 for 25, and 6 for 36 in a fighter group). There is NO GAIN of recovery downtime versus solo play in a Wizard group. However, the all-or-nothing and invaluable downtime reduction for winning a fight in group versus losing it in solo is roughly the same as for fighters. So, while fighter-only groups tend to roll, Wizard-only groups don't roll, they are just safer than solo play.

3) Draw your own conclusions for mixed groups. As far as I could feel, a Wizard in a mixed group doesn't contribute to the recovery downtime reduction of the group as well as an additional fighter would, but may provide greater flexibility against some forms of tougher opposition, so his or her contribution to the death downtime reduction of the group might be slightly higher. Also, Wizards need to optimize their mana expenditure to minimize the group's recovery downtime (if they spend too much mana, the group has to wait for them, and it doesn't compensate for a shorter fight. If they don't spend enough mana, the healers get exhausted supporting the fighters, and the Wizards have to wait for them).
