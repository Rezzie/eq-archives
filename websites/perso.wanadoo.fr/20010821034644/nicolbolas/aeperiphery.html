Functional description

These spells, obscurely called "PB" for "player-based" or "point-blank", are unique in several ways. First, they are the only non-targeted, zero-range damage spells which Wizards have: whatever the Wizard's current target is, mobs are affected in a comfortable radius around the Wizard (beware: although PB spells are often said to affect mobs "at melee range", they don't have a range, they only have a radius). The Wizard is not damaged by the spell, but should be prepared for immediate mob retaliation (PB spells refresh our memory about how safe it is to attack from a range). Second, PB spells are the only damage spells able to affect any number of mobs (and this allows maybe-abusive tactics which have no special name, but which I will call "PB gang bang" or "PB death camp" here, see below). PB spells have very variable stats (mana cost, damage and cast time) for their level. The recast time is moderately long (6 seconds), except for the level 34, 49 and 60 spells which have long recast time (12 seconds). With the exception of the level 34 Thunderclap, the resistance pattern seems standard, similar to that of Shocks; Thunderclap is all-or-nothing and highly resisted (like Force Shock), but, in spite of this drawback, it is still the brightest spell in the line, thanks to very nicely swollen stats - however, its use is best limited to low-blue mobs, or the resistance factor spoils the fun. The PB spells of level 24, 34, (and probably) 53, 56 and 59 generate a pushback effect of varying nature which may interrupt spellcasting (especially Thunderclap, which pushes like Force Shock does, with split-second stun interrupting spells for sure).

Visual EFX

Most PB spells create the same visual EFX as Columns, that is, lasting vertical blue or orange flames around each affected mob, which are a heavy trial for the graphic cards. However, at least the level 34 Thunderclap has a different and much lighter EFX: brief red sparks somewhat similar to the EFX of Shock of Lightning. Whenever PB spells are about to be used intensively, it's much advised to shut particle density off.

Critic

I initially perceived PB spells as utterly exciting: the Wizard's revenge in melee, here comes the berserker Wizard ! However, after taking many blows trying to use PB spells as short-range offense or self-defense, I came to think that they were not quite powerful enough in casual play to justify such an exposure. Speaking of self-defense: Root, or some other non-damage defensive tool, will serve you much better when you're attacked by multiple mobs. But PB spells are to be appreciated differently, depending on intended use and inherent performance. In casual play, when your ambition is to affect a small handful of mobs for the fun of it, you want a PB spell with good inherent performance. In "PB gang bang" tactics, the inherent performance of each spell is less important: you want to rotate two or three PB spells, with an overall damage concentration just sufficient to kill the mobs before mana runs out.

The PB spell line is the most uneven AE damage spell line in terms of inherent performance. Some PB spells are swift, others clumsy; some are weak, others strong; some do concentrated damage, others diluted damage. The level 34 Thunderclap is probably the most brilliant of all pre-50 AE damage spells; the post-50 Jyll's spells are lightweight, but offer much for the cost ; the strongest spells (Super Nova, Winds of Gelid) are clumsy and have awful damage/mana, but may compensate just because they're big; and most other PB spells are simply unattractive packages, good only for being played in a rotation with better spells.

Research tip

People seem to have trouble finding the Rune of Conception needed to research the powerful level 34 Thunderclap. I have looted this Rune a few times in City of Mist, near the zone, off mid/high-30s mobs.

Casual rewards

- Damage/mana gambling: when hesitating between memorizing a Column and memorizing a PB spell, besides comparing stats, consider that PB spells represent a greater personal exposure, but allow you to choose more precisely the center of the affected area (that's where you stand), giving you a greater chance to hit all the intended mobs.

- Rotating PB spells and Columns: at levels 8, 16, 24 and 34, you have both a new Column and a new PB spell available, with roughly comparable stats. Even if the PB spell alone would not be attractive, memorizing both spells can allow you (in group, and in melee) a quasi-chain-casting of 2 or 3 reasonably strong AE damage spells, for more spectacular results. Even without a special group organization, you might occasionally be able to play the hero that way and survive.

- Interrupting caster mobs: the spell-interrupting pushback provided by Cast Force, Thunderclap and the Jyll's spells has some value, because the spells are quick and cheap (whereas the Al'Kabor spells with the same effect are slow and expensive).

- One big shot: Super Nova and Winds of Gelid may allow impressive brutal finish-offs, consuming huge mana.

- Help finish the main mob, while starting to proceed with the next: the risk of retaliation from the additional mobs is higher than when you use a Column for the same purpose.

- PB carnival: at some levels (especially 24), many casters and healers gain access to a PB spell. A group having several spellcasters can agree to pull multiple mobs, and fire PB spells at will (shut particle density off !) The result will most probably be a mess, but can be efficient and funny enough. Other types of AE damage spells can be added, but PB spells are the most capable of hitting all the nearby mobs in a messy fight. Enchanters can help effectively by casting PB stun spells. This is a casual version of "PB gang bang".

- Helping lower-level players with trains: the PB spells being non-targeted, and able to affect any number of mobs, they are particularly suited for helping lower-level players in dungeons, with minimal effort: no skillful targeting needed, just stand near the zone and press the button once when the train comes. You must select the right-sized spell, maiming mobs without stealing the kills; or select a bigger spell almost killing the mobs, and zone after you cast it.

Advanced tactics: "PB gang bang"

Wy kill one mob with your mana supply, when you can kill twelve of them ? You need a specially composed and trained group (at least mid-thirties). The tactics are as follows: one buffed Warrior goes pulling a train of 10-12 blue mobs back to one or two Wizards who start rotating PB spells. The Wizards are protected by one or two Enchanters rotating PB stun spells (and possibly giving Rune buffs), and by one or two Clerics healing and (if available) giving invulnerability buffs. The fighters (and pets, if any) take care of the runners. The Wizard Sabbok describes "the core components of any 'pure' AE group" as "2 Wizards, 1 Enchanter, 1 Cleric; the other two spots can be filled by just about anyone, but preferably a tank and a high-damage meleer (rogue, ranger or monk)". A regular group using these tactics can gain lots of experience, and Verant doesn't seem to view it as abuse, as long as only a tiny minority of players get organized that way. Provoking that many mobs at once is daring after all (some Wizards sweat and whine when caught in melee with one blue mob: imagine twelve of them beating at you while you try to concentrate), and I don't want to imagine what happens when more than one party member goes linkdead. My knowledge of this is second-hand, because my regular group is not suited, and I don't care to create another group specially for that purpose. My last attempt to rotate Thunderclap and Cast Force, in the company of an Enchanter of insufficient level, was horrendous.

Requested improvements

The PB spell line is so uneven that the worst spells would deserve a revamp. Especially, the spells of level 8 to 24 are mediocre. But, the PB spells' potential for very-high-gain tactics being a kind of threat to gameplay balance, it's probably hopeless to claim "justice" for some of the spells of that line. PB spells are not the spells most in need of a fix - if a change comes one of these days, it is more likely to be a nerf, in the form of a mob count limitation.
