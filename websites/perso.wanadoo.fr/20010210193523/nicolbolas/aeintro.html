Introduction: AE damage and gameplay

Wizard AE spell guide menu - English only - Last updated: 25 October 2000

A) Purpose of this site

I chose a Wizard instead of another spellcaster, because I wanted to be free as the wind (teleporting at whim, instead of dragging the burden of a pet behind me); because I thought that having the "best" direct damage was the real thing; and because I viewed AE damage spells as thrilling and spectacular (remember the AD&D Fireball ?) Now, after a year and a half of playing (and still being intermediate-level), I lost most of my initial interest in AE damage, to focus on other aspects of play. This happened because, on the whole, AE damage is very unbalanced. A handful of AE damage spells are on the verge of being overpowered (Column spells from level 24 on, PB spells from level 34 on, Rain spells from level 53 on), while most of the others are very weak. Most overpowered spells also need a special preparation and mindset to be fully taken advantage of, and I don't have that mindset, being rather fond of casual, flexible tactics. AE damage spells (except Columns, and the post-50 Rains) don't have much place in casual, flexible tactics. I tried some heavy lobbying in summer 2000, so that the power of AE damage versus DD would be made more even, but to no avail. However, I strive to keep this guide updated, because the topic deserves it. I am still interested in devising the brightest possible assessment criteria for AE damage spells, criteria capable of teaching us something about the game. A concept which I also try and defend at all times is: no positive or negative comment is valid for all AE damage spells; to be valid, a comment must make a difference between the four main AE damage spell lines (Column, Al'Kabor, Rain, PB), or at least be explicitly limited to one spell or spell line. Some Wizards speak of "AE spells", either in good or in bad, actually thinking of this or that particular spell line (or spell); I hate that. By the way, if you want to know my overall ideas about the Wizard class, see my other essay on Wizards: profile and misconceptions.

 

B) The third main gameplay attribute of Wizards ?

The idea here is to weigh the importance of the various gameplay attributes, and determine how important AE damage is to Wizards, and how important it should be. Wizards have many abilities, and many of them underestimated, especially the defensive abilities: Wizards have brilliant defenses, either active (mob incapacitation) or passive (harm prevention / evasion). Although Wizards don't have the inherent personal defenses which fighters have (hit points, armor), they have, in reality, defenses which routinely allow them to kill mobs single-handed without taking any damage, and to markedly reduce the losses suffered by a group. Note that some defensive "abilities" of Wizards are specific spells, while some others are side effects or specific tactical uses of spells pertaining to another gameplay attribute (DD, Teleport and AE damage).

DD: direct damage is undisputedly the prime gameplay attribute of Wizards, the distinctive offense of which they are "masters", and this doesn't need much discussion. If you believe that the best defense is offense, then, DD is also defense in addition to offense (if only because it is range offense), and the defensive potential of DD counts in assessing the importance of DD as a gameplay attribute. Although some complaints are often heard about Wizard DD, it is generally considered as efficient and satisfactory.

Teleport: Teleport is something very exclusive, shared only between Druids and Wizards (although all spellcasting classes have Gate, and Necromancers also have short-range random Teleport which is passive defense). It comes naturally after DD, as the second most important gameplay attribute of Wizards. Teleport adds a lot to playing fun, allowing to join friends and change environment easily, as well as being a help for questing. There is a part of passive defense (personal and collective) in Teleport (although, in my experience, Evacuate is less useful than it may seem at first: groups feel safer when you memorize it, but they almost always prefer that you refrain from using it). Wizard Teleport may be seen as slightly superior to Druid Teleport, because it includes Teleport to the Planes, and some side spells related to the theme.

Mob incapacitation (active defense): "mob incapacitation" is defined as all means to affect mobs so that they can't cause harm to you and/or your allies ("all means" short of immediate death, which is a plain application of DD or AE damage). "Mob incapacitation" is offense by nature (because you are doing something to the mobs), but it is defense by destination (the outcome is that some harm is avoided). The champion of mob incapacitation is the Enchanter, but Wizards deal in several ways to incapacitate mobs, mostly: movement-hampering, stun, and aggro control. Wizards don't have, for instance: Charm, Fear, Harmony, Mesmerize (defined as a very long stun which is broken if the mob takes damage), and pets (which, in addition to being continuous damage sources, perform as defensive lures keeping mobs busy, i.e. preventing the mobs from attacking players). Details in the Defensive abilities sub-page.

Prevention / evasion (passive defense): "prevention / evasion" spells are pure defense: they don't do anything to any mobs, but, still, their outcome is that some harm is avoided. "Prevention / evasion" spells are many, and some of them are exciting (for instance Skin spells and Yonder). Details in the Defensive abilities sub-page.

AE damage: AE damage hardly needs a definition: it is a class of spells allowing to damage more than one mob at once, in a certain area. The basic AE damage spells are called Columns. There are 3 main other lines of spells which have a lesser overall damaging performance than Columns (and have some specific drawbacks), but have a distinctive advantage each: Rains can stack up to 3 hits on the same mob, for greater strength (but this can be done on only one mob, and becomes valuable only post-50); PB spells can affect any number of mobs (but they are risky zero-range, and the line is very uneven); and Al'Kabor spells have a wide radius (they got the crappiest "advantage" of all, it's worthless). There are also a few scattered spells, with specific side benefits (such as a moderately long stun for the level 54 Thunderbold), which don't fit in these lines.

How important should AE damage be to Wizards ? There are strong indications that it should be quite important. Wizards are often seen as the heirs of Advanced Dungeons & Dragon's Evocation magic, the most famous spells of which are the AE spells such as Fireball and Lightning Bolt. The content of our spell book looks like a confirmation: 22 AE damage spells pre-50, and quite some more post-50; that's a lot (other classes having access to AE damage own about 3 times less spells). Also, AE damage is the only obvious and consistent way in which Wizards can challenge their damage/mana ratio (other spellcasters, including healers, improve it with pets, DOT, or weapons). Should Wizards be the only character class stuck with an immutable ratio, because it is supposedly the "best" ? It might be a false rumor, but I heard that Geoffrey Zatkin once stated that AE damage was the "pet" of Wizards. One could expect Wizards to use AE damage often, in the same way Magicians are often seen in the company of a pet; AE damage should be the third gameplay attribute of Wizards, after DD and Teleport. Let's not forget, however, our non-"spellcasting" cousins the Bards, who may accumulate, in their own way, a lot of experience about the strengths and weaknesses of AE effects (many of their songs operate like our "PB" spells).

In practice ? Let's face it: miscellaneous active and passive defense is immensely more useful to Wizards than AE damage, on a daily basis. A Wizard can lead a successful career up to level 50 without ever playing an AE damage spell, and, only from level 52 on, may be "forced" to use Rains; not so with defense spells, without which Wizards would live a very miserable life in the intermediate levels.

 

C) "Mastery" of AE damage

The amazing Wizard

Most other classes will be amazed by the Wizard's speed when killing a mob single-handed (if able at all). A multiple kill is even more amazing, but much harder to score, except on very small greenies, so, a lonely Wizard is more likely to amaze newbies than to amaze players of equal level with AE damage spells. It is possible to amaze players of equal level in a group, when skillfully creating an impression of "could never have been done with direct damage" by using the right AE spells at the right time, with maximum effects, calculated exposure, and minimum mess (good luck !) AE damage spells can be viewed as the second way for Wizards to go berserk (the first way being chain-casting DDs).

Also, the visual EFX of AE spells are sometimes greater and more impressive than the EFX of direct damage. Many character classes can't generate much EFX, and will count on Wizards, more than on any other spellcaster, to provide the awesome fireworks.

Mastery versus other classes

If "mastery" of AE damage means the same as "mastery" of DD, then, Wizards should have at least one good AE damage spell per spell rank. But it is not true: there is a good Wizard AE damage spell available approximately every second spell rank, at least pre-50; the "mastery" versus other classes seems to improve post-50. Pre-50, Wizards can be seen as "masters" of AE damage only by default, if you view AE damage as generally weak and unreliable in the game, Wizards being the only class having many such spells, less lousy than average, with a few of them good or even powerful. Losing sting and adding tension

Some say that Wizards are powerful when they know what they're doing. This is all the more true when AE damage spells are used.

Whenever you are using AE damage spells, you are spending time and mana which you could spend on direct damage instead, so, first of all, you are missing an opportunity to shine with your DD, which is a proven, focused tool, with brilliant sting, able to swiftly and brutally dispose of particularly unwanted mobs, bringing relief to your group when needed.

On the contrary, whenever you are using AE damage spells, you are using a relatively non-focused (or diluted) tool, affecting indifferently all the mobs in the area, and affecting each of them less than DD would, so, you are giving up most of your ability to choose which mob you want dead first. Also, you are unleashing something relatively unpredictable, which is likely to backfire in some way or another. Typically, one or more mobs will turn on you - maybe you intended to achieve that result, but, if you end up more distracted or threatened than you wanted, this is your group's problem as well as yours. In short, you'd better make sure that you are repaid for giving up the reliable and proven DD. Now, your ambition may vary, and you may be content with moderate rewards, or exclusively with high payoff.

Practical mastery

While many Wizards don't believe that AE damage spells are any good, some intermediate-level and high-level Wizards derive special pride from having mastered the use of certain AE damage spells for consistent benefits. There's even a pair of possibly-abusive applications of the best AE damage spells, which you will find in the description of Columns and PB spells. Also, at level 52 and beyond, mastering Rains is essential. I hope that this site will, if not make AE damage spells more popular, at least encourage a few more Wizards to test themselves against this insolent magic.
