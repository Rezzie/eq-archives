Functional description

Rains are among the most complex of all damage spells. They are targeted range spells generating 4 "hits" or packets of damage, some (or all) of them dealt immediately, and some possibly delayed (or lost), distributed among up to 4 mobs in a comfortable radius around the target. There are 3 "waves" of hits at intervals of 3 seconds, so, if there are less than 4 mobs in the affected area, 2 or 3 hits may end up stacked on the same mob. The 2nd and 3rd waves are centered on the same spot than the 1st wave, not on the target mob, so, the mobs may move out of the area (for instance, in order to attack you) before the next wave(s) can hit them. You will often hurt yourself with the spell at short range, but the hits inflicted to you don't count in the 4-hit limit. More broadly, when players are affected by a Rain (cast by a mob, or by another player in PvP, or when a player is affected by his/her own Rain), each and every affected player can sustain up to 3 hits. But this is a general rule for AE spells, which doesn't reflect how the spells are supposed to perform when used by players on mobs: the spells are capable of affecting a limited number of mobs plus an unlimited number of players.

Th second most stringent limitation, and the hardest to figure out (I didn't figure it out by myself - I got a hint on a forum in early 2001 and tested it out), is the resistance pattern, which depends on the affected mobs' current hit points. A near-full-life mob has a fair chance to be affected by the Rain, or at least to be affected by the first hit. But, the lowest the mob is on life, the harder it resists, to the point when a mob with only a sliver of life remaining will have a 100% resistance to Rain hits. Rain spells are completely unable to finish off a mob, and are therefore best used in the early fight (when used in mid-fight, a resistance to roughly half the hits is to be expected. Note that Rains are not quite all-or-nothing, but partial resistance to a hit is very rare; on the average, unless used super-early in the fight, only 3 hits out of 4 are scored on blue mobs, or 2,25 hits out of 3, when the spell is used on a single mob). This severe resistance pattern is inherent to Rains, and can't be overcome, even with powerful debuffs: high-level Magicians have tried in vain. But I believe that the resistance has a purpose, which is to prevent Rains from being too good at replacing DD; they are rather meant as an early-fight boon, somewhat close to the concept of "takes away a fraction of the target's hit points" (when the mob is high on life, the damage may be a large fraction of a mob's hit points; when the mob is low on life, the damage tends to be moderate or null).

The cast time is average for an AE spell, but the recast time is long (12 seconds). The aggro from the Rain is said to be markedly inferior, for the damage, than the aggro from DD spells; I believe that this is a general feature of AE spells, but is felt more with Rains, because the hits can stack on a single mob, and more meaningful comparisons with DD can be done.

Note: players used to complain about a so-called "pet bug" (until spring 2001, when it was fixed): if player pets were present in the affected area, the pets were treated as mobs, and used to waste some of the 4 hits which were supposed to be dealt to enemy mobs, even though the pets were immune to the damage; this was diminishing greatly the potential of the spells. No more so: Rains now ignore pets (in non-PvP).

There is also an old false rumor circulating about Rains being usable only outdoors (maybe the Druid counterparts have that kind of limitation).

Visual EFX

Rains create an orange cloud pouring down in the affected area (they COULD have made a blue version; a green version exists for the poison Rains of other classes). The EFX is repeated with the 2nd and 3rd waves. Also, if your character stays still when the 2nd and 3rd waves occur, he/she repeats the arm-waving animation. The EFX is a moderate trial for the graphic cards.

Critic

Fist of all, remember that the spells are for the early fight only, so they won't really "replace" any other spell, whether AE or DD. The ability to stack 3 hits on one mob makes Rains hybrids between AE damage and DD; their efficiency is to be appreciated completely differently, depending on whether 1, 2, 3 or 4 mobs are affected. Strength and damage concentration also increase sharply post-50 versus pre-50: whereas the pre-50 Rains are better left aside (except maybe the level 4 Icestrike), the post-50 Rains are the favourite damage spells of many Wizards. When Kunark was released, and the strong level 52 Tears of Druzzil became available, some level 52 Wizards who had never considered AE damage started hearing about it, tried it, and suddenly went shouting throughout Norrath that AE spells were tremendous (when reminded that the pre-50 Rains are lousy: "don't know, don't care").

When used on 1 mob: Rains are the only AE damage spells which may damage a single mob more than DD, but, due to their functional limitations, this is only when you feel safe enough to provoke the mob in the early fight (sticking to a fighter, or possibly Rooted, but each hit may break the Root). One potential hit out of 4 is lost, but, if 2 or 3 hits provide enough damage and damage/mana, Rains may be worth casting. The average number of hits scored being about 2,25 out of 3 on blue mobs, it is better to check also the damage and damage/mana provided by 2 hits and 2,25 hits, to assess if the Rain is something more than a loser's gamble. The level 4 Icestrike is somewhat respectable for sheer strength; but the level 12-44 Rains are bad, because of insufficient damage and/or insufficient damage/mana (even if you know how to cope with the resistance pattern). The post-50 Rains are very good, both in damage and damage/mana, even with only 2 hits scored; if the resistance pattern wasn't the way it is, they would probably be the true upgrades to Ice Comet, with some functional limitations (Draughts being slow in chain-casting in spite of being quick in single-cast, and having insufficient damage/mana when used on high-hit-point post-50 mobs). Many post-50 Wizards like their Rains so much that they want the functional limitations inherent to AE spells to be alleviated, claiming that the Rains be turned into quasi-DOT spells hitting exclusively the selected target 3 times, regardless of the target's environment. I find this claim inaesthetic (Rains are also meant to be AE spells), and quite hopeless anyway (I'm sure that Verant is happy with the fact that Rains are able to replace DD only part of the time; justice or realism are other matters).

When used on 2 mobs: this achieves the full potential of Rains, with both a true area-effect dimension and fair potential damage per mob. Pre-50, the damage and damage/mana are fair if, and only if, none of the 4 hits is fully resisted (about 10% better than a Column used in the same settings); but scoring 4 positive hits is quite rare, and scoring only 3 hits is disappointing; post-50, it matters less if only 2 or 3 hits are scored (even only 2 full hits generate more damage than Ice Comet).

When used on 3 mobs: this is the weirdest use of Rains, because two of the mobs get one hit and one of them gets 2 hits (and double aggro), so, the results in terms of mob behavior can be quite unpredictable (but see the suggested tactics below). The 2 mobs which get only 1 hit (possibly resisted) are probably not affected by the Rain in a worthwile fashion.

When used on 4 mobs: this use is weak, similar to the use of an Al'Kabor spell, and can't be considered worthwile, even if the 4 hits are fully scored.

Rewards

The tactics involving Rains are rather straightforward, but a few side tactics can be creative.

- The level 4 Icestrike can be used by level 6-7 Wizards in conjunction with Root and Shock of Fire, in order to take down a mob quicker, even if it's not very mana-efficient.

- The post-50 Rains are used to give a sizeable damage/mana boost over DD in the early fight, whenever the functional limitations allow it (possible dispersion of the damage on unwanted targets), at the price of a memorization slot. Some Wizards underline the additional reward of creating less aggro than DD, for the damage.

- Most pre-50 Rains may be used on EXACTLY 2 blue mobs, without consistent hope of benefit versus DD or Columns, but maybe without waste, if the Wizard derives some particular fun from Rains.

- When used on exactly 3 mobs, Rains will generate double aggro on one of the mobs, and, if that mob happens to be one of the additional mobs (not the fighters' main target), there is a greater chance that this mob (and only that one) will be pulled away from the fighters, in view of parking it with Root. Columns are still more likely to cumulate damage/mana and mob control benefits, though.

- When a strong post-50 Rain is used on a single mob having a pet, the pet might be killed with a single hit in the first wave, so that the master mob gets 3 hits (the pet is killed "for free").

Requested improvements

In summer 2000, I tried to lobby (to no avail) for the two following improvements for the unattractive pre-50 Rains:

- enlarge the level 12-44 Rains, so that two hits outdamage the same-rank DD by at least 8-10%,

- give the pre-50 Rains +5% damage/mana, by means of adjusting the mana cost.

Alternatively, Rains should be able to generate more hits (at least 6), in order to perform more aesthetically as AE spells - this could require a downgrade in the damage-per-hit of post-50 Rains, though, and would probably need much reprogramming; I don't hope that such a change will be implemented.
