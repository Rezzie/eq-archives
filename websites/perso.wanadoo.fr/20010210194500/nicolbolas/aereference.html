AE damage assessment principles

Wizard AE spell guide menu - English only - Last updated: 25 October 2000

 

Number-crunching philosophy

There is a huge mass of calculations behind my work, but massive calculations don't prove anything as long as they don't go in the right direction. The essential part of the work is to define the rewards expected from AE damage spells, and to define the numeric criteria:

- accurately reflecting the feeling you have when you use the spells,

- revealing the rewards which you can (or can't) get out of the spells (rewards of which you may have a truncated or inaccurate feeling when you first use the spells),

- allowing to share the impressions and discoveries.

Nothing is final in what I write (I come up with new things and/or change my mind about something every month), but I think I have now gathered one of the best knowledge bases publicized on the topic.

Damage, mana-efficiency, and swiftness

The first thing you need to know about a spell is what kind of effect it has, on whom, and how; so, you will want to have a functional description covering range, radius, number of mobs affected, miscellaneous mechanical features, etc. The functional description has an influence on the assessment, but is only a preliminary.

All damage spells are meant to be lethal in some way or another. Direct damage spells are very lethal in every way you can imagine, but, for instance, you can feel some difference, beyond level 50, between "large" DD (Ice Comet) and "short and mana-efficient" DD (Draughts); the two are lethal in slightly different ways. This is what happens right from the start with AE damage spells: they don't have the standardized, optimized stats of DD spells, they may be lethal in one way and not in another, and you must assess separately their damage, mana-efficiency, and swiftness, if you want to know how they can help you kill your opponents.

Maximum potential and casual potential

The maximum potential of an AE spell depends directly on the number of mobs it is allowed to affect (or on the number of hits it is allowed to generate). Functional features such as range, radius, resistance, and other mechanics, are a measure of the difficulty to achieve the maximum potential.

Some criteria will measure the maximum potential of the spells, and will serve especially the most ambitious Wizards.

Other criteria will measure the easy, casual potential of the spells. I believe that the best way to assess AE damage spells on an even basis for casual play is to consider 3 mobs affected or 3 hits generated. All the AE spells are able to affect reasonably easily 3 mobs or generate 3 hits, and a Wizard will usually not want to affect more than 3 mobs without the backup of a specially briefed group.

Assessing AE versus DD

Your direct damage is your all-purpose first-choice offense.

You want to know in which conditions your AE damage spells can bring you higher (or more specific) rewards than your DD spells (maybe never).

So, the assessment criteria are basically a ratio of certain stats of your AE spells versus certain stats of your DD spells.

You may sometimes want to assess an AE spell versus another AE spell. I can't cover all cases, but some are worth a review (for instance, Al'Kabor spells versus lower-rank Columns; Wizard PB spells versus the PB spells of other classes).

DD spells chosen as reference for assessing AE versus DD (see table below)

Instead of "versus DD", I will often say say "versus Shock", because the Shock spell line is the only genuine, almost-continuous and drawback-free DD spell line (Bolts and all-or-nothing Shocks are scattered in the spell book, and are not genuine drawback-free DD). Problems arise at some levels where the reference is unclear, so, one DD spell will be the main reference, and the other DD spell will be a "backup" reference:

- Level 20: the only available DD (Force Shock) is an all-or-nothing Shock (an unreliable, non-chain-castable spell, with swollen stats, which is not a sound reference). But this disturbs the assessment of only one AE spell (Fire Spiral of Al'Kabor), and doesn't even disturb the assessment much. The level 16 Flame Shock is the "backup" reference at level 20, mostly for assessing chain-cast swiftness.

- Level 49: there are two Shock spells at this level (Ice Comet and Rend). I choose Ice Comet as the basic reference, because it is much more famous, and Rend will be used mostly when Ice Comet fails (spell not yet owned, cold-resistant mobs). Rend is the "backup" reference at level 49.

- Levels 51-59: the only DD upgrade (in strength) to Ice Comet is the level 60 Sunstrike. In the interval, Wizards get DD spells (Draughts) which are weaker than Ice Comet, but tend to enforce new standards of single-cast swiftness and mana-efficiency (at these levels, Ice Comet tends to recede to niche applications such as: chain-casting Ice Comet for highest possible chain-cast swiftness; casting on heat-resistant mobs until the cold-based Draught is gained; casting on Rooted creatures to limit the number of times when Root has a chance to break; kill-stealing, which is normally illegal, but is tolerated in some situations, such as guild events, when the guild is forcing its way through a dungeon to reach an objective deeper inside, and doesn't care much for what happens on the way). So, to assess the level 51-59 AE damage spells, a double assessment is useful, both versus Ice Comet and versus the highest-rank available Draught. Ice Comet is an interesting reference mostly for strength assessment (and chain-cast swiftness assessment, until the better Draught of Ice is gained), and Draughts are interesting references mostly for mana-efficiency and single-cast swiftness assessment. At levels 51-59, I will consider that Draughts are the main reference (so, unless otherwise stated, the ratios are versus Draught), and Ice Comet is the "backup" reference. At level 60, the reference is the bigger and better Sunstrike, which needs no "backup".

Assessing AE versus same-rank DD

You should generally want to use your newest, highest-rank spells, which should benefit you more than your older, lower-rank spells. So, you should prefer to use an AE spell and a DD spell of the highest rank accessible to you. This implies that your preferred AE spell and your preferred DD spell should be of the same rank (the highest !) This is why I am routinely assessing AE spells versus same-rank DD spells. Beyond level 50, "same-rank" should be understood as "highest-rank available" (because there isn't a Draught spell for each spell rank).

But you will not so rarely want to assess an AE spell versus a DD spell of different rank, for instance if you prefer to use a lower-rank damage spell for miscellaneous (and often good) functional reasons, or if you don't own all the damage spells of the highest rank accessible to you (if you didn't do the right thing). I can comment only a few typical such cases. Obviously, assessing an AE spell versus a DD spell of different rank will make you perceive the AE spell's damage criteria differently, and to a lesser extent also the mana-efficiency and swiftness criteria.

Using full spell damage in the calculations

Spells will not always score full damage. But it is difficult to state for sure that some types of spells score full damage (or a high proportion of full damage) more often than others.

I assume that, overall, there is no significant full-damage-scoring frequency difference between Shocks on the one hand, and Columns, PB spells and Al'Kabor spells on the other hand (although there is a suspicion that Al'Kabor is slightly harder to resist). Given this assumption, the assessment of these three types of AE spells versus Shock on the basis of full damage is valid. Exceptions such as Thunderclap and Force Spiral of Al'Kabor (which are area-effect versions of the all-or-nothing, highly-resisted Force Shock) do not introduce much discrepancy.

Rains are different, because they ARE easily resisted (except Icestrike). I try and take this into account by considering that a Rain has the routine potential to generate 3 full hits, not 4, and more attention should be given to the criteria based on 3 hits than to the criteria based on 4 hits.

Using starting damage in the calculations

"Starting damage" is rather self-explanatory: it is the full damage which a spell does when you barely have access to the spell rank. When you later gain levels, the spell's full damage improves a bit.

An AE spell is normally used as soon as it becomes accessible, and during some levels thereafter: until another, better, AE spell of the same line is gained; or until the reference Shock changes, and the new Shock is so interesting that the AE spell doesn't keep up; or until another, better, AE spell of a different line is gained (but, the mechanics of the different spell lines being different, it is potentially difficult to switch). Some AE spells will have a longer lifespan than others.

I use the starting damage of spells in my synthetic tables, because the starting damage is a fairly undisputed figure. But I have made calculations with later damage and maxed damage; the difference is usually very small (less than 0.5% variation in the ratios), and I mention later damage only when it has a particular importance (such as changing the ratios significantly, for instance in the case of Rains).
