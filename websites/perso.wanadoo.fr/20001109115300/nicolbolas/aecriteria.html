AE damage assessment criteria

Wizard AE spell guide menu - English only - Last updated: 25 October 2000

 

Foreword: I am proud to present here an unprecedented comparative overview of the statistics of the four main AE damage spell lines, in addition to explaining the concepts.

If you are unfamiliar with the mechanics of the four main AE damage spell lines (Columns, Al'Kabor, Rains, PB), you should have a short look at the four dedicated pages first, for a functional description. The present page is sort of advanced, not really meant for beginning players (the level 1 and 4 spells have even been excluded from the tables, because they have misleading strong stats, as well as a short lifespan).

If you have any trouble reading the tables below, make sure that you have read the assessment principles first (although I don't think you're obliged to). They explain on what bases the figures are determined. Reminder: the post-50 AE spells are assessed separately against Ice Comet on the one hand, and against post-50 DD (Draughts and Sunstrike) on the other hand. While the assessment versus Ice Comet (up to level 59) may be more profitable for the damage criteria (and possibly for the chain-cast swiftness criteria up to level 56), the assessment versus post-50 DD may be better for the mana-efficiency and single-cast swiftness criteria.

 

A) Damage criteria

What is "size", and does it matter ?

You may feel that your AE spell is "large" ("big") or "small" in many ways. For instance, you may feel that your spell is "large" if it does "large" damage per mob, or a "large" total damage. Or if it consumes a "large" amount of mana. But this is valid only for spells having a kind of standard stat profile: how will you qualify an Al'Kabor spell, which costs much mana but does little damage per mob ? How will you qualify a Rain spell, which may do large damage on one mob, but will generate small total damage (for an AE spell), because of the hit count limitation ? Speaking of "size" in an absolute way isn't very meaningful. "Size" matters mostly when comparing spells within the same spell line, with roughly the same stat profile: the higher level spells in the line will usually be "larger" than the lower level spells, or some spells in the line may be "large" or "small" for their level.

When comparing two functionally different spells, it is more precise to forget about "size", and comment separately on the damage per mob, the maximum damage generated, and the mana cost.

Strength (or its opposite, weakness) indicates if the spell does respectable damage per mob, regardless of cost. The weakest spells do less than half the damage of same-rank DD. Rains are potentially the strongest spells, provided that they hit the same mob 3 times, but this is difficult to achieve, and you can't do the trick on more than 1 mob (Rains not really "AE" damage in this case). Al'Kabor are the weakest spells pre-50, and are as strong as Columns post-50 (but cost is another matter).

B) Mana-efficiency criteria

Mana wouldn't matter much if you could win fights with a single casting of a spell, but it is not so. AE damage spells tend to munch your mana bar faster than DD, while not bringing the mobs down as fast as DD. You'd better make sure that you are doing the right thing, before you are out of mana and powerless.

The concept might seem weird, but damage concentration is a key concept of AE spell assessment (possibly approached through its opposite, damage dilution). Damage dilution is the main inherent drawback of AE damage spells, much more harmful than any chance of backfire. Here is the concept's rationale: assuming that two spells generate the same total damage for the same mana cost, the damage might be "concentrated" on a small number of mobs, or "diluted" among a larger number of mobs. The "concentration" is measured by the damage/mana per mob (versus the damage/mana of DD, of course).

Columns, and Rains stacking 2 hits, tend to create a damage concentration standard, and the standard gets a boost post-50. The spells doing the most diluted damage are: the Al'Kabor spells (in spite of a more modest boost also occurring post-50), the Rains hitting only once, and some PB spells. PB spells have very uneven damage concentration; only Thunderclap beats the same-rank Column. When sacrificing their AE dimension, and being used more as DD substitutes, pre-50, Rains have a slight chance to beat DD in damage/mana with 3 hits stacked; post-50, they tend to beat DD in damage/mana even with only 2 hits stacked, and they become favourites.

There isn't much difference, in most cases, between damage concentration and strength, but the difference is illustrated, for instance, when comparing spells of different ranks: if you use one of your old AE spells of low rank, you will probably perceive it as weak, but it might still do concentrated damage (good damage per creature, for the mana); such spells may have the potential to kill mobs over time with repeated casting, but have low practical value (some of your stronger spells will normally be superior, doing an equal or better job with fewer castings). A few AE spells are rather strong, but do diluted damage (the level 49 PB spell Super Nova, for instance); such spells have some practical value, and can contribute to kill mobs effectively in some cases, performing as over-expensive big-shot spells, but they can't be used alone, even repeatedly, to kill anything but low-green mobs (mana runs out).

Also, damage concentration is a direct measure of how easily a spell allows you to gamble with your damage/mana ratio (the more diluted the spell is, the more mobs you need to hit to earn a damage/mana bonus versus Shock).

You will find more developments on damage concentration in "multiple kill through multiple cast", in the rewards page.

C) Swiftness criteria

Fine-tuned swiftness analysis may not be as important as the assessment of damage and damage/mana, but some crude differences have an importance, because you can't afford to use low-swiftness spells when time is running out. Depending on whether you intend to use an AE spell once in a while or intensively, you will want to look at single-cast swiftness or chain-cast swiftness. "Chain-cast" swiftness is "so to speak", because AE damage spells are not chain-castable, having a hold time. But an AE damage spell with little or no hold time, which would provide a good performance without spell rotation, COULD exist; it doesn't, but Verant might introduce one, and would certainly care for how the chain-cast swiftness is affected.

This is a measure of damage-dealing speed if, for some reason, you want to cast an AE damage spell repeatedly in casual play. In spite of the hold time, Column spells and most PB spells are able to beat the damage-dealing speed of DD when cast repeatedly on 3 mobs. Rains are behind, although better post-50, and Al'Kabor spells catch up only post-50. If you rotate two spells, you can roughly add the figures. By the way, note that Draughts (except the level 57 Draught of Ice) have a lower chain-cast swiftness than Ice Comet (I pointed this out on the official forum, and this was the one and only answer which I got from Gordon Wrinn on a forum: he said that the lower chain-casting speed of Draughts was the price to pay for the fun of single-casting more often than with a big spell).
