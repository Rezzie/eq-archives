Wizards: profile and misconceptions

This text is primarily meant to help beginning Wizards decide if they want to pursue, assuming they have doubts. Wizards have the most extreme strengths and frailties, and are either very pleasant to play or very frustrating depending on player temperament. Read this before investing much time in your Wizard ! The text is organized around four common misconceptions: "Wizards are weak at low level and powerful at high level"; "the direct damage of Wizards doesn't shine that much"; "Wizards can't solo"; and "playing a Wizard is boring".

 

1) "Wizards are weak at low level and powerful at high level"

Yes and no. "Low level" is to be understood as levels 1-11 only. As a true newbie Wizard (that is, without the good mastery of game commands and deluxe equipment which a "twinked" newbie has), you will have a terrible time dying and getting lost repeatedly, at least up to level 5 or 6 (my own piece of advice: spend immediately 3 practice points in Piercing instead of magical skills; buy a Giant Snake Fang from another player for 6 gold; learn how to use the /loc command, and don't forget to program a /loc button and hit it before you die). Experiment with the newbie AE spells, they're actually good for their level (Numbing Cold 1, Icestrike 4, and Column of Frost 8).

At levels 7-11, you have enough mana to start "Root bombing" (blasting mobs while they are Rooted), and you can afford better gear (provided that you refrain from buying immediately all the level 8 spells, which are quite expensive), so you improve rapidly. Also, nowadays, newbies playing in Nektulos or East Commons (or Queynos Hills) make huge money out of High quality bear skins, sold to tailor players for 30 platinum each (that kind of money was unknown in my youth; by the way, I buy the skins, if you ever meet me).

At level 12, you learn the Shock of Lightning spell, and you reach your full direct damage potential. This is (for its level) one of the brightest direct damage spells you ever get (other levels at which you jump significantly forward are 24, 44 and 49). There's also the great comfort of Bind, of course. Try again the level 8 Shadow Step: it has improved since you first gained it, and becomes an effective means of evasion outdoors. Your best investment at these levels is still a good weapon (preferably Dragoon Dirk, or a Runed Totem Staff while you wait, or a Water Crystal Staff for the fun of it).

The next improvements are more discreet, more defensive and commodity-oriented: at level 16 you gain Leatherskin (very important damage-absorbing blanket), Invisibility, Heat Sight (infravision), Bind Sight (good scouting spell), and, at level 20, Tishan's Clash (stun, basically a spell meant to give your fighters a break), together with your first self-only Teleports.

Levels 20-23 are a trial, as far as offense is concerned, because the level 20 direct damage spell is not so good (Force Shock is big, but super-expensive and unreliable; it has more chances of being useful for spell interruption later in your career). Also, this is the time when you start having trouble complementing your magical damage with your melee capabilities (your melee performance becomes ridiculous as creatures grow tougher, and you will want to meditate when not casting, to reduce downtime; it took me much time to accept that). Also, at this stage, levelling up starts getting much slower. The best thing which can happen to you in your 20s is to gain a Solist's Icy Wand, which, although not a "weapon", performs as a monstrous upgrade to any kind of weapon you have known before. This item being very expensive in trade, you might as well choose Kurn's Tower (in Field of Bone, Kunark) as your adventuring target, as soon as you can Bind yourself there and Teleport back to your home base for shopping; maybe you will find a Wand.

At level 24, you're back at the peak of your firepower. The level 24 direct damage spell is good (a bit smaller than Force Shock at first, but reliable), and you have a good AE damage spell (Column of Lightning). You've also got all your self-only Teleports, and Levitate.

At level 29, the true golden years begin. You gain a lot of flexibility and independence with Bonds of Force (area-effect Snare), and you gain your first Portals (group Teleports). While you may focus on gaining any kind of equipment at these levels, I advise to make the Staff of Temperate Flux a priority: this is the brilliant tool with which you can become an artist at manipulating the mobs' hate in the best interest of your group. At level 34, while your direct damage seems to improve normally, you regain AE damage capability in the form of Circle of Force and Thunderclap (which is the most potent AE damage spell in the game, for its level), and you also gain Harvest (self-only mana boost).

I can't really comment on higher levels, but it seems that the choice of new spells beside direct damage gets markedly poorer from level 39 on (also, the AE damage spells are bad). The only spells looking somewhat exciting are the hate-reducing Concussion at level 39 (obtained through a hard quest), which brings the final touch to your distinctive art of manipulating mob hate, and the level 44 Gravity Flux (an AE damage spell which seems rather inefficient, but is fun, and can serve as a short self-only mega-Levitate, such as a "6 million dollar man" jump spell, with the added cost of taking damage). You gain a superb direct damage spell at level 44 (but don't mind your Magician neighbour, who gets almost the same). You become someone important socially at level 46, as the exclusive gatekeeper of the Planes of Hate and of Sky - in addition to being allowed access to the Plane of Fear as everybody else is. The fabled level 49 Ice Comet brings truly unequalled firepower, but there isn't much more in the level 49 spells except the clumsily spectacular Super Nova (big AE damage spell with awful damage/mana). It's possible that, at high level, some of your most valued intermediate-level tactics become too risky to be worthwile, as the mobs become able to kill you in a much shorter time than before, if you fall within their reach. Some high-level mobs even have the distasteful habit of "summoning" you when you tease them too much: they say "You won't evade me XXX", and, pop, you're teleported near them and they tear you to shreds.

Beyond level 50 is another matter. Verant had the dubious idea of introducing direct damage spells (Draughts) smaller and "swifter" than Ice Comet, instead of classic upgrades (in reality, from level 51 to 56 included, these "swift" Draughts inflict damage less swiftly than Ice Comet in chain-casting). The Lures, "almost irresistible" direct damage spells with not-too-bad stats, might be the true novelty. The upgrade to Ice Comet (Sunstrike) comes only at level 60. The post-50 AE damage spells are numerous and hugely boosted versus the pre-50 AE spells. I believe that Verant agreed to let us be masters of AE damage post-50, when they designed the post-50 spells, but they don't want to revamp the very-uneven pre-50 AE for some reason (probably the "don't disturb a gameplay balance which seems fine as it is" kind of thinking). There isn't any new funny concept, except the level 59 Flaming Sword of Xuzl (a temporary low-hit-point "pet" of dubious practical value), and the level 60 Disintegrate (which provides a chance to destroy a mob, leaving no corpse and with no experience gain !) The main issue with post-50 gaming might be that, in your early 50s, you are temporarily extremely powerful against mobs in their high 40s, but weaker and weaker afterwards when fighting level 51+ mobs, the hit points of which tend to rocket beyond what your mana supply allows you to kill (just as your melee weapons become useless in your early 20s, your spells possibly become useless in your mid-50s...) But this is yet unconfirmed, and might be one more manifestation of the usual whining.

To summarize: at any rate, don't look up dreaming to the level 49 Ice Comet spell. There's a rich life in the intermediate levels, and you can start having a taste of it as early as in your teens. Especially, if you play a Wizard of level 12 or higher and you're not satisfied with your offensive powers, learn to like them, or quit playing your Wizard, because your offense won't get better (only your defense and commodity spells will improve).

2) "The direct damage of Wizards doesn't shine that much"

Yes and no. Many Wizards believe that they should be able to outdamage anybody over the course of a fight; they complain that the Magicians' direct damage is almost as effective, and that all casters with pets outdamage Wizards easily. But Wizards are the masters of direct damage - the expression just doesn't have the meaning some want it to have. The comments below include a comparison with the Magician, and are for pre-50 (a few comments on post-50 issues are included in the previous section). Some comments on Wizards versus melee classes are in the 4th section.

Spell availability: Wizards get at least one genuine drawback-free DD spell per spell rank (except level 20, at which they inherit the dubious Force Shock: a big, swollen, unreliable, non-chain-castable, all-or-nothing spell, with a split-second stun which interrupts the spellcasting of mobs). The Bolts (which Wizards get at level 1, 8 and 16) are false DD (with a few specific advantages and mostly drawbacks), but come in addition to the genuine DD. The Wizard firepower also has an even progression in size, although the biggest jumps forward are achieved roughly at levels 12, 24, 44 and 49. On the contrary, Magicians get uneven DD depending on level. They get genuine (and quite decent) drawback-free DD at levels 1, 4, 8, 16, 24, 34 and 44. They get Bolts only (false DD) at levels 20 and 49. They don't get any DD at levels 12, 29 and 39.

As a result, the damage of the Magician's newest (genuine) DD spell fluctuates between 43% and 96% of the Wizard's, depending on whether the spell is real new or already old. The damage/mana of Magicians is more constant: between 86% and 96% of the damage/mana of Wizards. So, the point is not how much damage Magicians and Wizards can generate over time, burning their mana supply; the point is rather how quickly and effectively the damage is homed.

Swiftness: the distinctive skill of Wizards is the ability to throw in damage quickly on demand, either with a single cast, or by means of chain-casting fast and large damage spells over the course of one or two dozen seconds; so we were told, and so it is. Wizards will not so often want to chain-cast more than 2 or 3 spells, because it consumes a lot of mana, and it may not always kill creatures quickly enough to prevent deadly retaliation; but they can do it. The single-cast swiftness (damage / cast time) is a basic measure of DD spell versatility, and Magicians fluctuate between 45% and 86% of the Wizards' single-cast swiftness (their cast time is slightly longer). The chain-cast swiftness (damage / "cast + recast time") is a measure of continuous damage output speed tendency, an indicator of what happens if you decide to go berserk. Assuming the recast time is a standard 2.2 seconds for both casters, Magicians fluctuate between 45% and 89% of the Wizards' chain-cast swiftness (note that the lowest of these Magician swiftness figures are for low-level and for high-level; between levels 16 and 48, the swiftness is fair).

Specialization: all the DD spells of a Wizard are Evocation, whereas the DD spells of a Magician are shared between Evocation and Conjuration (and a Magician will normally specialize primarily in Conjuration). The level 20, 24, 34 and 49 Magician DD spells are Evocation; the absence of mana cost bonus and concentration-regaining bonus must be noticeable at level 34, and quite important at level 49.

Energy type: Wizards have a regular rotation of energy types (cold, heat and magic), so, they can always fall back on some not-too-bad alternate spell, if the first-choice spell encounters a specific resistance. Magicians have a lower versatility, relying mostly on heat-based spells; they have, for a change, magic-based DD only at levels 8, 24 and 44; and they don't have any cold-based spells.

Resistances: at intermediate and higher levels, mobs tend to have a higher average resistance to spells, and players don't score full damage from their damage spells as often as before (but this is felt mostly on the Planes). This was penalizing primarily Wizards, because DD spells are their sole consistent offense; but, in January 2000, Verant trimmed the Wizard damage spells so that they are "inherently less resisted" from level 35 on, and it seems that the fix was satisfactory, at least up to level 50. Magicians have their own approach regarding spell resistances: they enjoy debuff spells from level 24 on (but debuffing takes time and mana). Wizards have a very small free instant debuff (against heat and cold) from their Staff of Temperate Flux (when they gain it). The overall differences in swiftness and average damage scored, taking resistances and debuffs into account, are hard to assess. Note that Intelligence may play a role: most casters don't care to increase their Intelligence beyond 200, because the additional mana isn't worth it; but it is rumored that an Intelligence of around 230 allows the spells to be much less resisted on places such as the Planes. Wizards get a more marked advantage regarding resistances, post-50, with their "almost irresistible" Lure spells.

Resistance cap: This is a soft cap, not a hard cap, but, still, players have practically no chance (maybe 5%) to affect with spells creatures 6-7 levels higher ("deep red" mobs), whereas it is still possible to hurt them with weapons. As a result, Wizards are the most shut-off party members when the big mob comes. The limitation and injustice are serious in theory, but much less serious in practice (unless the Wizard is markedly junior in the party), because groups will almost always fight mobs up to light red only. Take repeated full resistance as a somewhat valuable signal that the mob may be very tough, and warn your group (they won't necessarily tell the difference right away between light red and deep red).

Fixed taunt: as a general rule, spells (damage and non-damage) add a fixed amount of hate on the caster's account in the hate list of their target, regardless of whether the spell was resisted or not. On the contrary, weapons annoy mobs on the basis of inflicted damage only; so, a Wizard will often get pounded down even though his/her spells did little or no damage, while the fighters are busy elsewhere or try in vain to use their "taunt" skill (in this case, the direct damage "shines" the wrong way). The classic mistake (unless you intend to make the mob hate you) is to recast immediately after a fully resisted spell: the mob didn't count the first spell for naught.

Blasting expertise: people will always know what to expect from an experienced Wizard, but they may not always know what to expect from a Magician in terms of blasting. Even when they have a good spell at hand, Magicians may perform as shy blasters because of their lack of personal defense spells, and they must also divide their attention between spell combat and pet management.

Overall "Magician versus Wizard" blasting performance per level: this is a guess taking all the above, also the availability of Bolts, into account.

Levels 1-11: practically equal to Wizards

Levels 12-15: much poorer than Wizards (Shock of Lightning can't be beat)

Levels 16-19: slightly poorer

Levels 20-23: slightly better (Bolt of Flame is a better bargain than Force Shock)

Levels 24-28: practically equal

Levels 29-33: markedly poorer

Levels 34-38: practically equal

Levels 39-43: markedly poorer

Levels 44-48: practically equal

Levels 49-50: much poorer (Ice Comet can't be beat).

In summary, you may feel that your direct damage shines enough, or doesn't, both versus melee types and versus other casters. The topic fuels kilometers of arguing on the forums. But remember that, in the first months of existence of the game, Wizards acquired a very bad reputation as kill-stealers, and Verant felt the need, throughout 1999, to adjust the game balance in favor of fighting classes. This is not to say that Wizards were forgotten in the enhancements; but they were enhanced in other ways than additional firepower. Also, Wizards have inherited most of the spellcasting legend of traditional roleplay (they tend to be thought of as the heirs of the Advanced Dungeons & Dragons' Magic-User Evocation spells, "the real thing" if one wants to play a caster), so, I suspect the class is kept slightly under-attractive in terms of sheer offense, to prevent a rush and allow the other spellcasters to gain respect. Still, Wizards often amaze their comrades with their killing speed. A few Wizards insist that they are so weak that no group could possibly want them, and this is quite a ridiculous attitude.

3) "Wizards can't solo"

I heard this awful misinformation so many times in my youth that I believed it for a long time. Although not among the best characters for solo play (for that, you need a Druid, a Bard, or a spellcaster with a pet - this includes Shamen from level 34 on), Wizards are able to solo effectively, at least much more effectively than fighters. It's widely advised to solo through the "hell" levels (30, 35, 40 and 45); it's supposed to get you quicker through, but I doubt it. The main, undisputed drawback of solo play is that you have no backup in case of trouble (the "unavoidable" trouble being losing connection; a solo caster with a pet may hope that the pet holds off the mob long enough to save its master, but a Wizard can't hope that). Though, I met a Monk-style Wizard having almost exclusively soloed up to level 30 with no equipment (only a Senior Apprentice Robe and a Giant Snake Fang), and happy with his "Wizard power", as he said.

Root bombing

This is the first consistently successful basic form of solo play, which you can use as a teenage Wizard, with a bit of melee between casts (by the way, don't forget to use that Shieldskin spell at level 16). Root bombing at levels 20-23 is very hard; but you can try it again at level 24, although it will be more mana-intensive than in your late teens (because the weapon doesn't help). It becomes easier again in the 30s and up, because you regain concentration from hits much more often when using Root to break free from the mob. At level 29 and after, Root bombing is a fallback option when you try to "kite" and Bonds of Force fails; you will never give up Root bombing completely, because it will still serve you in dungeons, where kiting is impossible.

Guerilla tactics

This is a facilitation of Root bombing. In some dungeons where there are many players keeping the creatures busy overall, you can choose a single opponent when you're ready, while taking advantage of some terrain features. For instance, places with a body of water (the ditch around Crushbone castle, the lake in Mistmoore) allow you to blast an opponent on the other side, so the creature must run around the water to reach you, giving you ample time to blast again and/or Root. Note that finding a spot from which you can blast the mobs without them being able to reach you (such a spot is sometimes found) is considered an exploit and a severe offense; only commonplace terrain features can be legally taken advantage of (by the way, the most commonplace terrain feature of which players legally abuse to avoid defeat is "zoning").

Kiting

Wizards may kite outdoors as Druids do, less safely, but more brutally (no need to wait for the DOT spells to run their course; the whole kill rarely takes more than 40 seconds). You can begin kiting before gaining Bonds of Force (at level 29), provided that you get yourself a Spirit of Wolf (to run faster than the creature, gain sufficient distance, stop, cast, then run again). But, the mob being running at normal speed, you don't have that much time to cast between the moment when the mob comes within Shock range and the moment when it reaches you. Kiting with the level 29 Bonds of Force (slowing the mob down) is much more comfortable, and doesn't require Spirit of Wolf at all (Root or Shadow Step will most often save you in case of trouble), but Journeyman's Boots add even more comfort (if only I had them !) Bonds of Force being AE, you will snare yourself when using it at melee range, so, you have only one safe attempt (Root bombing or genuine escape are your fallback options).

The tricky thing when comparing your kiting capability with that of Druids is the damage/mana of your DD spells versus their DOT spells, and also your mana supply versus theirs. As a general rule, you have enough mana to kill blue-conning mobs and sometimes a white-conning mob, but never a yellow (whereas a Druid will sometimes boast about kiting yellows or even reds. Maybe).

Quad kiting

This is the same as above, but on 3 or 4 mobs at once, with a Column spell. Druids do it too. The Columns are the only range AE spells having enough damage/mana to allow killing low-blue mobs through repeated casting (if you use Al'Kabor spells, the mana runs out even when trying to kill high-greens). Finding the right homogeneous low-blue mobs, regrouping them (so that you don't miss any with the narrow-radius Column), and kiting them to the end, requires more skill and is more risky than regular kiting; personally, I believe this is mostly for the pride of it, and the rewards aren't worth the trouble versus easier forms of solo. Note: so that the mobs stay grouped, the mobs must all be Snared, or none of them; and spellcasting mobs which, once in a while, stop for casting, will never stay grouped. Beware that the level 34 Column is the last one before level 51. Wizards in their early 50s show increased interest in quad kiting, because of an excellent damage/mana of the level 51 Pillar of Frost, versus the hit points of the high-40s mobs.

Choosing the right opponent

There's a lot of trial-and-error in this, of course. But the idea is that you will not necessarily be good at soloing the same mobs which other players are good at soloing.

- Avoid mobs having the bad habit of healing themselves repeatedly, even high-green (you just can't interrupt every heal with stun).

- Avoid mobs having a lot of hit points and hitting heavily for their level, especially for Root bombing; they will exhaust you.

- Avoid mobs having a known high resistance to spells, such as Ogre guards or Dark Elf Warriors (and it seems that all types of crocodiles are particularly resistant to Root).

- Low-blue spellcasting mobs (non-healing) are often easy prey (for instance Gnoll High Shamen, who had better heal themselves more often, and Evil Eyes): you won't mind much their weak spells, while you cast your superior spells (they annoy you more when they melee you). But beware that some caster mobs "con" below their real capability (for instance the Stone Skeletons in Lake Rathetear, or the Goblin Dirtcallers in Frontier Mountains). Opening with Root on the pet (if any) is usually the best thing to do, unless the master is low-level enough so that you can kill it with only two blasts. A cheap trick to get rid of the Rooted greenie pet when its master is dead: run beyond its tracking range, and the poor pet realizes it has no purpose, and commits suicide.

- Venomous mobs (especially spiders) are often easy prey, because they are usually slow hitters, with few hit points, and your Skin spell prevents the poisoning while it holds.

4) "Playing a Wizard is boring"

Many people were told (and believed) that Wizards were "very good at one thing" (blasting) and nothing more. Also, Wizards are the most mana-dependent class, so they "meditate all the time". Doesn't it sound boring ? But any class is boring, when played passively. I suspect that playing a Wizard has the potential to be much more exciting than playing a Warrior, a Magician, or a Cleric, at least up to level 50. Beyond level 50 might be another matter, but, the game environment being subject to frequent changes, you shouldn't worry too much about what your condition might be in a remote future. Overall, Wizards have a carefully balanced array of gameplay attributes including direct damage, Teleport, area-effect damage, mob incapacitation, and prevention/evasion. Not only is Teleport a powerful anti-boredom, but the versatile offense and defense of Wizards also give them an interesting multiple nature in combat: magical fighter at one end, aggressive Cleric at the other end, and taunt-artist Enchanter inbetween. Wizards can navigate between these roles, to perform best, depending on mood and circumstances.

- Acting as a "magical fighter" is fighting front-line and continuously to achieve victory, using magical offensive and defensive powers instead of weapons and armor. A Wizard performs as a magical fighter at least when playing solo.

- Acting as an "aggressive Cleric" is being a support character, focusing on keeping the group healthy (not by means of healing, but by means of blasting down excess sources of harm, including the indirect menaces represented by mobs fleeing to call their friends, and by means of possibly providing the ultimate evasion of Evacuate), sitting most of the time, and sometimes standing up to cast (most often at about the same moment when a Cleric would stand up to cast). This role isn't really passive, but is discontinuous, and not that much front-line: it's something like air forces supporting ground forces (supportive bombing, and troop transportation). This is probably what people mean when they say "boring", but it's arguable.

- Acting as a "taunt-artist Enchanter" is a compromise: being a support character, but in a front-line and continuous way, putting emphasis on defensive powers consuming little mana. Whereas Wizards don't have the numerous specialized tools allowing Enchanters to genuinely shut off mobs, Wizards are very good at fooling the mobs by means of combining basic incapacitation tools and an unequalled capacity for aggro control. You might not have realized that the "taunt" skill of fighters is in reality a defensive skill (protecting others from harm); learn that Wizards are the best taunters in town. The "art" is to taunt the mobs unpunished, like a bullfighter does (I even fancy wearing a provocative fiery red robe, although the stats are poor for my level; I doubt that the mobs make a difference, but I like it, and it gives me the right mindset).

Obviously, participating front-line and continuously in fights is more exciting than sitting behind as support and backup; being magical fighters is what Wizards sign for when they begin their career. But there's a tendency to shift towards being a support character as level improves. It's a double challenge for Wizards, as they grow older, to invent new exciting ways of fighting (when the older ways lose their breath), and to learn to appreciate being a support character part of the time, focused on defense and protection possibly at the cost of some offensive thrill.

Of course, a player who wants to be active as continuously as possible without investing much in improving his/her playing style should play a fighter, and a player who needs a plentiful array of spells to be happy should choose a Necromancer, Enchanter or Druid. But the diversity of Wizard spells is more than decent enough to fill the eight memorization slots, and the combat choices of a Wizard are stimulating (check my sub-essay on "exciting" Wizard fighting).

Nicolbolas Whizz (Aargh)
