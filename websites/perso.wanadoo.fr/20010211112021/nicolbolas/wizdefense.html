Detailed defensive abilities of Wizards

Back to the AE spell guide introduction

English only - Last updated: 21 January 2001

1) Mob incapacitation (active defense): (reminder) "Mob incapacitation" is defined as all means to affect mobs so that they temporarily can't cause harm to you and/or your allies ("all means" short of immediate and permanent death, which is a plain application of DD or AE damage). "Mob incapacitation" is offense by nature (because you are doing something to the mobs), but it is defense by destination (the outcome is that some harm is avoided). The champion of mob incapacitation is the Enchanter, but Wizards deal in several ways to incapacitate mobs: movement-hampering, stun, and aggro control. Wizards don't have, for instance: Charm, Fear, Harmony, Mesmerize (defined as a very long stun which is broken if the mob takes damage), and pets (which, in addition to being continuous damage sources, perform as defensive lures keeping mobs busy, i.e. preventing the mobs from attacking players).

Root: Root (lvl 4), Enstill (lvl 20), Immobilize (lvl 39), Paralyzing Earth (lvl 49), Fetter (lvl 58). Many classes have the superbly versatile Root, but only Wizards can't live without it, and have the incentive to become Root artists. Root is the Wizards' primary means of breaking free from melee. Beware that the spells have two distinct profiles, a "versatile" profile (Root, Immobilize) with short cast time and low mana cost, and a "double duration" profile (Enstill, Paralyzing Earth), which is of much less use because of the annoying added cast time and mana cost. From most testimonies, Root and Enstill have the same chances of sticking, and the same chances of breaking from magical damage (although I believed, for a very long time, that Enstill broke more easily). Melee damage doesn't seem to break Root at all. The Rooted mobs are harmless, provided that there is none of your allies within mob reach (unless the mobs also enjoy range attacks, such as spells, or, worse, can Summon). The first Root-based tactic which Wizards learn is "Root bombing": comfortably casting DD spells on a Rooted mob. Even when the level 29 Bonds of Force spell is gained (see "AE Snare" below), "Root bombing" still prevails in dungeons, and as a possible fall-back option outdoors when Bonds of Force fails. Now that (January 2001) Root doesn't overwrite Snare anymore, and does its own effect separately, Root helps, in conjunction with Snare, to give some meditation time near the end of a tough kiting. The second important Root-based tactic is "mob parking": Rooting one (or more) mobs somewhere away from where the fight happens. "Mob parking" is functionally similar to Mesmerize, although more complicated and much less powerful. Root can also be used for "runner pin-down", but is mediocre for that purpose (see "AE Snare" below).

AE Snare: Bonds of Force (lvl 29), Atol's Spectral Shackles (lvl 51). These spells are AE, that is, they are more expensive than single-mob Snare spells, and they can backfire; still, they are worth using on a single mob, and their AE feature can be put to use reasonably often. Note that I never succeeded in affecting more than 3 mobs at once with Bonds of Force, although some seem to think it can affect 4. The first important Snare-based tactic is "kiting", especially outdoors: the mob will be incapable of harming you, because you run faster than it does (of course, long-range attacks such as spell attacks won't be fully shut off). The second important Snare-based tactic is "runner pin-down": when a mob's life is so low that it tries to run, Snare effectively pins it down to the ground, and the mob still doesn't fight and can't go call other mobs for help, so it's an important contribution to a group's cohesion and safety. Furthermore, if you Snare the mob early in the fight, you get the added benefit that, if you later over-blast the mob, it can't reach you immediately for its revenge.

Stun (hybridated with some damage): Tishan's Clash (lvl 20), Markar's Clash (lvl 49), Tishan's Discord (lvl 51), Thunderbold (lvl 54), Markar's Discord (lvl 56). Note that Thunderbold is an AE damage spell, with a very interesting mix of damage and stun length. Stun shuts off a mob completely for some time. Wizards are probably the third best stunners in the game, after Enchanters and Clerics. In a static group fight, stun basically prevents some melee damage from being inflicted by the mob to the fighters, and also allows to interrupt the mob's spells. Interrupting spells is functionally a defensive move, preventing something unpleasant from happening (although it is more offensive, even functionally, when the interrupted spell is a heal). In solo play, you have no fighters to protect, so, stun spells will be memorized only when fighting a caster/healer mob (good luck, because they are tough !) Tishan's Clash is also one of the rare spells with which Wizards can assist a low-level player without stealing the kill.

Short stun and pushback (side effect of damage): Force Shock (lvl 20), Thunder Strike (lvl 29), Force Strike (lvl 44). These spells are essentially damage spells with all-or-nothing effects and relatively high resistance, so, their large mana cost is often wasted, and they're not very popular; they will be used almost only against caster/healer mobs, because the split-second stun interrupts spells without possibility of regaining concentration. The pushback can also, rarely, push a mob off a ledge. Two AE damage spells also have this effect before level 50: Thunderclap (lvl 34) and Force Spiral of Al'Kabor (lvl 39), but the latter isn't worth using. A few other spells have a pushback effect without split-second stun, either on players and mobs (but it is difficult to perceive for sure), or on players only. Those spells affecting mobs are supposed to be Cast Force (lvl 24), Shock Spiral of Al'Kabor (lvl 29), and the post-50 Jyll's series of PB spells. Those spells affecting players only (in PvP) seem to be the lightning-based Shocks and Fire Spiral of Al'Kabor (lvl 20). Pushback without stun may cause spell interruption, but concentration may be regained.

Evade (or hate reduction): Concussion (lvl 39). While Concussion doesn't make mobs physically impotent, it tampers with their ability to choose you as their opponent: it tends to make them "incapable" of attacking you, at your allies' expense. Concussion seems to carry a fixed hate increase (but small, smaller than Root, for instance), and seems to remove a fraction of the mob's hate towards you when not resisted. So, it cannot ever make the mob forget you completely, and a resist may be dangerous (but extremely rarely).

Taunt (side effect of various abilities): hate increase tends to make the mobs incapable of attacking your allies, at your own expense. Wizards are the best taunters in town, thanks to their damage spells and their Skin spells (and the free bonus aggro from the Staff of Temperate Flux). The Wizards' swift and consistent power to enrage mobs mustn't be underestimated: when combined correctly with other defenses, it greatly improves a group's health and safety.

Frenzy phase skip (effect of damage timing): mobs are said to go berserk like Warriors do, and inflict heavier wounds when, roughly, their life goes below 2 bubbles ("frenzy phase"). When the mobs' life runs even lower, they go into running phase (but some mobs are very brave, and never run; they must be killed outright). Wizards can time their DD spells so that the frenzy phase is skipped, and the fighters are spared the extra frenzy damage, at no additional mana cost. I was never able to measure this exactly, though; I treat it rather a rumor which makes sense. By the way, as a young Wizard, I was happy when my Shock killed the mob; as an intermediate-level Wizard, I am rather disappointed when I score the kill, because this means that a fraction of my mana was wasted: I like it better when my Shock causes the mob to turn its back and try to flee.

Runner finish-off (effect of damage timing): if the runner can't be pinned down, kill it with a quick DD. This is not "mob incapacitation" as I defined it to start with, because it is immediate death; but "runner finish-off" deserves to be cited here, as an alternative to "runner pin-down" with identical rewards in terms of group cohesion and safety (the fighters don't have to venture far away, and the mob can't call for help), and can also be cited as a combat phase skip functionally similar to the "frenzy phase skip".

Debuffing: Cancel Magic (lvl 12), Nullify Magic (lvl 34), Annul Magic (lvl 53). "Debuff" has two meanings in this game, the first being "put a curse on a mob", and the second being "remove a buff from a mob"; the second meaning only is relevant here. This "debuff" is most often used for defense (usually removing a damage shield from a mob, or removing a Charm from a party member) rather than for facilitating offense (removing protective shields; this is often done in PvP, but can also be done profitably on some mobs). Any spellcaster can do that kind of debuff, so, this is only a side ability of Wizards; but note that the Intelligence-based casters get the upgrades sooner than the Wisdom-based casters (and the upgrades are bright; one cast of Nullify Magic seems to do more than three casts of Cancel Magic).

2) Prevention / evasion (passive defense): "Prevention / evasion" spells are pure defense: they don't do anything to any mobs, but, still, their outcome is that some harm is avoided. "Prevention / evasion" spells are many, and some of them are exciting.

Shielding: Minor Shielding (lvl 1), etc. These spells (giving armor class, hit points, and resistance to Magic) are hardly worth mentioning, because all spellcasters are able to generate that kind of buff, and they might as well be considered part of the inherent stats of the class, especially when the spells are self-only. The good news is that they can be cumulated with most other protective buffs given by Clerics and Druids.

Skin spells: Shieldskin (lvl 16), Leatherskin (lvl 24), Steelskin (lvl 34), Diamondskin (lvl 44), Manaskin (lvl 52). These expendable damage-absorbing shields are brilliant, and allow Wizards to show more bravery than Magicians would ("skin tanking"). The Skin spells will absorb melee damage, but not falling damage. They will absorb most forms of direct magical damage, including Harm Touch, but they will not absorb magical DOT (as a separate effect, they will protect against melee DOTs such as poison, because the DOT won't even attempt to stick as long as no melee damage is taken). It is disputed whether the Skin spells help regain concentration from hits or not; at any rate, they don't protect against the stun from bash attacks. I believe that the Skin spells help against interruption because the hits create a smaller pushback. Under continuous attack, the Skin spells will usually not hold for more than one or two handful seconds, but this extra time is precious for breaking free with minimal wounds. Skin spells also act as an alarm signal: as long as hits are absorbed, you don't even have to watch your life bar, and you can concentrate on what you're doing; as soon as you start taking damage, it's time to be more careful and emphasize evasive action. Beware that refreshing the Skin spell generates high aggro. Skin spells are a must for solo play, and bring much relief in group (because your safety is roughly assured, and you can allow yourself to care more for the safety of others; the group's healers will also not be distracted by a dropping life bar of yours, unless the situation is serious). Necromancers have the same spells, but generally under-utilize them, because their pet suffices as a lure most of the time; and Enchanters have similar spells which can be transferred (Runes). By the way, the fact that the Skin spell is self-only is a kind of advantage in combat, because you can refresh it without losing your target.

Invisibility: See Invisible (lvl 4), Invisibility (lvl 16), Invisibility vs Undead (lvl 39), Improved Invisibility (lvl 55). Almost all character classes can turn Invisible in some way. Invisibility is acquired by Wizards moderately late, and is an enormous factor of safety when travelling or meditating. The champions in this field are the Enchanters, who have not only various forms of Invisibility, but Illusions as well.

Levitate: Levitate (lvl 24). Wizards are not the best levitators (short duration, no side benefit), but Levitate is great for preventing falling damage in mountain zones and pit-infested dungeons, and for flying high across some mob-populated zones while staying vertically beyond mob aggro range. Levitate sometimes helps you escape from opponents long enough to Gate (by means of flying out of a building window, over water, or off a ledge).

Yonder: Fade (lvl 4), Shadow Step (lvl 8), Yonder (lvl 29)

This quick and random intra-zone Teleport becomes almost obsolete as soon as you have Journeyman's Boots (except in duels), but can save you from unwanted combat outdoors, if you're lucky enough to re-appear far away enough to Gate. Try doing it twice in a row, if you're not sure that you're far away enough (you have MORE than 1 chance out of 2 to increase the distance with the second cast, that's a simple case geometry involving circles). The spell will never move you uphill (any upward slope or wall will block you), so, if you rely on this spell for evasion, stay close to the hill tops.

Sight spells: Sphere of Light (lvl 1), Glimpse (lvl 4), Eye of Zomm (lvl 8), Gaze (lvl 12), Halo of Light (lvl 16), Bind Sight (lvl 16), Heat Sight (lvl 16), Sight (lvl 20), Magnify (lvl 29), Chill Sight (lvl 39), Shifting Sight (lvl 39), Plainsight (lvl 55), Eye of Tallon (lvl 57). Although all these spells have very different mechanics, they all serve the same purpose: improved sight is seeing ahead or scouting, and scouting gives you useful information regarding your safety. Some sight spells are respectable: the light-generating spells are useful to Erudites and Humans; Bind Sight allows effective long-distance scouting and inventory of a mob-infested site (in addition to letting you watch places and mobs which your level doesn't allow you to approach), but is not effective in all zones (sometimes, the vision you get is frozen). Eye of Zomm is useful for exploring pits and falls, detecting pit traps, and finding a mob to lock as a target, so that you can Bind your Sight to it. The Eye can even open doors, and its drawback of pulling mobs (which is more or less severe depending on zones) can sometimes be turned into an advantage. There is a minor ridiculous trick with the Eye, which you can use if you are grouped with a Shadowknight or Necromancer currently low on life : the Eye is not protected against player attacks like other pets are, so, it can be lifetapped at no risk (but afterwards you can say "ouch, my eye !")
