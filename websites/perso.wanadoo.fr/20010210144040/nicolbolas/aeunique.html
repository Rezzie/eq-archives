AE damage spells scattered in the book

Wizard AE spell guide menu - English only - Last updated: 29 November 2000

These are two mini-spell lines (Gravity Flux, Damage shield) of two spells each, and a unique spell (Thunderbold) which could be described as an "AE Tishan's Clash". I don't cover AE Snare (Bonds of Force, Atol's Spectral Shackles) for the moment, because I focus on damage spells. Likewise, some other classes have other non-damage AE spells (AE Mesmerize, AE stun, AE Fear) on which I will not comment (but I believe that most of them are very good).

Functional description

Gravity Flux is a targeted range spell, which affects a number of creatures of which I am not sure yet (I succeeded with 5, and I read a long time ago that the number was unlimited) in a moderately wide radius around the target. The first step is immediate "magical" damage, and the second step is either almost-immediate "magical" damage (for mobs) or almost-immediate falling damage (for players; outdoors, the falling damage may be more than double the magical damage sustained by mobs). When you cast the spell on yourself, or on a mob on which you have cast Bind Sight, you will distinctly see yourself (or the mob) rise rapidly to above-tree-top height, then fall down, in a very short time (about one second and a half). When you cast the spell normally on mobs (without Bind Sight), you will often not be able to see the mobs go up and down (the movement is too short to be displayed, considering ordinary lag), and the two damage messages will be almost simultaneous. In my calculations, I consider that the two damage steps are as good as simultaneous. Affected mobs and players can move during the short super-levitation. However, you can't combine Gravity Flux with Levitate to both jump real high and escape the falling damage; Gravity Flux will make you bounce upwards only very slightly if you are Levitating. The cast time and recast time (10-12 seconds) are long. The spell is all-or-nothing, but the resistance frequency doesn't seem to be much of a nuisance.

Visual EFX

It is the same as for Levitate, and is only a light trial for the graphic cards.

Critic

My comments are mostly for Gravity Flux, of which I have first-hand experience, because I doubt that my information on the upgrade is complete. Gravity Flux is far from having the same good damaging performance as Columns (although it is gained at a level at which we could have expected to gain a Column), but is still way better than the level 39 Al'Kabor spell, doing fair damage per mob, and giving a symbolic damage/mana bonus for 3 mobs affected. The level 34 Circle of Force, although weaker (216 damage versus 314), may still be preferred because of the better damage/mana per mob (1.23 ratio versus 0.93) and quickness. Gravity Flux being all-or-nothing is also a drawback. But it is still worth an occasional use, if only for a change, it is very interesting for jumping (see below), and the capacity to affect any number of mobs (unverified) can't be quite neglected, so, it has a potential overall. The level 59 Invert Gravity, however, looks surprisingly weak (less damaging than lower-level Columns ? This is why I didn't bother to calculate the ratios).

Research tip

The spell was given to me as a present, and I couldn't check if the alarming rumor regarding Rune of Attraction was true. Some say that there are two undistinguishable versions of Rune of Attraction (which do not stack), and that only the Runes found on high-level mobs can serve to research the spell. This is annoying, because the other Runes (especially Rune of the Inverse) are relatively rare, and should not be wasted. I don't believe the rumor much, because I never found a Rune of Attraction which would not stack with the others, and there are other cases of a single Rune serving to research several spells of different levels (for instance Al'Kabor). It is also said (I have read first-hand testimonies, but such things can change overnight in a patch) that you can scribe the level 39 scroll made by an Enchanter, even if the scroll states that only Enchanters can use it; the left and right parts of Salil's Writ pg. 174 are not too difficult to find.

Casual rewards

- Gravity Flux allows most applications of Columns, except quad-kiting (long cast time, insufficient damage concentration).

- The spell has a very special and unique potential as a 6-million-dollar-man jump, allowing brilliant shortcuts in some places. As an example, I was able to gain the Concussion spell as soon as level 39, by means of "dueling" a high-level Enchanter who Gravity-Fluxed me up the walls of City of Mist. As another example, in dungeons where the way in and the way out are normally different because of pitfalls (Kaesora, Droga), Gravity Flux allows you to go in and out the way you want.

- Funny spell to use for massive thrills in the Arena.

- Particularly deadly and disturbing pre-50 AE spell to use on PvP servers. The fact that the second damaging step follows the rules of falling damage is probably a rare occurrence of a spell able to cause more damage in PvP (outdoors only) than in PvM. The drawback that the spell affects allies might be overcome if your allies wear all their "save magic" gear and buffs (they may be unscathed thanks to the spell being all-or-nothing) and/or have Levitate on.

Gravity Flux of other classes

Wizards share Gravity Flux with Enchanters, who get it sooner (level 39).

Functional description

Thunderbold is a targeted range spell, which immediately damages and stuns a number of creatures of which I am not sure, but probably 4, in a moderately wide radius around the target. The stun lasts a few seconds, like for the "Tishan's Clash" series of stun spells, but I do not know how the stun duration compares with the single-mob spells of similar level (about which there are conflicting reports; I suspect that the stun duration also depends on the mobs). The spell is all-or-nothing, with a frequency which should be similar to that of the single-mob stun spells. The cast time is unusually short, and the recast time unusually long (18 seconds). The aggro from the spell is probably above-average for an AE spell, because of the stun feature (but, as usual, if the stun is successful, your fighters have a few seconds to build up more hate and retain the mobs).

Visual EFX

I never saw it; I suspect it is a multiple version of the Tishan's Clash EFX.

Critic

A creative spell at last, giving an area-effect dimension to a stun spell which we have learnt to cherish. The damaging performance is almost as good as that of a Column, and the single-cast swiftness is very good. The stats are good enough so that the drawback of being all-or- nothing is compensated. The stun is a strong defensive component, giving the spell good value and versatility.

Casual rewards

- Thunderbold allows most applications of Columns, except quad-kiting (the damage concentration is a bit low, and the mob pack gets split between stunned and non-stunned). Especially, I believe that it is a good crowd control tool, not only because the stun gives your fighters a break, but also because, if used skillfully early in the fight, a behavior difference may be created between the mobs in order to split the pack, depending on whether they resist or not (you're supposed to rely on previous Snare or subsequent Root to deal with the mobs which resist the Thunderbold and come for you).

- Thunderbold may have a specific potential on PvP servers as a quick opening spell on a surprised enemy party, because the stun will give your party some time to gather and take advantage of the surprise.

Functional description

Damage shields are buffs which are not by nature AE spells, but perform as AE spells, because they affect several creatures around a player or pet - provided that the creatures attack that player or pet. Damage shields are undercover AE spells, making fighters happy with gaining automatic retaliation (each time a creature succeeds in hitting the fighter, it receives, in return, a fixed damage which cannot be resisted). Amusingly, low-green mobs tend not to be affected by the shield, because they miss their hits. Most of the damage is, of course, delayed, and the damage does not improve with level (at least for the level 4 Wizard spell; damage shields of other classes seem to improve). The aggro generated by the spell is generated when the buff lands on the target player or pet, and there is NO ADDITIONAL AGGRO when the shield deals damage (neither toward you, nor toward the player or pet wearing the buff), so, if you cast the spell before the mobs are aware of your presence, the spell will not make creatures mad at you at all. The damage done by the shield actually has no owner, and creates no ownership rights on the mob (experience rights and looting rights); a mob killed exclusively by means of a damage shield vanishes without giving experience or leaving a body.

Visual EFX

Very discreet, and occurring only at the time of casting, not with every damage packet generated by the shield.

Critic

The level 4 damage shield for Wizards has long looked like a kind of aborted experience, made only to teach us how these spells (abundantly used by Magicians and Druids) function. Now, there seems to be an intermediate-level upgrade coming with Scars of Velious in late 2000. The spells being able to distribute an indefinite quantity of hits to an indefinite quantity of mobs for a certain duration, with uneven distribution, my classical assessment criteria are not much valid, and what matters really is how many hits will be needed to equal or beat Shock in damage and damage/mana (you will normally want to get much more out of the spell than out of a standard Shock, or it's not worth the trouble). Whereas the level 4 spell is fair, I think that my information for level 34 is wrong (5 damage is extremely weak, see the other classes below; I suspect the damage is actually 15).

Casual rewards

This spell line is not important enough, for Wizards, to deserve much of my thought. Maybe the heat resistance bonus is of use against a few mobs.
