AE damage rewards

Wizard AE spell guide menu - English only - Last updated: 25 October 2000

This is a thematic inventory of rewards, meant for players taking special interest in the topic. The down-to-earth reward descriptions are in the line-specific pages (mostly Column, Al'Kabor, Rain, PB).

 

A) Affecting many mobs, generating lots of damage (and my golden rule of AE spell assessment)

Affecting many mobs

Affecting several mobs is what is primarily expected from AE spells. However, only PB spells (and maybe Gravity spells, I'm not sure) can affect mobs in real large (unlimited) numbers; the other spells have a hit count, with a limit inherent to the spell line. Column spells can affect easily up 3 mobs, and 4 mobs with difficulty. Al'Kabor spells can affect easily up to 5 mobs. Rain spells can rather easily affect up to 4 mobs, but are probably the least rewarding AE spells in terms of affecting many mobs, because they produce strong effects only when used on 1 or 2 mobs (they are hybrids between DD spells and AE spells).

Now, if affecting many mobs is a primary expectation, is it a reward in itself ? Obviously, it depends on how each mob is affected, and on how the effects (especially damage) add up.

Generating lots of damage

An AE spell usually inflicts less damage on a single mob than a DD spell. But adding up the damage done to several mobs may give a figure comfortably higher than the damage done by DD. This maximal damage figure should be handled with extreme caution, because damage has variable value. Some spells can generate lots of high-value damage in some settings (and this is excellent), whereas other spells (or the same spells in other settings) will generate lots of low-value damage only. Low-value damage is damage which you don't care to inflict: mainly weak, grazing damage, irritating the mobs without bringing them noticeably closer to death, or damage inflicted to a low-green mob, on which you wouldn't want to waste a DD (your fighters can dispose of those with minimal sweat expense), and also damage inflicted to a particularly dangerous mob, which you don't want to provoke before your fighters tackle it directly.

My golden rule of AE spell assessment

Affecting many mobs and/or generating lots of damage is rewarding if, and only if, the spell does something worthwile to each mob.

 

B) Brutal kill

Depends on: strength and other criteria

Doing strong damage per mob is a general reward of all damage spells; Wizards tend to like the spells doing the highest damage, and rightly so. Strong spells need to be cast a relatively small number of times to produce a result, so, strength is also a condition of swiftness in general (a weak spell may have a good single-cast swiftness, but only a strong spell will have a good chain-cast swiftness).

With respect to strength, AE spells are generally not very rewarding: with the controversial exception of Rains, a "strong" AE spell does about 75% of your strongest Shock's damage per mob; be content with it.

One big shot

Depends on: strength and damage multiplier

Even with a bad mana-efficiency and a bad swiftness, a spell may help you score a "brutal kill", provided that it is strong. A high damage multiplier tells you that you can score mass "brutal kills" with the spell, but if and only if the spell has good strength. The typical example is the level 49 PB spell Super Nova. A weak AE damage spell with excellent chain-cast swiftness could do the same trick through repeated cast ("bunch of quick shots" instead of "one big shot"), but there is none available.

Finish one and proceed to next

Depends on: strength and single-cast swiftness

This is one of the easiest and most discreet AE tactics. If the spell is strong and swift enough, you can time the casting so that the spell significantly helps your fighters finish off their current target, while starting to damage the additional mobs. This is not as brutal as using DD for finishoff, so, it is worthwile mostly if combined with a damage/mana reward.

Standing-in for DD

Depends on: all criteria, but mostly strength and damage concentration

AE spells may serve you more if you have no suitable (reliable and strong) DD for your level. Most Rains are potentially stronger than DD on 1 mob, but you will reject Rains because they're slow and unreliable, unless the strength difference versus your DD is important (the difference is quite important at level 4 and post-50). The same is somewhat true for other AE damage spells post-50: their use is encouraged by the absence of strong new DD.

However, unless a mere single-cast contribution is expected from you, "standing-in for DD" also requires that you can significantly help the kill through repeated cast without wasting your mana, so, damage concentration is important too. Depending on circumstances, swiftness may also be a factor.

 

C) Multiple kill through multiple cast

Depends on: damage concentration, and also chain-cast swiftness

Even if you don't intend to solo with an AE spell, measuring its single-handed kill potential is the hard approach for assessing how lethal it is. Even in a group, you are supposed to be able to pour a nice quantity of damage in the hard fights, and this often implies that you are able to kill (or at least severely wound) a mob single-handed when necessary. Leaving aside swiftness and defensive issues, you can do that only by making the most out of your mana; but playing AE damage spells is not always using your mana wisely.

Damage concentration is the AE damage/mana per mob, versus the damage/mana of DD. You might wonder why bother with the "damage/mana per mob": are AE spells supposed to be used on only one mob ? Here's my answer: if you want to kill a pack of mobs with an AE spell, the spell must be able to kill each mob separately (adaptation of my golden rule of AE spell assessment).

As a result, you WANT to know if your mana supply allows you to kill the considered mobs through exclusive, repeated use of your AE spell (assuming you have enough time and you are safe). The answer lies is damage concentration, and more clearly than you may think: an AE damage spell is able to kill a pack of mobs (with repeated use) if, and only if, the spell's damage concentration (%) is at least equal to the proportion of mana bar (%) which must be consumed to kill one of the mobs with DD.

Example 1: if you can kill a given mob with DD consuming 70% of your mana bar, a Column spell with a damage concentration of 70% will allow you to kill several such mobs at once instead of one, but you will consume all your mana instead of 70%.

Example 2: an Al'Kabor spell with a damage concentration of 40% may kill only the kind of mobs which you can kill with 2 bubbles of DD, that is, green mobs.

Boring math proving the above: let's say that you must consume N% of your mana bar to kill a mob with DD. You can look at it the other way: consuming your full mana bar, you are able to kill 100/N such mobs with DD. So, the damage/mana of your DD spell can be thought of as "100/N mobs killed per full mana bar". If you want to use your AE spell instead (and kill any allowed number of these mobs at once), you want to make sure that you are able to kill ONE mob by means of consuming your full mana bar, so, you want your AE spell to have a damage/mana of at least "1 mob killed per full mana bar". So, the damage concentration of your AE spell (your AE spell's damage/mana per mob versus your DD spell's damage/mana) must be at least "1 mob killed per full mana bar" / "100/N mobs killed per full mana bar", that is, 1/100/N, or N/100, or N%, QED.

An AE spell doing such diluted damage that low-blue mobs can't be killed with repeated cast should provide HUGE side rewards to be attractive versus other damage spells.

While the key criterion, for killing with repeated cast, is damage concentration, chain-cast swiftness also has practical importance (poor chain-cast swiftness comes from insufficient strength and/or long cast and recast times): having enough mana in store is fine, but killing the mobs before they can do excessive harm is even better, and you will somehow run out of time if your magic is too slow.

The exemplary records of killing mobs exclusively with repeated AE damage spells (made possible by high damage concentration and fair swiftness) are: Column quad-kiting, PB gang bang (so I call it), and post-50 Rains standing-in for DD.

 

D) Swift kill

Depends on: single-cast swiftness and/or chain-cast swiftness

Verant often insists that Wizard spells should shine more with their swiftness than with their sheer damage or mana-efficiency. Wizards have a rather clear perception of the swiftness of their DD spells (even if they like damage most); not so for AE spells. Swiftness is possibly the most vaguely perceived dimension of the AE damage spells, probably because the AE spells very rarely have an equal or shorter cast time than DD spells, and furthermore have a hold time (they aren't chain-castable), so they have an overall reputation of being slow. As an application of my golden rule of AE spell assessment, the spells can bring a reward in terms of mass swiftness if, and only if, they damage each creature reasonably swiftly; in other words, you may select the less clumsy spells, and try and take advantage of their fair swiftness on mass scale. Rewards usually don't depend mainly on swiftness criteria, but the swiftness criteria have a side importance for most rewards (see above and below).

AE spell rotation

An old trick to overcome hold time (and create an imitation of chain-casting) is to rotate two or three AE spells. The mechanical and size differences don't allow every AE spell to be rotated effectively with every other, but some combinations are fruitful (especially PB and Columns; post-50 Rains can possibly be rotated with DD). Usually, one of the spells will be better than the other(s), and the other(s) will fill in the gap during the best spell's hold time.

 

E) Damage/mana gambling

Depends mostly on: 2-hit and 3-hit damage/mana bonus

This is a basic motivation for using AE spells: generate more damage than DD for the mana, with tolerable added personal exposure and tolerable loss of brutality. This was my main focus when I first took interest in AE spells; but, with time, I came to appreciate other rewards more (especially "mob herding" below). Note that I'm not covering here the narrow high-risk-high-gain applications (I think this is rather "multiple kill through repeated use" above). Pre-50, the gambling is most often a kind of sport (to maximize performance), best limited to easy fights; post-50, it may be necessary to gamble on a more regular basis (to uphold contribution), because the DD spells are a bit short. I believe that an AE damage spell is good for damage/mana gambling if 3 hits generate a sizeable damage/mana bonus versus DD; it is even better if 2 hits also generate a bonus (the spell will serve more often, repaying the memorization slot). A spell which depends on hitting 4 times (or more) to generate a sizeable bonus (Al'Kabor spells, some PB spells, pre-50 Rains) can't be regularly played with tolerable risk and good success (use other spells instead). Note that PB spells, being zero-range, expose you more than range spells, so, they are inherently less good than others for casual damage/mana gambling. My own piece of advice: when gambling with your damage/mana, keep an eye on your group's health, and constantly consider reverting to good old brutal DD (or non-damage mob incapacitation) if the fight proves less easy than you initially thought.

Beware that, when you are using for gambling purposes an AE spell which is not of your highest rank available, the damage/mana bonus versus your highest-rank Shock is not as high as indicated in my tables; you may have to recalculate it. The typical recalculation is for Columns, which are such convenient spells that they tend to have an extended lifespan; I made a specific table.

 

F) Mass taunt, mob herding

Depends on: strength, swiftness criteria

Wizards are excellent taunters, through various means (DD, Skin spells, Staff of Temperate Flux). A Wizard can herd a mob about anywhere despite of opposite attraction. AE damage spells allow to move mob packs (towards you) and/or split them. A basic and efficient tactic is to AE Snare them first with Bonds of Force, so that they can't reach you easily. Now, the tactical reward depends completely on how your group operates, and on what you want to achieve (the reward may be null, or the mob-pulling may be a backfire instead of a reward if you are unprepared). Mob herding is easy in the early fight, but harder in the late fight, when the mobs have accumulated much hate against the tanks. Rotating two spells, or casting twice or more the same spell with moderate recast time, may be necessary. There would be tactically some room for a quick, strong and/or chain-castable AE damage spell, even with poor mana-efficiency, acting as a taunting "red button", but there is none (the proposed application has the same requirements than killing with "one big shot", with added need for swiftness; so, you may be tempted to use PB spells, but you will get immediate retaliation, which is annoying. Al'Kabor spells would be perfect candidates for the application, if only they were not so slow and weak).

 

G) Niche applications

Interrupting spells

Some PB spells and Al'Kabor spells interrupt spellcasting, either not for sure through pushback, or for sure (if not resisted) with split-second stun (like Force Shock does). Unless the spell is to be used at random, this is valuable only if the cast time is short, and if the reward is not paid for with weak damage and/or diluted damage/mana.

Debuffing

Wizards don't have means of putting resistance penalties or other lasting effects on mobs, except movement-hampering (Root, Snare). They have the Staff of Temperate Flux as a single-mob small debuff against heat and cold (but, even though the debuff is small, the Staff is very powerful as a distance gauge and aggro accumulator). Wizards could have AE debuff without single-mob debuff, in the same way they have AE Snare without single-target Snare. Why the idea ? First, there was, for a long time in 1999, some misinformation in most spell databases: Cast Force was said to drain Strength from the target, and I even read once that Enegy Storm was draining Stamina. This is all false. Second, I know at least one AE damage spell which has a side debuff effect: the level 49 Druid spell "Ice", which does a 50 debuff against heat (but lasting only a very short time). There are no such rewards for the Wizard AE damage spells, but, the game is changing often (I would say that Al'Kabor spells are good candidates for a fix in the form of added debuff against the energy type of same-rank DD).

Gaining faction

Some insist that using minor AE spells at dungeon entrances, when there are trains, allow them to accumulate quickly the desired faction adjustments. Low-level PB spells are the best for that purpose, thanks to their absence of mob count limitation.

Bar-room brawling

You may use AE damage spells just for the fun (or nuisance) of it, casting in the middle of mob trains or, worse, in PvP areas, where there is no player count limitation, at weddings (I hit 60 players at once with an Al'Kabor spell once, then died within seconds, if only because five pets came for me immediately). This is, of course, not a very consistent reward.
