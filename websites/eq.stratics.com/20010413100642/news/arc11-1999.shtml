Brad's Response:

I'll start by answering the questions you posed - if players want to break these out into separate threads, please feel free. Gordon and I will attempt to respond to players' responses, but keep in mind that Gordon is semi-unavailable (out of town until Monday) and I have a limited amount of time I can devote to posting (today being an exception).

Why can't melees bind in outdoor zones? How do you feel this will unbalance the game?

(I'll repost what I've said about this recently, and add to it a little).
Binding is set up the way it is and always has been for balance.
It is part of the death penalty and is there to pace the game.
The idea is that casters are more prone to dying (especially in group situations, in the depths of dungeons, when something goes bad) and thus can bind in more places. It still paces the game, however, because groups need to wait for melee characters to return. If melee characters could bind anywhere it would alter the pace of the game, further minimize the death penalty, and let well balanced efficient groups just scream through dungeons.
I realize some players reading this might think that sounds great, but there has to be a pace to the game and a penalty for dying.
We are also considering making it such that casters cannot bind in Expansion dungeons (the fact that they can now is actually a bug that is too late to fix for the rest of the world).
Another aspect of this is that minimizing downtime in general is PART of the challenge of the game. The better groups work together, the less downtime there is. This includes not letting people die so you don't have to wait for their return, but also includes efficient tactics, mana use, placement, etc. Efficiency, cooperation, interdependence, etc. are all key to playing EverQuest effectively. When something goes down wrong (for whatever reason, i.e. your mistake, someone else's, or random fate) the penalty is an adverse affect on efficiency, whether it's traveling, waiting, re-earning experience, etc. This is fundamentally part of the design as it relates to advancement mechanisms.

Why was feign death changed?

I will let Abashi re-post his several responses to this question and clarify and/or give the examples you are looking for.

If buffs from level 50 characters are unbalancing the game by allowing powergaming, why are you not putting some kind of limit on the levels you can buff? For example, you can't buff someone more than 20 levels lower than you are.

Because when we weighed the pros and cons, considered the long term effects on the game as well as how the players would react, we decided not to take action. I brought this up in another post to illustrate an example of us deciding not to 'nerf'. (I think the real solution, by the way, would be to scale the strength of the buff based on the recipient's level relative to the caster's).

Why do you object to Random spawning treasure? You have tried it in Paw (so maybe you are changing your position on this).

We're not against randomness in general, but we are against implementing randomness to the degree I've seen some players advocate (Diablo-style random loot). We feel the name and attributes of an item should have context relative to where and from whom you obtained it. But I think in general we could use more randomness and that's why you see more attempts at using the concept in Paw and elsewhere. I posted a detailed explanation of our position as it relates to random loot some months ago on alt.games.everquest - I can try to dig it up if you want more detail.

Why did it take so long for the Alchemy problem to be found when players were posting on the Shaman boards for 6 months that it was broken?

No real legitimate excuse here - we basically screwed up. We were testing it internally wrong (we were using GM commands to buff a Shaman to the appropriate level, which DID give alchemy to the character). We failed to test it properly by leveling a Shaman naturally which would have exposed the bug. When we heard people say it needing 'fixing', we'd test it as I described and when it worked we figured 'fixing' it really meant making the skill create more useful and effective potions, not that it was truly broken.

Why are you creating a group of 'second class' players who don't get the best items?

With all due respect, that's a pretty loaded and one-sided question.
Sometimes we eliminate items such that they cease to spawn. We do this for a variety of reasons, from simply doing so to make the game more dynamic to removing items that proved to be too powerful to removing items that were bugged. Unless there is a devastating problem with an item, we tend to leave existing items in the players' inventories. Regardless of whether one perceives this as 'fair' or not, it is fundamental to how these games work. I'd also like to point out that earlier players didn't have access to all the newer items in the game, for example items that would have been very useful for them earlier on but may not be useful to them now that they are higher level. This too could probably be deemed 'unfair' but is again necessary if we are to maintain an evolving game with an advancing player base and if we are to add content on an ongoing basis.
I think that the assertion that newer players are 'second class' is totally false. They have access to more items, better items, the economy is more mature and items are more accessible/cheaper to purchase, and there is more general knowledge about the game. I stand by my earlier statement that the game today is both generally significantly easier and also more balanced now than it was when we launched.

Why are you rewarding powergamers by setting up quests that require sitting for 12 hours to get a chance at the quest item when the same quest could be implemented as six 2 hour parts which any player could complete.

Guilty as charged. As Abashi posted recently, we will slowly but surely revamp the class specific quests (and other applicable quests/spawns), making them more balanced not only as they relate to each other but also in terms of how long it takes to obtain specific quest components.

Why are some of these spawns EXACTLY 12 hours apart which allows kill stealing wizards/druids to teleport in 15 seconds before the creature spawns and kill it and steal it from the group that has been sitting there waiting for hours?

This is a tactic that has been rearing its head more and more recently, and we've started talking about possible ways to address it in the Tuning Meetings. We are considering randomizing spawn times (for example, a six hour spawn might spawn every six hours plus or minus X minutes) as well as some other ideas. Please feel free to post any ideas you might have that would address this issue.

- Brad

In Response To Overcrowding
Community News [ 5:38 AM PST ] - Manga
Brad reposts his post on the Everquest Forum:
(I'm reposting this -- it was originally at the end of a long thread and was a response to another post. Some players started to respond to it by breaking down their opinions on the various zones, which I think is a great idea and will help us deal with these issues)I will attempt to address this issue by answering your questions:

1) Does Verant think the servers are overcrowded during peak hours?

Some servers are over peak population targets (for example, Fenin Ro). Unfortunately, new players continue to start on Fenin for a variety of reasons. Also, and more importantly, some Zones on all servers are over crowded. To me, this is the more important issue. Why are some Zones less populated? Why do many people prefer the more populated zones, play there, and then complain about over crowded servers? What could we do to attract players to some of the lesser used zones? These are the questions that I think need to be answered, and we'd love some feedback. It is a fact that many zones, even on the more populated servers, are underutilized and have room, while others are more than full. I think we can do some things to help (for example, re-tune dungeons, adjust loot levels/types, etc.) and I think the players can do somethingabout it too (make an effort to explore/use dungeons and other zones that are less populated, even if they are farther away, or you perceive they have less loot, or they are more challenging).

2) Is the game playable within their original design ideas based upon REAL WORLD server populations, as opposed to pre-release and design-doc server projections during these peak times?

The game is extremely playable, even on the more crowded servers, especially IF you make an effort to play in areas that aren't as crowded. I know that's what I and my guild do. Does it take longer? Yes, sometimes. Is it more dangerous? Sure, but we like the challenge. Is the loot not as good? Depends. And again, we (Verant) have been and will continue to tune existing dungeons, especially the underutilized ones, to draw more people to them. (Kerra Ridge is next, btw, after we launch the Plane of Sky. Then we'll launch Paineel, which has newbie areas, and then the Hole, which is a high level dungeon).

3) If a player feels they are wasting time by playing on an overcrowded server, what options do they have? Start over on a new server? Be transferred? Wait for the expansion?

We have no intention of allowing general character transfers between servers, and in any case, I don't think that would address the issue people are complaining about at all. I really don't. And even if it were helpful, the problems it would add in my opinion far outweigh the benefits.

My answer for a player who feels he or she is wasting time in a crowded area is to MOVE to other zones, wait for us to revamp and re-tune more existing zones, and wait for the Expansion which will increase the size of the world by around a third. Unfortunately, though, if enough players refuse to leave their comfort zone (say, bottom Guk) to head out and explore the Expansion and/or the revamped older zones, the problem won't totally go away.

4) Verant has taken steps to lessen the impact of overcrowding (Paw, Rare NPCs in open areas of zones that use to be un-utilized, bumping up Kithicor's Level, and itemizing/populating Kedge Keep). Does Verant think these moves (among others) has

A) Fixed the overcrowding issue (assuming there was one)?
B) Help alleviate overcrowding?
C) Help it a bit, but still hasn't solved the issue?

I'd choose B - it's helped, but many players continue to stay in their comfort zones, choosing the path of least resistance, and therefore continue to deal with the crowds.

Basically, I just think the people want to know your straight answer as to whether or not this is the way the game was envisioned and meant to be played in your eyes?

No, the vision (as it relates to your question) was that people would spread out more and 'do' a dungeon and then move on, as opposed to repeatedly camping spots they are used to camping. We will continue to address this issue as I've detailed in this posting as well as brainstorm on better designs that will hopefully alleviate some of this issue in the future (yes, that's purposely vague).

Is Verant waiting to see how the Expansion "helps" overcrowding (assuming there is a problem), and thus, REACT to overcrowding issues, or are you going to PRO-ACTIVELY approach overcrowding as a major issue and put into effect sweeping changes to alleviate these concerns?

I believe we are both waiting for the Expansion AND pro-actively tweaking zones. We also just launched another server. But as for 'sweeping changes', no, we're not going to make any sweeping changes to EverQuest. While the game has some issues and certainly its share of flaws, on the whole it is both the game we envisioned as well as the most successful online game of all time. Making sweeping changes to a successful game would be foolish at best. That said, you have my commitment that we will continue to address problems such as zone overcrowding (as well as many other problems) both in the EQ of today, the Expansion, and the future beyond.

-Brad

Gordon Explains Group Efficiency Vs Soloing
Community News [ 9:17 AM PST ] - Manga
This taken from this post on the Everquest Forum:
There is a bonus to experience given depending on how many in your group, but that bonus is linear. You don't receive an extra kick of XP for having 6 people as opposed to 5, other than the straight linear bonus.

But I would like to take this opportunity to see if I can explain exactly how grouping is more efficient. This will take a few assumptions, but I think you'll see that it's valid in the end. My figures here are very round, but are applicable in any situation.

Suppose you have a warrior with 100HP, who can deal damage at 50HP per minute. Furthermore, you are fighting gnolls with 100HP, who can deal 25HP's damage per minute, and pay 100XP points. Let's say your rule is that you fight until 50% health then heal to full before fighting again.

Ok, fight 1. It takes you two minutes to kill the gnoll. After the fight you have 100XP points, and are down to 50HP health. Since you decided to rest to full health when reaching 50%, you'll have to rest 150 seconds (2.5 minutes) before you can kill again. Let's just suppose that you don't heal while in combat to make the math easier. Your total yield is 100XP/4.5 minutes, or 22XP/Min.

Now, let's try two warriors, same rules, same level, damage spread equally.

Fight 1:

You and your partner take down the gnoll in 1 minute instead of two, since there are two of you. The gnoll only was able to deal 25 pts of damage during this time, which, spread between the two of you, puts you each at 87 health. Since you are now in a group, the gnoll gave 110 XP instead of 100, but it's split between the two of you, so you get 60 each. Here's the outcome after fight 1:

Health Remaining: 87 XP Earned: 60 Time Elapsed: 1 Minutes

After Fight 2:

Health Remaining: 75XP Earned: 120 Time Elapsed: 2 Minutes

After Fight 3:

Health Remaining: 63 XP Earned: 180 Time Elapsed: 3 Minutes

After Fight 4:

Health Remaining: 50 XP Earned: 240 Time Elapsed: 4 Minutes

Now, since you are both at half health, it will take 2.5 minutes to rest to full health, giving you a total yield of 240XP/6.5 minutes, or 37XP/Min.

Of course the actual figures will differ according to class and level, but the principle still applies. If you group, you get more experience, providing you take things harder and/or faster than you could solo.

All of that said, yes, some people like to group, others don't. Regardless, EQ is and will remain a group-oriented game. Some classes have been made to be more suited to soloing than others, and if you want to get by through soloing exclusively, you'll have to be one of those.

:>>>If Verant were to let casters bind anyone, anywhere, the gameplay of EQ would be greatly enhanced. This is a very old argument. I could talk about the fact that death is supposed to be inconveniencing. It’s supposed to be something that you really don’t want to happen. I could talk about how this design is just as inconveniencing for the casters as it is the melee types. I could talk about value added to druids, wizards, clerics, and necromancers who all provide post-death services to characters. When it comes down to it, some classes can do things that other classes can’t. That is the simplest explanation I can give without going into how a change in bind would cause a dramatic shift to many dynamics in the game. Some people don’t play melee-types just due to this disadvantage. Other people don’t play casters just because of the fact that a caster can’t take a hit. It’s all a matter of personal preference. Is a given disadvantage too large for you to play the class? It’s up to you to choose.

Rares By Any Other Name...
Community News [ 9:21 AM PST ] - Manga
A post made by Coraan on the Everquest Forum is replyed to by Abashi:
Abashi/Brad, please explain why it must be rare..

Items must be rare for the same reason that advancement is meant to be challenging. It is to provide a sense of accomplishment and pride that comes from achieving a goal. If everyone has an item, the importance and feeling of pride that comes with owning an item is severely diminished.

Imagine if you will, a server where everyone starts at level 50, every creature has 1 hitpoint, all items are sold on vendors for 1 copper, and nothing attacks you unless you attack it first… I really can’t see people playing EverQuest if we remove all of the challenge. If powerful or cool items weren’t rare, you’d never again here “Cool! Where’d you get that?”

Item rarity, and a large number of different items, has its roots deep within EQ’s text-based predecessors (MUDs). It’s fairly integral to the game.

But now you have made the MOB that carries the item be a very rare spawn as well!

The way in which rarity is handled varies by game designer. Either the spawn is common and the drop is rare, the spawn and drop are semi-rare, or the spawn is rare and the drop is 100%. People have commented at problems with camping, so we’ve moved further in the direction of rare roaming spawns with 100% drops. I guess it’s a matter of preference.

Why not make it spawn every 5 minutes, always carry the tiem, and make special items no-drop? This way the deciding factor in my obtaining an item will be my level, my skill, and my ability to play the game.

Some things are done this way, when we want something to be very common, but require some time to go get. One example would be the ice comet quest, which requires wizards to run around and collect things. Certainly some of this is dangerous, but the things they need to collect spawn very quickly and are just lying on the ground.

It’s all a question of whether we want the item to be rare (e.g. attach a sense of accomplishment to obtaining it) or want it to be common, but just require a little time on behalf of the people who want it. We listen and make adjustments in the spawn/drop rates to make people happy without removing that sense of accomplishment. We have a tendency to make some items too rare, and we work to correct it when it’s brought to our attention.

-Gordon Wrinn

Server Overcrowding
Community News [ 4:00 PM PST ] - Manga
Found this message answered by Gordon aka Abashi on the EQ Gameplay Forums
Righteous3D wrote:

The box says 1000 users per server is the target.
The box says "...by more than 1000 other players". This does not mean that 1000 was the target.

Gordon's reply:

Back in pre-beta, Brad published that the per-server target would be around 1500. In late beta, this figure was revised to be 1500-1800 simultaneous users at peak times, though it should be stressed that this number was for optimal server health.

When we have less than 1500 at peak, game-play during off-peak hours begins to degrade as there aren't enough people of given level and race/class mix for everyone to enjoy the game to its fullest. At over 1800, there are too many people on at peak for the people there to enjoy the game to its fullest during peak hours. But these are not hard limits, as the degradation in game-play is gradual on both sides of that range. This is not to say that some of the servers are not overcrowded (e.g. Fennin Ro), but most of them fall within the target for optimal game-play.

Righteous3D wrote:

I would like to see a figure that Verant is willing to stick to. "We commit to keeping the servers peak simultaneous logins to ____ users or less. ”If a server reaches more than this, we will try to have a new server online quickly thereafter."

Gordon's reply:

To do this would be very unrealistic. Each new server that comes online doesn’t take an equal number of people at peak off of the other servers. Look again at Fennin Ro, which hits around 2200 during peak hours. This server’s load at peak really hasn’t changed since about 2 weeks after release. We could add 20 more servers and I really don’t think Fennin Ro’s load at peak would change drastically. Sure, it would decrease a bit, but when we have a world where people are happy with their characters and friend, people tend to want to keep them.

Righteous3D wrote:

When is Verant going to lock servers to people who don't already have an existing account on a given server? Of course, if they don't allow people to move characters this will probably upset people trying to join friends online (word of mouth gets Verant a lot of sales).

Gordon's reply:

Some excellent points indeed. I’ve seen many posts saying that we should just lock down the servers so that no one else can join after X number of people are online. That’s a fine idea, until the person posting the suggestion tries to log in as user X+1.

Other people say that we should allow only so many characters/unique accounts to join the server. Again, that’s a fine idea, until we take into account that 5 times the number of people who will ever play on a new server log in as soon as it comes up to reserve their names or to just try the server for a few weeks.

Finally, as you mentioned above, there’s the idea of locking off existing servers to new accounts, and there are the problems with doing that as you mentioned. Also, let’s not forget that there is a certain amount of turnover on the servers as it is, as people decide to leave the game and as new people join. This is necessary to maintain a healthy environment.

Even the idea of letting people transfer characters is no magic bullet. There are many consequences to allowing this to happen. One, and not the least of these is turning a bunch of server economies to a huge artificial economy the likes of which the world has never seen.

Now back to the underlying question at hand, I should have information based upon the next new server by early next week.

-Gordon

Update to 'Nerfs' vs. Enhancements
Community News [ 10:05 AM PST ] - Manga
On the Everquest Website, Abashi aka Gordon Wrinn wrote the update of the list 'Nerfs' vs Enhancements:
Hello all,

I apologize for the delay. Below you’ll find the updated “Change List Since Release” based upon your replies to the first draft. Before we get into that though, I’d like to talk a bit about the types of things that you can expect to see on it.

I think that Brad fairly well established in his last post the need for change in a game such as this. In fact, nearly every publication relating to EverQuest makes reference to Norrath being a dynamic (read: changing) world. Changes have to happen in a game like this to keep things working correctly. Rather than debate the merits of changes in general, I’d like to discuss some of the issues posted in the thread in reference to the way this list is compiled:

Bug Fixes Listed as Enhancements

Rather than leaving this list a true “Enhancement vs. Nerf” list, we’ve modified it to consist of “Changes to the game and their effect on gameplay from the player’s perspective.” The main reason behind this is that many bug fixes that have been done have been called “nerfs” if they result in the player no longer being able to do something they once were able to do. A good example of this is when high level pets (39+) were weakened. This change was made because the original data entered into the system was entered incorrectly. It was in every sense a bug, but when fixed, it was deemed a nerf.

Item and NPC Changes

New items come into the game and old items are taken out. Some items might come out to make room for a new one, while others might come out due to a balance issue associated with it. We’ve learned that it’s not right to change existing items in a negative manner. Some of you might remember Lustrous Russet Armor, and how we made the mistake of changing the class restrictions on that existing item. These particular items were always meant to be “Warrior only”, however it was wrong for us to take it away from those who had it. Another example would be the change to the weight reduction on the Box of Abu’kar. Even though the weight reduction on this box was a bug, it was wrong for us to change an item that people already had.

Even though we try very hard not to change existing items, sometimes it’s necessary due to a large imbalance. One example would be the stat increases made to the Armor of Ro, and another would be the problem with the Treant Fists that contained unlimited charges of Complete Heal. But this is a rare occurrence. Our preference when changing these items is to replace them with a new item of a different name. That way everyone who worked for the old item does not lose anything due to the change.

NPCs are much like items in that they change constantly. They are routinely added and removed from the world, their stats are adjusted, and their loot pool changes.

In both cases, we typically will not be announcing it. Norrath is a world where people are meant to figure things out on their own. Just because “Bob the Paladin” was level 20 and dropped the Holy Sword of Reverence yesterday in East Commons doesn’t mean that he won’t be level 40 and drop the Shield of Holy Protection tomorrow in Lesser Faydark. I’ve seen people ask, “How do we know that the loot/the NPC has changed?”, and I’m forced to ask, “How did you know what it was in the first place?”.

In the list below, we noted the removals of Rubicite, the Manastone, and the Box of Abu’Kar. In the future, it will really be atypical for these types of things to be announced in advance.

“Change X is listed as a plus, it’s obviously a minus”

There will no doubt be some disagreement as to the effect of a particular change on gameplay in general. For instance, I’m looking at the list now, and I see “Boil Blood replaced with Dooming Darkness” listed as a minus under the Shadowknight class. I personally disagree greatly with this especially considering the recent upgrades to Dooming Darkness.

Of course this disagreement is inevitable. It’s nearly impossible to get a group of 150,000+ people to agree unanimously with a change. Perhaps the closest thing we’ve seen as far as a change in the game would be the addition of Clarity, though I imagine some people think this spell should have been added to more classes.

Missing Changes

There is actually a number of changes that are noted in the thread that did not end up in this list. Many of them just didn’t occur, or didn’t occur during release. For instance, I’ve seen several comments in regards to Corpse Bomb and Umbral Messenger being removed from the game. These were both taken out during beta. Some of the other things, such as “Meditation speed reduced” are just plain false. The effect of meditation and the amount of time that it takes to regenerate mana has been changed or enhanced a few times, but in no direction but up. I imagine what may have happened is that some people may have gotten some new gear and forgot to take that into account. When my necromancer puts on his +INT gear, he’s got to meditate nearly 2 bubbles before being at full mana again.

This is not to say I didn’t genuinely miss a change or two while compiling the list. It is very possible that I may have missed some, and will add them as I become aware of them.

Plans for the Future

One of my first goals here at Verant is to increase the amount of information provided to the public as part of the patch message. It’s not just the patch message either, it’s all communications that come from us. In the near future, I plan on bringing a list of goals to the public, goals that we here at Verant will strive to reach. No one here will try to tell you that there is no room for improvement in our communications. If the level of our communications were perfect, I certainly wouldn’t have been brought on.

But these changes will not happen overnight, and for those of you are willing to hang in there with us we are very appreciative, and we sincerely apologize to those we’ve missed.

Thank you,

Gordon Wrinn
Internet Relations Manager
Verant Interactive, Inc.

Gordon on Feign Death
Community News [ 8:44 PM PST ] - Alethal
Hello there,
I just got done going around and talking to people about the way Feign Death was, and how it is now.

Before:
When someone would feign death and later stand, the creatures they feigned on would remember them, but would not come hunting for them until they engaged in combat with something else. As an example, if a monk pulled a guard, feigned, and stood back up when the guard started returning to his post (assuming the guard was far enough away not to be triggered normally), the guard would continue to his post and seemingly ignore the monk. The guard would not return to pursue the monk unless it engaged something else in combat. For instance, if this guard later ran out and killed a moss snake, it would then remember it was mad at the monk and take off after him (assuming the monk was still in zone). This was a bug.

I can understand how some people perceive this as better than it is now. Since the creature would not remember the monk unless it fought something else, it gave monks the impression that they were forgotten, even though this was not the case.

In addition, if the monk feigned enough times, the MOB would forget him regardless. This was also a bug.

First Change:
When these bugs were first fixed, so that Feign Death operated as designed, the creature would ALWAYS remember the monk. This certainly did cause trains, and is the reason why it was changed to what it is currently.

Now:
When a monk successfully feigns, any creatures under level 32 will forget him. In addition, any creatures that have returned to their spawn point will also forget him when he finally stands up. However, if the monk stands up before the creature has returned to their spawn point, there is a chance that it will remember him. Of course, it is now possible that some creatures will never have a 100% chance of forgetting you (roamers), since they have no spawn point to return to. However they still have the same chance to forget as static creatures that have not yet returned.

Feign Death is now an all around better spell than it was before, since the creatures do have a chance (up to 100%) to forget the feigner entirely. This is why you see it listed as a positive change on the game. However, it does become less effective with the addition of each roaming NPC.

I’m looking forward to hearing from some people who’ve tried it at length since the change.
-Gordon

The updated list - "Nerfs" vs. Enhancements
Community News [ 7:44 PM PST ] - Alethal
Here is the updated list (HUGE) posted by Gordon aka Abashi:

Hello all,
I apologize for the delay. Below you’ll find the updated “Change List Since Release” based upon your replies to the first draft. Before we get into that though, I’d like to talk a bit about the types of things that you can expect to see on it.

I think that Brad fairly well established in his last post the need for change in a game such as this. In fact, nearly every publication relating to EverQuest makes reference to Norrath being a dynamic (read: changing) world. Changes have to happen in a game like this to keep things working correctly. Rather than debate the merits of changes in general, I’d like to discuss some of the issues posted in the thread in reference to the way this list is compiled:

Bug Fixes Listed as Enhancements
Rather than leaving this list a true “Enhancement vs. Nerf” list, we’ve modified it to consist of “Changes to the game and their effect on gameplay from the player’s perspective.” The main reason behind this is that many bug fixes that have been done have been called “nerfs” if they result in the player no longer being able to do something they once were able to do. A good example of this is when high level pets (39+) were weakened. This change was made because the original data entered into the system was entered incorrectly. It was in every sense a bug, but when fixed, it was deemed a nerf.

Item and NPC Changes
New items come into the game and old items are taken out. Some items might come out to make room for a new one, while others might come out due to a balance issue associated with it. We’ve learned that it’s not right to change existing items in a negative manner. Some of you might remember Lustrous Russet Armor, and how we made the mistake of changing the class restrictions on that existing item. These particular items were always meant to be “Warrior only”, however it was wrong for us to take it away from those who had it. Another example would be the change to the weight reduction on the Box of Abu’kar. Even though the weight reduction on this box was a bug, it was wrong for us to change an item that people already had.

Even though we try very hard not to change existing items, sometimes it’s necessary due to a large imbalance. One example would be the stat increases made to the Armor of Ro, and another would be the problem with the Treant Fists that contained unlimited charges of Complete Heal. But this is a rare occurrence. Our preference when changing these items is to replace them with a new item of a different name. That way everyone who worked for the old item does not lose anything due to the change.

NPCs are much like items in that they change constantly. They are routinely added and removed from the world, their stats are adjusted, and their loot pool changes.

In both cases, we typically will not be announcing it. Norrath is a world where people are meant to figure things out on their own. Just because “Bob the Paladin” was level 20 and dropped the Holy Sword of Reverence yesterday in East Commons doesn’t mean that he won’t be level 40 and drop the Shield of Holy Protection tomorrow in Lesser Faydark. I’ve seen people ask, “How do we know that the loot/the NPC has changed?”, and I’m forced to ask, “How did you know what it was in the first place?”.

In the list below, we noted the removals of Rubicite, the Manastone, and the Box of Abu’Kar. In the future, it will really be atypical for these types of things to be announced in advance.

“Change X is listed as a plus, it’s obviously a minus”

There will no doubt be some disagreement as to the effect of a particular change on gameplay in general. For instance, I’m looking at the list now, and I see “Boil Blood replaced with Dooming Darkness” listed as a minus under the Shadowknight class. I personally disagree greatly with this especially considering the recent upgrades to Dooming Darkness.

Of course this disagreement is inevitable. It’s nearly impossible to get a group of 150,000+ people to agree unanimously with a change. Perhaps the closest thing we’ve seen as far as a change in the game would be the addition of Clarity, though I imagine some people think this spell should have been added to more classes.

Missing Changes
There is actually a number of changes that are noted in the thread that did not end up in this list. Many of them just didn’t occur, or didn’t occur during release. For instance, I’ve seen several comments in regards to Corpse Bomb and Umbral Messenger being removed from the game. These were both taken out during beta. Some of the other things, such as “Meditation speed reduced” are just plain false. The effect of meditation and the amount of time that it takes to regenerate mana has been changed or enhanced a few times, but in no direction but up. I imagine what may have happened is that some people may have gotten some new gear and forgot to take that into account. When my necromancer puts on his +INT gear, he’s got to meditate nearly 2 bubbles before being at full mana again.

This is not to say I didn’t genuinely miss a change or two while compiling the list. It is very possible that I may have missed some, and will add them as I become aware of them.

Plans for the Future
One of my first goals here at Verant is to increase the amount of information provided to the public as part of the patch message. It’s not just the patch message either, it’s all communications that come from us. In the near future, I plan on bringing a list of goals to the public, goals that we here at Verant will strive to reach. No one here will try to tell you that there is no room for improvement in our communications. If the level of our communications were perfect, I certainly wouldn’t have been brought on.

But these changes will not happen overnight, and for those of you are willing to hang in there with us we are very appreciative, and we sincerely apologize to those we’ve missed.

Thank you,
Gordon Wrinn
Internet Relations Manager

Another Post:
There's actually several different types of dynamic quests that run in EverQuest, however they could be broken up into two main groups: Minor and Major.

Minor quests do not run across all servers simultaneously. They are scheduled based upon availability of actors and petition load. They actually run quite frequently, but have a relatively short duration and typically don't show up on the news sites since they are such a common occurance.

Major quests run across all servers. They are likewise scheduled into a window of time where they must run. This is to minimize the chance that people find out about the quest in advance and just "show up" at the place where it starts (or ends).

The weakness in this system is that the majority of quests tend to run during peak hours. The reason behind this is that more people tend to be available to run the quest during that time, as much as there are more people available to play. We're certainly interested in running more quests during off-peak hours and are working to bring more people into the Guide Program who can guide at those times. In fact, if you have the strong customer service skills, the knowledge of the game that we need, and the ability to guide during off-peak hours, I invite you to fill out the Guide Program application at http://guideapp.989studios.com

As to the schedule for when these quests run, I'm sorry but that will never be publicized in advance, as the whole server would show up to participate. As much as we'd like to involve everyone on the server at one time, that usually doesn't work out well for the participants.

This letter from Brad posted on the Casters Realm, refering to the previous posts entitled 'Nerfs' vs Enhancements:
First, I want to thank everyone for continuing to offer feedback and for your help in compiling this list. I think it's a great step in bettering our relationship with many customers as well as increasing the effectiveness of communications between Verant and our player base, both ways. And as Abashi mentioned, we will have the first revision to this document up soon. I believe it will continue to show that we've both been very busy enhancing EverQuest since launch and that while some changes might be perceived negatively from one perspective that all in all EverQuest is a better game now than when we launched.

I also want to further clarify that we are looking for additions, changes, and subtractions to the game in terms of content and functionality, and whether you believe it was a good or bad change, or whether it affected you and the game positively or negatively. Some posts appear to be lists of negatives that are complaints about the game in general, and while we appreciate your feedback and opinions, these are neither nerfs nor enhancements and won't become part of this growing document.

Lastly, I'd like to address what I perceive to be a common theme throughout this thread, and that's a resistance to change. I think some players, while they want the game to change in general, and for new and exciting things to be added, don't want change that affects their character, especially if they perceive that change to be negative. I understand this and have been victim to 'nerf's playing MUDs and other similar games. Some players also don't like the fact that earlier players were able to use tactics, exploits, etc. to progress in the game, but by the time they would have had the opportunity to use similar means, we'd changed the game.

I understand this, and this is why this thread has grown with many long and passionate posts.

I must submit to you, however, that the administrators and keepers of an online, persistent game MUST continue to make changes to the game in order to preserve balance and to address areas of the game that become contrary to design, balance or intent. There is no way one can launch a game like EverQuest and then simply sit back and let it go. The game changes on its own, the knowledge base increases, the average player advances from low level areas, to mid, and then to high -- the entire game dynamic is altered through natural evolution. And it is our responsibility to protect the long-term health of the game and unfortunately this is at times at the perceived expense of a group of players. Unfortunately, sometimes the good of the many must outweigh the good of the few.

We definitely don't like making 'nerfs' - in fact, we hate it! It pains me personally to see players upset with the game and team behind the game. I'm sitting here writing this post after midnight at home on my own time not because I was told to, or because I'm afraid if I don't I'll have less money in my pocket, but because I and my team have poured our hearts into EverQuest the past three and a half years and it hurts me to have some people think poorly of us. This game is and has been our baby whom we released to the world nine months ago, and we all care deeply about it. Yes, Verant and Sony are out to make money with this game, but this doesn't mean the team behind the game only looks at everything as business. It's my opinion that great games can ONLY be products of passionate, creative teams who think of their creation as art and as a labor of love. And I'm sure the majority of you, regardless of how upset you are, agree that EQ is a great game. And I really believe this document as it grows will show that we don't make significant 'nerfs' often and that most changes we make not only help the game as a whole but individual players perceive them positively as well.

But the fact is, if the game is to continue to evolve (and it must), tweaks need to be made. Just about ANY change could be construed by a player as negative. For example, the upcoming change to Paw. Most people are excited about this, because it's pretty clear we need more mid level content. But how about those lower level players who hang out in Paw regularly because it's not so crowded... while most players might consider the changes to Paw a positive, the few who have consistently hunted there might consider it a negative. But we will go ahead and make the change in the interest of the greater good and the game as a whole.

A single player game is different for many reasons, and I really think quite a bit of the negative feelings in this thread result from some players not being used to a game like EverQuest and making assumptions that are simply not valid in this new and emerging genre. In a single player game two things are usually true: 1. you can assume the game will continue to be the same game you paid for, for as long as you play it (barring the occasional patch, which you the player choose whether to apply or not) and 2. the vast majority of single player games you don't play for month after month after month, developing a character you feel very attached to, becoming very passionate about your time in the game and the relationships you've made, and consuming more and more content, becoming very familiar with the feature set of the game.

The benefits of a persistent online game are pretty clear... you can play for much, much longer... you can interact with friends... you can watch a world change and be added to. I think most players appreciate this and that's why so many people are flocking to games like EverQuest. Yet some players feel, clearly by many of the posts in this thread, very upset when they find out over time that many of the tenets they are accustomed to in single player games don't exist in massively multiplayer online games. The games do NOT stay the same, and hence I've seen EQ called EverBeta and the post-release period called Beta 5 J. Some people just want us to leave the game alone, or at least leave it alone as it pertains to what they're used to in-game.

But we can't.

We are constantly adding new content and features. Sometimes, though, a change (even though we thought about it in-depth and tested it) doesn't work out the way we'd intended. Sometimes it takes time to realize this. Sometimes it takes time before players expose a flaw in our thinking, or find a way to take advantage of a change that is contrary to both our intent and to the balance of the game. And I don't blame the players one bit for this. But when situations like this are discovered, we must address it lest the long term health of the game and its balance be harmed and the game as a whole become worse, affecting everyone.

For example, the Plane of Fear. We launched it, thinking it would be a very hard zone. We even posted a message reminding people of its high difficulty and of the peril they might involve themselves in should they travel there. And since it was to be such a difficult zone, we populated it with very powerful items. It turned out, however, that we were wrong. Players found better tactics than we had, and were able to hunt in and conquer the zone far more easily than we expected. What could we then do? One of three things, I suppose: 1. leave it be, 2. downgrade the items, or 3. upgrade the difficulty of the zone. I imagine some players would have preferred we'd chosen option 1 or even 2, but we chose 3. Option 1 would negatively affect the whole game, because the items found in Fear are significantly better than elsewhere (thereby rendering other parts of the world unattractive to hunt in). Option 2 would not only upset the players who've already earned the item, but would also be going against the intent of the zone (that it would be very hard, and that the treasure would be very powerful). So we chose 3, and I continue to believe that was the right choice. Certainly, the best case scenario would have been we launched the zone perfectly, and certainly were we all perfect you all would probably be much happier. But we aren't, and as long as we continue to add to this game both in terms of content and features we WILL make mistakes and we WILL underestimate the ingenuity of some players.

Another example, but of a slightly different variety, is kiting. We knew about kiting for quite some time. It was never our intent that spells like SoW would be used in combat, rather the intent was for it to be a travel spell. But at first kiting didn't seem to be a problem and so we left it alone. Then, as the game continued to evolve and as tactics became more effective, we looked at the situation again and found that kiting had turned into a means of advancement NOT in synch with the many other ways to advance in the game. In fact, it was so effective that on some of our newer servers the number of druids and other classes that could kite alarmingly outnumbered other classes being chosen. This is another example of when we MUST take action. A key part of balance in an online game like EverQuest is that means of advancement are at least relatively in synch in terms of how long it takes to advance. Clearly if a, b, c, and d are all ways to earn experience, but d is significantly more efficient, players will be compelled to choose d more often than a, b and c. Unfortunately, some people perceive these sorts of 'nerfs' as an attempt by us to slow advancement down in general, or that we are alarmed at the rate at which some hard-core powergamers advance. But this couldn't be further from the truth, and another purpose of the document I posted was to illustrate that many of the changes we've made have rather made the game much EASIER than it was at launch. The 'nerfs' are rather to bring means of advancement as close as possible in terms of efficiency, not to slow down all means of advancement in general.

In summary, I think much of this turmoil simply stems from the fact that this is a relatively new genre and that games like EverQuest have brought online persistent gaming to a lot more people (several orders of magnitude more people than, say, MUDs) who are both very interested in the new genre but also unaccustomed to how they often vary significantly from single player games. Yes, in some sense games like EQ are and always will be in 'beta'. Yes, the game you bought will NOT be the same game in three months, or in six, and you cannot depend on the status quo to remain the same. No, the designers aren't perfect and as long as they add content and features they will make mistakes and add new bugs and problems. Yes, they will often have to address these issues and some players won't like it. Yes, players will almost always outsmart the designers and find new and unexpected means by which to play the game. No, these new tactics and/or exploits aren't always in the best interest of the game's overall balance and might have to be addressed. Yes, the players who 'get there first' will have some advantages over those that don't (but then there are many advantages to 'getting there later'; for example, more knowledge, easier accessibility to more powerful items, etc.)

These are simply the facts. Maybe we're wrong (and maybe our competitors, and all the MUDs that have existed for years were wrong), and maybe someone will come along and create a massively multiplayer online persistent RPG that has none of these 'problems' but all of the 'benefits' single player games have. Certainly, even though MUDs have existed for years, this genre in particular is in its infancy. I'm quite confident that we've only collectively touched on the tip of the iceberg so far. Heck, until we have 'Holodeck' technology, the sky's the limit. But in all fairness, the EverQuest team here at Verant has quite simply created and designed the most popular, successful (and in my not so humble opinion the BEST) MMORPG to date. It continues to grow (yes, we'll be launching another server soon), and it continues to sell like crazy over 9 months after its release (mostly by word of mouth, by the way), and honestly if all of you were not so addicted to this game and overall having a blast, you'd not be so passionate, playing hours and hours a day, and responding to this thread's original post with over 200 detailed responses in 24 hours. You have my pledge that the game will continue to overall get better, and I really believe that as the game matures we will need to do fewer and fewer 'nerfs'. And you also have my pledge that our communication to our players will improve radically. We are very guilty of not explaining adequately why we do things, or what we've changed, and I apologize for this. We WILL do better (go Abashi, go!). If we have to make a hard decision and make a change that negatively impacts a class or some such, the very least we can do is let you all know the details and the how and the why.

Thanks for listening,

-Brad

    Just a little mention of the Forums that are here at EQ Stratics. We here at EQ Stratics provide message forums of all topics. If you have questions specific to the classes of Everquest try browsing and perhaps posting at these forums:

    EQ Bards
    The Bard’s stock-in-trade is his ability to play songs. His songs are actually spells of a sort, and their magical affects last as long as the Bard continues to sing and play the correct instrument. They have some passing knowledge of the Warrior ways (mainly for self-defense), but their main focus is always their art, and so they will never be as skillful as Warriors.
    EQ Clerics
    The Cleric is a holy man endowed by his patron deity with the power to heal, and, to a lesser extent, call upon the wrath of his deity to smite his foes. Prayer is how the Cleric gains his ability.
    EQ Druids
    A Druid is completely at home in the outdoors, and is a friend to all animals. Because of this, no animal will ever attack him unless first attacked by the Druid. They are spell casters whose focus is all things natural, allowing them to call upon nature for aid. They share some woodland skills with their Ranger brothers.
    EQ Enchanters
    The primary focus of the Enchanter’s art can be summed up by the name of the arcane order to which he belongs: Enchanter. Their spells are crafted to enchant people, places, and things. This art allows them to charm beings and make them fight for their new master, or magically enhance the capabilities of a sword. They also have some skill in offensive spells, but are never as skilled as a Wizard.
    EQ Magicians
    Magicians are the summoners of the arcane arts, and wield the power to call forth everything from a loaf of bread to a huge Fire Elemental to fight for them. While not as adept at offensive spells as the Wizards, Magicians still wield considerable power in this area as well.
    EQ Monks
    Monks are dedicated to the pursuit of shaping their bodies into a pure weapon, and because of this they nearly shun all weapons and most forms of armor. They are very religious as well, and the gods are known to bless them, not by prayers and spells, but by magically enhancing their attacks.
    EQ Necromancers
    These are the dark brotherhood of the arcane arts, dabbling in death. Through evil spiritually based spells, they are able to animate dead bodies and skeletons to do their bidding, as well as having spells which leach life from their victims.
    EQ Paladins
    Paladins are the very picture of a holy knight, fighting for the cause of good in all aspects of his life. They share some of the power of a Cleric, and a good deal of the fighting ability of a Warrior, but have a few powers all their own.
    EQ Rangers
    The Ranger is a meld between a Warrior and a Druid, sharing some of the skills of both. They are at home in the outdoors.
    EQ Rogues
    They are the secretive, mysterious class of the world, who sneak about in the dark. Some say they are assassins, and some that they are nothing but common thieves, but the truth lies somewhere in the middle, and they are capable of both. They are quite skilled with many weapons, but they cannot stand toe to toe with a Warrior and prefer to make the kill by a sudden attack from the rear, at which they are extremely skilled. They would much prefer to not be discovered at all, or if they are, to not be there for their opponent’s blow to land. The darker skills of picking locks and stealing are also their forte.
    EQ Shadow Knights
    The evil twin to the Paladin class, Shadow Knights derive all of their powers from the evil gods whom they serve. They are a meld of Warrior and Necromancer, and share some of their abilities and spells.
    EQ Shamans
    They are similar to the Cleric, but are more of a tribal witch doctor and are typically found amongst the more primitive races. Their primary focus is largely healing and other Clerical type spells, but they also have a good deal of offensive spells.
    EQ Warriors
    Warriors are the masters of armed combat, in all its many forms. They are at home on the battlefield, and are trained to take punishment as much as dole it out, so their Hit Points are the highest of any class.
    EQ Wizards
    Wizards are the master seekers of knowledge, and their talents and spells are focused on this goal. They use powerful spells to not only see things from afar, but to transport them there as well. This thirst for arcane knowledge has also made them the true masters of the strongest form of defense: offensive spells of pure destruction. In their ability to do magical damage, they have no rivals.

    Brad McQuaid on EverQuest Issues
    Community News [ 6:44 AM PST ] - Mr. Freeze
    The following is from a two section reply from Brad McQuaid, on the alt.games.everquest forum:
    xlarthen@my-deja.com wrote:

    Brad,
    After playing EQ almost since its public debut, I have seen your responses to several posts and have read your annoucements. However you seem to 'skirt' around some very key issues. Please take the time to answer these questions.

    I don't intend to 'skirt' issues, but I do often purposely fail to reply to issues that I've addressed before and/or are addressed by other players as well if not better than I could have addressed them :)

    #1 - Why is there patch after patch dealing with supposed 'game/class balance' issues any very few dealing with game engine bugs? There are game bugs out there that have been there since I purchased the game last April (i.e. Zoning bugs, group bugs where people can't invite or disband w/o logging off and back on, MOB pathing bugs).

    I assure you we continue to fix probably 100 times as many bugs than we implement negative 'nerfs'.

    #2 - In reference to question #1, why are the vast majority of class balance patches negative in their effect and why is there no discussion of these patches before they come out?

    The vast majority of class balance patches as well as patches in general are not negative (if you really want me to post a list of enhancements we've made to the game since release, I can or someone else can). I'm not sure what you mean by no discussion... just about everything related to EQ is discussed both internally and all over the internet.

    You have stated that you have a vison for EQ but the fact is that you have released your vision to the world and you're having people pay you for it. As such, you have created a community of players and I think as a community we should have some say in changes to the game.

    Sure, and you do. We read newsgroups and message boards all of the time and have received great feedback and ideas, and have also been made aware of problems in the game and areas where we've made mistakes. I think we have a great track record of both listening to our customers and making a big effort to communicate with our customers. Is the process perfect? Heck no, but it's good, and it's getting better.

    I think the huge success of EverQuest is indicative of both the fact that we made the game a lot of people wanted, and that we continue to maintain and update a game that a lot of people want.

    I.E. The Feign Death changes: I do not have a monk but the numerous posts by monk players that I have read lead me to believe (can that many people be wrong?) that the unannouced changes (meaning no one saw them coming) adversly affected the monk class and that there was nothing wrong with Feign Death to begin with. Such sweeping changes should be brought to the EQ community at large. This should not be done via poll but rather an 'email discussion'.

    I don't think an 'email discussion' with 150,000 people is practical. I do feel polls can be very valuable, but not always -- it is our job to safeguard the game as a whole and often players will look at an issue only from their perspective (and they should -- this isn't necessarily a criticism).

    You make these changes based on what you would like to see done but seem to forget that you and your staff are not the only players of EQ out there.

    Of course we aren't, but we are also responsible for the short and long term overall health of the game.

    I believe that you would have a *much* happier player base if you involved us in some way in the decision making process (and i'm not talking about polls, I mean in some way where we know we *are* effecting the outcome). It starts to ruin the enjoyment of the game if large effect changes are made simply because of a disgruntled vocal minority or because "verant just thinks it should be this way"

    I hate to break it to you, but the message boards and newsgroups are the epitome of a vocal minority :)

    The "Verant just thinks it should be this way" almost implies we are making arbitrary decisions for the heck of it, and I assure you that this couldn't be farther from the truth. We have tuning meetings in which controversial issues are discussed and debated in detail, and hard decisions are often made, and never lightly.

    #3 - Several times I have seen patches added without comments in the patch text file. Can we have it where *all* patches small or large are commented? I understand that if new items or quests are added you may not want to advertise what/where they are for roleplaying reasons but simply saying 'added new items/quests'would be nice so we're not wondering is something now gone or added or did they fix a bug, etc.

    I agree -- we need to do a better job at communicating changes via the patch messages, even if we do have to be vague as per your example. You have my commitment that we will improve in this area.

    #4 - When are more quests going to be added and when are the broken ones going to be fixed?

    All the time. There are always new quests being added and we fix broken quests whenever we are made aware of them. I'd venture to say the number of quests in the game now relative to when we released has probably increased by 25%.

    I feel that it detracts from the enjoyment of the game when you take real time to run your character from one place to another and back again only to find that there is no 'end result'.

    I agree.

    Also, there are some quests out there that seem to yield squat in relation to the time/level of difficulty it takes to complete the quest. Can this be fixed as well?

    They are -- many if not most of the quests that people felt yielded too little of a reward, whether it was in terms of items and/or experience, have been 'fixed'.

    #5 - Events. Several questions here. I have been on when some events have occured and read about others and they all seem to start around 11pm or later eastern time. For those people that have to work or go to school the next morning or are just not nite owls, this time frame is not acceptable. Can more events be schedule for early evening eastern time?

    We can try, but the larger events require many GMs and Guides and therefore often have to be scheduled around when a large body of these people can be online at the same time.

    When there are events, again the ones I have seen and read about, they all appear to be geared to high level characters. Are there plans to have events where low or mid level characters have a chance to survive and 'win'?

    I don't think that's entirely accurate -- there have been and continue to be quests that are geared toward all sorts of level ranges, and given that the majority of our player base is approaching what I'd call mid-level, most quests and events should be focussed in that area.
