I'm thinking, for example, of the orc shoulder pad quest that gave ringmail out like it was candy to anyone who could defeat a legion (9th and above). I hardly think it can be considered an exploit to turn in multiple pads when the npc giving the quest asks you to bring him all the pads and belts you can find, and these items are practically worthless outside of the quest. Who can blame players for turning in all the pads they may find? Someone at Verant wrote the quest that way on purpose (albeit misguidedly). The real abusers on that one were the dwarves who, rather than defeating the legions themselves, simply bought all the pads they could find. But hell, after an afternoon in CB I would find myself with 10-15 shoulderpads in my possession, full well knowing that there existed an npc who wanted me to bring him these pads in exchange for ringmail. For me to turn them in was within the pretense of the game world, not a loophole, but an intentional piece of code written by some designer. Not to turn them in would have been folly. It is the people writing the quests who need to balance the game; it is not the responsibility of players to tiptoe around anything they sense might upset economic balance. Where else can the line be drawn? Everytime you kill a monster do you stop and say, "Hmmm, now this loot I'm collecting off this one, does it seem to be reasonably proportioned to the difficulty of the monster? Better destroy it, might be unbalancing." I somehow doubt it.
