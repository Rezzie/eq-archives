Introduction

The magician is a versatile class. He or she are at home in a group at all levels or soloing up into the higher levels. The class, however, really shines for soloing in the mid levels from eight to the high twenties. The class can be successfully soloed beyond the twenties, but the game is designed to move you into dungeons once you get into the thirties. Dungeons, by their nature, are best approached in groups to reduce the risk to the player. So up through the twenties you can still find great hunting grounds outside where the magician’s solo skills are some of the best in the game.

In soloing these mid levels, however, there is a wide range of opinions on how to properly approach engaging mobs*. Each person has their system and vehemently defends it as the most effective way to win. Each system tends to focus in a narrow area (e.g., heal, hit, or nuke) based on the person’s perceived understanding of the statistics involved (e.g., healing points vs. nuking damage) or their idea of the most efficient use of resources (e.g., most efficient use of mana). Players tend to apply these ideas generically to a wide variety of situations and claim that their way is clearly superior to other player’s methods. As a result, new players (and some experienced players) have a difficult time sorting out all the strategies when deciding how to approach hunting the mob s in the game.

*MoB, MoBs - Mobile OBject. Refers to any object within the environment that maybe targeted and attacked regardless if it is moving before said attack.

The difficulty is that each system is right and each system is also wrong depending on how it is applied. How you engage a mob is very situation specific. The situation, then, not favorite style or statistics really dictates how the combat flows. So in order to consistently win engagements, you need to use a mix of methods to fit the situation.

It would clearly be impossible to define every situation you will encounter and develop a winning approach for each one. We can, however, develop some criteria to recognize a limited set of situations that will cover most engagements you will face in EverQuest. We can also take the tools that the magician has for engaging mob s (e.g., direct damage spells, weapons, healing spells), group them based on how you use them in combat, and create set of engagement building blocks a player can mix and match to fit the situation. We can then apply the building blocks to our limited set of situations to define a set of specific hunting strategies that will cover most of the engagements you will face with mobs up to your mid twenties.

The Tools: Our Starting Point

The magician has a range of tools available to him to bring down a mob. These tools fall into a set of six groups based on how you use them in combat. Each of these groups of tools will have specific advantages and disadvantages when you use them in combat. Our first tasks in developing hunting strategies are to layout the tools and define how they group together.

 top

Damage Spells

The first group of tools is the magician’s damage spells. Second only to the wizard, the magician can bring heavy firepower down when needed. These spells come in two varieties:

Direct damage

  • Shock of blades
  • Fire bolt
  • Shock of flame

Area of effect

  • Rain of blades
  • Column of fire

Advantages

  • Heavy damage ranging from 35 to 150 hp (mid level spells)
  • Cast from a safe distance
  • Short cycle time to cast next spell
  • Allows you to pull a mob to where you want to fight

Disadvantages

  • Often resisted by higher-level mob (little damage, lost mana)
  • Can misfire and fizzle (no damage, lost mana)
  • Will cause a mob to aggro on you if you burn too much
  • Requires downtime to recover mana

While you can cast damage spells from a safe distance, if you burn too much the mob will ignore your pet and come right for you (aggro*). All mobs have a natural tendency to want you over your pet. The mobs seem to have a radius of interest around them. For example, a crocodile, spider, or snake usually will only switch targets from the pet to you if your standing right on them. Orcs, kobalds, and skeletons have a much larger aggro radius and will switch targets even with you at a distance. Your pet hitting and taunting the mob usually holds its attention; though sometimes they may turn to run your direction your pet most likely will draw the mob back. This scenario occurs when you are positioned at the outer range of the mob’s interest radius. If you hit the mob, however, with several big damage spells he will ignore your pet and concentrate his attacks on you. In a multiple mob engagement being aware of the above is critical since your pet cannot hold all the mobs attention. Once you start burning the mobs your pet has not engaged will immediately jump on you. When this happens, it is very hard for your pet to get the mobs off again.

*Aggro - Aggression. Refers to any MoB that will attack your character.

Healing Spells

These spells allow you to heal your pet of which there are two. One is a level 8 spell named “renew elements” and the other a level 20 spell named “renew summoning”. Casting these spells you use your mana (25 or 100) to raise the hit points on your pet by either 33 - 50 or 150 - 200.

Advantages

  • Cast from long range (safely out of mob’s radius of interest)
  • Gives the pet additional rounds to cause damage
  • Does not aggro mob
  • Cannot be resisted
  • Builds alteration skill (the only spell that does this at low levels and prepares you for the malise spell down the road)
  • Reduces pet downtime after engagement (do one or two heals for full health)

Disadvantages

  • Long cycle time
  • Limited amount of hit points added vs. damage done by a damage spell
  • Can fizzle (no heal, small mana loss)

Healing your pet is a misunderstood set of tools. Players often seem to apply measurements to the healing tools that lead them into ignoring the benefits and favoring burn spells. The measurements generally fall into one of three areas:

  • Mana efficiency of a burn versus heal spell
  • Single hit damage dealt by a mob versus heal points of heal spell
  • Speed of a burn versus healing spell

These measurements do not capture the impact of the heal spell and ignores the main benefit of the heal spell, your pet now has extra rounds. If we shift to viewing damage over time, the heal spell does well as an alternative to a burn. A level 16 pet can hit for 20, bash for 8, double attack, and has a damage spell (water and fire pets). While I have seen many sets of numbers on the water pet’s ice spell, no one really seems to know exactly what it hits for. Roughly best guess is 15 to 20 for a level 16 to 20 pet. There are various ways to arrange the numbers, but the pet should hit from 15 (partial hit) to 70 (full impact) in a round. With high agility pets, you can go several rounds without being hit. The damage over time for two rounds would be 30 to 140 and for three rounds would be 45 to 210. The heal spell in this range will heal about 40 or so. Given that the average mob in this range hits from 15 to 30, you can get a minimum of two rounds attacking and more than likely four to five rounds with one heal.

Therefore, the heal spell can be fairly efficient creating roughly 30 to 210 damage for a spell using 25 mana points. A mob may hit for 20 points versus the 40 point heal however, a pet gets in several rounds and can inflict significantly more damage during retaliation. While a burn spell will bring a mob down faster; a heal uses less mana than multiple cast of high mana burns, cannot be resisted, and does not aggro the mob(s) on you.

The healing effect is more pronounced when you have a higher-level pet fighting a lower level mob (e.g., a yellow or black pet on a blue mob). Your pet hits more often, does more damage per hit, and gets hit much less. One heal in this situation is often all you need to finish the mob and you can meditate while he is fighting to recover mana.

There is some controversy on the effect of healing the pet causing an aggro tendency. My experience has been that healing my pet does not cause mobs to aggro. I have discussed this with several clerics and magicians who agree. Many clerics use healing of pets as a standard tactic (their heals are much larger than yours). Test this out by fighting two blue mobs. Stand back at max range (outside of mob’s radius of interest) and heal your pet. I have never had a mob come off the pet and aggro on me. Do the same experiment and hit one of the mobs several times with your best damage spell. He will likely leave your pet and run right to you.

The downside of this spell is the long cycle time. When fighting a higher-level mob with a lower level pet, you cannot always cast the spell fast enough to keep up with damage being delivered. This is more acute the higher your mob’s level (e.g. level 20 mob verses a level 8, they just hit harder).

Overall, heal is a useful tool. It is, however, very situation dependent. Healing tends to work best on pets with high agility, good regeneration rates, and large damage spells (water pets with ice spell, air pets with stun). It also tends to work better with pets that are higher in level than the mob because the pet gets more hits in and takes less damage. Since healing your pet cannot be resisted, this tactic tends to work well against mobs that have high resistance to magic (damage spells don’t work on the mob) and situations where damage spells will immediately aggro a mob on you. Healing is not better or worse than a direct damage spell, it is a tactic that must be applied in the correct situations where its advantages can shine.

Pets

Pets are what define the Magician class. Pets are your main weapon in combat and a highly effective killing machine when used in the right situation. Do not leave home without one. We have a choice of four and you could write a long article on which one you feel is best. I would propose that there is no best pet; they each have an application based on the situation (are you getting the theme here yet). When I develop the hunting strategies in the last section, I will recommend which pets apply and how you fight them in the specific situation.

Advantages

  • Highly efficient use of mana (for one cast you kill many mobs)
  • Cannot be resisted
  • Large variety and diverse skills
  • Special abilities (e.g., water pet cannot be poisoned and can hunt asps/spiders)
  • Act as buffer for you while you escape
  • Attack is considered magic for level 10 and higher pets (you can hunt things other class have to have magic weapons for)

Disadvantages

  • Sometimes it attacks when should have just sat there
  • Path finding AI can cause you to lose pet (see bullet above, “Taunting Master”)
  • Takes some of your experience for kills when you are solo

Pets are extremely important to magicians because we have no crowd control spells or kiting spells. Necros can live without their pets since they can cast a darkness spell to slow the mob, a fear spell to dismiss the mob, or a root spell to stop the mob. Necros DOTs do enough damage to kill mobs when combined with their crowd control. Magicians, on the other hand, need their pets to take and do damage. That is why we have four of them with different abilities. With our damage spells and a pet in combination, we can bring a mob down fast and reduce our need for crowd control. The key is to use the right pet for the situation and apply the damage at the right time.

Pet Buffs

Because magicians are a pet defined class, we get to help out our pets. With the burnout series, damage shields, and focus items, our pets get beefed up well beyond other classes. While the necros and druids can kite without a pet we get our balance from having very strong pets and not needing to kite.

Advantages

  • Casting cannot be resisted (you are casting on your pet)
  • Damage from the shield cannot be resisted (6 - 9 hit points each time your pet is hit)
  • Significant increase to your pet’s abilities
  • Can all be cast prior to combat so you focus on managing the engagement while in combat (can also recover some of the mana before you enter combat)
  • Does not tend to aggro mobs (the damage shield damage adds to your taunt factor, but at 6 hit points a shot it does not tend to cause them to turn on you)

Disadvantages

  • Cost some mana to cast these
  • They tend to wear off at bad times (like in the middle of combat)

These buffs are highly effective and allow you to inventory mana for later use in combat. The mana you cast in a burnout will provide a benefit during combat at a future time. You can meditate and regain the mana prior to combat so that you enter the engagement with full mana. The damage shield also helps store mana since you cast it right before combat and then it applies each time the pet is hit. Six hit points of damage at mid levels adds up quickly when your earth pet is getting hit frequently.

Melee Weapons

Magicians can use a variety of blunt and piercing weapons. At lower levels you can wade into battle with your pet and attack like a warrior. As you move up in levels you have a harder time staying in combat for very long with a high level mob. Pick one type weapon and focus on it so you can keep your skills up on that weapon class. Ultimately, your pet will do most of the melee but you can still get a hit or two in (that is why you can cast bandages). I will also include throwing weapons in to this group. Since you can cast throwing knives, you will have the ability to build this skill at no cost to you.

Advantages

  • Allow you to help you pet at lower levels
  • Throwing weapons can hit for 3-6 points a throw to add damage with little risk to you (like the damage shield, it adds up)
  • Does not cost mana in battle
  • Allow you to take some hits and even out some of the damage taken by pet
  • Balances downtime with your pet (Pet takes less hits and recovers faster)
  • You can cast decent weapons that are magic enabling you to engage mobs that require magic weapons to hit

Disadvantages

  • Hard to take damage from higher level mobs (you do not have the hit points)
  • Need to keep skills up (you will not hit higher level mobs with low skills)
  • Hard to cast spells while you are in melee and being hit

At some point your ability to melee becomes limited. You cannot wear real armor and you do not have the hit points to withstand attacks. In early and mid levels, however, you can still get in and get a hit or take some damage. It all depends on the situation and what is needed. As your levels rise, you will end up spending less time toe-to-toe with the mobs and we will discuss the use of melee combat in the next section.

 top

Hit Point Protection

This is the last group of tools. You have several spells that will increase your AC, hit points, various resistances and use of bandages. You are the only class that can really use bandages regularly since you cast them versus buy them (they are very expensive to be used in quantity). The shield and phantom armor lines will either add additional hit points or increase your AC. This reduces how much you get hit and the damage you take, protecting your hit points. These spells can give you an edge if you need to melee or are caught off guard.

Advantages

  • Cast before combat so no chance of misfiring while engaged
  • Provides improvements to important statistics and hit points
  • Cannot be resisted
  • Cheap use of bandages allows you to build this skill quickly
  • Bandages can heal as good as early cleric heal spells (when you build the skill)

Disadvantages

  • Phantom line takes a reagent to casts
  • Improvement to some statistics are minimal
  • Does not add enough protection to allow you to melee tougher mobs
  • Bandages only heal to 50% of your total health

Overall it pays to cast the shield line of buffs. The phantom line, however, only adds minimal boosting. The money it costs to cast the phantom line should be saved for times when you need an extra edge. A recent change to the phantom line has given it a regeneration effect on hit points making the whole line more useful.

Bandaging is a skill that should not be overlooked. While I have found that it does not provide a lot of help during combat when solo, it can significantly reduce your downtime after combat. Also when you do group, you can heal the tank while he is fighting; something all tanks appreciate. The key is using the skill to build it. Since you likely have high intelligence (your primary stat) you will learn bandaging quickly. Hit points regenerate slower than your mana due to mediate and a high bandage skill lets you build to 50% of your total health very quickly. Practice on people sitting and healing, they appreciate it and your skill goes up fast.

Combat Roles: Tools to Building Blocks (or Tactics)

Understanding the tools available was the first step to developing hunting strategies. To make the tools useful, however, we need to determine how to use them during an engagement, tactics. How you use them depends on what role you are playing in the engagement. The role you play can be defined in two dimensions: who is the primary tank (you or your pet) and who is the primary damage dealer (you or your pet). There is also one specialized role you will play at the beginning of each engagement regardless of who deals damage or tanks. These roles will become the building blocks to execute our hunting strategies.

There are four specific roles you can play in combat and one general role prior to combat:

 top

Wizard of the day

Okay, let’s face it, you have some really great direct damage spells and it is a lot of fun to burn a mob when you get a chance. You make a pretty good wizard on occasions and have a built in tank to help protect you. This wizard role works well, when used properly, and is one of the primary roles you can play.

Description

The wizard role requires your pet to tank and you to be the primary damage dealer using damage spells as tools. The goal here is to burn the mob down quickly with a series of damage spells while your pet acts as a barrier to keep the mob off during casting. Use your pet buff spells to ensure pet damage while burning and then send the pet to attack the mob while standing at a safe distance. Ideally you want to position yourself outside the mob’s radius of interest so that your pet has time to taunt the mob back when it tries to aggro on you. You should, however, be close enough that you can use the pet report command to check the status of your pet’s health. Use your highest-level damage spell and chain cast it until the mob goes down. I usually hit with two burn spells then hit pet report (you should hot key pet report) to check my pet’s health and continue burning.

Implications

There are several significant implications in using this role:

  • This is the fastest way to take down a mob. Your damage spells are powerful for your level and can make quick work of many mobs. No need to wait several minutes, like a necro or druid, for a DOT spell to work.
  • This method is somewhat unreliable. Many mobs are resistant to damage spells. Ghouls, mummies, dry bones skeletons, sand giants, and red mobs will all resist your spell completely or partially. The result is you will not bring the mob down as fast as you wish and you will waste mana in the process. Damage spells also have a tendency to fizzle. The fizzle uses mana and causes no damage. The result of the above two implications is that you can quickly use up mana with little impact.
  • You will have significant downtime between engagements. Damage spells will quickly use up your mana. As a result, you will need to find a place to meditate and most likely need to recover close to full mana. The recovery time becomes more significant the higher your level.
  • The mobs will likely aggro on you. Even when you take precautions, eventually a mob is going to ignore your pet and come straight for you. This is especially true in situations with multiple MoBs. You have little in the way of crowd control spells and your pet can usually only take one MoB at a time. A MoB not engaged with your pet will turn on you. You will then need to finish off one of the MoBs quickly allowing your pet to pull the MoB engaging you.

Applications

This role applies in many situations:

  • You need to take some hit points off a MoB quickly. In many situations you are faced with a tough yellow mob and need to give your pet a “head start” on damage. Your damage spells will allow you to burn the mob down and reduce the number of hit points your pet must deal with.
  • You need some crowd control. Damage spells are the magician’s form of crowd control. If your pet cannot handle the multiple mobs, you can burn one of them up quickly. The mob will likely turn to attack you, but you can try timing your spells to bring a mob down allowing your pet to reengage and cope with only one mob. This is particularly true if you have an annoying low-level green mob or two that is interfering with your attack.
  • Your pet is in trouble and needs help fast. Once your pet is down in health you can burn up the mob to save your pet. Since your pet has likely caused significant damage, the mob will not aggro on you as quickly and you can usually kill the mob down before aggros to you.

Pet Cleric

Your pet can deal significant damage. It can double attack, kick, bash, dual wield, and avoid being hit in many cases. On numerous occasions, it really doesn’t need you to jump in and do much. You just need to keep your pet going by healing it when required. This is one of the most misunderstood roles and can be extremely effective when used in the right situations.

Description

In this role your pet tanks and also provides the primary damage while your tools in this role will be healing the pet. By standing back at the limit of the mob’s radius of interest, you use your heal spells to keep your pet alive. Since your pet deals the damage, give it all the advantages you can through the use of pet buff tools. This type of engagement takes longer than the wizard role with the damage shield accumulating significant damage on the mob and the burnout spell turning your pet into a killing machine. The trick is remembering the long cycle time on the heal spell and to start healing before you thinking you need to. A water pet with its high regeneration rate in this role works even better. Keep your pet’s health up in your target window so you can continuously monitor his status. Periodically use the F8 key to check the damage on the mob and see how the battle is going. Then hit F1 twice to get you back to your pet. If you get behind on heals, the long cycle time on the heal spell will force you to have to switch roles to save your pet. If you are keeping up with the damage or your pet’s regeneration rate is helping to maintain its health, you can sit down and meditate until your pet’s health starts to decrease.

Implications

This role has several surprising implications:

  • Deals significant damage with high reliability. This role is the most reliable way to kill a mob. You pet’s damage cannot be resisted, the damage shield cannot be resisted, your heal spells cannot be resisted, and your heal spells rarely fizzle (even if it does the mana loss is small). Using this method ensures little chance of wasting mana and damage is transmitted at full power to the mob.
  • Does not tend to aggro the mob. Casting heals over and over again does not aggro the mob. Remember the experiment we ran back in the tool section. Even in multiple mob environments, the mobs will stay on your pet if you are outside the mob’s radius of interest.
  • Allows short downtime between engagements. Since heal spells cost little mana and you can usually meditate during the engagement, you will have minimal recovery time needed to regain your mana after the battle.

The role works best with a good water pet vs. other pets. The pet cleric tends to work best with pets that have high agility (water, air), strong regeneration (water), and good damage (water, earth), and a strong damage spell (water, fire). In some cases with tough mobs or mobs that are significantly above your pet’s level, you will not be able to keep up with the damage the mob is causing. However, with a good water pet equal or above the mob’s level this role works well. The water pet can deal lots of damage with both its melee attacks and spell attack (more so when it is at an equal or higher level than the mob). Because of its high agility, the water pet attacks more before it gets hit again. This amplifies the effect of heal with extra rounds before the mob hits. The water pet’s high regeneration rate also amplifies the heal effect and its high damage allows it to last a long time against most mobs (combine this with its agility and it can often outlast an earth pet and deal more damage).

With high level or tough mobs you cannot always keep up with the damage. If the mob is several levels above your pet (or a tough mob) your pet cannot get in enough hits or damage spells to offset the damage the mob is delivering. This affect is larger the farther away you get from level eight (up to level 20 when you get the new heal spell). The mobs hit harder and their damage is greater in relation to the heal spell. Surprisingly, the damage shield will cause significant damage if given enough time to work. However, there are times when you just cannot cast fast enough to keep up with damage dealt to your using heals.

Applications

This role applies to a variety of situations:

  • You cannot afford to let the mob aggro on you. There are some mobs that cause indirect damage that can be a real problem. Mummies cause rabies, asps and other snakes or spiders hit you with strong poison, and mobs with root spells can hold you in place so you cannot escape. In these cases it is better to let your pet handle the mob while you stand at a distance (you can always toss throwing knives if you want to contribute).
  • You need reliability against a mob that is resistant to your damage spells. In some cases, your burning does not have visible benefits and you have little choice but to heal. On a ghoul, mummy, red mob, and sand giant (okay maybe he is for later levels) you can use your tools that are all irresistible and highly reliable to bring down the mob.
  • You want to minimize your downtime. When dealing with mobs that are lower level than your pet, you can let the pet do the work while you meditate and quickly move to the next one (water pets regenerate fast and with one heal after the battle you are ready to go on).
  • Your pet can handle the mob with little help from you. In cases where your pet is equal level or higher than the mob, there is no need to do a lot of burning. The pet can bring the mob down while you keep watch on the situation around you and heal once or twice when needed.
  • You have multiple mobs that are at or lower than you pet’s level. You can often heal and avoid having the unengaged mob aggro on you. This only works in cases where you pet has the advantage. You may have to switch to the wizard role if the fight goes bad. Also, see my article on managing engagement risk to get an idea of when you should just run instead of trying to engage multiple mobs.

Magi Warrior

At early levels it is fun to get in and mix it up with the mobs in melee combat. This role allows you to get in hits early in the game, knowing that as you progress you will be less able to bring a mob down with your staff or dagger.

Description

Early in the game you can deal about as much or more damage than your pet. As a Magi Warrior you tank for your pet and you are the primary damage dealer. After you pull, you step in and start to melee with the mob. The key here is to position yourself on the other side of the mob so that your pet is hitting the mob from behind. This gives the pet a bonus for hitting the mobs back. You can hit with your melee weapon (a primary tool) and time your damage spells (another primary tool) so you can burn between the mob’s hits. Since you are in melee already, you do not need to worry about having the mob aggro on you. In this role, your pet acts like a DOT spell dealing damage. You can back away when you need to and let the mob hit your pet a bit evening out the damage taken and reduce your downtime between engagements. Think of this role as you and your pet acting as a two-warrior group to drop the mob. Obviously you should use your damage protection and pet buff tools here as well. You will need to learn to time spell casting and use your direct damage spells to pull the mob’s hit points down.

Implications

There are no real surprising implications here; they tend to be straightforward:

  • Melee skills need to be up to date. To play this role you need to keep up your melee skills. If you are playing wizard or cleric too much, your piercing, 2H blunt, defense, and offense skills will degrade. It will become increasingly more difficult to actually hit the mob when you get to higher levels.
  • Get the best armor you can. You are limited to raw silk armor during the early stage of the game unless twinked by a higher-level player. Buy the raw silk armor or take up tailoring to get this armor quickly. Use all your player buffs to protect you and bandage after the battle.
  • Know when to back out of a fight. The higher level you get the sooner you need to back out of a fight and let your pet get take some damage. Pay attention and do not let the mob beat you down while you are not looking.

Applications

There is a limited set of applications for this role:

  • You are early in the game and can still take mobs on equal footing. Over your first ten levels or so, you can speed up your leveling by taking the lead and fighting with support from your pet.
  • You want to keep up your melee skills. At mid levels where your pet takes most of the damage, you can sit back and cast. With this strategy, however, your melee skills will quickly get too low to do any damage to higher-level mobs. It is not a bad idea to periodically get in as a Magi Warrior and keep up your melee skills.

Pet Band-Aid

There are times when you just need to take it on the chin to help out your pet. As you get to higher levels you will be able to do less damage in melee combat. It will no longer make sense for you to go toe-to-toe and try to do damage. Your toe-to-toe time will be more to take the heat off your pet to extend his life.

Description

In this role you tank while your pet is the primary damage dealer. You are going to be taking the damage and drawing the mob’s attention while your pet can hit and cast uninterrupted. You do not, however, want the mob to aggro on you. You want to be able to step back when needed and let your pet take damage again. To prevent aggroing the mob you will use your melee weapons to attack. Your primary tools in this role are your hit point protection tools and your melee weapons. To engage with these tools, get to the other side of the mob from your pet while staying out of the mob’s striking distance. When you are on the opposite side move in and hit the mob with your primary melee weapon. The mob should turn to face you and your pet will now be hitting the mob from behind and getting the bonus. Monitor your hit points closely and step back out when you reach one to two bubbles of health. The mob should then turn back onto your pet.

Implications

The implications here very straight forward:

  • Get the best armor you can. Your magician was not designed to take hits long term. As a result he cannot wear really spiffy armor. Still get your raw silk armor early and be grateful for any higher-level armor you can obtain. Ensure you add phantom armor and shielding buffs. They do not add a lot, but everything will help.
  • Do not stay in too long. You are still going to want to have a chance at escaping. If you get too much health taken off, your agility and running speed will be reduced. Pull back at one to two bubbles and decide if you need to run or gate.
  • Melee skills do not matter. It would be great if you could inflict some damage with your staff, but in the end it is your pet that is doing the primary damage. This role will work even if you have not been keeping your skills current.

Applications

This role has a limited set of applications:

  • Your pet is down on health and needs just a little more time to finish the mob. This is the primary reason for using the role. You need to step in and take some punches to keep your pet alive a little longer. This role works best with a pet that can really deal damage with his spell (water, fire). You also should step in while your pet still has enough health to cover your escape (20-30% health). When you step away, you want to judge where the mob’s health is and decide when if you need to make a break for it.
  • You want to balance out damage to reduce downtime. Sometimes you will want to share damage with your pet to equalize out the party heal time. This is particularly true with earth pets. They have low regeneration and taking a few beatings will reduce the pet’s downtime. The trick is to not make your pet wait on you to finish heal. You need to balance the damage taken so both of you finish your healing around the same time.

Puller

Pulling a mob is a specific role you must play at the beginning of each engagement. This is one of the more important roles you will have since it sets the tone of the battle each time. In the end, the only part of the engagement you really have control of is where you fight the battle. Picking your spot is what pulling is all about.

First, you need to spend a little time looking over the area you are fighting in. You need to find the spot where you want the battle to end up and there are several things to look for. Obviously being near any guards in the area or being near a zone would be helpful. They will help ensure your escape if things go bad. You also want to look for the flow of the mobs in the area and they generally follow set patterns movement. In many cases there is a “highway” that they move on. In Everfrost there is a mob highway south of the South Tower. In the Oasis of Marr there is the Orc highway inland and the Gator Alley right along the beach. In most areas the mobs appear and move in a predictable fashion.

You need to find a spot that is away from the traffic and near friendly help or escape routes if possible. You do not want to start your engagement in the highway. Fighting in the highway will usually lead to multiple mobs show up uninvited and spoiling the fight (i.e., 3 snow leopards in Everfrost or a puma or mummy at the Oasis). I find that the edges of the zone or up next to mountains can work well. The mobs will not naturally cross these boundaries and tend not to move toward them. The spot becomes more important as the mob becomes more aggressive (e.g., aggressive orcs vs. passive crocodiles).

Once you have your spot picked you have to get your mob there. I generally use a long-range spell like Flame Bolt (long range and the 45-47 damage will not usually aggro the mob on you). I set my pet up in the spot (pet sit command) and approach the mob flow. I will generally cast the spell and start to move back looking for the non-melee damage message. I keep the mob selected and, as I approach my spot, I send my pet out to meet the mob right behind me. In some areas with no aggressive mobs you can pick a spot very near the mob trail and just send your pet in. This method works well hunting crocodiles at the Oasis.
