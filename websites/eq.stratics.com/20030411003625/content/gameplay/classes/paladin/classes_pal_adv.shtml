Playing a Paladin - Advanced

 Introduction   Attributes   Races   Levels and Advancement   Spells   Weaponry   Hotkeys   Conclusion 

Introduction
Paladins are a combat-hybrid class in EverQuest, combining the qualities of Warriors and Clerics, with more of the former than the later. Where a Warrior is the unquestioned master of the brutal give and take of toe to toe melee combat, and the Cleric the best healer on the battlefield; a Paladin combines qualities of both into a single class. It is a not sum, but rather a blending where some parts are lessened or left out to avoid overpowering other flavors in the mix, and the result is a class that is surprisingly effective and resilient. Don't play a Paladin if you want to be known as a powerful caster; you won't be. You will receive spells, and you will be using them; but Paladins are secondary casters, limited in spell selection and mana reserves. A Paladin's true strength is his ability to function to roughly eighty percent of the full combat effectiveness of the Warrior, while retaining the casting ability as a reserve. While playing a Paladin, remember the mix is roughly three-fourths Warrior to one-fourth Cleric, playing accordingly, and you'll excel.

Skills

Paladins take their skills from both parent classes. The basic combat skills (All weapons, offense, defense, taunt) follow the level cap formula of (Level * 5), which is five lower than the level cap of a Warrior. Additionally, the Paladin receives most advanced combat skills up to five levels later than the Warrior will. Bash is enabled at 6th, Dodge at 10th, Parry at 17th, Double Attack at 20th, Riposte at 30th, Disarm at 40th. However, once received they will quickly progress up to the Paladin's level cap, and soon be an integral part of the battle. Additionally, Bind Wounds can be learned at 10th, and can serve the Paladin equally as well as it does the Warrior. A recent addition to the class is Archery, available at 1st level, but one that can help make the Paladin's life a lot easier on the battlefield. Archery caps for Paladins at 75.

The spellcasting skills start arriving at 9th, with the addition of all the basic casting abilities (Abjuration, Alteration, Conjuration, Divination, Evocation and, of course, Channeling). Meditate doesn't come until 12th, but once it does the Paladin is a fully enabled warrior of his God, able to fight with a high degree of efficiency. All spellcasting skills level cap at the same formula, (Level * 5).

Abilities

The Paladin's unique ability is Lay On Hands. When used, it will heal up to one thousand (so I'm told) hit points. After laying on hands, a Paladin will be without this ultimate intervention of his god for the next Norrath day, roughly ninety minutes of real-time. Remember to use the targeting properly when attempting to lay on hands; if you try to save yourself with it, but fail to press F1 to target yourself instead of the monster you've been dueling with, your laying on of hands will be wasted on the monster before you, and most likely lead to your anguished death. When it comes time to 'push the paladin panic button', the sequence is F1, find LOH hotkey, push hotkey. Always force yourself to watch what you're doing, and to breath before just blindly stabbing at the keys; when you need the effect, you can't afford to waste it with a miskey.

Spells

When a Paladin receives his spells, he begins to feel complete. When he receives Meditate, he truly is complete, and will only add to his arsenal as new levels arrive. While only Rank 1, the Paladin's spells enable him to function far more effectively as a solo combatant; and provide useful backup and relief for the group's dedicated healers. The ability to cast Minor Heal and Cure Poison enables the Paladin to begin tackling fights and monsters he might have avoided before. At Rank 2, at 15th level, with the addition of Lesser Heal and Cure Disease, the Paladin really begins to have a lot of fun. All spell ranks beyond 2 only add to the fun.


Character Creation as a Paladin: Attributes
Paladins basically come in two flavors, melee based or magic based. Either is viable, but considering the class is a tank with magical abilities, rather than a mage with some tank aspects; the prospective Paladin is perhaps best served in the long run by focusing on the tank side. Most Paladins use their magic as a compliment to their melee, not the other way around; magic is used before and after combat, and very sparingly during. While battle is joined, let your weapons speak for you; afterwards use your connection with your god to prepare for the next.

Physicals

Namely, Strength, Stamina, Agility and Dexterity. Unlike in games like the ever-popular Dungeons and Dragons, EverQuest's attribute systems are a bit more nebulous, and not as they are in other games. The attributes in EverQuest do have impact on the character, and on the way the character will play; but it is different than what a veteran D&D player would assume, and requires one to examine closely to determine the differences.

The most obvious benefit of Strength is increased encumbrance capacity; each point of strength is one pound that can be carried without penalty. As a tank class, Paladins will be called upon to carry the battle to the foe, which typically demands the best armor possible, and weapons to match; just this basic tank gear can easily weigh quite a bit, upwards of 80 or 90 enc even before standard carry gear like backpacks, food and water are considered, and to say nothing of all that loot you'll be taking out with you. But the other benefits from Strength are just as important. Many players complain about their 140 STR Ogre Warrior not hitting for more with the rusty longsword than their 90 STR Human Warrior; there is a possiblity, unconfirmed at this time, that higher strength might more directly affect the damage dealt at higher character levels. Rather, and this is also unconfirmed but suspected by some in the game; higher Strength increases the overall average damage inflicted. Meaning, specifically, a higher Strength character will hit for maximum or near maximum damage more often than a lower Strength character. Aside from damage, Strength affects the Attack rating of the character; increasing Strength increases the Attack rating. Strength may do other things as well.

Stamina is perhaps the most 'dull' of the Physical attributes, but is important. It has the most direct affect on Hit Points gained per level. Stamina also directly affects the fatigue bar, the yellow bar under the blue mana bar. Your fatigue bar will last longer, and return quicker, with a higher stamina. In my opinion, Paladins have a sufficient starting Stamina, and those Paladin races that have a low starting Stamina have other weakness that are more important than Stamina to be addressed.

When most people consider Agility, they think of defense; and this is true in EverQuest. Agility directly affects your character's defenses in two ways. By increasing the Defense rating, and also by adding an 'agility bonus' to each AC gained from a piece of armor. This will result in the character getting hit less often in combat, which enables him to have a higher combat hang-time; possibly living where others died. Agility also has a small affect on the Attack rating. Finally, Dexterity affects several things. The Attack rating is impacted by Dexterity, as is the likelihood of successful Channeling rolls (to avoid spell interruption). Dexterity also affects trade skill rolls like Blacksmithing or Jewelcrafting. And when wielding a weapon or item with a special effect, the check to determine when and how often the effect goes off is based on your Dexterity.

Mentals and Socials

Specifically, Wisdom, Intelligence and Charisma. Each will have direct impacts on the character throughout his life, but for a Paladin, only one has a crucial relevance. Wisdom is that attribute. For a Paladin, as with a Cleric, Wisdom is the attribute key to the Paladin's magic; determining mana. Wisdom is also rumored to have small effects on skill learns and skill success rolls, and also in resisting some types of spells (Charm).

Intelligence, on the other hand, affects the Paladin only in the area of skill increases. Higher Intelligence results in fewer attempts per skill increase, for all skills; low Intelligence Paladins will have a more difficult time working up Trade skills, skills requiring specific uses like casting or Sense Heading. Having a low Intelligence can be very frustrating, but is not a game breaking disadvantage; merely an annoying one.

Charisma will impact faction adjustments and the prices NPCs offer you during transactions. If you wish to become a merchant, you may wish to consider a high Charisma. As a combat class, however, you can skip the points here and easily make up the penalty you'll pay when buying and selling by orchestrating a few extra kills.



Character Creation as a Paladin: Races
The single most defining decision in a character's creation after that of class is of race. The race a player selects for his character will determine how other players react to the character, where the character begins, how NPCs will react, and even restrict where in the vast world of Norrath a character can safely travel. It is an important decision, for roleplaying reasons equally as much as statistical ones, and should never be rushed or made in haste. You'll be stuck with this character for a long time if you decide to play it, months certainly; be happy with your decision. There are five races that can choose the path of a Paladin. They are Dwarves, Erudites, Half-Elves, High-Elves and Humans. Each has strengths or weaknesses, in one form versus another. The choice will determine the type of paladin you play, not just during interactions, but also on the battlefield. Certain races are better suited to certain aspects of the Paladin's lot than others. Keeping these limitations in mind will prevent resentment over an inability to dominate in a weak area of your chosen race.

Dwarves

These short, hardy folk are renowned for being resilient, strong and quite stubborn. In historical fantasy literature, they tend to be intensely loyal, deliberate in decision, fanatical in pursuit of a grudge or slight, and are often portrayed as stalwartly shouldering great burdens as a people. In EverQuest, they are always strong and sturdy, with good hands and common sense. They pay for these advantages with a slightly lower nimbleness, a slow speed of wit and a decidedly off-putting demeanor.

Dwarven Paladins begin with unmodified stats as follows: 100-95-70-90-88-60-55. First, make note of the makeup presented you. The Strength is quite good, +25 over the EverQuest average of 75; Stamina is high as well, +20, and Dexterity at +15. Agility is only -5. Wisdom starts +13, while Intelligence is -15, and Charisma -20. Dwarves have the highest Physicals, and the highest STR, STA and DEX. They share the second highest WIS (with Erudites). However, they have the lowest INT and CHA, and also the lowest AGI.

As always, character design ideas and notions will vary widely from player to player, one seeing advantage in one combination of increases while another thinks exactly the opposite. But, typically, when designing a Dwarven Paladin, it's probably a good idea to emphasize the strengths of the bases, and simply accept the drawbacks presented.

If one wishes a more 'balanced' design, the points can be placed into WIS, resulting in 100-95-70-90-108-60-55, which would give over +25 in the two main prime attributes for the class (STR and WIS), and provides an excellent magical compliment to the dwarf's already good physical side. Alternately, the points can be used to boost the low INT, resulting in 100-95-70-90-88-80-55, for design even more balanced across the attributes than the WIS option; only one attribute left significantly below average.

If more focus on the physical is desired, you can emphasize STR (120-95-70-90-88-60-55) or AGI (100-95-90-90-88-60-55), DEX (100-95-70-110-88-60-55), or even STA if you desire (100-115-70-90-88-60-55).

Popular and/or recommended 'splits' would be STR/WIS (110-95-70-90-98-60-55), STR/AGI (110-95-80-90-88-60-55), and STR/DEX (110-95-70-100-88-60-55). Splits involving the already low INT or CHA are not recommended, due to the fairly minor effect this would have.

Erudites

The High-Men are a race of humans who diverged from the 'main racial heritage' of merely normal humans in the past, and have since focused their personal and societal development towards the acquisition and application of knowledge and power. In historical fantasy literature, they are typically presented as aloof, reserved, intense, usually either very patient or very impatient, and quite skilled. It is important to note that Paladins are the only melee oriented class available to Erudite characters; all other choices are casters.

Erudite Paladins offer an interesting mix of starting stats: 70-75-70-70-88-107-80. Note the distinctions. Strength at -5, Stamina average, Agility -5, Dexterity -5, Wisdom +13, Intelligence +32, Charisma +5. Their Physicals are the second lowest among the paladin races, and are only barely above the lowest. On the Mental side, however, they share the second highest WIS with dwarves, and hold the highest INT, hands down. They also benefit from a not so low CHA, though it is only the middle of the pack amid the paladin races.

Due to the more than adequate INT, and perfectly respectable WIS, Erudite Paladins typically focus on the physical side during design. Since the Paladin is a melee class, an overemphasis on the mental side presents serious potential to slow growth for Erudite Paladins. The already high mentals can support everything a melee class might want to do (and often doesn't have the INT to back up). Also, armor and weapons loadouts can get quite heavy until high level magical and rare items are obtained; encumbrance problems always nip at the heels of melee characters with low STR. Having said all that, a highly recommended design for Erudite Paladins is STR heavy, 90-75-70-70-88-107-80. A 90 STR is on the low side of what's considered acceptable for melee types, but can be made to work; especially with some judicious application of low and mid level magic items that enhance Strength. If one is determined to not invest in Strength, a design that pumps into AGI (70-75-90-70-88-107-80) or DEX (70-75-70-90-88-107-80) can certainly be made.

On the mental side, only the WIS option has any real ring of reasonableness to it, and that comes out 70-75-70-70-108-107-80, which would give a very mental heavy design. If you've just got to have the smartest tank on the continent, INT yields 70-75-70-70-88-127-80. At that level of INT you challenge the robe wearing types to debate contests and regularly win, and will have little trouble keeping a wide range of skills kept up, but will show your weakness on the battlefield when you struggle alongside your melee brethren.

A few viable split designs are STR/WIS (80-75-70-70-98-107-80), STR/AGI (80-85-70-70-88-107-80) and STR/STA (80-85-70-70-88-107-80). Splits involving INT or CHA are not recommended, because such low Physicals in a melee character demand serious attention during character design.

Half-Elves

Walking one foot in each of two worlds, Half-Elves face the conflicting demands of two societies, and often receive vile epithets simply from their presence. In historical fantasy literature, half-elves were wanderers, explorers, recluses, carefrees and loners. They are often presented in varying manners as bitter against the resentment often given them by both humans and elves, as elves with more sense than typical elves, as humans with more grace and beauty than typical humans, and as those who bridge two separate cultures. In short, there are a variety of 'acceptable' personalities applicable to a Half-Elven character without receiving odd looks from other role-players.

Half-Elven Paladins are the second best balanced race available to the paladins, at 80-75-90-85-65-75-75. The physicals are average, the only remarkable stats being the highest AGI and second highest DEX; Strength is +5, Stamina is even, Agility is +15, Dexterity is +10. Half-Elves pay for their balance in a very important area though, they have the lowest WIS. Wisdom is -10, Intelligence is even, Charisma is even.

As with their roleplaying background, Half-Elves can pick most any statistical direction and enjoy good results. The low WIS will have a direct impact on their casting abilities throughout their careers, and so many Half-Elven paladins choose to place points there; but the WIS can be left alone and other options pursued, certainly.

Thus, a popular design is the WIS option, 80-75-90-85-85-75-75. If the WIS is left alone, the next most popular is STR, at 100-75-90-85-65-75-75. Because of the unique balanced nature of the race, but with a critical low in magic and with substandard though not criminally low physicals, Half-Elven designs don't typically branch out into pumping the other stats. The most common split is STR/WIS, at 90-75-90-85-75-75-75.

High-Elves

EverQuest takes a bit of a turn from the way some fantasy sources present their racial mixes, in that there is no 'plain elf' race in the game universe. There are only Wood Elves, presented as the common run of the mill elves (much as humans are the common men against the Erudite's more lofty status), and High-Elves. As with the high-men, the High-Elves are elves that branched from their 'country cousins' to more directly pursue the 'cerebral' and 'serious'. This isn't to say Wood Elves aren't these things, but rather that High-Elves are more typical of such qualities. Think Erudite, but with ears and nightvision, and with that peculiar Elven bent. As with Erudites, Paladins are the only melee oriented class available to High-Elves.

The starting stats for a High-Elf Paladin are: 65-70-85-70-100-92-90, which is decidedly bent toward the Mental. High Elves have the lowest Physicals, just a bit below the Erudites. -10 Strength, -5 Stamina, +10 Agility, -5 Dexterity; the lowest STR and STA, tied at the lowest DEX, but the second highest AGI. Mentally, they have +25 Wisdom, +17 Intelligence, and a +15 Charisma; which gives them the highest starting WIS and CHA, and the second best INT.

With such a low Strength, you can well imagine the problems High-Elven Paladins have struggling to shoulder the burdens of a tank. Most designs place the emphasis on STR, 85-70-85-70-100-92-90. Almost any design that doesn't put points into STR will leave the character literally struggling, but the only option that might win out over the STR is AGI, at 65-70-105-70-100-92-90, if you swing that way.

Splits could go STR/WIS 75-70-85-70-110-92-90, STR/AGI 75-70-95-70-100-92-90 or STR/STA 75-80-85-70-100-92-90.

Humans

And finally, we're brought to the normal Humans. We all know Humans, we 'play' one in real life. Humans are a curious blend of oddities, with obsessions, indifferences, loyalties and cruelties. In historical fantasy literature, every range of Human action and notion that can be found in real life and history is represented. You can be heroic and stalwart, timid and cowardly, bookish, brutish, cultured, eager, indifferent, angry, driven, complacent, anything. All is possible with a Human. It's important to note that as constructs, most 'created fantasy races' tend to be more narrowly defined than Humans, basically because we know how weird we are, and will make the constructs more 'easily labeled'.

As one might imagine, Humans are the most balanced of the possible Paladin designs. 85-80-75-75-80-75-85. They have the middle position of the Physicals and Mentals, and are not so far behind the leaders, or ahead of the losers, in either. Physically, they stack up pretty well with a +10 Strength, +5 Stamina, Agility even, Dexterity even. Mentally, again without major fault, +5 Wisdom, Intelligence at even, and +10 Charisma. They have the second highest starting Strength and Stamina, an anomaly only because so many of the Paladin races are decidedly non-physical; and the second lowest Dexterity. Both Wisdom and Intelligence are middle of the road, but the Charisma is the second highest.

Predictably, Human Paladins don't begin with any major weaknesses, which in itself is a strength. However, they also don't begin with any glaring strengths, which in itself can be a weakness. The Half-Elf Paladins try to be a middle of the road design, but don't quite make it; Humans are. Anything can be emphasized without leaving other things markedly low.

Because Paladins are a melee class, STR is a common design, 105-80-75-75-80-75-85. AGI 85-80-95-75-80-75-85 or STA 85-100-75-75-80-75-85 are also possible. Or one can go WIS 85-80-75-75-100-75-85, or possibly INT 85-80-75-75-80-95-85. Splits often go STR/WIS 95-80-75-75-90-75-85, STR/AGI 95-80-85-75-80-75-85 or STR/INT 95-80-75-75-80-85-85.



Discussing the Paladin Class: Levels and Advancement
As has been repeatedly stated, Paladins are a melee class. That bears repeating, because often players in EverQuest will head into a class thinking it's something it's not. Based on observation, most clerics play as if they wish they were paladins, and many paladins play as if they were clerics. Each is a mirror opposite of the other. Paladins are three parts tank, one part healer. Clerics are three parts healer, one part tank. Above anything else, keep this firmly fixed in mind when you play your paladin; you are a tank. To be technical, you're a self-filling tank; you can burn off some fuel, then replenish it yourself.

1st to 5th Level

A Paladin is much like most any other class in the game during this phase, and is especially like the other Warriors and combat hybrids (Shadowknights, Rangers, Paladins). Find something, kill it, sit down for a brief rest, rinse-repeat. The caster classes may struggle a bit more here because they don't have meditate yet, and their spells are still weak, but there are no large differences between one character or another. Fortunately, this phase goes very quickly.

Offense, Defense and your Primary weapon skill (almost certainly either 1-Handed Slash (1h-s) or 2-Handed Slash (2h-s)) will require little attention to maintain. Taunt and Sense Heading should be hotkeyed, the former on your primary combat keybank, the latter on your travel keybank, and used at least as often as required to level cap. Secondary and Tertiary weapon skills (the other of 1h-s or 2h-s, and probably 1h-b) should be level capped as you are able to during your rise in levels, which come fast in this period. If you level before level capping all your skills, just switch back to your primaries and continue the procedure. You'll find that once you enter the next phase of your character's growth, everything you're pursuing will catch up to your level.

Archery is the one tough one. Archery is a skill that requires a continual expendure of cash, unlike the other combat skills which simply require a one-time, up front, investment in a weapon. Arrows, even self made for 4cp - 6cp each, can begin to drain a young character's resources. For this reason, it's often best to leave Archery largely alone until you enter the second or third phase of development.

1st to 5th Level: Equipment

Through this period, expect to use the newbie weapon, and then an appropriate rusty weapon that you find. Also, Cloth armor is what you should get used to. Towards the very end of this phase you may be able to obtain Patchwork armor, which is a great upgrade from Cloth for a fledgling tank. Money from newbie monsters is simply very hard to come by; you should make enough to pay for food and water, perhaps fishing bait and poles, and not much else.

6th to 10th Level

Paladins receive Bash at 6th, their first special combat skill, and their only alternate attack. Bash requires a shield to function, and is a good excuse to acquire one if you haven't prior to this point. The increase in AC will help out, and the added features of additional damage and good caster interrupt will assist you during battle. At 10th, you receive Dodge, your first automatic combat skill. Train one point and watch it start to rise. By the time you reach about 50 points, you should begin to notice at least one dodged blow per battle. Also at 10th, Bind Wounds becomes available. Unless you know a Magician who can summon bandages for you, this is a very expensive skill to practice, and a frustrating one as well. You have to reach a high level of proficiency, well past 60 or 70 points, before you'll begin to notice significant benefit. This phase will seem to drag on and on though, as Warriors start to look like a better deal than Paladins with their more accomplished abilities, and your lack of magic.

At 9th, the first of three major landmarks arrives for a Paladin, spells. Rank 1 Cleric spells may not seem like much to jump around for, but after all that time of being promised another facet of your character, to finally receive it is a great blessing. Immediately proceed to your guild and train one point in each of Abjuration, Alteration, Divination and Channeling. Then start practicing your spells. Unfortunately, you'll never match the mana reserves of a pure Cleric, effectively count yourself as your level minus eight for purposes of determining mana in the fashion a Cleric would. But if you make it a point to always have less than full mana, unless heavily engaged in combat, you should soon find your skills in magic rising nicely.

6th to 10th Level: Equipment

If you haven't already so attired yourself, you should be in full Patchwork very early in this phase. Typical prices for Patchwork are 2gp to 5gp per piece (7 pieces makes a full set of Patchwork, as Verant hasn't implemented patterns for all the possible body location), and you should be charged on the low end of the scale if you provide the tailor with a ruined pelt or two per piece. As you move through 8th or so, you should start to collect leather and rawhide armor, which should run you about 5gp to 7gp per piece. You'll be able to find leather for all body locations, and by the time you reach 10th you should be in full leather with a few low level magic items in various slots (studded or spiked collars, rusty spiked shoulderpads, dervish rings, things like that). If you know a smith, or if you hang out in heavily populated zones, you might even manage to acquire a piece of banded or ringmail here and there, but it will probably still be a bit out of your price range.

Also, right as you hit 6th, you'll start to really chafe against the limited abilities of your rusty weapon. Priority one for this phase should be to upgrade your weapon. The 'normal' versions of the weapons (Longsword, as opposed to Rusty, Tarnished or Fine Steel) are prohibitively expensive; so much so that you'll never buy one (by the time you can properly afford store prices for these 'normal' weapons, you'll be using magical or other unique weapons). There are two major options for rising tanks; the infamous Minotaur Axe, and a Fine Steel weapon in your chosen flavor. For 2h-s wielders, you pretty much have the Fine Steel 2-Handed Sword or Axe as your best option for now. The Minotaur Axe should run you 4pp or less, especially if you're on Feydwer; prices may be higher in Halas or Qeynos. The Fine Steel 1h-s weapon should cost between 5pp and 10pp, but 8pp to 10pp is the 'sock-it-to-the-buyer' area of that price range. The Fine Steel weapons typically sell to vendors for about 3pp to 4pp, so asking 5pp to 7pp is a reasonable profit over that, and provides a service to your fellow player. Hopefully, young paladins will find a kind soul who's not eager to graft from those younger than he. You'll also need to buy a shield, look through the stores to get a feel for what's available to your race and class, and maybe ask over auction if anyone has what you're looking for, then make the purchase. The shield you get now will probably be carried through the next two phases or so.

Archery is a lot more feasible here, if you keep it to a dull roar and make your own arrows. Roughly 3pp should buy you a fletching kit and a Hemp strung Hickory bow from a more accomplished Fletcher, and get your first hundred or so arrows worth of components. Getting Fletching up to about 50 shouldn't cost so terribly much, and will give you the ability to make basic Class 1 arrows costing about 6cp each with a success rate better than 75%. Practicing Archery requires either an understanding group (willing to let you stand off and shoot arrows, instead of engaging in melee), or requires several hours of hanging around areas where the fights are white or better to you and shooting into the backs of MOBs others are fighting.

11th to 15th Level

There are two waypoints in this phase. Meditate comes at 12th, the second major landmark for Paladins, and you'll suddenly wonder how you ever got along without it. Indeed, prior to 12th, you'll find soloing incredibly painful as you complete a fight and then enter a period of casting Minor Heal, sitting for five minutes to restore some mana, casting more Minor Heal, etc... After Meditate though, you replace the Sit button on your primary keybank with Spells, so you simply complete a battle, cast some post-battle spells, then click your Spells hotkey and use your mouse to start Meditating. Also, after Meditate, you'll notice memorizing spells takes far less time than it once did.

The other waypoint is at 15th, when you receive your Rank 2 spells. Of these, the most welcome is Light Heal, which will be a sorely needed upgrade to the now inadequate Minor Heal. Further, you receive Cure Disease and Lull, both of which have their places. You also get Hammer of Wrath and Ward Undead, both spells that represent the first chance you have to work Conjuration and Evocation, respectively. It's recommended you wait until now to put points into these skills because of the way EverQuest handles the 'learning the basics of...'. By waiting, you'll save training points, and have a higher rating to show for it. In any event, because of the high casting costs and low initial skill levels, these are painful spells to practice. Some tips for doing so are shown a bit later.

15th is when you should reach your skill cap for Archery, at 75. This is lower than most combat skills will ever reach, but even at only 75, you should continue to see good use from your bow. At this point, the cost of arrows isn't so draining, and you should be able to afford as many as you like to use.

11th to 15th Level: Equipment

Here you should being to notice the limitations of Leather, just a little. By the time you reach the end of this phase, you'll really be itching to be in metal armor, and the MOBs you're fighting will be reminding you of it. Ring is readily available for your income level at this point, and if the proliferation of cheap Banded armor continues as it seems to be, you may be able to start jumping right into Banded. By the end of this phase, you should be in mostly metal with some leather and magical.

Your trusty Fine Steel weapons should continue to serve you through this period, though you'll notice some jealousy pangs as you start to become aware of the unique weapons in the game. No monsters in your level, at this time, require magic to hit, so you should be able to hold out just a little longer.

As for Archery, there's little reason not to upgrade from Hickory to Elm; you should expect to pay from 4pp to 7pp for an Elm bow strung as you like, depending on the fletcher. Or you can work your fletching up and construct one yourself; a few tries with a skill in the 50s or 60s should yield a bow.

16th to 20th Level

Another two waypoints await you here. The first is at 17th, Parry. Like Dodge, this is an automatic combat skill, and one you'll soon learn to love. By the time you get to about 50 points, you should see one parry per battle. Combined with Dodge, the duo make for quite a few shots that simply don't hit you. Toward the end of this phase, when both are reaching into the 90+ points area, you should see them go off several time each during a battle, especially when dueling blue conned MOBs.

The other waypoint is at 20th, when you finally receive Double Attack for your last major landmark. During the previous and current phases, you had to suffer Warrior Envy as you watched Warriors and Rangers run around with two weapons, and as you watched them both out perform you blow for blow. Especially as you near 20, you should notice you have a lot of difficulty taunting MOBs off fellow Warriors simply from the sheer amount of damage they're dealing. Double Attack changes that, finally moving you back into contention as a respectable Tank.

While you saw an increase in 'paycheck' from your looting as you went from phase to phase, you'll notice a definite increase here, especially if you move into the pure dungeon zones for your adventuring. Befallen, Cazic Thule, Guk, Unrest, High Hold Pass and other zones will become like second homes to you, yielding as much as 20pp to 30pp for an evenings' adventures if you play your cards right and get a bit lucky in your loots. It is during this phase that you'll really start to feel like a part of the world, no more merely ignoring auctions, but actually looking at what's for sale, and determining if you want it, rather than simply shrugging it off because you know it's out of your reach. Further, you'll be far more free to travel without constant fear of instant death from anything moving. This freedom is rather enjoyable, and enables you to explore the continents.

16th to 20th Level: Equipment

You'll definitely notice if you're not in metal armor, and even your ring will start to feel a bit weak. Again, with low prices, Banded becomes a very appealing option. In this phase, a very important crossroads is reached in terms of Equipment; and since Equipment is what defines a tank, it's a very pivotal choice. Will you upgrade your weapon first, or your armor? Either can be correct if you select the proper zones to adventure in.

If you pick armor, moving into a mixture of Banded and Ring, with the odd bit of magical, should cost you about 100pp. Full Banded will probably run more, somewhere around 120pp or 130pp. You'll notice the lower frequency of hits, and the lower damage most hits do. If you ever doubt your armor purchases after having made them, simply go fight a MOB, then fight another MOB of the same type with your armor in your backpack, and question no more.

If you pick weapon, you can go either unique, or Combine. Combine weapons are available from the Gypsy camps scattered through the realm of Norrath, research the EverQuest websites to determine where to find one. Not all camps carry all Combines, and not all camps are agreeable with all characters (some are KOS Good, others are KOS Evil, etc...) But once you find a camp you can visit, and that carries what you're looking for, expect to pay between 50pp and 65pp, depending on Charisma, for a 1h-s Combine weapon like a Longsword or Scimitar. For a Combine 2-Handed Sword, expect to pay from about 90pp to as much as 110pp. The advantage of Combines is their stats are identical to the familiar Fine Steel weapons, and they're readily available with no waiting once you have the needed cash.

Unique weapons are solely available through the players' market, and are subject to availability and location. If you're after a specific weapon, research the EverQuest websites to determine where it spawns and go with cash in hand to those areas, making Auction calls for it. Most weapons that are in your price range will go for between 100pp and 150pp, things like Polished Granite Tomahawks, Bone Bladed Claymores and Shiny Brass Halberds. The advantage of Uniques is they tend to have slightly better stats (a bit more damage or a bit less delay than comparable combine weapons), and also tend to have a few low to mid range magical effects. If you can put together a group, you may want to try and fight your way through the throngs of item farmers (those who camp an item repeatedly) to see if you can't have a shot at killing the MOB dropping the desired item yourself; but don't hold your breath for the highly desired items. Let's just say there are some extremely rude and greedy people playing this game.

The point of upgrading your weapon is twofold, however. To increase your weapon efficiency (if possible), and to also add the ability to tackle magic-only MOBs, like the ghouls that have probably been plaguing you for a while now. Some zones, particularly undead zones like Befallen and Unrest, require enchanted weaponry to make in headway in. The loot makes up for it, but first you have to make a downpayment.

Also, if you're following the Sword and Shield route, you'll want a magical shield. The easiest to get, either by fighting (if you can make it past the hordes of farmers in Crushbone) or by purchasing, is a Shiny Brass Shield. Expect to pay 15pp to 20pp on Feydwer and be able to find one without too much effort, and up to as much as 30pp to 40pp on Antonica depending on how far from Freeport you are, with more effort. The point of getting a magical shield is the ability to bash magic-only creatures without getting the dreaded INVULNERABLE message in combat. If you're not going to get a magic shield, you might as well get a magic 2h-s weapon for fighting in such zones. While it is not very important when fighting in heavy undead zones, because many undead are magic only, you should be able to upgrade from an Elm to Ash bow at this point, and will probably appreciate the change. There is a price jump, from roughly 3pp in materials costs to about 20pp, but an Ash bow will serve you well. If you purchase an Ash bow, expect to pay between 25pp to as much as 40pp, depending on area and the fletcher. It might help if he knows you personally. Unfortunately, you'll likely be unable to afford a coveted Oak bow until the next phase.

21st to 30th Level

Here, you have a few things to look forward to. At 22nd you receive your Rank 3 spells, including Root, Center and Invisibility to Undead. There are good uses for most of the spells you receive. At 30th you gain access to Rank 4, which brings Healing (upgrading from Light Healing), Expluse Undead (upgrading Ward), Soothe (upgrading Lull) and Stun (adding a spell that produces a Bash stun). All are welcome. Also at 30th you get Riposte, which will further upgrade your melee skills. When Riposte goes off, you get an additional attack against the MOB. Since you get a Riposte check against every swing of the MOB, it's possible for a character, with enough luck and skill, to Riposte several times in a single round. Riposte attacks don't replace your normally allotted attacks., but rather supplement them.

21st to 30th Level: Equipment

As you enter this phase you'll really be raking loot in, all kinds of unique, interesting types of weapons, armor and equipment, and also cash. Depending on where in the world you're fighting, you'll have had the ability to add any low to mid level magical item you like; earrings and rings, bracers, collars, shoulderpads, belts, boots and other items that bring attribute bonuses or add some type of ability. In fact, you'll feel almost compelled to obtain these items, as your tank status will urge you to continually better yourself and your equipment. At this phase, you'll want to be working on as much as twenty or thirty points of bonuses to your base attributes, and possibly even have multiple 'bonus attribute configurations' (one for combat, one for a different type of combat; like STR and STA for meleeing vs. DEX and AGI for archery, CHA for selling loot, INT for working trade skills, etc...).

Armor wise, you should certainly be in full metal armor, at least banded, very early in this phase. Store bought plate is still prohibitively expensive, and, like normal weapons, will likely remain so. By the time you can truly afford to purchase it, you'll most likely be wearing various forms of magical unique armors (like Rubicite, as an example). At this phase, you'll start to adventure in zones dependant upon the possible loot; not just for the cash value of the loot, but for the actual loot itself. Tanks always need the best possible equipment, and fighting against ever more powerful MOBs will continually remind you of this.

Regarding weaponry, your basic Combine or other mid level unique weapon should continue to serve you well; though you'll begin to eye more powerful options, and to investigate rumors of Holy Swords. As far as the players of EverQuest know at this point, some Holy Swords do exist, but are fairly rare, and hard to get. Be prepared to camp spots for upwards of a week to obtain even a known sword like Ghoulbane. Other swords are little more than myth at this point, though Paladins continue to eagerly track down their existences.

Somewhere in this area, you should be able to obtain an Oak bow, strung as you like. When getting an Oak bow, be sure to get a good one, worked with the tools you request. Examine some fletching sources on the web, and have the bow done just as you like. There is a sizable price difference between an Oak and Darkwood bow, so you'll probably carry your Oak bow for several weeks, at least, before being able to lay down the platinum on a Darkwood bow. Constructing these is quite expensive, and purchasing them even moreso. You may wish to buy one from someone selling, as it eliminates the chance you might make a very expensive failure on your own fletching roll. Paying 100pp over the materials cost is a small price to pay for the guarantee of not paying any more.

Beyond 30th Level

If you don't know what to do with yourself past 30, you need more help than this guide can provide. There are high level dungeon and adventuring zones all the way up to, and past, 50th. Cazic Thule will support adventurers as high as the upper 30s, Lower Guk is supposed to support into the mid 40s. Kerr Keep, a dungeon located beneath the waters of Dagnor's Cauldron, doesn't start until the low 30s. Mistmore, in Lesser Faydark, is designed to begin in the extreme upper 20s and hold you right on to 50. Permafrost Keep, on the Everfrost plains, is supposed to be similar to Mistmore, maybe starting a bit higher. And the lair of the red dragon, Solusek A and B, should certainly give you enough to do. Beyond that, you can always visit various evil zones and amuse yourself killing guards that plagued you so when you were but a wee Paladin (there are probably a lot of Paladins with scores to settle against Ogre, Troll and Dark Elf guards), or track down Giants and Griffins, which will bring cheers from the younger characters as their hated nemesises fall one after the other.

While I haven't confirmed it, the Paladin spell ranks seem to follow a formula; levels between the two previous ranks plus one. You get your first rank at 9th, the second at 15th (interval of 6), third at 22nd (interval of 7), fourth at 30th (interval of 8). Following this pattern, I would expect to see the fifth rank at 39th, and the sixth at 49th. Check with your guild for the spells.



Discussing the Paladin Class: Spells
A Paladin's spells complete him, give him balance and focus. It's kind of like a Jedi Knight, without the Force he's just a guy with a cool sword. Magic brings balance to Paladins, gives them a sense of completion, and properly enhances their abilities to make up for the amount of melee power they yield for the magic.

Rank 1

These spells are all fairly minor, and work only four of the six possible magical skills, but are very exciting to receive. For half the period you wield Rank 1 only, you will be without Meditate, but don't that damp your enthusiasm. These spells make you more powerful, more useful.

Courage, (Buff-Courage), ABJURATION: Adds minor increases to the target's Armor Class and Hit Points. This is the beginning of a line that buffs AC and HP, and the buffs get drastically better as you get higher in rank. The Clerics have access to the higher end of the line, and it can get very massive. This spell should always be up, at all times. It's a great way to practice Abjuration, and it's worth about a free level in hit points, which is A Good Thing; compared to not having it.

Cure Poison, (Cure), ALTERATION: Will remove a Poison effect from the target. Since Poison is one of the most common, and first, types of Damage-Over-Time effects one runs into, the ability to clear it can be very useful. Something about Poisons that confuses a lot of young Paladins and Clerics is Poison comes in varying degrees of strength. Sometimes you may have to cast the spell multiple times to clear the Poison effect.

Flash of Light, (Miscellaneous), DIVINATION: Causes a brief period of Blindness in the target. It's unclear as to whether or not this spell really affects combat or not; casters will still cast against you, fighters will still hit you. If their chances are degraded, it isn't a major difference. The one truly useful thing about this spell is it can be used as a poor man's Fear to open combat with; Blind a target then follow it with your bow. All Blinded targets will instantly attack you when the effect goes away though.

Minor Healing, (Healing), ALTERATION: Your first in the Healing line, the most often used line in the entire Paladin arsenal. Even this very minimal healing will be extremely useful, and serves to shorten your down time after fights. This spell begins the Paladin's role as the post-battle healer, see the spell tactics below.

Sense Magic, (Miscellaneous), DIVINATION: This is a bugged spell. Originally, it was supposed to do what it sounds like it should do. However, Verant left the Beta ability to simply right click any item and receive the full stats in the game, and thus all the identification spells are largely ineffective. Sense Magic does have one important benefit; it's a very easy way to practice your Divination skill. While resting or fishing, repeatedly cast it on yourself to increase Divination. Divination may seem kind of useless right now, but the Divination school of spells that arrive at higher ranks will become much more useful.

Spook the Dead, (Fear), ALTERATION: A creature specific spell, it will induce Fear in an Undead target. If not resisted (any target that will commonly resist your Ward line will probably commonly resist this spell as well) will be Feared for short time. It's a great way to split a pair of undead up, or to get time to heal or run in the middle of a tough fight. You can also cast it and run after the fleeing undead firing arrows into it, or hitting it in the back. It may be a little dishonorable, but Evil must be punished!

Yaulp, (Strength), ABJURATION: A short term (roughly four or five rounds) spell that provides a temporary increase of Armor Class and Strength. Referencing the comments about Strength in attributes, don't expect this to increase your damage; but if you check your Attack Rating before and after Yaulping, you should see it increase during the Yaulp. Cast this in tough fights, especially when double teamed.

Rank 2

These will come none too soon, during 13th and 14th, you'll start to grow very weary of the machine gun casting of Minor Healing for small chunks of health each casting, and you'll be anxious for a more rounded spell experience. Rank 2 provides it, and gives you greater flexibility in combat and healing.

Cure Disease, (Cure), ALTERATION: As the name indicates, it removes the Disease effect commonly imparted by Mummies. Unlike Poison, however, Disease typically is removed with a single casting of the spell. Disease is a very dangerous, and irritating, Damage-Over-Time effect because it effectively cancels the action of the normal regeneration process. During the fight, the Diseased character will only go down in HPs; and even sitting, the regeneration rate will barely be above even.

Hammer of Wrath, (Summoned Weapon), CONJURATION: This spell summons a magical, non-droppable 1 handed blunt weapon that can be wielded by the Paladin using the 1h-b skill. The stats are strictly middle of the road, and are certainly not better than Fine Steel Warhammers or the Screaming Mace, but if you have no other magic weapon, it's better than nothing. It can also serve to enable you to fight back to your corpse, but would require you to upkeep your 1h-b skill. This is also the first chance you get to practice Conjuration, which can make trying to cast this spell very frustrating.

Holy Armor, (Buff-Armor), ABJURATION: The beginning of the other buff line available to Paladins, this line simply increases Armor Class, and (so the rumor goes), offers a slight increase to damage absorption. Like Buff-Courage, this line should always be active. Something is wrong if you don't see those two yellow shield icons in your buff stack.

Light Healing, (Healing), ALTERATION: The second in your Healing line, it replaces Minor Healing, and none too soon. Initially, it will take most of a bubble of mana, but will yield good results. You can complete a healing period much quicker with a smaller number of Light Healing castings than with Minor Healing castings, which enables you to get to Meditating that much quicker.

Lull, (Lull), ALTERATION: Begins a line of spells that enable you to better control your fights, especially when hunting static spawn points, or when encountering from a distance multiple MOBs. When you successfully Lull a MOB, it will have it's aggressiveness reduced; this enables you to attack it's brother right next to it without bringing both hurtling across the land to you. When casting Lull, you receive one of three messages; a no message result that indicates the spell didn't work, a result indicating the MOB's aggressiveness was lowered, and a result indicating the MOB resisted the spell. A resist will often bring the non-Lulled MOBs in the area after you, so be ready for this result, and run if necessary. Soloing is made possible by this spell.

Sense the Dead, (Miscellaneous), DIVINATION: The first spell in the Divination school that most players will find to be of some important use, this can enable you to locate undead MOBs in the area, and hunt them down. While not very useful with static spawn points, with wandering undead, it can enable you to find, or avoid, them. When cast, it will turn you toward the undead. If it's moving, you'll have to cast several times, each time moving and recasting, to narrow in on it.

Ward Undead, (Ward), EVOCATION: Your first spell in the Ward line, offering direct damage against undead targets. This can be a very frustrating spell to practice because prior to Rank 2, you have had no opportunities to practice Evocation. One of the best ways is to target the corpse of an undead and cast repeatedly; your skill will rise in this fashion, and is the most efficient method to raise the skill to a useful level. It can be used to turn a tough fight in your favor, to pull undead to you, and to finish off fleeing undead. Just remember to not burn all your mana, in case you or someone else needs some healing. Nothing invokes more feelings of helplessness than to watch someone die because you threw one too many Wards.

Rank 3

By now you should be an accomplished caster, both in and out of combat. Rank 3 again expands your roster of spell selections, and enables you to delve further into the non-melee segments of the battlefield. It can be tempting to start relying more and more on your magic, but struggle to remind yourself to use your swords first, your magic second. That said, there are some very useful spells here.

Center, (Buff-Courage), ABJURATION: The second in this line, it replaces Courage by providing a greater boost to Armor Class and Hit Points. Again, maintain this spell up at all times. Endure Poison, (Resist), ABJURATION: A minor line of spells for Paladins, it yields enhanced resistance to Poison effects; enabling you to more often avoid becoming poisoned. Use anytime you're fighting Poison wielding MOBs.

Halo of Light, (Miscellaneous), CONJURATION: A spell that's very limited in use, but occasionally it will come in handy. It summons a light source that will enable you to see in the dark. Even Infravision can leave you stumbling around blindly in the forest looking for a zone exit, and trying to find your corpse like this can be very frustrating. Use this spell to ease that pain.

Invigor, (Cure), ALTERATION: Simply enough, this spell returns color to a character's Fatigue bar. During long, drawn out fights, step back to cast this spell on yourself and the other tanks, and enable all to better continue waging the battle. When you need it, you'll understand how useful it is.

Invisibility to Undead, (Miscellaneous), DIVINATION: Another spell mentioned earlier under the heading of 'Why Divination can be useful'; this spell does just what it says. Undead will not see you, enabling you to slip past, or to pick your targets without having to continually fight low level undead trash while seeking a source of evil truly worthy to be dispatched by one of your talents. It's also useful for retrieving corpses buried in the middle of undead zones.

Reckless Strength, (Strength), ALTERATION: This spell is an upgrade to Yaulp, more effective, with a longer duration, but also with a minor penalty to pay. It yields a greater boost to Strength for a longer period; but the bonus wanes at a steady pace, and will continue to detract until you reach ten points below your normal value, at which time it will fade. Useful for a battle followed by a recovery period.

Root, (Miscellaneous), ALTERATION: By now you'll have seen this spell wielded by casters to great effect; stop a MOB in it's tracks. It's a great way to free a fleeing MOB from reaching fellow MOBs that it will return with. Another use is to Root a MOB, then stand off shooting arrows or throwing Wards into it. Or Root and retreat to cast healing and maybe even catch a quick Meditate. Root has many uses, all of them effective.

Rank 4

By now you should be an accomplished Knight Templar, effectively wielding steel and mana to devastating effect. Rank 4 provides yet more tools in your ever endless battle to bring justice and peace to the lands.

Expulse Undead, (Ward), EVOCATION: Upgrading Ward Undead in that line, this spell has double the performance of the previous version, and will be quite useful at your higher level as you battle against powerful Undead.

Hammer of Striking, (Summoned Weapon), CONJURATION: A better version of the previous spell. The stats are unknown, but may be equal, or better, than some of the more appealing 1h-b weaponry. Healing, (Healing), ALTERATION: Finally, an upgrade to Light Healing. While you are still never a replacement for a Cleric, this latest in the Healing line will continue to provide you with the ability to grant rest and recovery to those in need. Including yourself.

Soothe, (Lull), ALTERATION: Upgrades Lull, with better results and duration than the previous version.

Spirit Armor, (Buff-Armor), ABJURATION: Upgrades Holy Armor, granting a much better result. As always, this spell should be maintained at all times.

Stun, (Miscellaneous), EVOCATION: The first non-Undead specific chance you get to use Evocation, it will produce a stun effect in the target if not resisted. This can be very nice as it's a period where the target's not beating on the tanks, something every tank will greatly appreciate.

Symbol of Transal, (Buff-Courage), ABJURATION: The next upgrade in the line, it requires a reagent (material component) to cast, but is well worth the effort. It will provide a massive buff of Hit Points to the target. Highly recommended, but should only be cast right as you begin a hunting session.

Use of Spells

As mentioned repeatedly, your magic isn't your primary focus; your steel is. But your magic is highly useful. Even in a group with a cleric, you can be a very useful force towards keeping the group running smoothly. A few things must be noted before you'll fully understand the recommended tactics here.

A Paladin has fewer mana points than a Cleric does; remember, your mana bar doesn't start counting until you hit 9th. So a 17th Cleric who's Out Of Mana (OOM) will have to meditate for a lot longer than you will at 17th. In fact, you can usually cast a full mana bar of healing, meditate back up and cast another full bar and be fully meditated again before the Cleric's Full Mana (FM) again. Clerics are there to cast healing during battles. Paladins are there to cast healing before and after battles. This seems simple enough, but a lot of Paladins don't see it. It's ok for a Paladin to enter battle with low or no mana; for a cleric, it's a waste of time. When you see your puller running toward you with MOBs in tow, hit him with a heal as he runs up. After the battle, tell the clerics to sit down and start meditating while you start topping people off (puller first, then tanks evenly, then everyone else evenly). Try to get the clerics used to trusting that you'll burn your mana after the last MOB falls on heals, so they can start meditating back to full mana that much sooner. The quicker your healers are full, the quicker the group can tackle another tough fight.

After the fight is over, immediately target group members and start healing. Heal until you're OOM, then sit down to meditate. If you ever wonder how the so-called power levelers manage to get so much XP, it's through little tricks like not wasting time where they don't have to; if you want to talk with your group, cast your heals, then sit and click Meditate, then start talking. Now you're doing two things at once! And better, you're keeping that valuable battle-mana in the bars of the Clerics for the next fight.

Spell Practice

The skills that back up each spell begin quite low, and can make for a very frustrating spellcasting experience until you get them raised properly. This is compounded in the case of two skills that you don't receive until Rank 2, and that do not lend themselves to readily available practicing methods.

Channeling is simply the skill involved with the speed of your casting, and in recovery of casting if an interruption event occurs during the casting. Channeling will rise with any magical use, regardless of what other skill is being used. It is usually the first magical skill that level caps each time. No special effort required.

Alteration is the skill involved with all of your healing and curing spells, and with other spells like Spook the Dead, Yaulp, Root and Lull. Because healing is cast often, repeatedly, and because skill has low mana cost spells available to it, there is very little that needs to be done to maintain this skill. Simply casting healing on everyone you can find when Rank 1 spells are first received will cause the skill to raise. You can cast on yourself even if you're not hurt, it will still practice the skill. Alteration is typically the second skill level capped.

Abjuration affects all of your buff spells. Simply casting the buffs as they drop, and buffing group members, friends, newbies and anyone else nearby will be sufficient to keep this skill maintained. As with Alteration, simply cast the spells as often as you can find a target, even if it's yourself, when you first receive Rank 1 spells, and this skill will fill out just fine.

Divination is a head scratcher for most Paladins, but if you look ahead at the spells on the higher ranks, you'll see some spells that anyone can find a use for that use Diviniation. At Rank1 you receive two spells that will let you practice this skill. The easiest way to practice is to cast Sense Magic on yourself repeatedly when waiting for any reason, while fishing, sitting to heal, waiting for group members to return. This process will bring your Divination up nicely, and enable you to be fully prepared to begin using the more appealing spells when you receive them.

Evocation, however, comes at 15th, and produces some extremely frustrating hours for most paladins. The spell that uses it at Rank 2, Ward Undead, is a fairly expensive spell to cast; taking most of a bar of mana. Further, it must be cast only on undead, which can be hard to find sometimes. And the endless fizzling produced by the lack of skill in Evocation isn't designed to improve a paladin's mood. The best way to practice Evocation, therefore, is to find a newbie skeleton somewhere. Kill it with a Ward, a single Ward will cause it to crumple, and will give you some practice. Then sit next to the skeleton's corpse and cycle between meditating to regenerate mana, and casting Ward on the undead corpse. This will cause your skill to increase, a few hours, at most, should bring your skill up to a very respectable level, and eliminate the fizzling problem that had you tearing your hair out at the roots. Ward is still an expensive spell to cast, but the corpse targeting will allow you to practice very efficiently without having to constantly search for targets.

Conjuration is the final skill, and the one least used by paladins. Some don't even bother to work the skill. If you do, be prepared to spend a lot of time fizzling and meditating. The only way to aleviate the pain of Conjuration practice is to wait until Rank 3, when you receive Halo of Light, which has a lower mana cost than Hammer of Wrath. But since there are only three skills on the known Paladin spell lists that use Conjuration, it may just be a trinket kind of thing to practice the skill.



Discussing Weaponry: 1h vs. 2h
This is one of the most infamous 'fights' in all of EverQuest. It's a lot like the 'Superman could beat up Luke Skywalker - no he couldn't!' fights you used to have when you were younger. Verant has largely done a pretty good job of balancing this game, and even in areas where it's not totally even, it's still fairly close. There is no one 'best' weapon, or one 'best' weapon skill. Read that again. Think about it. Now, understand; most games are unbalanced, in that with a little research into the numbers behind it, you can find the one 'killer combo' that gives the most advantage over the other combos possible. Gamers are used to broken games, and are trying to apply those rules to EverQuest. So far, they're not succeeding.

So, here's how the 1h vs. 2h argument breaks down for all involved. First, you must understand weapon efficiency ratings. Divide the Damage by the Delay, round to three decimal places, and you get a number that looks a lot like a batting average. This number represents the default efficiency of the weapon.

Notice I said default efficiency. Meaning the weapon's efficiency when used in a completely controlled test environment. We all know that doesn't happen in EverQuest, you never get perfect conditions, and no two fights are exactly alike. So the ratios are only a rule of thumb. They're a convenient thing to look at and rub your chin sagely when pondering a weapon purchase. Typically, the good 1h-s weapons have ratios in the 0.200 vicinity. For example, a Fine Steel Longsword (6/28 0.214), the popular Minotaur Axe (8/37 0.216) and the less common 1 Handed Dwarven Axe (6/26 0.231). There haven't been any reports of any 1h-s weapons past 0.250 that aren't unique, class specific weapons; examples, Painbringer (Warrior Only 7/23 0.304) and Ghoulbane (Paladin Holysword 15/34 0.441). Most of the best 1h-s weapons are rare loot drops, are magical, and are highly sought after.

Typically, the good 2h-s weapons have ratios in the 0.250 to 0.300 range. For example, a Fine Steel 2 Handed Sword (12/43 0.279), a Halberd (14/50 0.280), or a Dwarven 2 Handed Axe (14/43 0.326). Some of the best known 2h-s weapons have ratios that reach into the 0.350 and higher range. Examples, Executioner's Axe (Warrior Only 25/50 0.500), Bone Bladed Claymore (17/45 0.378 but with a nasty attribute hit), or Shiny Brass Halberd (16/48 0.333).

Now, here's where you have to be able to use your mind a little to understand the concept of 'neither is better, just different'. There are two things to focus on when considering weaponry, aside from any beneficial or negative enchantments (like Polished Granite Tomahawks have a great Strength enhancement, and Bone Bladed Claymores have a heavy Agility hit); the amount of damage it does, and the delay factor involved.

Sound simple enough, but it's where most players stumble and miss the details. Most players focus exclusively on damage. It's somewhat understandable, everyone likes to see big numbers. Most players look for a weapon that will give them a big number when they hit.

What these players are completely discounting is the time between hits. Take two very common examples, the Minotaur Axe (8/37 0.216) and the Fine Steel Longsword (6/28 0.214). On Efficiency, these weapons are all but identical. The Minotaur Axe hits twenty-five percent harder, but is twenty-five percent slower. So it doesn't matter which you pick, right?

And it's on this one point, this one question, where the two schools of thought diverge completely. If you think Superman's king, he'll always be better than Luke Skywalker. If you like the young Jedi Knight, you'll never like the Man of Steel as much. There is data to support either position, but it largely comes down to a semantic issue.

Delay directly affects how many swings you can get off in a one minute fight. Faster weapon will receive more swings. In the case of the Fine Steel Longsword and Minotaur Axe, you'll swing twenty-five percent more often, but do twenty-five percent less damage, or vice versa. There are some situations where you're certainly better off with one vs. the other (for example, against casters, where interruption of spells is critical to success; another example, in tough fights where you may miss often; missing with one out of four swings isn't as critical when you're swinging twelve times as it is when you're swinging only eight; or when you want to stun or demoralize an opponent, higher single swing damage totals tend to do this more often), but largely it's a matter of personal taste.

So that considers just weapon vs. weapon. It does not include in the factoring the presence of the special combat attacks like Bash (and Slam, for the large races) and Kick. For Warriors, especially Large Warriors, it doesn't matter. They can use 1h-s and a shield and still bash or kick, or they can use 2h-s and still kick (and slam if they're Large). But for Paladins, they can only Bash when equipping a 1h-s with a shield. Simply put, when wielding a 2h-s weapon, Paladins are not as combat efficient as Warriors wielding a 2h-s weapon, or even a Ranger (who also retain Kick). The efficiencies of Kick and Bash are much more murky than those of the known weaponry, but they do add to the overall efficiency in combat. Going to 2h-s without a special combat attack very well means yielding points of efficiency to the Warriors and Rangers. This may not preclude consideration of 2h-s as a viable combat option, but is a definite factor when comparing the performances of the classes to one another.

And none of the above considers the combat situation with the Armor Class enhancing qualities of the shield equipped, or with the possibility of using the shield as an extra slot from which to garner magical enchantment bonuses (+10 Magical Resist from a Shiny Brass Shield, or +9 Strength from a Darkwood Shield, for example).

When you add it all up, Paladins tend to be more suited to 1h-s and Shield than 2h-s, but it's still a fairly minor issue. Nothing says you can't use 2h-s, or that you have to use 1h-s and shield. Personal tastes, availability of equipment, hunting styles, group configuration, mood and much more will factor this choice. But perhaps, after fully thinking the facts through, you'll be able to make a more informed position; and not merely one based on rumor and hope.



Discussing Hotkeys
Not an area that gets much attention in EverQuest write-ups, but one that will directly impact your efficiency during play; especially during combat. You only get six buttons per hotkey bank, and have to use the mouse arrows or the (faster) shift-number keys to change banks. Those six keys start out filled with extraneous stuff like fishing and begging, but quickly grow crowded. Before you know it, you're branching out into two, three, even all six of the banks. You place your buttons just so, and grow quite accustomed to them.

Because you only get six slots per bank, it's important to group them by bank for the maximum amount of relevance and efficiency; you don't want to have to continually shift key back and forth between banks to get at buttons you need on a regular basis. Place the buttons that you need for your play style, and change them only when you need to; you'll find you get quite unconscious about where a button is, and will find yourself stabbing at it even after you've changed the position.

I recommend players set up one, possibly two, combat hotkey banks; the primary and secondary. It can also be of great use to have a 'travelling', 'goofing off' or 'corpse recovery' bank. You may find use with a spell bank, or an inventory bank.

First, look at your keyboard, if you're not a touch typist (and therefore know the keyboard by heart); the A key for 'Auto Attack On' is right next to your 1 - 6 number keys. You can rest your hand on the left side of the keyboard and still operate the mouse with your right. If you want to get really fancy, remap movement keys to the left side of the keyboard in some fashion to enable you to never touch the right side of the keyboard (including the number pad for movement). But even if you switch between both sides of the keyboard and/or between movement and mouse control, you can operate your melee attack without hotkeying a button for it. That saves you a slot right there. Now, you have a Ranged Attack; this is probably a good choice for your combat bank. The chance to take bow shot will often come and go quickly, you don't want to miss it do you? Bash, if you're equipping 1h-s and a shield, is another key you'll certainly need. If you have Meditate you need a Spells button (so you can mouse click the Meditate button, which you can't hotkey, sadly); if you don't have Meditate you'll need a Sit button available somewhere. You need Taunt during combat, gotta have Taunt or your casters will hate you. And you have spells that you might want to get off at a moment's notice. There's also Lay On Hands, Sense Heading, skills like Fishing, containers like a Backpack, console commands like /time, /corpse and /loc.

Having said all that, here are the bank setups I use.

My Primary Combat bank is on Shift-1, and is where I spend the majority of my time. Range Attack, Bash, Spells, Taunt, Healing (whatever Healing Spell I currently am best at), Ward Undead. Ranged Attack so I can switch from melee to range quickly, which I often do in combat. Bash and Taunt should be obvious. Spells is in my primary combat bank because I will complete a fight and immediately cast and then meditate. The Healing slot is there because I hate fumbling for the mouse in the middle of a battle, someone could die before I get the cursor over to the spell icon and click it; likewise, Ward Undead is there for the same reason regarding mouse fumbling. When I stop hunting in a heavy undead area I'll replace Ward with something else, Sit again maybe, or a spell like Yaulp.

My Secondary Combat Bank is on Shift-2, and is my emergency bank. I have Lay On Hands, Sense Heading, my healing and curing spells, and the sit command. There are two reasons I have LOH on the secondary bank. One is I got tired of occasionally hit the wrong key and wasting my LOH for the next ninety minutes. The other is it keeps me on my toes, when I shift over to my secondary, I know I'm in serious trouble.

On Shift-3, I have my travel and corpse recovery keys. Sense Heading, /Corpse, /Location, /Time, /Sit and /Fishing. When I'm journeying from one zone to another, I use this bank because it has what I need. I can check my direction, see what time it is, drag my corpse after me, see where I am, sit down to rest and start fishing with my rod in hand (I'm a Master Fisher, by the way). To program your own buttons with console commands (even with things like shouts or default emoticons or says), click the Socials button on the main screen (the F10 windowed screen), then pick a button you're not using and rename it, and type the slash command in the box below just as you would from the command line.

I haven't filled up any other banks, but there's no reason to not do so; you can change them at anytime. Maybe have one whole bank for your spells, or for opening your backpacks? How about an Emote and Roleplaying bank full of things like /say Thanks very much, I greatly appreciate it!, /em waves excitedly at you, /em looks sorrowfully at the corpse, or whatever else you might like. Design a control interface that best enables you to play, and have fun! But do put some thought into it, so you can play better, and earn the respect of those you adventure with.



Final Thoughts on Paladins
This is a document written in a serious manner, but this is a fun game. Enjoy it. Explore the world, see the sights. Fight strange and powerful monsters, defeat evil from one coast to the other. Meet great people, make wonderful friends, and have a blast. If you can do all this while staying in character for a 'Paladin', mores the better. There is more to the game than simply power leveling.

If you need a starter kit for what to do when not frantically killing in a quest for more experience, try this. Newbie areas are a source of wonderful fun. As you wander around, or transit through, these areas, target the players around you and check their health. Heal those who are hurt. Respond to Disease and Poison calls with cures. Buff up people who seem to be having trouble. Stop and lend a hand with some heals and maybe a few arrows or sword swings if someone's being hard pressed to win a fight. Not only is it a lot of fun for both you and the newbie (I know I loved it when I was a wee little Paladin, to have a big character come by and drop a buff on me, or cast healing), but it's a great way to meet people and make friends. The newbie you save now might be the 30th level you group with later. And he'll tell his friends you're an ok guy. It all adds up. Think of it as EverQuest karma, of the good kind.
