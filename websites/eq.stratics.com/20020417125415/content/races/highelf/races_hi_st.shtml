Starting as a High Elf

 The Low Down   Is There More?   The Paladin   What Do I Do?   Newbie Combat   What Next?   Conclusion 

The Low Down
The good: Well for one they have the highest base wisdom than any other race. That plus they also have high intelligence to go with it. That makes a great combo when making a cleric, paladin, or even a magic-user. They have the best base charisma of any race too(good for enchanters). They have high agility. They also have infra-vision, which is a good commodity in the lands of Norrath. Another thing that is great and not mentioned is the fact they can wear small and medium armor(I'll explain more below).

The bad: They have low strength, low stamina, and low dexterity.

The low strength and stamina make people overlook this race for clerics and paladins because they think they will be better as a dwarf with really high stats in both categories. A little news flash to everyone: THIS GAME IS BALANCED. That means no matter what race you choose, you more than likely will be equal to a different character that has the same class but different race. I will admit though, at one point in time, I thought different. That was before I tried playing a dwarf paladin and cleric. I got sick of dieing from the low agility (no base defense), and the fact that I was barely learning skills. Then I tried a high-elf paladin and fell in love with this race. Granted I had low strength and stamina, but I had a heap of wisdom, agility, and charisma to start out with. One tip if you make a high-elf paladin would be to throw all your bonus points onto strength. You won't regret it.

 top

Is There More?
Yep, you guessed it, there is. You know how I was saying about the highest base wisdom? Guess what that means? That means you have higher resistance to spells that effect the mind. And what is even better is the fact that it is already a high stat before you even have to touch it. It gets even better. You also have high base charisma and that means cheaper prices. That might not mean much in the beginning, but wouldn't it be nice to shave some of the cost off the 950 platinum for that brand new plate chest? Remember how I was talking about high-elves being able to wear small and medium armor? What is so good about that you ask? Well remember Felwithe shares the continent with the little gnomes and dwarves. "So what!" you say? Well, when I was grouped with a gnome or a dwarf, I sometimes would find medium sized armor that I already had but was small sized. Boy, you don't know how much it can bring a smile to your friend when you say "I have a spare so and so piece of armor, do you need it?", and if they do, you just beefed your party's defense and saved him gold just because you have versatility on sizes. This also makes it easier to shop too. What if you're in Freeport and you need armor? Well if your small, you'll need to go send out a search party just to find a shop that sells small sizes. If you're a high-elf you can shop at any place that sells either small or medium sized armor. This alone can save lots of time, especially in Freeport with all the zoning.
 top

Why Choose One for Paladin?
All you warriors are out there laughing right now. "Haha, with low strength and low stamina he is in trouble picking a warrior type class." I'll let you warriors think that, but to be honest with you, my high-elf paladin was way better than my dwarf paladin. So now you're asking, why? Well remember that little stat called Intelligence? That helps your skills to skyrocket. At level 8, I had over 100 AC and with my sword I had 180 attack. I was almost as good as my friend who was a level 11 dwarf warrior. That is why I love this game. It is so balanced that anyone has freedom to choose whatever race and still have a formidable character compared to other races.
 top

What Do I Do?
Well once you make you character you'll end up inside Felwithe. The number one thing you do if you're a magic-using class is to scribe those 2 starting spells in your book. The reason I say this is because if your really new, you might drown or attack a guard by accident and you lose the spells if they aren't scribed (unless EQ has changed that recently). Now the next thing to do is read your other 2 scrolls, and you'll notice one is a pk note(I usually destroy that) and your guild note. If you right click the guild note it will give you your first quest: finding your guildmaster. Once you do this, you get a nice shiny little robe. The next thing I always do is try to find the bank and other various shops that I will need to know for future reference(more or less get to know your new town). If you picked a magic-using class, now is the time to memorize those spells. Some people might be wondering why I waited so long to say that? Well there is nothing worse than waiting to memorize a spell then to accidentally get killed and have to go through the long wait again. I always wait till it's monster wuppin time. Now you're ready to bust a can out on some monsters.
 top

Tips for Newbie Combat
First off if you picked the paladin or cleric, you're going to have a tough time finding things to kill. Paladins and clerics have to use the warrior approach to start the combat. This means running up to a monster and attacking it, and going into combat. So why go somewhere else then? Well, there is nothing like running after a monster to attack it when a magic-user hits it with a spell and poof there goes your monster and your wasted effort. Clerics will soon get enough money to buy the spell struck which will give them this option too, but for the paladin he has to suffer. What I did is I went to Kelethin and fought there, but you might wanna try to get to second level before going there because orc pawns spawn there, and seem to creep up on you. This saves you from walking to Kelethin alot. Ok now here is a run down on what to kill at 1st level: bats, wasps, decaying skeletons. At second level I'd kill the same things and add: pixie tricksters and black wolves. But what I did is I fight wasps because they seemed to give good experience, and also there seemed to be more of them. Now at level 3 you can add in orc pawns. I caution you to that because they sometimes carry weapons, and also people try to hog them because they have armor on them sometimes. You also can press your luck with the hatchlings, but I stayed away from them completely at level 3. Now at level 4, I'd try to kill more pawns and try a hatchling. If the hatchlings are whooping you, then I'd go to something that deals less damage.
 top

What Next?
There are a few things you can do now. Kill more of the same things, form a group and try to tackle orc centurions, venture to Butcher Block Mountains, or even venture to Steamfront Mountains. In my personal experience, every choice up above is a good one. I suggest trying them all. Personally, I went to Steamfront because I was familiar with it and I knew what to kill to get to 5th level the fastest. A lot of gnomes are going get mad about this but I killed spiderlings. I also know of another creature in Butcherblock that is good to kill, but is sometimes hard to find and that is skunks. But if you want to stay in Greater Faydark, I'd choose to kill everything in your path until 5th level, and then forming a group to go orc hunting. The best part about orc hunting is the fact that Crushbone is right there in Greater Faydark. That is a great place to increase your levels. So from level 5 to about level 15(I heard) it will be your new home. No matter what level you are though, groups are probably the best way to tackle Crushbone since trains and crazy spawns happen there often.
 top

Conclusion
I hope I gave you enough information to make your choice for race an even harder decision. Also this race is kind of fun to role-play. The high-elves have always been snaughty and better than everyone else. If you're around other role-players, then you might have some fun watching their reactions to some of your comments. Good luck, and hopefully you'll find happiness with the high-elf.
