The Simple Art of Online Role Playing: A Practical Guide

Introduction

That's right, the simple "art" of role-playing. Maybe some of you reading that headline scoffed, but the fact can't be denied: there is no science to role-playing, no method that can be easily applied to ensure success. That said, "art" is the only remaining classification broad enough to cover the myriad of elements that can come into play when "role-playing" a character. It may sound a bit high-minded, but it's true all the same, and the sooner all us online RPer's accept it the sooner we'll realize that there's more involved in successfully role playing a character than simply choosing an unpronounceable moniker and chopping up monsters. Yet, realization and implementation are vastly different things, so to make the transition easier for you gaming masses I've decided to write this article and provide some simple guidelines. Read on brave warrior/cleric/thief/druid/paladin/ranger/wizard/magician/enchantersheesh

Your Life. With Monsters.

As the title of this article suggests, role-playing a character well is not all that difficult, provided, that is, you follow a few logical guidelines. The first of these "logical guidelines" I'll comment on is perhaps the most important, far-reaching, and easily implemented. It is this; role-play what you know.

Are you a long-lost prince from some ancient civilization? Anything close? If not, why attempt to convincingly role-play a character you know nothing about? Trying to do so doesn't make sense. Now, don't get me wrong I'm not saying online worlds should be populated by college students, graphic designers and other such uninteresting creatures, but rather that these residents adopt an "online" persona that somehow correlates to their "real-world" persona. To illustrate: I am a writer in real life, a scribe online. This simple (and rather obvious, admittedly) likeness between my personas allows me to role-play with a confidence I would not have were I attempting to be, say, a mercenary. Role-playing my character feels natural and reacting to unexpected situations doesn't require referencing my library to find out what I should say or do. So, while escapism is all well and good, don't leave everything of yourself behind when you play your character lest you find yourself up the proverbial river without a paddle.

Moving on

Where Did the Big Voice Go?

This is a question many from the old pen-and-paper days might ask themselves at some point as their electronic avatar picks clean some recently chopped baddie. While they root about the slimy rags of the deceased, pulling out gold and silver, weapons and armor, they may just miss that big voice that used to tell them what the point was. We are saving a princess? Killing an evil sorcerer? Discovering long-lost relics of a perished race? Good to know, good to know. As the saying goes, "you don't know what you have till you lose it." Such is the case with that ever-mysterious figure, The Dungeonmaster. He never quite made it into the online RP world. That being the case, your little character has a tough question to answer and it's you who must answer it. Get ready

Why am I here?

Yes, before you can even begin to play as your character the character has to have some sort of purpose, some focus in it's virtual life. If you want that life to be at all interesting needs to be something more than "I want to be the best at everything, have the best of everything and generally kick everyone else's butt." Sure the above is simple, clean, quick - but damn, how boring. Sadly, a disgusting majority of the online RP world adheres to that very mantra and you'll no doubt spend many an online hour fending off conversations that begin with, "Do you know where the Sword of Suchansuch is?" Be assured then that if you do imbue your online character with any sort of viable purpose in his/her life you will, quite literally, make your world a better place. How's that for heroism?

Do Not Pass Go

When you first created a character in one of the many online RPG worlds, you may have noted the option of creating a "backstory" for your character. It was a good idea on the part of the programmers, but, far too often players get carried away when composing this "backstory" and only end up confusing the motives of their character and boring anyone who tries to read the gobbledygook they've typed in. You can avoid being one of those unfortunate persons by keeping one thing in mind; your character has no story until you play him/her through one. This "backstory" you've been invited to compose should really be called a "prelude" and consist of nothing more than simple background information. Compose a few notes on your character's personality and, most importantly, the reason he/she is venturing forth into the world. Any more than that and you risk having a character with limited options in life and the way he/she will react to any given situation. Keep It Simple, Stupid. You can have plenty of depth to your character without the epic consisting of ten thousand words behind them.

Consonants Do not Make the Man

Far too often in my wanderings I have happened upon adventurers who possess a very particular affliction. A twisted, painful affliction that seems to reside permanently above their heads, glowing there in grammatical confusion: their names. These "names" are horrendous mockeries of language, a jumbled mix of opposing consonants that would turn a man's tongue to jelly were he to attempt actually pronouncing the terrible things. Thankfully the word-based communication most prevalent in online communities prevents such an unfortunate occurrence, but still it does allow the almost agonizing task of typing these names in to occur. So, on behalf of my fingers and the dainty extremities of all my like-minded players, I issue a plea: Enough with the 12 letter, 10 consonant, 2 vowel names!

Now I know I am stepping on sensitive ground here, but something must be said. I don't think anyone likes having to scroll their text screen back to give a tell because they've already attempted and failed trying to spell some non-sensical moniker 3 times in a row. Was it "Mbnockilt" with an "a" or an "o"? Such unnecessarily overcomplicated names greatly increase the already somewhat arduous task of typing messages. In addition, I tend to subconsciously "pronounce" names I read in my head and when I come across something like "Mbnockilt" I can't help but grimace as the word bounces for a few moments around my skull. Mub-nok-ilt? Mib-no-kilt? It's just aggravating. Please, I don't care if you enter the uninspired ranks of the DBZ fanatics, just stop the madness!

There is No "PC" in "EQ"

This section is on etiquette. Don't let that stop you though, this isn't a bunch of "don't talk dirty, don't curse, play-nice-with-others" blather. This section is quite the opposite in fact. If you're role-playing a dark-elf necromancer and happen to run into a halfling cleric, logic dictates that there would be some tension there. While you may not want to haul off a blast the guy, a few well-worded threats, insults and general nastiness are completely within the bounds of the world Verant created. I'm not saying you should make life miserable for the little guy (you shouldn't, that's when petitions get noticed), but some in-character name-calling is, like I said, dictated by logic (unless you're one of those rare halfling-loving DE's*rolls eyes*). However, it occurs to me some might go a bit far with that line of thinking and I would like to clarify things by providing some dialogue to my Dark Elf meets Halfling scenario.

Acceptable:

Dark Elf: "Get away from me, you half-grown pustule, lest I feel the need to feed my pets."

Halfling: "Back off yourself, inkie, these be Vale woods. Unleash yer creatures here and I'll shove their bones done your throat."

Unacceptable:

Dark Elf: "Move it thimble-d***, before I sic my pets on your stumpy a**!

Halfling: "Just try it, b****, I'll see their bones halfway up your backside if you do!"

Ok, maybe it is a bit of "don't talk dirty, don't curse" blather, but hey, dem's da rules, folks!

Prithee, Stop Talking Like That

Dialogue is where it all happens. Everything else in this article is applied in dialogue; purpose, backstory, etiquette - the written word is the culmination of these things. Don't fidget, though - I'm not going to spout the attributes of pronouns and adverbs, or request that all EQ'ers have a pocket dictionary on hand for spelling purposes, such shortcomings are understandable when trying to type and play a game at the same time. Rather, I simply remind all that Everquest is not an Arthurian world, and trying to speak in Old English is far from necessary. If you want to, fine, but never feel that it's required for another person to take your role-playing seriously. I've come across many an eager newbie who saw fit to assault my eyes with a horridly mismatched set of "thee's", "thou's" and "tis's" in a misguided attempt to impress. The only thing they succeeded in was looking foolish, and many were forced to endure a five-minute lecture much akin to that which I now type (though a few were experienced enough to /ignore my commentary*shrugs*). So, once again, keep it simple. Speak in a manner that comes naturally, don't worry about sounding like some Knight of the Round Table, just enjoy your character.

In conclusion, I know many who read this article disagree with the entire idea of role-playing in EQ, insist it is not possible and generally poo- poo anyone who says otherwise, but to those unenlightened souls I simply extend my pity. I have personally spent many an hour enjoying the company of elves, dwarves, magicians and knights, all of whom handled their characters masterfully and made playing EQ an engrossing experience.
