Jewelcraft Guide


Guide to Jewelcrafting

 Things Needed   Crafting Jewelry   The Enchanter   Stone/Metal Combinations   Skill Advancement 

Things Needed for Jewelcrafting
You'll need the following items in order to begin your foray into Jewelcrafting (each is followed by approximate cost, where applicable):
  • "Legacy of Jewelcraft" Manual (1gp, 2sp, 9cp)
  • Jeweler's Kit (1pp, 2gp, 9sp)
  • Jewel Stones (1gp to 125pp each)
  • Bars of Metal (5gp to 125pp each)
  • Adequate skill (5 or higher is recommended)
As you can see, Jewelcrafting is quite expensive. However, the benefits of crafting your own items--particularly if you are an Enchanter--far outweigh the costs of doing so. We highly recommend that you spend at least 5 training points towards your Jewelcraft skill before your first attempt at crafting an item, unless you have a huge horde of gold and platinum that you can spend towards failed attempts.

Crafting Bejeweled Items
A practiced jeweler can expect around a 20 to 50% return on their investment. An Enchanter, however, can expect far greater profits by selling magically enchanted items to fellow players (see below for more information concerning Enchanters and Jewelcraft). The demand for these items will always be high, as the supply is somewhat low due to the fact that most players are apprehensive about pouring so much coin into a single trade skill. Players can always purchase magical jewelry from NPC shopkeepers, of course, but when presented with the choice of spending 25pp at a shop or giving 20pp to a player, most sociable people will choose the latter.

In order to begin your career in this field, purchase one bar of silver and one of the cheapest stone you can find--generally quartz or malachite. Place both items in your Jeweler's Kit and press 'Combine'. If you're successful, a piece of jewelry (most likely a ring) will appear in your inventory. If your skill roll fails, both items will be consumed and you will have produced nothing.

The Enchanter and Jewelry
The combination mentioned above is, if anything at all, an ideal pairing of character abilities. In order to shape a character into an enchanting jeweler, however, one must expend quite a bit of time and energy. Not only are the Enchant Metal spells comparitively rare (see the Enchanter Spells List for further details), but your first Jewelcraft-related spell, Enchant Silver, will only be gained upon reaching level 8. To make matters worst, the most powerful spell, Enchant Platinum, will be available to your character at level 34--quite a long way off for most players.

The most important thing to remember when attempting to create enchanted adornements is that the power of the echantment is directly proportional to the quality of the materials used. We won't present a full list of possible magical jewelry here, but a chart of some of the more common enchanted trinkets may be found below. The following chart is a result of extensive work with Jewelcraft; about 200 pp worth with a level 8 Enchanter using the Enchant Silver spell. These are the findings. (Note that not all of them are magic, but they still make interesting items. Keep in mind the magic items bar of silver must be enchanted for it to yield its magical properties). The character used possessed a 21 skill and was able to make most of the following with a few tries each.

Jewelry Items Needed Effect
Silver Carnelian Wedding Ring Silver Bar + Carnelian (None)
Silver Rose Quartz Engagement Ring Silver + Star Rose Quartz (None)
Silver Emerald Ring Enchanted Silver + Emerald +4 vs. Fire
Silver Jade Ring Enchanted Silver + Jade +5 Mana
Silver Pearl Ring Enchanted Silver + Pearl +4 vs. Poison
Silver Peridot Ring Enchanted Silver + Peridot +4 vs. Cold
Silver Topaz Necklace Enchanted Silver + Topaz +4 vs. Disease

As you can see, the majority of beginning items are rings, with necklaces, bracelets and earrings becoming more available with higher quality metals. In addition, the magical bonuses conferred by wearing the above jewelry are useful but minimal. Expect much more powerful benefits at higher levels of enchantment.

Stone/Metal Combinations
The above table should be plenty to get you started, because even these starting items require a significant amount of skill and an even more significant amount of money. For example, the jade ring is the cheapest--with a 105 CHR it costs approximately 4pp per attempt, and about a 25% success rate with a skill of 21.

Obviously, you want 20 points into Jewelcraft before even thinking about working with the more expensive stuff. When your skill raises 5 or so points, and you have 10 plat to throw away on experimenting with a peridot, then you can play with that.

The effect of a particular stone is constant, meaning that if jade gives +mana when combined with enchanted silver, it will also give +mana when combined with enchanted electrum. Obviously, the power of the enchantment depends on the metal (platinum is better than gold, which is better than electrum, which is better than silver).

However, the type of jewelry (bracelet, earring, necklace, whatever) produced by the stone/metal combination is unpredictable (though constant for a given stone/metal pair; you won't find that some of the time you get silver hematite rings and some of the time you get silver hematite bracelets, silver + hematite always = ring). For example, silver and malachite combine to produce a ring, however electrum and malachite combine to produce a bracelet. Once again, look above for specific examples of items produced with Jewelcraft.

Skill Advancement
At the end of P4, I had a bunch of characters whose assets I liquidated to experiment. One of them was a rogue who only had 4 training points at the guild, so I trained four points into jewelry and sold all my stuff for about 15pp and had another 25 in the bank, so I was working with about 40pp and kept selling back my successes.

I systematically tried to raise my skill by starting with the cheapest gem, working with that for a while until I advanced or decided that it was too easy to cause advancement.

Beginning with a skill level of 4 and ending with 8:

Gem (w/ Silver) Successes Failures Skill
Malachite 5 4 4
Lapis Lazuli 4 3 4
Turqoise 3 5 4
Hematite 2 2 4
Hematite 3 3 5
Cat's Eye Agate 0 1 5
Cat's Eye Agate 1 2 6
Cat's Eye Agate 1 0 7
Cat's Eye Agate 4 5 8
Bloodstone 4 2 8
Onyx 1 7 8

While working with onyx, my skill went up to 9 and I was out of money.

A couple of notes:

  1. Half elf rogues aren't very well liked in Felwithe, where I conducted this research, so I paid 7gp per silver bar (ouch) which I believe is the 'maximum' price (min is 5.25gp). Also I was selling back the successes at a substantial loss. A more well-liked, higher Charisma'ed character could have gone on longer with 40pp.
  2. My INT and WIS were standard, 75 and 60 for half-elf rogues.
  3. I had the book on me and had flipped through the pages several times. Call it superstition, but I think it helps. I have no proof of this, but it's only 1gp.
And observations:
  1. I only seem to have seen advancement when I was working with stones that I had a less than 50% success rate with.
  2. If after 6 to 9 attempts, I saw no advancement, I moved on to the next stone. I was impatient (and poor) but I think it worked well for the skill up to 8; i probably should have worked with bloodstone more before moving on to onyx.
  3. Successes and failures seem equally likely to raise skill. In fact, I more often gained skill on a failure than a success.
  4. However, failing more than is necessary because you're working with a more difficult stone than is necessary to raise skill is expensive. See the onyx example.
Even at a skill of 40, electrum and higher grade metals are still very difficult to work with. Start with the lowest cost item and work up to increase skill. You will want a high WIS, INT, and CHR. Wisdom to reduce failures, Intelligence to advance your skill faster, and Charisma to reduce costs of components and increase sale price. In addition, being an Enchanter in this field is optimal, due to a naturally high CHA and INT, and, most importantly, the ability to enchant their own jewelry.
