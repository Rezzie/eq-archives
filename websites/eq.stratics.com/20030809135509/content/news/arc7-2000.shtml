*Today's Patch*

Today we modified the EverQuest client to implement the following changes:

- Corrected a bug that would cause players to crash if they entered the game, exited to the server selection screen, and tried to reenter the game.
- Corrected a bug that caused delays when switching between UI modes due to the text wrapping change implemented last week.
- The /book command (/book #) now accepts pages 1 through 50, and will switch to the proper page.
- Opening your spellbook will automatically cause you to begin meditating providing that you have the skill.
- Removed the /meditate command since it is no longer necessary.
- You can now activate a hotkey while the spellbook is open by pressing the corresponding number-key.
- The "/target [name]" command can be used to target other players by name. Please note that this ability is subject to the range to the target.
- EverQuest will now write the original default colors when creating the EQCLIENT.INI file, rather than the new colors that were put in a few weeks ago.
- The @-character can be used in emotes to indicate a target. For instance, if you target "a gnoll" and type "/emote laughs cruelly at @", the output will be, "Playername laughs cruelly at a gnoll".
- People with the /LFG (Looking for Group) flag set will now have their over-head name prefixed with an asterisk. Remember that you can search for people looking for a group by using the "/who" and "/who all" commands. Typing "/who all ranger 40 50 lfg" will return a list of all rangers between 40 and 50 that are looking for a group.
- The "Sense" series (Undead, Animal, and Summoned) will no longer point you towards people's pets.

*Note on Voice Recognition*

Last week we implemented voice recognition technology in EverQuest. To use this new feature, please refer to the EQROKManualSupplement.doc file in your EverQuest directory. Please note that you must already have a voice recognition engine that is compliant with the Microsoft Speech API (SAPI), version 4.0. The voice recognition technology was tested with Dragon NaturallySpeaking(r) v4.0. Check with the developer of your software to find out if it is SAPI 4.0 compliant.

Abashi on Linkdeath
GeneralNews - 7:45 AM PDT
I found this here on the Official Everquest Forums

There is a problem that has been been brought up here in that past, quite a few times, but has always been dismissed as too difficult, or not worth the effort to fix.

I am talking about how linkdeaths, accidental and losses of connectivity to the game servers, plague so many of the players of this game.

Accidental linkdeath is no one's fault, really. It happens to me often, even on a cable modem. Some classes are not terribly bothered by it, other than the frustation of having to log back in, but other classes are terribly wronged by a design feature of the game that punishes certain players whose connections are not as good as others.

When a player goes linkdead, or logs out, any summoned items or pets the player has disappear. I remember playing as a Magician, summoning my best pet, getting all my food and water, starting out on my adventure, and going linkdead. I'd log back in, and literally have to spend another 15 minutes summoning my things. 15 minutes lost to something that I am sure was never a "balance" issue in the game. (How can you punish a player if his connection is not as stable as another player's?)

I appreciate the time that you took to write this up. Let me see if I can explain the current technology is as much detail.

Pets can only exist in the world when their owner also exists in the world, meaning that once the owner disappears, so does the pet. At the same time, the character goes linkdead, that character cannot reenter the game until they are removed from the world.

Once a character is removed from the world, the world has no knowledge of the existence of the character. As such, the world no longer processes data relative to the characater, since the character does not exist. That is why NORENT objects are removed as the character is being removed, since the world has no knowledge of the character once they are gone.

Certain parts of the description above are core system mechanics for EverQuest that it wouldn't make sense to change after several years of getting them operating just right. That said, we do have some plans to improve the current situation in the works. I can't comment specifically on them until we have some new things implemented and tested on the Test Server.

-Gordon

Aradune On the Clipping Plane
GeneralNews - 2:24 PM PDT
I found this here on the Official Everquest Forums

First a little background on the problem:

What Brad promised is that he understood what the claim was and that we'd look into it. At no point have we agreed that there is a problem. The screenshots that were posted only show that the when the rain turns off, the fog moves out past the max clipping plane for an instant, then back in. This isn't indicative of there being any problem with the way that the plane works. Many zones have clips that are determined by the area. For instance, Toxxulia Forrest has a max clipping plane that is pretty close because we wanted the zone to appear eerie.

We're still looking into the issue, but it's not the highest of priorities at this time. The most likely "fix" will be for us to make it so that the plane doesn't extend beyond max when the rain turns off. The net result of this fix is basically nothing, which is why it's not a high priority at the moment. We have already confirmed that the min/max clipping plane boundaries are indeed set to the appropriate limits.

-Gordon


Then Aradune followed up with:

Here's the deal:

The bug with the rain is very low priority.

Slightly higher priority is having a game designer to through ALL outdoor zones and revisit the max clipping distance associated with the zone.

IF the zone doesn't have an artifically limited clip for effect (eg. Toxxulia Forest), the game designer will make the max clip the farthest it can be based on the max distance used when exporting the zone (it can't go farther, else things will cease to draw correctly).

As stated before, several months ago when I posted on the subject, this is low priority (getting FA style quests in the game, revamping lesser used dungeons, working on Velious, etc. are all higher priority).

Other than the wierd rain bug and the fact that apparently several zones could indeed have their max clip increased, I do NOT believe there is any 'bug'.

-Brad


And lastly he said:

We DID, in my opinion, get to the bottom of it: for some reason, either in code or in data, many of the zones' max clipping planes were apparently reduced at some point.

I think your problem is with the priority we are assigning this issue, and I apologize that it's not high enough for some of you.

The plan is to have a game designer go into the data for each zone and move the clipping plane maximum out as appropriate.

If that fails, we'll look at it in code (which would involve a programmer). But my inclination is that the data change will solve the problem.

Again, it's not very high on the priority list. The FA style quests are (and are near being ready for testing, btw.), as are the dungeon revamps (which will be focussed on after the FA style quests).

Discussion of the Week: Fun
GeneralNews - 6:16 AM PDT
I found this here on the Official Everquest Forums

Let’s talk about “fun”.

Last week I queried everyone’s thoughts regarding a “Discussion of the Week”. Though the number of different responses was almost as great as the number of posts, many of them dealt with fun, or with concepts related to fun.

I think that it can generally be agreed that EQ’s customer base is very diverse, and with that diversity comes many different types of players. EQ strives to provide as many different avenues for “fun” as possible, leaving it up to the player to make decisions regarding what avenues they wish to pursue. Some find the social aspect of the game to be the most fun, while others have a good time by advancing in levels.

Some measure it in items, while others prefer character development along the lines of mastering trade skills or languages. Still others want to personally witness as much as possible within game.

Our goal with EverQuest is to entertain you for the greatest amount of time possible, and to do this we must provide activities that the players consider “fun” in nature. I’ve spoken many times about the concepts of risk and reward, but those concepts can be broken down to the perception of fun. Setting pure socialization aside, as it is one way of having “fun” that does not require a challenge, the goal is that “rewards” provide excitement, entertainment, etc. (cf. Fun), to the players that overcome challenges.

Last week we discussed camping, how it impacts the game, and what the driving forces behind it are. I think that from the responses to that thread, we can agree that camping relates to the concept of “fun” in one way or another. Players either enjoy camping itself, or enjoy something that camping will provide, if not the process itself. The social gamer might enjoy camping because of the opportunities for socialization provided in the downtime between kills. The leveling gamer might enjoy the prospect of raising in level. The item gamer looks forward to the items. More indirectly, perhaps the exploring gamer doesn’t enjoy camping for experience or loot, but gets fun out of exploring new situations, for which they need the levels and items to do, without explicitly caring for those levels or items.

The questions I’d like to pose are:

What is it about EverQuest that entertains you? If you had to label yourself as a particular type of gamer, what would that type be? Though I’m sure that everyone has particular issues that they feel could use improvement, everyone either has fun in game, or foresees the prospect of having fun sometime in the future. For instance, there are players that feel that once they reach their particular goal (whether it is one of items or levels), they *then* can go have fun.

If you are of the type that does not feel they are having fun now, and foresees fun once reaching a specific goal, what is that goal? What is it about that goal that makes you feel you can only have fun once you reach it? Internally, we refer to it as a “comfort-level”. What defines that comfort-level to you? What do you foresee will be “fun” once you’ve reached that comfort-level?

When Kunark came out, we raised the level cap. If you define yourself as one who must achieve a comfort level prior to having “fun”, did the raising of the level limit also “raise the bar” of your comfort level, effectively putting you far below it? Or did you enjoy the level-cap increase? I ask because before the level cap was raised there were thousands of people who were out having “fun” in game. Once the cap was raised, the focus for these people quickly shifted back to the “must-level-fast” philosophy.

Assuming that you have “fun” outside the tangible effects of levels and items, do you feel that “fun” is a just and suitable reward for a risk? Do you have fun outside of the scope of items and levels? Will you give some examples of where you’ve had fun without a tangible reward? While I understand that players who value only items and levels typically do not have fun outside of the acquisition of those rewards, I would imagine that most fall somewhere in between one player-type an another rather than directly in the middle of one of the ones I’ve defined.

If you could make any change to the game, feasible or otherwise, to increase the amount of entertainment you get or fun you have in the game given your particular gaming style, what would that change be? Other thoughts? I’m looking forward to seeing discussions between the players on their various perceptions of fun.

This should make for a pretty good discussion. Often here on the boards, we fail to get a good impression of the different types of gamers out there. It’s not unusual to see two posts on the opposite side of an issue related to “fun” claim to both represent the majority. I really doubt there is a “majority” for most issues in EverQuest, because as in life, very few things are black and white.

This is an opportunity to jump in and be heard.

-Gordon

Subtopic for the Discussion of the week: Random Loot and Camping
GeneralNews - 7:55 AM PDT
I found this here on the Official Everquest Forums

I've noticed that many of the responses to the thread "why camp" still suggest randomizing loot, beyond that which is present in Kunark. Certainly in Kunark, we've been trying contextual randomness (randomness that makes sense in the environment) on good loot, while leaving the best loot on standard "boss" NPCs. We've been fairly happy with the results, as it allows the casual player a good chance to pick up loot that will work for their class without "requiring" them to camp.

Still, many continue to suggest that we further randomize loot. I'd like to map out an example that shows why this isn't always the best answer. I think that the biggest misconception that I see here in regards to random loot is that the items will be more available. This isn't the case, as we still need to control the rate at which items enter the world. Randomizing loot isn't going to cause this rate to increase.

Let's say that CoolItem has a 20% chance to drop off of BossFrog in Lower Guk, who spawns 100% of the time on a 20 minute timer. This means that in two hours (six spawns, assuming he were killed instantly after spawning, which is close enough to the case), the frog would have just under a 74% chance of dropping CoolItem at least once. A group choosing to camp BossFrog would have a pretty good chance of getting 1 item in two hours.

Now, lets say that it was random. We can't increase the overall rate that CoolItem enters the world. So lets say that we give 20 frogs (still on a 20 minute timer) a 1% chance of dropping the item. Anyone care to guess how many of *these* frogs you have to kill to have the same 74% chance of getting the item? 135. Assuming the dungeon was completely camped, and that you could only kill one frog every 20 minutes, it would take 45 hours to have a 74% chance of getting the item. Suppose the dungeon isn't that crowded and you can take a whole room with 4 frogs in it every 20 minutes, it would still take 135 frogs to get that 74% chance, taking over 11 hours. The single-spawn 20% drop frog would have a 67% chance of dropping at least 6 during that time.

This same logic follows for suggestions that have the creature spawn in any number of random locations. If we have 20 places that BossFrog spawns, on a 1% spawn, and give him a 100% chance of dropping the item, we're still at 135 Frogs to get that 74% chance of him spawning.

As you can see, the level of randomness that some people are suggesting would create more frustration than people currently cite.

-Gordon

Discussion of the week: Why camp?
GeneralNews - 7:41 AM PDT
I found this here on the Offical Everquest Forums

I had a thought earlier and figured we could have a bit of a discussion about camping. Now before everyone jumps in and says, "because the game forces you to", let's agree that for whatever reason, people decide to camp. Perhaps so many people are camping that newcomers feel that they have to camp, but you still have to make that choice: Am I going to camp or am I going to adventure? Perhaps the game encourages it by rewarding camping. If that is the case, wouldn't the logical solution be to remove those rewards? More on this later...

So here's some questions:

1. Why camp?

Is it because you feel it is more profitable? Is it more profitable to sit in an overcrowded dungeon fighting over spawns and being on loot lists than moving out into the lesser-used areas of the world? I'm reminded of a recent Kunark review where the reviewer, an avid EverQuest player, indicated that there's more than enough room in Kunark to go off someplace with a group, but he couldn't get his group to do anything but sit in one area and camp a spawn.

2. Why not spread out to some of the lesser-used areas?

This question will inevitably bring about discussions of bindpoints, risk, reward, etc, or talk about how loot "sucks" in other areas. However, at the same time it begs the question, why do people choose to do something that they find boring and without challenge. Again I ask, is loot in any unused area really worse than what you'll get by camping a crowded dungeon, fighting over spawns, and being on a list? Certainly risk is less, but risk is essentially a small amount of experience in a well-balanced group. Do you feel that there is more net gain in the crowded dungeons? Can two groups taking turns killing 3 critters that spawn every 20 minutes in a room really be more profitable than going off someplace on your own, even given increased risk?

3. Do you actually enjoy camping?

There are people who do, and I'd like to get an impression of how many people honestly prefer camping. We've made several changes in the past few months to make camping less profitable, leading to a slew of complaints from this group, so I know that they are out there.

4. What do you propose should be done?

Some say that random loot tables are the only way to stop camping, but people don't just camp loot, they camp experience as well. We've all seen people move through the game staking out solitary 6 minute spawns for the purpose of camping experience.

Keep in mind that anything you suggest has to scale to 2K people online per server, all trying to do the same quest.

---opinion---
My feeling is that people camp because they feel it is more profitable, whatever "profitable" means to them. I personally consider "having fun" to be "profitable", to an extent greater than getting experience or loot. I won't go in certain dungeons because they are camped. I enjoy places where you zone in and you might be the only group there. Other people however often value loot and experience over fun, or believe that they can only have fun by acquiring loot or experience. Some people feel that they can only go have fun once they've acquired all of the loot and experience that they'll ever need.

I really don't think that random loot is an option for loot-camping, as people will still pick a spawn to camp and kill there until they get the item. Certainly, it won't lead to everyone camping the same NPC, but will still result in all of the NPCs being camped. I feel that as long as there exists the relative assurance that "If I spend enough time in this one area, I will sooner or later get the item".

As far as experience camping goes, I feel we can only get rid of it if we make it less profitable than other things people could do. Perhaps if we could programmatically detect XP camping, we could put something in to counter it. We've been adjusting spawn times that were set incorrectly on some NPCs in the world, causing them to spawn too rapidly, leading to it being a profitable camp, but what else do you think that we should do?
---opinion---

My last question is:

5. If we do whatever is necessary to get rid of camping, by making it less profitable, how many people will that *really* upset?

People camp because it's easier, faster, safer, and more profitable to do so than to move through areas and do something more exciting. People dislike camping because it's boring (without challenge), yet this situation is created by it being easier, faster, safer, and more profitable. Completely randomizing loot will only make more NPCs worthy of camping, not reduce the amount of camping going on.

Logic suggests that if people camp because it's easier, faster, safer, and more profitable, the way to stop camping would be to make it harder, slower, more risky, and less profitable. Yet, any time we've done that with an aspect of the game, it creates a *large* number of upset people here on the boards. If we make something harder, we're trying to eliminate soloing. If we make it slower, we're trying to hurt the casual gamer. If we make it more risky, we're trying to make you die more, which is supposed to make you play longer and pay us more. If we make it less profitable, we're trying to force everyone to join uberguilds.

In all honesty, any time we've made something harder, slower, more risky, and less profitable, we've been trying to motivate people to play the game in a manner that we would consider more fun. Should we stop trying to do that? If yes, should we stop trying to address camping?

PvP Teams Revisited:
GeneralNews - 8:22 AM PDT
I found this here on the Official Everquest Forums

Hello,

I posted this in another thread but it seems to warrant its own.

The biggest difficulty in regards to PvP in EverQuest is that EverQuest is, and will remain, a primarily PvE (Player versus Environment) game. The addition of PvP to the game should not change the basic fundemental mechanics of EverQuest. PvP in EverQuest has to result in "EverQuest with PvP" rather than "PvP EverQuest", if you get my point.

One of the largest issues on the PvP Teams servers is the subject of cross-teaming. Players of different teams get together for battles. Due to the PvE focus of the game, this has to be allowed for sake of game balance. Though some have suggested that cross-teaming, either in groups or guilds, not be allowed, it's something that will need to remain for the PvE game.

The problem comes in when cross-teaming is applied to PvP. Cross-teaming creates a situation that is often referred to as the "invulnarable healer" scenario. Basically, one group of PCs (Team A) attack a group of people on Team B, while supported by a healer from Team B. In this fashion, you have a situation where a member of one team is taking an active role in the deaths of members of their own team.

There is another "invulnarable healer" scenario that is more common and often just as bad as the cross-teaming scenario. This one revolves around a group of players from Team A attacking a group from Team B, while supported by a healer/buffer who is far superior in level to anyone from Team A or Team B. For example, a group of level ~20 trolls go on a killing spree in Greater Faydark, while supported by a level 50 healer/buffer. The problem in this case is that someone out-of-range is directly contributing to the deaths of players below their range.

On Friday, I spoke with one of the programmers about possible solutions for the PvP Teams servers, assuming that people in general believe these issues to be a problem. Suffice it to say that there is no easy solution. Each one has its disadvantages and "loopholes". For an example of some of the solutions:

1. Disallow cross-team buffing/healing
2. Disallow out-of-level (+/- 8) buffing/healing
3. Combination of 1 & 2.
4. Redefine "Teams" servers to GvE (good versus evil) servers and implement idea #1 on that basis, rather than on cross-teaming.

The problem with all of these solutions is that they have an undeniable impact upon the PvE aspect of the game.

The final and best idea that we were able to come up with allows things to continue as they are now in regards to PvE, and only places restrictions on the basis of PvP. In this idea, any player who has taken damage from another player would not be able to be healed/buffed/receive any beneficial spells from anyone who wasn't on their own team or in the +/- 8 level range for a given amount of time (a few minutes), or until they zoned.

In the cross-teaming "invulnarable healer" scenario, the person from Team B would be able to support people from Team A for PvE engagements, however once a member of Team A took damage from another team, they would not be able to receive support from anyone on Team B for a few minutes. In a real game scenario, the maurading band of trolls in Greater Faydark couldn't be healed/buffed by a high-elf assuming that they were taking damage in their attacks.

In the out-of-level scenario, that same band of level ~20 trolls wouldn't be able to receive support from a 50th level cleric.

There are some disadvantages to this, such as in the following:

4 Trolls are grouped with a high-elf fighting PvE encoutners in a dungeon. Humans come in and "slap" the trolls every so often thus keeping the high-elf from assisting them. Perhaps this should just be a disadvantage for cross-teaming in general. Perhaps this is just a risk that people take from grouping with the enemy.

What are your thoughts on these ideas? What other issues do you see causing problems in regard to this idea? Do the benefits (e.g. reaffirming the servers as PvP Teams)outweigh the potential disadvantages?

-Gordon

Warrior Damage Output vs Monk Damage Output
GeneralNews - 12:00 PM PDT
I found this here on the Official Everquest Forums

For the past several weeks, the complaints by monks here on the board (most of the complaints anyway) have been that they no longer outdamage warriors. The tests that I did were only to confirm that that wasn't true. Yes, I conceed that monks cannot takes hits as well as warriors, but that's never been where the disagreement has been. When people said, "Monks no longer outdamage warriors", I had to disagree, which is why I ended up testing it out. However, when people now say, "We can't take hits like a warrior", I agree with that.

Dealing out more damage than the warrior doesn't mean that you'll beat the warrior in a duel, nor that the ratio of damage dealing to damage taking capability is constant. The benefit of extra damage has more weight than the disadvantage of being defensively inferior.

I'm sure that some would say, "If a warrior can solo creature X in two minutes and be almost dead at the end, then a monk should be able to do the same in a minute and end up almost dead". This would be an example of constant ratio between offense and defense and the two classes. This however isn't the case. If the ratio were constant as suggested, there would, again, be no reason to have the warrior, because the monk's other skills would make them preferable in all cases. Not to mention, there's an added benefit any time a creature is killed faster than normal, a benefit that can't be measured directly against the power of the class, but more against its usefulness to the group.

Would a group, for instance, like to kill things in 2 minutes and end up OOM and near death at the end? Or would the rather do it in 1 minute and end up the same way? They'd want it done faster in all cases. That's what monks and rogues add as melee classes.

Concerning everyone saying that the test wasn't "valid", I fully agree with that from a scientific standpoint. Many more combats would have to be carried out than the ones I could do over a couple of hours yesterday. To be honest, its not really my job to test things of this nature, I just do it as needed so I can speak with first-hand experience.

Though the specific percentage point differences can't really be considered averages, I do think that the test fairly well answered the question: Do monks outdamage warriors?

There was one interesting thing in the test that I didn't mention in the post yesterday, concerning the one time that the warrior outdamaged the monk. The warrior was using his best combo out of the 5 weapon combos I tried, the monk his worst (bare knuck).

The damage ratios were determined by seeing how long it took for the character to kill the NPC in question. The monk completed the task in under two minutes in every case but one, where it took 2:03. The warrior was over two minutes in every case, with its fastest time at 2:01. The monk's best time, by the way, was 1:30.

-Gordon

Latest change in the works
GeneralNews - 7:21 AM PDT
I found this here on the Offical Everquest Forums

Hello all,

I thought I'd drop by and let you know of a new feature that we are trying out on Test. Last week it was suggested here on the boards that we implement a "Looking for Group" flag that players can set and view in order to help people get together quickly, allowing more time for adventuring. We've decided to try it out and it's on Test now.

The /lfg [on|off] command will set or unset your "LFG" flag. Players can then use all of the "/who" and "/who all" filters to find each others. For instance, "/who all rogue 45 50 lfg" will return all rogues between 45 and 50 who are looking for a group. "/who lfg" will return all players in the current zone who are looking for a group.

Your LFG flag will automatically unset when you enter a group. One final note, though anonymous and roleplaying characters can set their LFG flag, they will not show up in filtered /who lists. In other words, the first example above wouldn't return any anonymous rogues, however "/who all lfg" would return everyone in the game who is LFG (subject to the 20 person limit).

Assuming all goes well in testing, this change will most likely be in the next patch.

-Gordon

No.

If it isnt.. WHY NOT?

Deity selections are designed to have positive and negative effects upon what a character can do in game and how the NPCs react to them. We want that change to be permanent, as we don't want to see a situation where people switch deities based upon what is convenient at the time. For instance, if a really cool deity-specific quest or item comes in game, we don't want to see people flocking to a new religion.

As it was established that a change in religion would not be allowed, the game architecture was built in a way that now makes such a change impossible. Your faction (which comes from your race, class, and deity) is determined when you first start your character and can't be changed on the fly.

This extra weight is bogging some of us down. Every set of planar plate weighs 37.2 stones. Vermiculated weighs 36.

Patch Message, August 1, 2000
GeneralNews - 8:11 AM PDT
Greetings everyone!

Today's patch contains some changes that we are sure everyone will enjoy:

- Canned emotes (e.g. /point) now work on NPCs. In addition, the range for canned emotes has been increased.
- /who all "guildname" should now work for players who have /roleplay turned on.
- Preferences for the mouselook will be saved in your options file. Those who turn mouselook off each time when starting EQ will only have to do it once more.
- The "Chat Filter" tab in "Options" now has an option to filter messages caused by damage shields hitting others.

*Fast Drop*

Our most exciting change today is an option that allows players to avoid accidentally dropping items on the ground. The new client allows players to set "Item Dropping" preferences on the "General" options tab. The three options that can be set are:

- FAST DROP: Items are dropped without confirmation.
- CONFIRM: A confirmation box will be displayed whenever you try to drop an item.
- NEVER: You are not allowed to drop items on the ground.

Alternatively, you can also set these options using the /fastdrop command. The arguments are:

- ON: Same as option "Fast Drop" on the General Tab.
- OFF: Same as option "Confirm" on the General Tab.
- NEVER: Same as above.

Please note that whatever your setting, coin will always be dropped without confirmation. Additionally, the FastDrop option only applies to items being dropped upon the ground. Handing items to NPCs will still work regardless of your FastDrop setting.

It is highly recommended that you use option "CONFIRM", as GMs will not reimburse any items that are accidentally dropped.

- The EverQuest Team
