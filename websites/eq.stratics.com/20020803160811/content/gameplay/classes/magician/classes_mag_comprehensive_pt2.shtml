Summary

The Situation Framework:

Establishing the Playing Field With our combat roles established, we now have the building blocks (or tactics) to develop our hunting strategies. Our strategies, however, will be situation specific so we need to start by understanding the parameters we will use to define our specific situations. Most all combat situations in EverQuest can be defined by three parameters:

Relative ability of the combatants to do damage to each other

This is the primary set of parameters that will define our situations and can be expressed by three factors:

Your level in the game

This factor will determine how limited you are in approaching a situation. Early in the game (up through level 12) you can effectively tank and deal damage to a mob. You hit hard enough and often enough to do significant damage and can still absorb the damage a mob can dish out. In effect, you can still have melee (magi warrior) as a legitimate role to play. The loss of melee ability in later levels, though, is made up by your receiving more powerful spells. At mid levels you are able to cast direct damage spells in excess of 150 hit points damage. You will trade your melee for spell damage (and your roles must adjust).

Your level relative to the mob

This factor is routinely determined by what color the text is in response to your considering the mob. There are four results we will work with for our strategies: green, blue, black/yellow, and red. This factor affects your ability to deal damage to the mob at your current level. As you move up from green to red, you will miss the mob more, your spells will be resisted more, the mob will hit you more, and the mob will hit you harder. This makes situations involving black and yellow mobs significantly more dangerous than ones involving blue (or green) mobs. This factor will play a primary role in our strategies.

Your pet's level relative to the level of his spell

While you increase in level and your power relative to a specific mob changes (you get relatively more powerful since the mob's level stays the same), your pet will always be within two levels of the spell used to cast it. So with a level 12 pet spell the pet can be 10 to 14. We should think about pets relative to where they are in the four level cycle between new pet spells. So at the first level of a new pet spell (i.e., 12, 16, 20, etc.) pets are very powerful and can con yellow or black to you (the likelihood of this happening decreases as you progress to higher levels. At level 16 I still got yellow pets and at level 20 I still could get black pets). At the second level of a pet spell (i.e., 13, 17, etc.) you have progressed and your pet is still casting around his spell level so those yellow pets at level I are now black to you at level II. This effect continues up to level IV right before you get your new spells (which I'll refer to as hell level).

This factor is the primary determinant on your ability to take on mobs that are yellow or black to you. It is amazing to watch a yellow pet rip into a blue mob. The mob cannot hit the pet and the pet deals damage at an amazing rate. As soon as you turn it around, however, a yellow mob will knock your blue pet around in short order if you are not using the right tactics. The higher your absolute level, the more important you pet becomes and the more this factor defines a specific situation.

Special ability of the specific mob

Not all mobs are created equal. When mobs are conning blue to you these special abilities really are less important. When a mob cons black or yellow to you, however, the mob's special abilities become a factor in determining the situation. A mob's special abilities can be broken down into three categories:

Ability to inflict extra damage

This ability usually comes out in non-direct damage. Snakes can poison you. The poison from a yellow Asp can be very deadly to you. Some mobs can give you diseases. So even if you kill it, the disease may still kill you. Other mobs can stun you and allow them to hit you for extra rounds.

Ability to resist your attacks

Some mobs are very resistant to your attacks. Mobs such as ghouls that are very resistant to magic can nullify your big direct damage spells. Some mobs require you to use a magic weapon to damage them.

Ability to take large amounts of damage (toughness)

Not all mobs are created equally tough even if they con the same color. Some are just inherently tougher than others. Mobs you fight in your mid levels tend to fall into four very broad categories:

  • Casters When taken alone these are the weakest of the mobs. There strength, like yours, is in their spells and in their ability to thrive in a group. I was able to defeat many yellow casters even in my hell level (albeit at the cost of most all of my mana). The key will be playing to their weakness. You must avoid getting close enough to let them root you (they do still hit very hard) or use other spells on you. Maintaining your mobility around them is important.
  • Animals These are the next toughness group. Animals tend to be defined as things you might actually see on a nature program (but not always): elephants, gators, snakes, bears, wolves, mammoths, spiders, bats, rats, etc. These mobs tend to lack on the intelligence side and are not that hard to defeat. They can, however, dish out and take a lot of punishment relative to their level.
  • Warriors These come in a variety of types, many with the label warrior attached to them. They tend to hit very hard relative to their level and can take a lot of damage. My most common problem with yellow warriors later in the spell cycle was just running out of mana before I could bring them down. My pet would soon follow the mana down and I would be stuck trying to zone. Most intelligent races have warriors (orcs, aviaks, centaurs, gnolls, lizard men, etc.)
  • Undead These guys are a special case. They combine many of the characteristics of the other groups. Many can cast (dry bones skeleton), they tend to resist spells (ghouls, dry bones skeleton), they can dish out damage, take it like a warrior, and can also disease you for extra damage. They likely fall between animals and warriors in toughness (decaying skeleton excepted).

Number of mobs involved in the engagement

Fighting one mob versus two or three will involve different tactics. The risk of your losing the engagement goes up dramatically as more mobs enter the combat. As mentioned earlier, you have no real crowd control spells. You must rely on your pet and some artillery type direct damage to control the situation.

We can now put these parameters together to define what we mean by a combat situation. We will use the level of the mob relative to your level (i.e., green, blue, etc.) and the four level cycle around your pet's spells as our primary parameters. Using these two factors quickly cuts down the number of situations we should address and provide an easy framework for identifying what situation you are working in. You can always con a mob to see its level and using the four level cycle we do not have to develop strategies at each individual level. So a situation example may be, a blue mob when you are in the first level of your spell cycle (level 8, 12, 16, 20, etc.).

Next we can layer in the other parameters. The specific abilities of the Mobs should limit how deep into the cycle we apply one of our strategies. So we will be able to define a strategy for attacking yellows in level I of the cycle that applies to all mobs; but the same strategy should be limited to less tough Mobs as you move to level II, III, and IV of the cycle. We will also develop a specific strategy for attacking mobs that are highly resistant to attack and lay out which situations the strategy can be used in. There is also a specific strategy for dealing with multiple mobs, and we will limit which situations we should use it in. Lastly, your level in the game will determine how long you can use Magi Warrior as a specific role or tactic.

So our situation framework breaks into two axes: Mob level relative to you, and the four levels between pet spells (e.g. level I, II, III, IV) with level I being the level you get your pet spell and level IV being hell level. Below is the blank framework:

Hunting Strategies: Bringing it all together

We now have all the elements to build our hunting strategies. Our goal is to understand where we are in the spell cycle and determine what we want to attack and develop a strategy to go get it. We do not want to be reactive and just take what attacks us. We want to proactively determine what we want kill and become the hunter.

Chain killing the blues

Description

This should be your primary hunting strategy at most levels. While yellows and blacks are good experience, they can be tough to find. There are many places to kill blues in abundance and taking down several quickly can build your experience faster than finding yellows and taking the downtime that comes after the battle.

The idea here is to kill a series of blues with minimal effort from the magician and very short downtime between the battles. You should be able be able to move from one blue to the next to get the maximum experience from this strategy.

Roles

Your primary role here is as a pet cleric. The pet should tank and do most of the damage. Pull the Mob with a low level spell (little mana used) to draw it to a safe place. Your pet should be buffed and damage shielded to ensure it does the maximum damage. Once the battle starts sit down and meditate (you did chose a safe pull point, right?). Monitor the mob in the status bar and use pet report health (hot key it) to check on your pet. Let your pet go to about to 70% on his health and stand up and heal. Sit down and med. At 50% heal again. In general with a single blue mob in these levels that should be close to enough. If you get down to 40%, switch to wizard for the day and at 20% pet health switch to pet band-aid.

The key here is you can minimize downtime because you should rarely need more that one heal, and you recapture your mana during the fight. Your healing should also not draw the mob onto you and cause you to lose hit points. The spells you are casting are not resisted and if you keep up your alteration ability up, your heal spell doesn't fizzle much (no wasted mana). Your mana regenerates much faster than your hit points when you meditate so by avoiding direct melee contact with the mob you reduce overall downtime. Your pet can regenerate while you are looking for other Mobs, and you have very little downtime between engagements.

Levels

This approach works great in the first three spell cycle levels. In the II level you may tend to use more than one heal and at level III you may have to shift to the wizard role. The type of mob here is less important. Fighting blues your pet does well and with a little help from you can easily win out.

Pet choice

For this strategy the water pet works the best. It hits for good damage and its ice spell also does damage. These two in combination can bring a mob down fast. It regenerates very quickly so you spend little time while it heals and your heal spells only add to its quick regeneration. Its agility also means that it gets hit less so it will need fewer heals to stay in the battle. It has fewer hit points than the earth pet but should not need the hit points while fighting blues.

You should, however, spend some reagent to cast a good pet. Early in the cycle a yellow or black pet will do and later in the cycle a black or high blue pet will do. I was getting yellow pets in the first cycle when I was a level 16, at 20 a black or blue was the best I could do. The difference in level really makes an impact in how quickly your pet can take the Mob down and how much downtime you or you pet needs to spend after the battle. You end up having to heal less and the pet takes fewer hits.

Power nailing yellows and blacks

Description

The reality is that magician is one of the few classes that can consistently take on yellows solo and win. Yellows and blacks give great experience and you should not turn down an opportunity to take one on when it is appropriate. This power-nailing strategy should supplement your chain killing blues in the early part of your spell cycle. As you are hunting blues you should know which yellows and blacks are in the area and actively look for them between the blues. Do not use this as your primary strategy unless there is a very ready supply of appropriate yellows and blacks. It will take too long to hunt them when there are plenty of blues around to challenge you.

Roles

Pulling is significantly more important when dealing with yellows and blacks. With the blues an unwelcome additional blue is not normally a major issue. You really cannot, however, handle more than one yellow/black at a time. So choosing a safe pull spot is very important. Find a place off the main track and only pull when your target is not going to run across several of his buddies to get to you. Mobs that are high aggro types (aviaks, centaurs, orcs, goblins, etc) require careful attention to allow you to pull only one at a time.
There are two primary strategies here based on the type of pet you like to play: cleric heavy and wizard heavy. Both work fine and are really more a matter of your own style of play. Both require you to make sure your pet is buffed with both burnout and damage shield spells and you should have on your phantom armor.

  • Cleric heavy This is the strategy to use if you are using a water pet. You want to control the Mobs tendency to aggro on you. The yellows and blacks usually hit hard relative to you and can bring down your hit points quickly. The cleric-heavy strategy makes use of the pet cleric role to minimize the aggro factor.

    Ensure you have only pulled one mob. Then put your pet's health in the status bar. The yellows can hit hard enough that you want to monitor your pet's health. When your pet gets to 80% health, start your first heal. You need to start soon because of the delay in casting the heal spell. Cast your second heal when your pet is around 70% and your third at 60%. This method will usually result in your casting three heals pretty quickly. Do not fall behind in the damage but adjust your casting based on how fast your pet is losing his health. The water pet's quick regeneration and agility will really help out here and since it cast a damage spell the mob will start to lose hit points quickly.

    At 50% to 60% pet health, hit the F8 key and look at the mob's health. If your pet is well ahead of the mob keep healing. If you are even or behind, you should switch to the Wizard role. The positive is that your pet has done all the damage up to this point so you can cast several damage spells before the mob will want you. Stand back and hit the mob with your best damage spell several times. You should keep the mob in the status window and use pet report health to check your progress. No matter how things are going you should switch to the wizard role when your pet's health hits 40% (there is no objective to reduce downtime here, use all the mana you need to survive).

    At 20% you should jump in and switch to pet band-aid. You need to take some hits to give your pet time to do more damage. Do not lose your pet. Once your health gets to two bubbles back away and let your pet step back in. If the mob has more than a sliver of health left, then you should run. Get some distance between you and the mob. If you can zone, fine; otherwise, stop a safe distance away and gate.

  • Wizard heavy This is the strategy to use if you like using the earth pet. We are not as concerned about the mob aggro factor since the earth pet will constantly cast root to keep it away from you. So start with the wizard role. As soon as the pet roots, start blasting with your best direct damage spell. Even with your blasting the earth pet is still going to get hit a lot as it taunts (make sure the damage shield is on and the mob will pay). Keep the mob in the status window and do pet report health (hot keyed) after every second cast. Judge how your pet is doing versus the mob.

    At 20% pet health you should jump in and switch roles to pet band-aid and stay there until you are at two bubbles of health. At two bubbles take the same action as above.

Level

Type of mob and level will make a big difference here. It is at the yellow and black levels that the differences in the mobs start to really show up. In the level I spell cycle you can handle most yellows of all types. This is not always true, so ensure that you are ready to run the first time you engage a new mob. In the level II spell cycle yellow warriors become more difficult and should be avoided or approached cautiously. In the level III cycle you can likely handle casters and potentially animals; the undead are questionable. In level IV cycle, casters are really all you can handle at yellow. You should bring them down quickly. Use the wizard heavy style with a water pet instead of earth. The danger in attacking casters is not the hits, but the spells. Water pets will take them down faster (damage spell) and hit more frequently to interrupt the casting.

Pet choice

The type of pet depends on your style as laid out above; however, it is imperative that you cast several pets to get the best one you can for this strategy.

Crowd control the magi way, taking on the blue mob camps

Description

One of the bigger balancing issues is that magi do not have real crowd control spells. We have no harmony, lull, root, snare, clinging darkness, or fear. Our crowd control is our direct damage spells. We take down a single mob fast so that he does not have time to get to a buddy or help out a friend. That said, we can take on more than one mob at a time. I find that in the right situation, two blues are very well within our capabilities.

Now I know someone out there will immediately discuss how he took down four Sand Giants with an air pet while they were a level ten and happened to be away from the keyboard when it they did it. I believe you and I think that was fabulous. My experience and comfort level is that two blues in a controlled situation is enough of a challenge. I have taken down three blues before myself, but I had a clear escape route to the zone right next to me so I could hang in to the end. So adjust the number to your risk tolerance, but I will write this for two blues.

You need to understand the idea of a controlled situation. This is a situation where you chose from the beginning to engage multiple mobs and plan around it. Often an uninvited mob will show up while you are taking on a single blue and you must decide if you can take down the first one fast enough to engage the second one.

The multiple mob situations usually arise around camps. These are a great place to get experience. There are many campsites that you can visit and several non-campsites that have a couple of mobs together you can handle. Since these sites usually spawn very regularly, they can provide a steady stream of blues for you to chain kill. The mobs that hang out in camp situations also tend to be social and help each other.

Roles

Pulling is tough in this situation. Most camps are at least three so you need to stagger the spawn. You want to kill one or two of the mobs at a time where you can engage the remaining mob after you have healed. The spawn will then move to a group of two then one or three singles (the ideal). Here are some situations you can look for that will let you pull safely:

  • Two blues and a green Often the green will not come for you if you pull one of the blues. You will end up with two blues on you, which you should be able to handle. If the green comes also you can wait a second and see how you are doing, then gate or run if you have to.
  • One mob away from the others There are times when you find the mobs in a format where two to them are grouped and one is off by itself. Pull the one and start with him. Wait and heal and take on the next two. You then get the stagger.
  • Only two mobs in the camp Some other magus came by, did not read this article, and tried to take on all three. He got one and then died. The bottom line is that there are only two and you can handle that. Pull them, but get far enough away that if the other one respawns you will not have him join.

In other situations you will likely need help. Ask a druid for Harmony help or find someone that wants to help break the spawn. If you are desperate and bound close by, pull all three and let your pet take out one, gate and return for the other two.

With only two mobs pulled, start as a pet cleric. You do not want to aggro the mobs too soon. Stay at max distance and the pet can usually taunt and keep the mobs attention. Make sure you put the fire shield on, as your pet will be hit a lot. Heal to keep up as best you can. When your pet gets down to around 50% health, target a mob and cast column of fire. It is one of the best area spells in the game. It has a tight radius and does not tend to haul in other uninvited mobs like a rain spell does. I know that targeting the mob is not conventional wisdom (you should target the pet). The problem is that column of fire is so tight that if you cast it on your pet many times you will not hit either of the mobs or just one of them. The mobs, however, tend to group on top of each other when they attack and if you target one of them you usually hit both of them. You want to hit both, and one of them is going to jump on you no matter what you do, so make sure the column hits for maximum damage. Use the assist key to see which one your pet is targeting and shift to wizard. You want to hit the pet's target with one or two damage spells to hasten its demise and free your pet to help you. You can time the spells with the mob hitting you just like it was single combat. If you cannot kill the mob on those two casts run right at your pet and see if your mob will stick to the pet. If so, wizard your pet's mob one more time, then shift to pet band-aid and take it on the chin until your pet can free up to help you. You can usually do some damage to a blue at these levels if you have kept up your melee skills. At this point you are committed. It is hard to escape with a mob beating you. Let your pet finish his prey and take your mob back from you. If you have mana left, shift to wizard and try to burn it down. Otherwise get in there and alternate beats with your pet.

Level

This strategy works on blues in the first two spell cycles. By the third level your pet has a much harder time keeping up with blues. You can do it but you need the best pet possible. Casters present a special problem. They can stand back and heal the warrior mob or cast spells on you. You need to take the caster down first.

Pet

The earth pet works best here. Its root spell can hold one of the mobs in place and let you only deal with one mob personally. The fire shield on the pet causes damage to both mobs as they initially hit him. The hit points of the earth pet give you some time to work with. The higher the pet's level, the more effective this strategy is for the player. A yellow pet on a blue mob will make short work on the mob (it may be able to engage both before you have to switch out of cleric role). As you move up in the spell cycle the high level pet is more important. When you are only tossing a blue "kind of dangerous" pet against multiple blue mobs, you are pushing the limits.

Looting greens for fun and profit

Description

There are times when you need to raise some cash for that next level of spells or a piece of special equipment. You can do it by taking on the blues, yellows, and tough mobs, or you can pick on some greens and get revenge for when they use to kick your butt. As you progress up to higher levels this strategy works better. At level 20 I went into Blackburrow and basically cleared the level. I was able to take on the cloverleaf room and its elite guards and Gnoll Commanders with very little problem (it was 5 or 6 greens all together). I made a bundle of money and carried out a lot of good loot (spiked collars go for several PP on the open market and more than a PP to vendors). Plus, I really enjoyed dropping one Gnoll Commander (some were still low blue) after another with ease (revenge). The key here is to find an area several levels below you that you can clear and take all the benefits. You can easily take on four or five greens at a time at these levels.

Role

I start out as a cleric and try to target one green at a time. You can take on several but I only do that when I have to clear a room. The other greens in the area will not usually bother you unless they are in close proximity. There is much less of a chance that you will attract a group. You can usually cleric a green without having to even do a heal. With a damage shield on the pet and Burnout going the pet will cut down the mobs. You can heal the pet when he starts to get down around 50% or so between the engagements. It will take many greens (when done one at a time) to get your pet that far down. Remember you are not here for experience; you are here for the loot.

If you do have to take on more that one green, you should stick with the cleric role as long as possible. Sooner or later one of the mobs will slip by the pet and come to you (no crowd control remember). Switch to magi warrior (you can tank a green) and engage the mob. Do not let him run. Keep an eye on your pet (pet report health) but handle your mob. If you start to draw a crowd, move closer to your pet and use assist to quickly bring down his mob and move the two of you to the next one. If a caster shows up, it is your responsibility to go get it. If you are doing this right, there will be few if any blues in the area. So you should be able to use your melee skills to full advantage. The problem arises once the greens start hitting you. It is hard to get a spell off. It also does not pay to start off as a wizard. You will just draw the mobs to you too soon. Once engaged, the magi warrior role allows you to use spells as best you can.

Level

This works at all levels in the cycle. It is important to pick an area you are familiar with and you know has few blues and mostly greens.

Pet

This strategy works best with an earth pet. The earth pet's root spell lets you engage the greens one at a time and prevents them from running. Its high hit points allow it to defend you if a rare blue or black shows up. Its lack of agility lets you maximize the effect of the damage shield against multiple greens. The fire pet can also work because of its high damage and built in fire shield. The fire pet, however, can only stop a runner with a kill from its damage spell and its low hit points will not help you against a blue or black in the area. Also against multiple greens the fire pet's damage may not hold up as well, leaving you stranded with several greens to take on. A good blue pet is fine here. Yellow or black is great but not needed (save the malachite).

Tanking the lower levels: Whacking Blues and Blacks black-and-blue.

Description

Up to level twelve or so you are still an effective tank. For these levels your strategy can include you taking some of the beats to equalize downtime. You and your pet work as two equals in downing the mob. Any of the above strategies work as well, but this is an added way to progress using less mana and likely less downtime.

Roles

You start out as a magi warrior. As your health drifts down to 60% or so, back off and let your pet take some damage. You can switch roles to wizard and wait for your pet to get down to 45% and open up with direct damage spells. If your pet gets to 25 to 30% jump back in as a magi warrior and become the primary tank. Save enough mana to gate and if you get to two bubbles move away from the fight. If your pet dies you should gate. Obviously this is a judgment call. If the mob is at a sliver of health then hang in there to finish him and use your bandage skills to get you back up in hit points.

Level

This strategy works at all levels in the cycle up to level 12 or so. It is, however, focused on blues at the later levels in the cycle and blacks / blues in the earlier levels. You can use it on yellows but they hit hard and your difference in level will usually preclude you from getting a lot of hits in. Your pet is a better tank at that level.

Pet

Either the earth pet or water pet will work fine in this strategy. I prefer the water pet because of his ice spell. He is casting damage while I am hitting. No need to root here, so the earth pet is less useful in my opinion. Also, do not waste a lot of Malachite on recasting pets. A good blue one is fine; black or yellow would be helpful.

Making the special yellows turn yellow

Description

We discussed in the previous section that many mobs have special abilities. The most common is high resistance to magic, magic creature (need magic weapon to hit) or poison. These are areas we need to address with a specific strategy. These types of mobs are perfect for a magician. Other characters will usually stay away from them and they are in abundance in many places. Asps, for instance, have strong poison and will take a normal character down. Ghouls have a high resistance to magic that makes casting on them less effective.
When the mob is blue to you the effect is not as noticeable (unless you have to have a magic weapon to hit it). At yellow and black they deserve a special look to ensure you do not run out of mana before you even scratch them. So let's look at what we can do to take on these mobs.

Roles

These situations usually require us to be a pet cleric starting out. The pet cleric cannot be resisted. With a good damage shield and his magic attack, our pet can take on these mobs. Your pet's attack is considered magic when your pet is beyond level 10. So he can go after mobs that others need magic weapons for. Remember that your summoned weapons count as magic, so you can also get hits. Heal your pet until he is down to 50% or so. Drop in a direct damage spell or two. They will hit for less than they should but still add some damage. At 40% jump in and change to pet band-aid. You have a magic weapon so you can do some damage. Your pet will need some extra time here to kill the mob so your jumping in at 40% is needed versus the later time we use in other strategies. Trade beats until you are at your two bubbles and back away. Evaluate the mob's status versus you and your pet and determine if you need to run to a safe distance to zone or gate.

You normally want to keep your distance from these Mobs if they can cause disease or have poison. In those cases, do not jump in to be the pet band-aid until your pet is at 20% or so. You need to minimize your exposure to the extra damage from the mob. Try to avoid the Mob that got your pet to 20% until it is black or blue to you.

Summoned Mobs are a special case. Their special ability is a resistance to fire spells and being a magic creature (need a magic weapon to hit). You, however, get ward summoned at a low level and can cast magic weapons. You can apply the tactics above. When you drop in your damage spells use ward summoned to hit the elementals.

Level

This strategy works at levels I and II in the spell cycle. Once you get beyond those levels the yellow mobs are tough without the special resistance.

Pet

The water pet works well here. It is the only pet immune to poison, and it seems to handle disease well. It can get in and mix it up. The ice spell is additional damage and since you are not really going to add damage with your spells, the earth pet's root is not as useful as in other situations. You need to cast a good pet here. You are relying on the pet's melee skills to carry the day and those are best when there is a level difference in your pet's favor.

Soloing blues out of hell

Description

The last level before you get your new spells is hell level. Your pet is weak compared to the mobs. The weak pet makes attacks on yellows and blacks very risky. Even the blue mobs can be tough. You really cannot chain kill because your downtime can become very long as you regain mana and health. The goal, then, is to isolate a single blue you can solo without interference, heal, mediate and repeat.

Roles

The roles here are the same as for power-nailing the yellows. Since you can choose a water or earth pet and do fine the roles depend on your pet just like in power-nailing. You should not have to take the same damage, though, as the blues will go down easier.

Levels

This strategy is for the level right before you get your spells (level IV).

Pets

Either water or earth pets will do. Adjust your tactics based on the pet. A higher-level pet is a help, but most of your pets at this level will be blue or green. Do not waste the malachite to cast new pets over and over.

Risking the reds

Description

There are a variety of people that go after red mobs. As a magician you are one of the few classes that can do this. I personally have not had much success at this. I also find that the downtime and lost time from running or gating from a busted attack makes soloing reds less productive. But, there are many who like it so I have included this strategy for completeness.

The main problems for reds are that they hit very hard, you and your pet have a hard time hitting them in melee and they tend to resist most of your direct damage spells. You cannot, then, expect to attack a deep red and win. The deep red is one that is many levels above you. You want light or shallow reds. They are likely Mobs that show up as either a red or yellow depending on the individual Mob you consider.
The goal is to create a strategy that will allow us to go after a Mob that can resist most of what we toss at it.

Roles

These mobs will resist most of your direct damage spells so save the mana. Start out as a pet cleric and immediately start healing. The pet cleric also is the role that cannot be resisted. The fire shield hits for non-melee damage each time the pet is hit and the heal spells cannot be resisted. You are not casting on the mob so there is less of a chance for him to aggro on you. Keep the healing up as fast as you can until your pet gets to 50%. At that point you have to make a decision. If you are ahead of the mob, keep it up and make a check at 30%. If you are way behind, run, put some distance between you and the mob, and gate or zone. If your are somewhat behind at 50% get ready to chain cast a pet.

In chain casting pets we will have a new pet up and running just at the old one dies. Have your pet spell memorized before you start (put it in one of the old direct damage slots). At 50% have" /pet get lost" typed into the dialog box but do not hit return. At 30% start casting your new pet. As the new pet is about 10% from being born hit return and kill the old pet. The new one will appear and tell it to attack the mob. Cast fire shield and heal it (not sure burnout is worth it for the short time the pet is around). You must watch your mana. You may be able to squeeze out three pets.

When you have cast your last pet, step in and take some beats, pet band-aid, to try to extend the pet's life. At two bubbles of your hit points left, run.

Level

This really only works at level I in the spell cycle. Others may have had success later in the cycle, but level I is my risk tolerance.

Pet

You absolutely need the best pet possible at the beginning of the engagement. Up to level 16 it should be a yellow pet. I would also recommend a water pet. Hit points are not as high as the earth but they are high enough. The water pet's agility will make up for the difference and the water pet will last as long or better. The high regeneration is needed to assist in the heal spells and the ice spell at least has a chance of hitting with a yellow pet.
