How To Manage Engagement Risk and Survive Solo as a Low to Mid Level Magician

 Introduction   The Framework   The Framework Applied   Triggers and Rules for Escape   Preventing Triggers and Minimizing Trigger Impact 

Introduction
 EverQuest is, ultimately, a game about grouping and social interaction. It is a game about taking the strength of a warrior, the healing of a cleric, the air cover of a wizard, the debuffs of an enchanter, and the strengths of other characters to create a team that will bring a high-level mob to its knees. That said there are times when it is fun to just go out solo and take on the world by yourself. The problem is that the designers designed most of the characters to function most effectively in a group. As your character gets higher and higher in level, he or she needs to group more and more to survive. The magician (with his pet), however, is one character that can survive into its mid twenties solo and still take on challenging mobs. To be successful and consistently survive, you need to understand how to balance the risk of engagement to a reasonable level before you engage and understand when the tide has turned and it is time to run after you engage. The key to balancing and managing engagement risk is in understanding and mitigating the factors that drive the risk. In the following sections we will define these factors and create a framework for understanding how to balance engagement risk. Using this framework, we can define a set of simple rules that indicate when the risk has shifted against us and we need to escape. We will also use the framework to develop a guide of actions to take before you engage to shift the risk as far as possible in your favor.

The Framework
 The first step in managing the risk of engagement is to develop a framework to describe and measure the risk. The framework should describe the risk of engagement in a way that defines all the factors that affect the risk and allows us to quickly assess and measure those factors to guide our actions.

Engagement risk has two broad dimensions that should encompass all the factors that affect the risk:

  • The probability of escape from the engagement
  • The lethality of the mob engaged

The idea, then, is to balance these two dimensions to ensure your survivability. So the more lethal the mob you engage, the more assured escape must be if you are to survive if things go bad. Each of these dimensions can be broken into three factors that define the dimension and fully describe the drivers of engagement risk.

Factors that drive probability of escape. There are four methods that you can use to escape an engagement: running to a zone, casting a spell that removes you from the fight (gate for a magician), running to guards, running to a group of friendly players who will help you. Your probability of successfully using any of these methods is driven by three factors:

  • Security of the escape path. For a path to be secure it must be a short distance and clear of mobs for a running escape. So for a highly secure path you must be standing very near the zone, guards or friends and there cannot be intervening mobs between you and the escape point. For a casting escape security means there is enough distance between you and the mob to allow you time to cast the spell before you are engaged. In the case of a casting escape you must be free to actually cast spell (i.e., you are standing still and no mob is hitting or stunning you to interrupt the spell).

    An example of a low security path would be the lower levels of a dungeon. In a dungeon there is a restricted path to the zone and it tends to fill with intervening mobs as they spawn behind you. Being within view of the zone or guards would be a secure path. Standing at maximum distance from your pet while he fights the mob is also a secure escape position (you have time to cast a gate spell before the mob can get to you and the mob's engagement with your pet lessens the chance the mob will try to stun you).

  • Certainty of escape method. Each of the four methods has varying levels of certainty based on their ability to ensure your escape.

    • Zoning is the most certain method. Zone points are always at a set point, always available, work every time and will remove you completely from the threat.

    • Spells (gating) is the next most certain. You don't have to run any distance to gate, it removes you completely from the threat, and it is always available if memorized. The downside is it takes a few seconds to cast and can fizzle or be interrupted.

    • Guards are the next level. Guards are usually at a set point but some will travel. They will usually have enough power to quickly overwhelm your opponent (not always). Most of them are aggressive enough to help you if you can get close but they will not absolutely remove you from the threat. Guards, then, are a good bet but not completely certain.

    • Relying on a group of player characters is the least certain. There is no guarantee that the group will be there when you need them (they are hunting mobs themselves after all) or be willing (or quick enough) to help when you get there. Depending on their levels they may not even have the ability to save you if they wanted to.

  • Involvement in the engagement. Low involvement in the engagement helps ensure your ability to escape. By involvement in the engagement I mean your level of entanglement with the mob and the engagement's resulting impact on your health and mana level. If you are highly involved in the engagement you will likely reduce your probability of escape by lowering your health as you are hit, lowering your mana as you cast, and reducing your chance to run or cast if you are directly fighting.

    • If your health has been beaten down then your chance of running is lower. The mob only has to hit you a few times as you run to stop your escape. If your hit points are low enough, your speed if affected as you run (slows you down). Hit points are a big issue with magician characters. As a caster your hit points are low and you cannot wear heavy armor. Your AC is low then by definition. The low AC and HP means that you need to consider carefully situations where escape requires you to cover longer distances.

    • Low mana also reduces your chance of escape. If you have been highly involved in the engagement by burning the mob, you may not have enough mana left to cast a gate spell. The closer you are to the level of mana needed to cast gate, the less safety margin you have. Without your gate you have lost a viable escape route.

    • One on one entanglement with a mob prevents your ability to get a head start on a mob and allows him to interrupt your gate spell. Also your ability to take on mobs one on one is limited by your low HP and AC. A magician must be very careful to manage their level of engagement. This factor has much less effect on our tanking friends who can survive much longer toe to toe and, as such, preserves their ability to stay in a fight longer and still escape.

    Overall, then, you highest probability of escape is if you are fighting a mob close to the zone point and letting your pet do most of the fighting. To make this escape dimension useful in a framework, however, we must distill the above factors into an easy rule to differentiate a high probability of escape situation from a low probability of escape situation. If we make the assumption that we can mitigate the certainty of escape issue if two methods of escape are available, we can create the following rules:

    • High probability of escape exists if two secure escape methods are available to you and they are obtainable. So we need two methods available. You should always have your gate spell available if you are not engaged so if you are near a zone or guards or a group of player characters you have two methods available. They must, however, be secure. So it must be a short distance, clear of mobs. To be obtainable you must have more than one bubble of health, enough mana to cast gate, and not be engaged in one on one combat.

      So out in an open field in view of the zone and the guards you have a high probability of escape if you have enough health to reach them. If you can see the guards and your gate spell is loaded, you have a high probability of escape if you are not engaged one on one and you have the mana to cast the spell.

    • Low probability of escape exists in the absences of any part of the above rule. If you are in a lower level of a dungeon you likely have a low probability of escape. There are likely no guards, player groups may not be near by and the zone is at the surface with potential mob spawns in between. Your gate spell is all you have with certainty. If you are in an open area out of view of the guards and zone point (like in many parts of Butcher Block or Lesser Faydark) then you are again relying on your gate or a long run so the probability of escape is low. You may still be able to outrun a mob to get to the guards (a SOW helps), but the longer the distance the less likely you are to get to them. In general, if you are alone and not near a zone point you have a low probability of escape. This doesn't mean you can't escape, it means you must start sooner and take less risk in what you attack.

Next we need to define a similar set of rules to measure the lethality of a mob so that we can define our balance between lethality and escape.

Factors that drive the lethality of a mob. The ability to escape described above needs to be balanced with the lethality of the mob to get a clear picture of the risk of the engagement. There are three factors that drive the lethality of a mob. Once we understand these factors we can define a set of rules to characterize high and low lethality in an engagement and balance those with our rules above to determine the actions we need to take during the engagement.

  • Capability of the mob. This factor is a direct driver of mob lethality. We can divide this factor into several characteristics.

    • First is the level of the mob. By using the consider function we can evaluate how powerful the mob is vs. our current level. For a magician, mobs in the green, blue, black, and yellow range are usually fair game. Obviously the higher the level the more certain we have to be of our escape route.

    • A mob's ability to cast a spell (even a stun spell) also weighs in here. A black mob with the ability to stun can be far more lethal, in some cases, than a yellow without that ability. Shamans and other casting classes are also dangerous to engage. The magician's power to solo higher-level mobs comes from his ability to have both the pet and the magician inflicting damage at the same time. A spell caster can remove you from the engagement with a stun and put the burden of engagement on your pet. The mob's capability to stun also slows your ability to run for a zone point and thus allows him to inflict more damage on you without your being able to respond. With you removed from attacking, the odds can quickly shift to the mob.

    • Speed of the mob is important as well. A mob that is very quick can hit you very fast and inflict significant damage in a short time. Also, if they can outrun you, you have to be much closer to your escape point. A giant spider, for instance, is very slow. You can easily outrun it. As a result, you can be much farther away from your zone point than if you attack a gnoll that can easily overtake you.

  • Number of mobs. The higher the number of mobs in an engagement the lower your chances of surviving. You can easily solo one mob with your pet. More than one becomes more of a problem. You ability to successfully engage a higher-level mob requires you to cast damage or healing spells during the conflict. If a mob is hitting you, your spells are frequently interrupted. You just don't hit hard enough to bring down a high level mob with your staff or dagger. With one mob, your pet can engage while you stay out of the fray. You can control when you need to step in to take a few hits while you pet recovers. You can stay out of range and burn like a wizard if things go wrong. It is possible to engage multiple mobs successfully; it just raises the risk of the engagement. Do not underestimate the havoc a green mob hitting you can cause while you are trying to support a pet attacking a yellow.

  • Social status of mobs. Tied to the number of mobs involved factor is the social status of the mobs. Some mobs are very aggressive and they tend to support each other. This means if you attack one, any other one in the immediate area will also join in. Before you know it, you have three mobs hitting your pet and two coming after you. Also many mobs have relationships with others not of their kind. In Everfrost, for instance, the snow leopards seem to like the giant spiders. I have often found a lone blue giant spider and initiated the attack only to have several snow leopards come along. I end up engaging four blue mobs vs. the one lone blue I had anticipated. So a mob's social status, aggressiveness, and availability in an area can significantly alter the risk of an engagement.

Overall, then, a low-level non-casting single non-aggressive mob is our lowest risk for engagement. There is not much news in that observation. The key here is to again distill the above factors that drive lethality into a simple set of rules that will define a high lethality situation and differentiate if from a low-level lethality situation. The following rules should provide the definition we need:

  • A high lethality engagement exists if a red mob is involved, multiple mobs are involved (other than two blues), or a caster is involved. A red mob is a wild card. It is hard to tell how many levels above you it is. Even a lower level red is more than a challenge for a solo magician and pet. To engage a red you are taking a significant risk (it can be done, you just need to balance the risk with two or more highly secure escape methods). Multiple mobs are difficult to defeat. You will likely be drawn into one on one combat. This is not a bad situation for a tank, but you can't stand toe to toe. My experience has been that anything more than two blue mobs at once is a wild card. I have (as many of you have) defeated three blues on several occasions. I have also gotten set to the Promised Land by three blues as well. The situation can easily get out of control and your escape ability will evaporate rapidly. For three blues, a blue / yellow, blue / black, two blacks or two yellows, you are in a high lethality situation and you need to manage the risk appropriately. Casters are also a wild card. A blue that can cast can quickly shift the balance of the engagement with a simple stun spell. As soon as you discover a caster in the group, you have entered a high lethality situation. Social tendencies of the mobs are an overlay here. The more social the mob the more likely you are going to end up with more than two mobs attacking you. So the social driver is baked into the rule by limiting the number of mobs you are engaging.

  • A low lethality engagement exists when you are engaging a single non-red non-casting mob or two blue mobs. This is pretty easy for me to remember. I did not mention greens here. Most greens do not really want to engage you. Many times you can pull higher-level mobs and the greens will stand still. If you do have greens attacking, you need to eliminate them quickly to allow you to focus on the higher-level mobs.

The Framework Applied
 Okay, so what does all this mean? We need to take our definitions of probability of escape and lethality of mob and create a framework that allows us to understand how to balance our risk and take appropriate actions based on that risk. The two by two matrix below should help us accomplish this goal:

Taking our definitions of probability of escape and lethality of mob, we can place ourselves on the grid during an engagement. Most engagements will start in the upper right hand corner (engage at will) block. In this corner we are engaging one of any non-red mob that is a non-caster or two blues and we have two escape routes available to us. You are likely out in an open area with your gate spell up and within sight of a zone, guards, or some solid friends. This is the bread and butter corner. You should be able to fight and win in this corner all day.

There are times when you may start in the bottom right corner (maintain escape route). You are engaging low lethality mobs but you only have one real escape route. This would likely be in a dungeon or out in the middle of a zone with no ability to reasonably get to the boundary, guards, or a friendly group. In this case you must look carefully for anything that will nullify your escape route and defend the route or immediately execute your escape plan.

Sometimes you just have to see if you can take that red mob or those fours blues. You may feel that two blacks just cannot be that hard and you just have to at least try to take them down. These types of engagements are high lethality and place you in the upper left (establish control) corner. It is okay to take the chance, but you need to understand that the risk here is high. You need to make sure you have two very secure escape routes. This means you will likely have gate up and are standing a very short distance from a zone or very strong guards (you escape routes need to be as absolute as possible). The key here is to understand if you are in control of the situation. If you feel like things are deteriorating quickly and you cannot maintain control, then do not blink, just escape.

You should really never start in the bottom left (immediate escape) corner. It is a place that you end up when things go wrong. I believe that many players get killed and frustrated because they start in this bottom left hand (immediate escape) corner. You are out gunned and you do not have adequate ability to get away. The key is to use the simple rules above to understand when you are in this lose / lose situation and to escape without thinking about it.

If everything were as simple as understanding your starting corner and following the instructions, life in EverQuest would be simple. It is not. The hardest part of the game is to realize when you have migrated from one corner to the next. You need to immediately realize when you have gone from the upper right to the lower left. It is not always obvious. It is, however, easy to recognize the trigger events that cause you to move from one corner to the next and then set up some simple rules to respond accordingly. The next section explores these triggers and establishes simple rules to help understand when it is time to call it quits and just make a run for it.

Triggers and Rules for Escape
 Most players die in EverQuest for the same few reasons. They either start in the bottom left corner of the framework (immediate escape) and do not realize the danger (or do not care, it is just a game after all), they do not recognize when they have shifted into the bottom left corner from a safer condition and continue to fight, or they do not take the actions in the upper left (establish control) and lower right (maintain escape route) corners to keep them out of the immediate escape area. The goal, then, is to understand the trigger events that lead a shift from one framework corner to the next. Then based on those trigger events, we can define some simple rules to quickly evaluate your position on the matrix and lead you to the correct action.

Immediate escape triggers and rules. There are four triggers that should lead you immediately to the bottom left corner (immediate escape). They do not require any thought you just immediately execute your most secure escape.

  • Any red mob shows up once the engagement begins. It does not matter which corner you are in. It does not matter what you are doing or who you are engaging. Once a red mob shows up, immediately escape. Do not think, do not consider, just run or gate. The reason is simple. No matter where you are on the framework matrix, you are going to quickly end up in the bottom left corner. A red mob immediately puts you in the high lethality side. A red mob will also make short work of your pet and remove your ability to gate (thus removing one of your escape routes). Even if you have several other escape routes you must start immediately moving to exit or the red will overtake you and engage you one on one removing the running escape. They tend to hit very hard relative to your level and your HP and AC are not likely enough to compensate, thus decreasing your chances of escape by running.

  • The arrival of a new mob takes you to three mobs in the engagement. Again, it does not matter which corner you are in or what you are doing just escape quickly. Facing three mobs, you have immediately entered the high lethality side of the matrix. With three mobs to work on you, the chance of a one on one engagement just got real high. As we have said before, a one on one engagement clearly removes (or greatly decreases) your ability to gate (a primary escape method). And you will likely be slowed down from running to your nearest help. I try not to think on this one. While I have survived three mobs while soloing, there are just too many things that you have to consider and too many things that can go wrong. I set this rule in stone and do not think about it.

  • Your pet dies and your are in a high lethality or low escape position. So if you are starting in the upper left (establish control) or lower right (maintain escape) corners (or have ended up there) escape immediately if your pet dies. This is another trigger event I do not think about. If your pet dies then something has likely gone wrong. When you are starting in one of the higher risk corners you have very a small safety net. In the few seconds it takes you to figure out what went wrong, it is likely that you have either lost control of the situation or lost your one escape route. In either event, the framework says you have shifted lower left and need to escape. I prefer to gate or zone and figure out later what happened.

  • A new mob arrives while you are in the high lethality or low escape framework corners. Again, you are in a high-risk area. If you are in the upper left (establish control) corner the addition of another mob just pushed you to a more lethal situation and your ability to control the situation just dropped dramatically. Escape quickly. If you are in the lower right corner (maintain escape route), the arrival of a new mob just put your escape route into jeopardy. Maintaining it is going to be difficult. Again, it is very hard to quickly evaluate all the variables (your health, pet health, mob level, etc). I prefer to escape and regroup.

These four rules are easy to remember and they do not require you to think. In each case escape and come back later. There are events, however, that allow you a second or two to think before you act. These events occur when you are in the "safe" corner in the upper right corner. The next section will review these triggers.

Delayed escape triggers and rules. When you start in the upper right corner (engage at will), you have a lot more leeway in how you engage. There is more of a safety net. Most trigger events (outside of the first two immediate ones above) will move you to the upper left corner or lower right corner and you have several seconds to consider what to do. When a trigger event increases the mob's lethality, you move to the upper left and must quickly answer the question, "can I control the new situation?" If you cannot, then you have just slipped to the lower left corner and should immediately escape. When a trigger event removes an escape route you need to quickly answer the question, "can I maintain my one remaining escape route?" If that route is about to be compromised, then you have just moved to the bottom left and need to immediately escape. The specific triggers to watch for are:

  • Your pet dies. If your pet dies while you are in the upper right (engage at will) corner, it may not be a big deal. You need to understand that you have immediately shifted to the left and are in the upper left (establish control) corner. You must quickly determine if you can maintain control of the situation. If the mob is almost dead or it is a single blue (that you can likely kill alone) then you can probably establish control of the situation and continue the fight. You need to remember, however, that you are in a high lethality situation and any additional mob now requires you to immediately escape.

  • Any of the mobs you are engaging, or a new mob, is a caster. Having a caster in the area immediately increases lethality and shifts you to the left on the framework matrix. You need to ask yourself if you can quickly defeat the caster and reestablish control of the situation. If it is a blue caster, and the only mob you are facing, you can likely defeat it with you and your pet. If it is a higher-level non-red caster you need to evaluate where its damage is vs. yours. If you are still in control then keep going. If it is a new caster that has shown up, you need to quickly evaluate your HP state and that of your pet vs. the mob you are engaging and consider the level of the new caster. If your pet can finish off the current mob and the caster is blue, then you can engage the caster and let your pet join you when he is done. Otherwise, you should escape before you get caught one on one.

  • Your health drops below two bubbles or your mana drops equal to that needed for a gate. If you are in the upper right zone (engage at will), you need to quickly evaluate your situation and understand that you have likely just shifted down to the lower right hand corner (low escape probability). With less than HP two bubbles or your mana below gate casting level, you have decreased your probability of escape. You need to determine if you can still reasonably maintain your escape abilities. At two bubbles of health you have a limited amount of time to run and with mana below gate you, obviously, cannot gate away. If you are close to killing the mob or the mob is blue, then you should continue on. You need to be aware that you are in a high-risk area now and be attuned to other triggers that will cause you to escape. No matter which corner you are in, two bubble left or mana below gate should cause you to evaluate your situation and ask if you are still in control and able to maintain an escape route.

  • Any new mob shows up. If a new yellow or black mob shows up you need to realize that the lethality of the situation has increased and you have been pushed to the upper left (establish control) corner. Next you need to evaluate the situation and determine if you can still maintain control. If your mana or health is down significantly, you will not likely be able to maintain control and should move to immediately escape. If your pet has been doing all the fighting and you are still at 100%, you may be able to control the situation. Finish off the original mob and tank a little for you pet. If it is not going well, back off, let your pet engage and escape if your pet dies. If a new blue shows up, you need to do the same evaluation, but you should likely be able to handle the second threat if you have most of your mana and health. Remember that anytime you are looking at three mobs you should be running or hitting the gate icon.

  • You are engaged one on one. Once this happens, you are have been shifted down to the bottom right (maintain escape). You need to evaluate your chance of escape. If you have engaged one on one to give your pet a break, then you are still in control of the situation and can back off and still make a break for it. If you are engaged because there are two mobs, you need to look at your capability and determine if you can survive (your gate escape is gone, all you have left is your ability to run). If you are not confident of your ability to disengage, you need to start moving to your best available escape point. Do not wait too long.

Overall, then, there are only a few triggers you have to recognize: red mob shows up, new mob arrives, mob is a caster, mana or health drops low, or your pet dies. Anytime one of these triggers occurs you need to judge the impact and determine where you are on the matrix. With the immediate escape rules you do not need to think, just escape. Outside of the four immediate escape situations, you have a few seconds to think but still have to act quickly.

The above rules should help you survive by guiding you on when to run for it (or gate). The downside is that you will likely lose your pet and you may have a long walk back from where you zoned or your bind point. But if things go wrong and you do not escape, you still have to start over from the current bind point and you have to spend time recovering your corpse vs. engaging new mobs.

Knowing what drives the risk in engagements, we can also determine how to minimize the chance of a trigger occurring or minimize its ability to move us out of the upper right (engage at will) corner immediately to the lower left (immediate escape) corner. I will explore some tips in the next section.

Preventing Triggers and Minimizing Trigger Impact
 There are a variety of preventative things that you can do to keep a trigger from occurring and, thus, keep you out of trouble. I have used my framework and our understanding of what drives engagement risk to list out a few of them.

Pull mobs out of traffic to avoid the new mob trigger. One of the most common triggers is a new mob showing up uninvited. One way to minimize the probability of a new mob showing up is to pull your prey to an area out of traffic and away from the other mobs that can support it. Most of the mobs that move around do so in a predefined manner. You need to establish the traffic patterns in an area and pull away from it. For instance, in Everfrost there is an area south of the south tower that is a mob freeway. There are always a wide variety of mobs moving on the freeway. When I first found this area, I would run up and engage a mob in the high traffic area. Soon I would have additional orcs or snow leopards or giant spiders taking large chunks out of my magician. Most of the mobs in this area are very social (and aggressive when their friends are attacked). Once I understood the traffic pattern, I started casting my pull spell from max distance and then turned and ran towards the south tower. This pulled the mobs out of the traffic and actually helped me establish another escape route since there are always very helpful higher-level players at the tower (this is a big mammoth hunting area). The same is true for patrolling mobs. Pull them away from the patrol area and engage them one on one.

Learn how to properly use your pet to avoid the pet dies trigger. In general you really should never lose a pet if you are starting in the upper right "safe" corner of the matrix. You should, however, follow the following steps:

  • Cast your best buff on the pet (usually fire shield and burnout)

  • Use your most powerful damage spell to pull (usually shock of blades or firebolt at these low to mid levels)

  • Let the pet fight the mob until the pet is down to 50% health (monitor with pet report command)

  • Evaluate if you pet is ahead of the mob (compare the HPs of each and see if the mob is down farther in HPs than you pet)

  • Burn once with a powerful direct damage spell if your pet is in danger

  • Move on the other side of the mob from your pet

  • Review your pet's health again and step in if your pet is losing ground on the mob (you take a few hits and your pet strikes the mob from behind and gets the bonus for attacking from behind)

  • Back off and let your pet take some damage

  • Step back to max range and burn if things are still out of control

Using this method, I generally only lose pets when a trigger happens that forces me into immediate escape and then the pet is lost protecting my get away.

Meditate when your pet is fighting to keep mana and health up. When your pet is fighting, select the mob and sit down and mediate. If you have pulled the mob out of traffic, you should be safe. Watch the mob's HP bar and have your pet report health. When your pet gets down to 50% evaluate the situation and look at your pet's health vs. the mob's health. Take action to save your pet if you need to. If everything is okay, continue to mediate. You will regenerate your mana quickly and be ready for the next mob by the time the current one is beaten. I use a water pet most of the time they regenerate very fast. By meditating, my pet is not always waiting on me to be ready.

Avoid quick shifts from the "safe" corner to the "high lethality" corner by limiting the level of mob you engage based on your pet's level. It does not take much to lose control of the situation when you are fighting a mob that is too far above your pet. The problem is that as you level your pet does not. Pets are generally a level or so above or below the level of the spell they are cast from. So a pet cast from a level eight pet spell will be a level or so above or below level eight. So when you are on a new spell level (4, 8, 12, 16) your pet may be yellow, black, or blue to you. Once you move up a level, say to 9, your pet will likely be no more than black to you. On the next level up, you usually get blue pets, and on the level before you get your next spells you get blue or green pets. So there are two take aways from this:

  • First always cast the best level possible if you want to go after the big game. I know that it cost money to do this, but you need a yellow or black pet to go after yellow mobs. Spend the money and you will avoid losing your pets more often. Just remember that above level 12 it becomes much harder to cast yellow pets and you may have to settle for black or blue.

  • Second limit the mob level you go after. On the first two levels after you get new spells you can safely jump yellow and black mobs. On the third level you should limit yourself to blacks and blues and on the last level before new spells take on blues and maybe a black if you have a secure set of escape routes.

Fight in locations that maximize your chance of escape. This seems obvious, but I still see people all alone in the middle of Everfrost jumping a red mammoth. So pull toward guards, attack within sight of the zone, make friends with higher-level players near your attack point (give them food, bandages, and drink), group for dungeons, etc.

Focus on non-social mobs to avoid drawing a crowd. Spend some time observing which mobs in your area never get help from other mobs. Snakes (giant and large) are good examples. Asps in particular do not help each other, your water pet is immune to their poison and they give good loot. Rats also get no help from friends (giants are good experience). There are a variety of other non-socials that reduce the risk of the new mob arrives trigger.

Finish one mob at a time in multi mob engagements. When I start with two blues I try to use the assist command to help my pet knock off one mob quick. With two active mobs there is a greater chance that one is going to engage you one on one. One on one you have a much harder time casting damage spells (you have to time them). Let's face it; the magician's strength is not dealing direct damage toe to toe with a staff at close range. You need to be away from your pet so you have the choice of moving in or burning. This also applies to green mobs. Clean up a green that is engaged first so he does not interupt your spell casting.

There are a varitey of guides on many websites that have additional tips. As long as you understand the drivers of engagement risk you can evaluate how these tips will help you stay alive longer and let you take down strong mobs with minimal risk to yourself and your pet.
