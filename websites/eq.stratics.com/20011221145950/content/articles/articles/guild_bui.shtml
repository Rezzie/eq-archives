Forming a Player Guild

The Building Blocks of a Successful Guild
Ok, so you've maxed out your character and acquired the flaming sword of almighty destruction. You've flung spells far and wide and matched steel against the fangs of ancient dragons. So now you are wondering the same wonders that so many online gamers have pondered throughout the years; "What now?". Well, for many who have mastered the lands they call home, the answer is to form their own guild.

This is not so easy as it sounds. Unlike many problems encountered in ORPG's, you cannot expect a guild that you've formed to flourish just because you throw huge amounts of money, spells, or any other easily acquired commodities at it. However, there are three things you can put into your guild. These three things are time, creativity, and leadership--perhaps the most valuable commodities in any large multiplayer game.

Some of the most vital steps to creating a successful guild are taken before it is even formed. Concieving the structure of the guild you seek to form is a monumentous excercise in creativity. When writing your guild's charter, there are two things you should take care to keep in mind.

First, think about what aspects of the game you play you enjoy the most. When your guild is formed, wouldn't you like it to be composed of people who all enjoy the same aspects of the game? A guild where everyone wants to go off by themself or in smaller groups rather than all going to together to have fun will not be very effective. A guild that is fragmented in interests has taken the first step toward being forgotten in the vast pile of dead guilds.

Second, try to make your guild creative and fun. Most likely, there are already dozens, if not hundreds of guilds already established in your home game. Many of of these are probably very large and prosperous, whereas yours is yet very small. Therefore, you must find a way to make people want to join your guild over all the other ones available. While being a good recruiter can go a long way towards this end, If your guild is creative in concept and sounds fun, people will be able to envision themselves as a part of it, and this will make them want to join.

Once you have created the charter or basis for your guild, you will still probably have something missing from it: members! Members are, of course, the most crucial part of any guild. So now you should probably go out and try to recruit some. This, like all parts of creating a guild, is not as easy as it at first sounds.

Most guilds have some sort of system to organize the membership. This will probably include some form of leadership. Some guilds have only a Guildmaster and a Assistant Guildmaster, while others may have an elaborate rank system. Whatever leadership you have chosen to implement, you will need people you can trust to help you lead the guild once it grows too large for you to lead alone. The best place to turn for these people is to your own gaming friends. Surely you have made friendships with some fellow adventurers during your travels, and have adventured with the same group for an extended period of time. These friends are an excellent place to turn for leadership for your new guild.

Now that you hopefully have a core of leaders for your new guild, you need to fill in the rest of the structure with members. After all, that leadership core is going to need someone to lead! When forming a new guild, perhaps the best place to turn for new members is to new players of the game. The reason for this is simple: these newbies are often easy to recruit, as they don't know the game very well and are often looking for someone to help them learn their way around. Recruit members slowly at first, since you will have to train them all in the ways of the world. Another good reason why recruiting new players is a good idea is something I like to call the guild rank/experience ladder. If you recruit newbies, they will eventually become more experienced, and gain rank within the guild (if your guild has a ranking system). If the first members you recruit are newbies, and you continue to recruit newbies, then those first members will end up being both the highest-ranked and the most experienced members of your guild. This will eventually lead to a good blend of experience levels in the guild, and will help avoid the problem of having highly experienced players in low ranking positions within the guild.

Once you have some members in your guild, it's time to really test the structure of people you have set up. Send a call out to all your members asking them to come and participate in some guildwide event, such as a dungeon crawl, monster hunt, PK hunt, or other fun recreational exercise. If you get a signifigant majority of your guild membership to show up, congratulations! Your new guild is now well on it's way to becoming a successful one. If not, try to contact the members who did not show up for the event and find out why they did not. Communication between the leaders of a guild and the average members is very important.

Beyond these first few tips, I can offer very little advice on how to build up your guild. From here on out, it will really be a matter of what your members want that decides what kind of steps you should take in order to expand further But remember, the key to every successful guild is friendship between the members. Unless there is a true sense of comraderie between them, your guild will end up the proverbial long walk off a short pier.
