Human Monk (Freeport)

Creation
Beginning
Training
Advancement

 

This guide will assume that you've decided to make a human monk, and want to begin play in the Freeport area. It will also be a newbie guide, assuming that you do not have access to higher-end gear (or, to be honest, gear of any kind). I won't cover making hotkeys, or other basic interface issues; there are other guides for that sort of thing.

Creation:
Pick a human monk, following the Tranquil. Don't get the face with the radiation symbol on the back of the head, I beg you. I know it looks cool, but it seems like *everyone* picks that face for their monk. Come up with a name however you like, but if you use something from Eastern philosophy, please have some clue what it means. Stats: All of these numbers are confusing, aren't they? To be honest, don't sweat this stage too much. Any mistakes you make can eventually be corrected with gear, though it may take a fair amount of work. The ones highlighted in green are worthy of note. Personally, I would suggest bumping STR up to 90, and then splitting the remainder of your points between AGI and DEX. This isn't gospel, though. I've found that a balance seems to work quite well. You can, of course, go overboard on one attribute or another, and have a perfectly functional fighter. In a purely subjective sense, Lanzhou, my 'AGI - heavy' monk, had a harder time of leveling than Dendrite, my current character, who has more balanced stats. I can't stress enough that stats are a matter of personal choice, and almost any combination can be viable. If you want to hit like a freight train, go for strength. If you'd rather imagine your monk dancing nimbly about, dump a bunch into AGI. If it's a blur of fists you're after, maybe DEX is the way to go, or STA for preternatural endurance. In the long run, you'll be able to change the stats to most anything with equipment, so don't sweat it too much. My recommendation would be to go for a balanced approach, with a STR heavy monk a second choice.

Beginning The Game:
You'll show up in West Freeport, not far from the city gates. From where you appear, the area to your left, outside the walls, is unsafe. Everything else inside the walls is a nice, comfortable home. A ways off to the right is a place called the 'Theater of the Tranquil.' You'll want to find that place as soon as possible. You're carrying a note, which you must give to Puab Closk, who can be found in the Theater, on the second floor of the Ashen Order guild house, which is surrounded by a bit of a moat. Give him the note, and he'll give you a guild tunic. Interestingly enough, you actually appear to be *less* clothed when you put on the tunic than when you were naked.

Training:
While you're standing in front of Puab, it might be a decent time to talk about monk skills. You are a living weapon. You can fight with your hands and feet, or can pick up various different flavors of blunt object if you prefer. Your hands alone are as effective as that shiny sword you see the other guy carrying, if not more so. What's better still is that they improve in damage every 5 levels, and later in your career will begin to get faster, as well. In addition to 'normal' melee, you're capable of performing a number of special maneuvers, beginning with Kick. You'll learn a new move every 5 levels, each one faster or stronger than the last. For the time being, put 1 training point in Dual Wield, Dodge, Hand to Hand, Kick, and Mend. You'll acquire more skills as you go, and it might be worth your while to pick up a splintering club or a staff to play with, but that's far from critical. As it stands, you're ready to begin kicking a little (rat) tail. Here are a few things to keep in mind about monk fighting:

  • Always fight with both hands. You're the only class out there that can dual wield from level 1, which means that right off the bat you've got twice the offensive capability of any other level 1. Don't tie up your second hand with a lantern, or anything that isn't a weapon. There are plenty of light sources that don't require a hand. Try to get a fire beetle eye and place it in one of your 8 main inventory slots.

  • Fight light! Once you get past the very first levels, don't let your weight go over 14. You begin to lose some of your defensive abilities, and at later levels, being only a pound or two overweight can really hurt. Every piece of equipment (even coins) has a weight value associated with it (though some are 0.0) so keep that in mind when someone offers you a new pair of pants.

  • Mend: This is a particularly neat skill, and a bit of a gamble. Until you reach level 20, mend can either do nothing, heal 25% of your total hit points, or deduct 25% of your total hit points. Don't rely on mend to win fights, as the one time you do count on it will undoubtedly be the time that it hurts you. It's a neat ability, but not a crutch. Until your skill reaches 100, use it every time it recycles. You need all the practice you can get.

  • Bind Wound: This is expensive for new players, but very much worth it in the long run. Bind wound takes a few seconds, uses up a bandage, and restores a variable amount of hit points, based upon your skill level. At higher levels, you can heal more than 40 points per bandage, which is nothing to sneeze at. You can easily get yourself back up to half health with Bind Wound, but not beyond. Throw in a mend, and you're at 75% health and ready to go out killing. If you're not in the mood to buy bandages, bored magicians may summon them for you. Ask nicely, and don't be a jerk if they say no.

  • Specials: Every so often, usually every 5 levels, you gain a new special attack, with a spiffy name, a cool animation, and a little bit better damage. They're the things that make monk fights so fun to watch. When the time comes, run to the guild and train in your new skill, then raise it through combat. Sometimes it's a little frustrating to see all the misses when you're just learning, but it's worth it in the long run.

  • Double attack, Feign Death, and Instill Doubt: You'll get these at level 15, 17, and 18. By that time, you'll be getting into this whole monk thing, and should already have found one of the sites on the web that specializes in monks.

Advancement:
Once you've trained your skills, it's time to go punch things a bit. For levels 1-3, either East or West Freeport will do. Kill white/black con creatures (target them, and press 'c' - the information you get might save your life) until you're blue in the face, and you'll be 3 in no time. At that point, you can stay in the safety of Freeport and kill *lots* of fire beetles, or you can head out into the world. Try North Ro, or East Commons, but don't wander too far yet. If you die, you have to be able to find your way back to your corpse, after all. Half the fun of this game is finding new places on your own, so I won't spoil that for you. You'll learn quickly that certain creatures are aggressive, certain creatures are social (they help each other in fights) and certain creatures are both social AND aggressive (ow). When you meet up with the third type, discretion is often the better part of valor. Every thing you kill will tend to leave some remains, which you can sell to merchants for small change. Small change eventually builds up to larger change, and soon you'll find yourself buying cloth armor, and then eventually cured silk armor, which will cost you a few platinum per piece.

Beyond this point, the world is your oyster. Once you reach level 6, you have nearly 24 hours of online time to find your corpse before it vanishes, so don't worry overmuch about dying. Explore, have fun, and deal out a few beatings. Remember that you are a monk, and don't be afraid to roleplay some. If you've got questions, a polite /tell to a higher level monk is never a bad idea, but understand that they're busy folk, and may not have the time to help. A little humility never hurts, and you'll find that most people will take the time to help you if you're polite. Good luck, and happy monking.
