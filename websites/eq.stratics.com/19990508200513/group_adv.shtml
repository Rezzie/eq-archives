Advanced Group Tactics

 Introduction   Communication   The Leader   The Party Member   Multiple Enemies   Pre-Fight   Conclusion 

Introduction
Well, it seems that many people have read Intermediate Group Combat. If you haven't, you need to, as it the only 'prerequisite' for this document. In that document PPI covered the absolute basics of group combat, including analyzing the function of each class in a party and basic skills for each class to pay attention to.

In this document, we're going to extend and move into more advanced territory, primarily to do with complex interactions between parties and enemy monsters.

Some examples of things that can go seriously wrong while in a party:

  1. The party has two people scouting for enemies and suddenly, they _both_ yell "Incoming!!" and charge back with big yellow monsters on their tail.
  2. As a caster, you are meditating, and suddenly you see your parties health bars dropping... ALL of them.
  3. The party sees a group of monsters, and someone mentions an attack... and immediately, half of the party runs directly into the midst of the enemies and begins slashing indiscriminately.
  4. You are in charge bringing a monster back to an ambush, when... low and behold... the entire party has decided to head back to town for supplies, leaving you with a new friend and no party favors.
  5. You come back with the next kill hot on your tail only to find the rest of your group has engaged some wandering creature nearby without telling you, leaving you to hope they finish the other in time to save you from whatever you brought back.
These are all serious group no-no's (just a few of many). Most often they occur because there is a lack of a basic party of any good group. Communication. Without good communication skills, your party is effectively acting as a bunch of solo characters who just happen to be fighting the same enemy (and that's if you're lucky).

Most good groups have a designated leader, who acts as a ringleader in a circus, directing the actions of those around him, while avoiding the lions and elephants and gnolls... Gnolls!?

However, sometimes groups will function perfectly well without a leader. This usually occurs when a group is comfortable with each other and knows their roles and what needs to be done. These groups will not usually have a leader per se but instead someone (usually a support character) who watches the battle from the outside and keeps everyone informed of what's going on. It is then this person's duties to back the group off so that they do not attract the attentions of wandering creatures that would endanger the group if they attacked. If you are in one of these groups, you're probably very familiar with a good majority of the rest of this document, but keep going, you just might learn something.

We're going to cover basic communication skills, leader responsibilities, and then a related bonus section on multiple enemy combat that uses most of these ideas.

Communication

Without communication, the party is doomed. Those things mentioned in the Introduction _will_ happen, and they will not be fun. Even in parties with good communications, sometimes bad things happen. Mostly because someone either wasn't paying attention, or because someone forgot to mention some action they were taking that could affect the other members of the party. For example, they were going out 'fetching', luring a monster back to the group for an ambush. Groups without a leader can be much less fun to play in because without someone to give direction you are more often than not left standing around waiting for someone to figure out what should be done next. (Note: this is different from 'natural' groups who don't require a designated leader)

While most online games have a barrier to communication, the keyboard, there are ways to work around this. Firstly, if you're working with a group of friends or some people you often group with, develop a shorthand that is easy to keep up with. Most of my groups have about five messages that are vitally important and also quick and easily abbreviated:

"OOM" (Out of Mana): For a caster, this is a _huge_ important message. Always give warning for both low mana (LOM) and for complete empty tank. If the party doesn't know their healer has no more heals, or that their wizard has no more mana for root or burn... well... someone is going to die. Good healers and burn support characters will be able to read their mana bar and call exactly how many shots they have left. It is more important for the healers. For the burn support characters LOM should suffice.

"INC" (Incoming): If a group is using the tentacle camping method (sitting in one place and reaching out and bringing monsters in), this is a key message, as it means that the designated fetch has found something and is coming in with it. A huge warning to prepare for combat (ie: stand up and stop meditating or healing).

"[Name] Back out": Get out of melee with the mob. There are others that can take the damage. Sometimes people just don't realize they've just been mangled (lag or just plain blindness, I never miss 'YOU have been mangled for 50!' messages but that's just me). If the leader keeps an eye on health and directs combat movement, it greatly simplifies the jobs of other party members (though it adds to the leader's responsibilities).

This command is the most often ignored. Many times it is because the person loses the message in the scrolling text and other times they just wand to get a couple more hits in. It is the party's job to be watching for the leader's commands and a lso the calls from other party members. Make sure you pay attention. If you think that you should get just one or two more hits in before stepping out DON'T. Your leader (if competent) has good reason to call you out. Step back immediately. If it is because you were getting low on health step back and sit down for 3 combat rounds at least. This gives the party's healers ample time to attend to you, and also makes the monster less likely to attack you when you rejoin combat.

"MR?" (Mana Report): Just something that all casters should get in the habit of announcing. It allows the leader to effectively judge the relative 'power' the party has left, both in terms of healing and in terms of direct burn. A good leader always knows what the mana levels of party members is, and if he/she doesn't, ASK.

"Rest": Pretty straightforward. The party is mangled and needs to camp out. Should only be mentioned when the party is not in any direct danger nor is sitting on somewhere that could become dangerous (ie: spawn point). Again, it is the leader's responsibility to find a safe place to call this.

The Leader
Well... either you are one, you've been one, or you will be one. Regardless of how good or bad you are at playing EQ, it is likely that you will eventually be called upon to lead a party. If you are decent at it, you'll garner some respect and likely get to be leader more often. If you get the group smoked... well, let's just say that finding a group might be more of a problem than leading one.

Experience makes a leader. The leader would have never gotten to that point if he had not made a few mistakes and learned from them along the way, though. Make sure if you do make a mistake to admit that you were wrong and try to make up for anything that may have happened as a result.

The leader has many responsibilities, and actually doesn't have to be a front line combat type. I (Drew) play a Magician most often and have been designated leader quite often. I usually am no where near melee range (though I do mix it up as needed to save people who are hosed), and that helps me keep my mind clear while directing things. Just a personal preference, other leaders may have different experiences.

The following items should be considered the leaders responsibilities:

Finding the Enemies:

Whether the party is a roving band, or is doing the tentacular thing, finding the enemies is the leaders prime responsibility. For roving bands, it's amazing how often the leader turns out to be the tallest member. Merely because it's easier to follow a tall leader who doesn't disappear into the weeds (*snicker* halflings *snicker*).

Planning and Organization:

One of the leader's jobs is to keep everyone organized and aware of the situation and their role in it. This is important both in and out of combat. Keep the party together and make sure everyone knows the score.

A strong leader should be aware of your party member's abilities and limits. This includes a strong working knowledge of all of the spells and abilities of those members of your party. A good leader will direct the Bard to play the correct song for the situation, and have the Shaman cast specific spells to turn the odds, etc. If you are not very familiar with all of your member's skills, ask them and begin learning a little about classes aside from your own.

The leader should be adept at directing actual attacks during combat, as well as indicating the order of combat for multiple enemies (see Multiple Enemy Engagement section below for ideas). This is especially important when dealing with dangerous monsters with special attacks, or those that have friends nearby. It also is important in planning escape routes should your party end up with more on their plate than they can handle.

Loot order:

Though this may seem trivial, it is vital that the leader keep a close eye on loot distribution. If a member is getting shorted, it's likely that he/she will depart for other places soon. Never good to lose a party member to those sorts of things. Autosplit (when working) takes the edge off of this problem but players still enjoy that birthday surprise type feeling of looting a mob. Even if you are not following an order just make sure everyone loots on now and then.

A good leader will also keep their group hunting monsters that are both interesting and profitable. If you lead your group around killing things that you could have taken alone, people will bore quickly and leave the group to go fight by themselves. Group fighting is _the_ most efficient way to improve your character as well as being the most fun. A good leader will make sure this is extremely apparent. A bad leader can sour players' perception of grouping.

Combat Coordination:

This is where a good leader can make the difference between killing the enemies and getting the entire party killed. This is more of an art than a science and incorporates many skills. The main skill a good leader has is the ability to multi-task. This is because he/she must engage an enemy, while constantly surveying the situation for changes, and while directing other members of the party in actions. Often when I am leading a party I end up breaking my own rules of breaking off combat at 2 bubbles of health because my attention is rarely on my own hit points. Leading is very rewarding but also can be very risky. Your first priority is to keep you group together and alive and your second is to your own health. This does not mean you should be reckless, but good leaders will always take calculated risks for the party.

A very important skill for any leader is to know when to fight and when to flee. As the leader in combat this is your most important duty. If you are with a good party they will not run until the leader calls for the run. This means they are placing their lives on your assessment of the situation. Do not expect to achieve this until you have proven yourself a strong leader. When the situation is getting ugly you are better to call the run earlier than later. Waiting can be fatal, and if you are going to escape a bad situation, you should attempt to reduce the 'downtime' the party will have recovering. Heck, if you called it too early, you can always make another attempt.

While in larger combat, it is often good for a party leader to be familiar with and use the many 3rd person views to scan the area around the combat for possible dangers and to get a more 'removed', strategic view of combat. Some people prefer the overhead view, but I'm very much for the long distance shots of the combat, as they give ample viewing of the area around combat while still allowing a good view of combat.

Respect:

As a leader you must earn your group's respect. This is done by showing that you know what is going on are able to command the group to peak its efficiency. Respect will be gained in time through successful weathering of bad situations. It is in these situations that the leader is really put through their paces and when the group will become forever loyal or mistrusting and being ignoring commands. Don't ask people to do things that you would not do yourself. The battle described later in this document gained the leader some respect but if it had gone the other way he could have easily lost some of the loyalty of his group.

The Party Member
Unless you are the leader of the party, it is your job to follow orders to the best of your abilities. Though that may sound like no fun, it truly can be cathartic to be 'The Tank' and know that you can and should throw yourself at the enemy. Or 'The Healer', who just watches health and makes sure no one goes negative. When a party member, your life is simplified greatly because you really have one or maybe two tasks to concentrate on. As long as everyone plays their part, the party will do very well. It is amazingly fun to be part of something larger; especially is that something larger is ripping through the beasts of the area like a scythe through wheat.

Following a part:

As long as your leader is competent, just relax and play the role you've been designated. Try not to do anything that would directly endanger the party, and use your skills to the best of your abilities. See the Grouping 101 guide for ideas and suggestions for each class.

As a member, it is your duty to provide information to your party leader regarding your status. Casters, periodically report mana; warriors, mention that you are out of food and water, or that you need to rest. These things make the leaders job a little easier and are appreciated.

Also, do not hesitate to inform the group about changing situations, including additional monsters, changes in health, or mana. It's never good to have additional monsters enter a fight, but it's worse if no one realizes they are there.

Multiple Enemy Engagement
Story time: Ok... your fetch has just announce that he/she has not one, not two, not three, but FOUR big yellow aggressive things (for example, Minotaurs). This is a bad situation, as your group has only taken one and occasionally two of the beasts. You can either panic and bug out, or you can try and fight the horrible odds. Let's follow an example party and see how they do against the monsters. This is told from Vogyd's perspective.

Leader:

Teffo, L11 Halfling Cleric (Jim)
Party members:
Zanith, L10 Human Paladin King, L10 Half Elf Bard Vogyd, L12 High Elf Magician (Drew) Adaemus, L11 Dwarf Warrior
We had all rested up after a combat with 2 of the beasts, when we hear, "Oh... that's bad." from Zanith, our fetch. He had been scouting looking for a minotaur or two in the middle of the area where they spawn... when all of them spawned. He high-tailed it out of there with four minotaurs on him, and informed us as to our fate. "4 incoming." We briefly discussed our options, and then decided to try to smoke them.

The Bard switched to a combat anthem, and we waited for the enemy to arrive. As soon as Zanith arrived, I dropped a Fire Shield on both him and Adaemus. With four enemies, that mana cost would easily be made up for in damage done to our tanks. Teffo began to heal Zanith, and then we began to melee the beasts.

After a few seconds, we realized this was going to be close fight as both of our tanks were below 50% health, and I began searching for a monster with lowest hps. This would be the monster that was being attacked by our warriors. Most of the monsters were slightly damaged (from the Fire Shields) and eventually I found one at half life. I then burned it down the rest of the way. Teffo began to heal Zanith and Adaemus while fighting one of the monsters he had engaged long enough to draw its attack onto himself and away from the damaged warriors. King had also drawn one onto himself and resumed his attack on the next one targeted by the party.

Quickly, the odds shift into our favor, as we have now a 5 vs. 3 advantage. Again, the warriors concentrate on one enemy, and I begin to fry the monster as it attempts to flee having dropped to about 20% of it's health total. While I am burning, the tanks are very low on hps, so Teffo backed out of melee to heal while the tanks switch to yet another enemy. After dispatching my runner, I step into combat to make sure that others have cover, as Zanith was near 20% (he backs out), and I take some hits from one of the mostly undamaged minotaurs (which quickly reduces my hps to half). Zanith is mostly restored by Teffo's spells and returns to combat. I back out, and dispatch another minotaur who had decided to try and leave the area (low on health) with a few spells.

I am now extremely low on mana, so I save the rest in case of an emergency and step into melee range of the remaining beast. Adaemus and Zanith quickly maim the enemy and we cut it down from behind as it attempts to flee.

Analysis:

This little story demonstrates many key events in good party dynamics. Primarily, it shows that with good teamwork, a party can take out groups of dangerous (yellow!) monsters very effectively as long as certain rules are followed.

  1. Concentrating attacks on one enemy removes it from combat, lessening the damage done to the party as a whole.
  2. When engaging multiple enemies, make sure that they all are concentrated on players who can take the damage. Tanks. If a spellcaster becomes a target, the party is in big trouble as without the fire support bad things happen. Thus, in large combats, the spellcasters should stay out of combat as much as possible until things are less chaotic (ie: fewer enemies) and should NOT burn randomly. That is the surest way to draw an undamaged beast onto you. Bad mage.
  3. Spellcasters should endeavor to finish weaker monsters off quickly. This allows the warriors to get on to more important business. Also is much safer for the spellcaster (as mentioned in #2), as the monster is already really angry at the tanks who did most of the damage.
  4. Good communication. Zanith told the party what was going on well before bringing the enemies into range, allowing the party to have a good look at the situation before committing. Everyone responded to 'Back off' commands, and they also concentrated their attacks well on successive monsters. They all knew that I was finishing off the 'runners' so they could concentrate on the monsters in their face and not on the ones that would be coming back with friends after healing a bit.
Overall, this fight was one of the best I have been in ever. Everyone did their part and there was very little confusion. Everyone knew what was happening and trusted the other members to do their part. I hope everyone gets a chance to experience this sort of coordinated action at some point in their EQ career. It's nearly indescribably cool.

Pre-Fight Planning
Any time the group is considering attacking a group of monsters, or is being attacked by a group, it is imperative that the group concentrate their attacks (as we saw above in the story). When the aggressor in a combat, the party has the advantage of pre-planning which of the monsters is the 'primary' target. There are a few logical ways to analyze the targeting order in a combat.

1) Enemy spellcasters. These HAVE to die, or the party is hosed. Primarily, this is offensive casting enemies like wizards and necromancers. These are a parties worst nightmare in groups because sometimes you can't get to them and prevent them from casting spells. This also applies to those monsters with special attacks (stuns or drains and the like). They are also mush easier to take out because of their low hit points (hopefully!).

Tactics: Have _your_ offensive caster take them out preemptively. Usually, starting a fight by having your caster burn on the enemy caster is effective because then the enemy caster is occupied. The only problem is that all of the enemy casters friends are on their way to kill your caster too. That's where the tanks step in front and occupy them. It's not too difficult to distract a mob if you hit it in the head with a sword. Thus, the fight is now divided, casters on casters, and tanks on tanks.

Hopefully your tanks are taking out the enemies by concentrating their attacks. It is important at this stage to get a tank on the enemy spellcaster. Vital. Even having a tank with another enemy on his back while he attacks the caster is worth it. This is for one reason. Interruptions. If your tank can induce interrupts, and your caster is unaffected, the fight is over. The other caster is going to be eating sword and spells; that's never a good combination. An excellent tank for enemy spellcasters is a Monk. Lots of attacks means a higher chance of interruption. Also works with any higher level warriors types with dual wield or double attack (or both!).

2) Weaklings. There are always a few lower level monsters in a group of enemies (at least you hope there are). These should be prime targets. Why? Because they have less health, and can be taken out quickly. Never underestimate the power of 2-3 green monsters will have on a fight with a blue one. The sheer quantity of hits can be deadly as well as the power of those attacks.

Tactics: Either target them first, or have your spellcaster pick them off with concentrated fire.

3) Damaged mobs. As mentioned previously and similarly to the Weaklings... the sooner they're dead, the less damage they'll do.

4) Runners. This is the most dangerous type of enemy for a party in a dungeon. If one mob slips away after being damaged in combat the leader might as well call for a mass retreat while everyone can still make it out. In a dungeon,a damaged creature fleeing from combat will run deeper into the dungeon calling every monster it finds to attack the party that nearly killed it. If it does not call red creatures that would kill your party outright, it will call enough weaker creatures to overrun your party in a brutal fashion. This is called a train. You don't want it.

Tactics: These creatures should be dealt with by the mages or one of the warriors if your party is lacking anyone who can cast burn. _Never_ let them go unless you want to see how ugly a dungeon can really get.

5) Faction. Attacking some creatures will lower your standing with other races and mobs. As Drew or the dwarven guards of Kaladim might tell you, killing Crag Aviaks will lower your faction standing with many groups. In general, if you con the creature (hit 'C' while it is targeted) and it says that it 'looks at you indifferently' you are best to leave it alone or you are going to get in trouble with some faction.

Conclusion
Leaders can be the savior or damnation of a group. Keep that in mind and pick your group leader wisely. Oh, and note, the person who forms the group doesn't have to be the leader, but it helps, because then they can control the group composition. If someone is being dumb, they should get punted, and having control over group composition can be just the sort of 'enforcement' needed.

Stay tuned for more from PPI.

Good luck and good hunting.
