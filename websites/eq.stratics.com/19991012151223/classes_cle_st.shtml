Playing a Cleric - Starting Out

 Introduction   Creation   Training   Hunting and Learning   Training Strategies   Spells   Weapons and Armor 

Introduction
Clerics are priests and priestesses who have been given the power by their deity to heal and help others. They are a valuable asset to any group in the role of the healer. In addition, Clerics are given special powers to combat the undead forces of Norrath. No other class can stand up to these evil forces as early as the Cleric.
 top


Creating a Character
The point at which you actually create your character, the choice of race and starting attributes, truly defines your character and is the most important part of your overall character development. Don't underestimate it.

There are two distinct aspects of a Cleric that must be considered at this early stage. The first, and most obvious aspect, is that of spell casting. A Cleric is first and foremost a spell caster, and as such you need to make sure he or she will be ready to handle that role. In this aspect, Wisdom is of prime importance. There is, however, another aspect to the Cleric, that of the Warrior. While the Cleric's role is primarily that of a spell caster, you will find that they can hold their own in melee as well. They will never be as good as a true Warrior or even hybrids such as Rangers and Paladins, but they do have more skill in this area than most spell casters. Exploit this slight advantage. In groups, you can help beat on your enemy while maintaining your healing role. By doing so, you help kill the creature faster, and the creature is allowed to do less damage to your party. To exploit this advantage, you need to tweak up your Strength and Stamina a bit.

So where does all this leave us? It leaves us with 3 separate attributes that we may need to adjust with our starting points;however, splitting those points up among 3 attributes is typically unwise. So let us forget about Stamina. Stamina affects our total hit points. While at early levels this may help, in the long run, it's just a waste. Clerics are not expected to take the brunt of the attacks. That's what fighters and monks are for. Your typical "tanks" as they are called. So let's leave Stamina alone. That leaves us with Strength and Wisdom. This is where your race makes a difference in how you adjust your attributes. Some races will have high Strength and low Wisdom. If this is the case, then you will want to put most of your points into Wisdom to bring it up. Some races will be opposite, and you should put some points into Strength to bring it up. Other races are rather well-balanced. In these cases I suggest putting most into Wisdom since it is of prime importance.

Your First Training Session
First thing you need to do with your new character is find your guild and hand over your letter. As a cleric, the guild tunic doesn't mean much at this time. You can wear it for a few extra AC points, but it won't affect your appearance any. In addition, you will soon be replacing this with some patchwork armor, so don't worry if you lose it. Once you have turned in your note, you will receive some experience and 5 training points. Right click on your trainer and spend your first 5 points on 1H Blunt. This is the skill corresponding to your starting weapon.

Hunting and Learning - the Formative Years
As with every character class in the game, Clerics will spend levels 1 to 3 or 4 playing solo most of the time. These early levels should be used not only to gain experience, but also to learn how to effectively use your chosen class. As indicated in the Spell Strategies below, you will want to purchase the spell Strike as soon as possible. Once you have this you will need to use it often during combat to learn effective spell timing. Spell timing is learning to time the casting of your spell in between hits from your enemy, thereby avoiding having your spell interrupted.

As is always emphasized in EverQuest, make sure to CON (CONsider) every target before engaging it. By doing so you will quickly learn what you can and can not handle. For 1st and 2nd level, I suggest that you stick to targets that CON as "looks like you would have the upper hand". These will give decent experience while not being unbeatable at early levels. Levels 3 and 4, you should start trying to concentrate on "looks like an even fight" type targets. These will maximize your experience gains. During all levels, however, don't just run around looking for these types of targets. Kill any targets of opportunity that are equal to or weaker than your target type.

Early on, start practicing your spell timing. Once engaged in combat, try to time your casting of Strike in between the enemies hits. When engaging easy targets that offer little threat, you should also practice "on the fly healing". This is done by hitting F1 to target yourself. Then heal yourself, being sure to time the casting between hits (because your target will still be hitting you). Then hit F8 to toggle back to your enemy. Practice this often enough so you become proficient. Being able to do this effectively could very well save your life in the future.

As you get closer to level 5 you will want to start focusing your hunt on targets that give decent loot. Spells aren't free, and soon you will be needing to shell out at least a platinum or two on spells.

For Dwarves, you will want to start focusing on Large Skunks and Large Spiders. Skunks are typically level 3 or 4, and large spiders typically levels 4 or 5. This means you should be sure to CON the target first, and be sure it is not "looks like quite a gamble". The skunks will occasionally give scent sacks which can be sold for 1 gold and change, and Large Spiders will occasionally give spider silk which again sells for 1 gold and change. In addition, they will both give excellent experience because even the weakest ones will be "even fights".

For Humans, you can start earning money a little early, and continue to do so by killing Fire Beetles and Klacknacks. They will very often give fire beetle eyes which can be sold for around 2 silver each.

Training Strategies
Properly and efficiently using your training points each time you level can make a huge difference in how a character turns out at high levels. You should have already spent your first 5 points on your 1H Blunt skill, so that you could maximize your weapon skill at low levels. As you fight, your 1H Blunt will continue to rise on its own. Each time you level for the first 2 or 3 times, you will want to use your training points on 1H Blunt. In some cases you will find that you can only train a few of your points before "Reaching your limit in the chosen skill". This is fine and means that you are getting good training on the field. Simply save the extra points. You will want to continue to train 1H Blunt each level in this fashion until the guild master starts charging you for the training. This should happen around level 5 or 6. Maybe a little earlier or later. In addition, when you reach level 4, you will want to spend that 1 point on Channeling. This is an important skill that is used when casting spells, when scribing or memorizing spells, and is used to help prevent being interrupted when casting. By training one point in Channeling, you will "Learn the basics", and from now on the skill will go up on its own. Don't waste any more than 1 point in this skill, as it is one that rises fast through use. Any extra points you should have before level 8, you should spend on either 2H Blunt or if you wish, on a trade skill. I suggest 2H Blunt since this allows you more flexibility in choosing a weapon; however, now that trade skills are functional, you may want to play with them.

When reaching level 8, you will want to dump all 5 of those new points into Meditation. Here is one skill that different people are of differing opinions on. Some will say to only use 1 skill point such as you did in Channeling. The reason I tell you to use all 5 is because Meditation is an extremely important skill. It allows you to recover mana quicker; however, at low levels, I find that it doesn't work so well, and has a tendency to rise slow. By putting 5 points into training you start out a little better and will rise faster in the skill. Besides you will usually have the extra points at this stage anyway.

By now you will have stopped training in 1H Blunt and your 2H Blunt (if you chose to train it) should be fast approaching the point where pay is required as well. Should you find yourself with skill points that are truly extra and lying around, spend them on some of your weaker magic skills such as Conjuration, which for a Cleric tends to be low. This is because we have very few spells that use it. Those spells however are handy, and you will want to be proficient lest you waste precious mana trying to summon food and water.

Spell Strategies
From the very beginning I recommend buying only the spells you absolutely need until you have money to spare. The reason for this is that Clerics have the worst of both money situations. They must spend money on spells and on decent armor. In addition, due to the fact that Clerics get each "spell rank" at higher levels, their spells tend to cost more. Your rank 3 spells will cost around 6 platinum per spell on average.

At rank 1 (levels 1-4) I recommend that you buy only Strike, and I suggest getting that as early as possible. This spell makes hunting much easier, and will start to teach you the basics of "Spell Timing" - that is, timing your spells between enemy hits.

Rank 2 (levels 5-8) is a little tougher. Here, there are lots of good spells and some fun ones as well. Of prime importance is Light Healing, and Holy Armor is a close second. The spell Summon Water is an absolute must get and is also cheap. I suggest buying Summon Water first thing after making level 5. By doing so you will no longer be buying water, and you will be able to save your money faster; therefore, getting the other spells you need faster. Next I suggest getting Light Healing followed by Holy Armor. In addition, the spell Ward Undead is excellent if you should be hunting undead. If you can spare the money I suggest getting it, but it's not a priority. Another useful spell is Gate, but you can live without it, if you can't spare the money.

At rank 3 (levels 9-13) spells become expensive. First off, buy yourself Summon Food. This is a cheap spell (one of the only cheap ones in this rank), and you will save more money by no longer having to buy food. Next on the list of priorities would be Center and Hammer of Wrath. Get Center first, as it is the king of all buffs at your disposal (at this level), adding 50 HPs to the target's max HP. Root is another useful spell that you will want to get when money allows.

Weapon and Armor Strategies
Clerics unfortunately have to worry about both armor and spells. You do however get one break, and that is on weapons. As a Cleric, you can summon very decent magical weapons for your own use starting with the spell Hammer of Wrath. This allows you to concentrate your money on just spells and armor.

The first type of armor you will want to get, and early on, is patchwork leather. This can be bought relatively cheap from other players (usually around 2-4gp per item), and offers rather decent protection. As a Cleric, you won't be playing the role of "tank" all that often, and because of this, you can usually get away with wearing cheaper armor than warriors. Just because you can wear plate armor, doesn't mean that you have to. Your money should be spent on spells first, and only if you have extra money lying around should you waste it on improving your armor beyond patchwork.

Adventuring in dungeons with groups can be a great way to increase your armor, because most often you can find armor on monster in these depths.
