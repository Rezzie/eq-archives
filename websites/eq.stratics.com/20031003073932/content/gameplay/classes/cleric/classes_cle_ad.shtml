Playing a Cleric - Advanced

 Pre-Introduction   Introduction   Cleric Theory   Character Generation   Skills   Spells   Solo Tactics   Group Tactics   Miscellaneous   Conclusion 

Teffo's Guide to Transcendental Cleric Power
This document is designed for players who are proficient with the interface of EverQuest. If you are not, please read the eqmanual.txt and additionally please read Intermediate Group Combat and Advanced Group Tactics if you plan on ever joining a group with your Cleric. Additionally, please read EQSS's Playing a Magic-User for more general information on casters vs. NPCs.

This is advice for intermediate/advanced players who may or may not know some of the more effective tactics for Clerics. It starts slowly, with some concepts to keep in mind as a Clerics, and moves into detailed group combat tactics and spell analysis.

This guide does not walk you through the early levels! 'Guides' (using the term loosely) that walk you through levels teach you nothing.

Read. Learn.



Introduction
Clerics are healing incarnate. While a Cleric is around (and has mana) your chances of dying are greatly decreased. This makes Clerics one of the most 'in demand' classes in all of Norrath (especially by fighters of all kinds). Many groups will not even attempt certain enemies or areas without a Cleric, and for good reason. Clerics save lives. It's just that simple.

Healing, while the main job of a Cleric, isn't all that a Cleric can do. In fact, this class is extraordinarily versatile. Buffs, heavy armor, and a bit of melee make Clerics all around useful. About the only thing they can't do is do it all on their own. Clerics are designed to work in a group. But even in a group there are things to learn: Healing is a good start. You are a mediocre cleric if you can heal well (including the right people at the right times). A truly great Cleric knows how to use all of a cleric's abilities in concert to achieve astonishing results.

You just haven't felt alive in EQ until you've taken 3-4 white and yellow enemies, with the entire party ending at about half a bubble to one bubble. Only a great cleric can drag a group through that sort of hellish furnace without panicking and losing a few party members. And when you do have that experience... you'll never look at a Cleric the same way again.



Cleric Theory
1) Healing makes a body good.

Healing is such a key ability. It is the primary determinant in any fight, both survival and recovery. If you can heal during a fight and your opponent cannot, you should almost always be victorious. Healing also increases the effective level of party by almost 1-2 full levels. A group of level 10 characters with a good Cleric can easily function as a group of level 12 characters with respect to the individual difficulty and group difficulty of enemies. This increase in 'effective level' tremendously increases the amount of experience and loot that a group can acquire.

2) Undead? You should have stayed dead.

Clerics break Undead like twigs. Almost all of their primary offensive armament is designed to wipe undead opponents across a rough surface at very high velocities. Ward Undead, Expulse Undead, and later versions are at least as efficient as Wizard burn with respect to mana/damage ratio against the walking ick. When fighting Undead, which would you rather have? Frail Wizard? Or a partial tank that can also save your life while reducing Undead to char? Right. So bring a Cleric or two with if you expect to find some corpses walking around.

3) Soloing? *cough cough* Not.

In general, Clerics have huge problems dealing damage in combat. At early levels, they can solo rather effectively. Middle levels it begins to get rough, because Clerics just don't have the health to take the damage that blue or white enemies will dish out. At middle-high to high levels, you are almost impotent solo. You just can't do enough melee damage without double attack or dual wield to keep up with enemies that are striking you for 40-50 damage twice around. Even with healing.

While fighting undead, your damage output increases rather tremendously, however you basically become a Undead-only Wizard (in full plate). You burn your enemy down and then have to meditate for long periods. And may your God help you if more than one enemy simultaneously decides you're lunch. At least you can go invulnerable and zone before they finish the meal.

3) Groups LOVE you.

So, Clerics can't solo. Wow can they group! Even if the world were populated with 25% clerics, there would still be an effective shortage. Clerics are that amazing in a group. Even multiple clerics do not get in each other's way - they simply keep adding to the group.

What makes a Cleric so useful to a group?

a) Damage output.

While not able to put the hurting on an enemy that a normal fighter types can, they still have a melee attack which can add up the damage in a long fight. The real damage output of a cleric is their spells. Start to think of Healing as indirect burn. The AI thinks this is true and so should you! If you keep a fighter fighting, they're going to do MUCH more damage than you would with those 80 points of mana used as burn.

A Cleric's offensive magicks are not as powerful as any pure caster, but Clerics have some unique advantages when casting burn. When a Wizard or Magician casts burn (especially area affect burn), it had best be from maximum range or you can expect a very bloody mage body to hit the floor in the near future. Clerics, on the other hand can burn with near impunity because of the tremendous protection their armor affords them.

b) Defensive buffs.

These are as much or even more important than healing. Clerics can layer spells on friendly party members like bandages on a mummy, and should. If you do not buff your party, they take more damage. If they take more damage, they are in more danger, if they are in more danger, you must expend much more mana trying to heal them. This is a vicious cycle. Your defensive buffs make your healing spells about 20-50% more efficient. Use them on almost everyone who will get into combat (pure casters like buffs, but don't really need them if they play well).

c) Tanking.

Clerics can tank for a group, but only for a limited amount of time. When the group is engaged in multiple enemy combat, a Cleric can safely take some heat off of the primary tanks while they finish the first enemy. When the first enemy is slain they can pull the next one from the cleric (who can survive for quite a while) and finish that one with much less danger for the tank. Try not to get two enemies on your primary tanks. Spread the damage around, it's more effective, and less dangerous to any individual.

d) Defensive utility spells.

Clerics have a bevy of utility spells that can make a combat situation (and non-combat situation) a heck of a lot less complex. We'll cover them later in detail, but remember: Lull before combat, Fear at the start of combat, Root near the end of combat. Basically, these spells allow you to divide and conquer enemies similar to the Enchanter. These are subtle effects that may not seem that impressive, but make a huge difference.



Character Generation
Race:

Possible choices: Dark Elf, Dwarf, Erudite, Gnome, High Elf, Halfling, Human.

Almost every race has its healers. The race choice should really be one you feel comfortable playing. But remember, that your race choice has some effect of the 'type' of cleric you play. Your overall effectiveness will be dependent on your ability to maximize strengths and minimize weaknesses of your race to your advantage.

Clerics come in basically two types: Support caster and Combat caster, though a good Cleric can adapt to fit either role as demand requires.

Support casters are usually Dark Elf, High Elf, or Erudite. These races have lower physical statistics (Str, Agil, Stam) and generally can achieve slightly higher wisdom (and thus more mana). Most often Support casters provide combat overview, act as coordinators, while castings spells to maximize combat effectiveness.

Combat casters are much more of a 'get in, get dirty player. They stand in the middle of a fight directing things, dealing damage, healing, and tanking for the group. This is a true test for a Cleric. The ability to perform all the duties required while in melee range with an enemy or two can stress test even the most battle hardened player. The natural Combat casters are halflings and dwarves, because they have tremendous physical attributes (Str,Agil, Stam).

Other races have more balanced statistics and thus have parts of the Combat Caster and the Support Caster in them. The Humans are best at this considering their balance. The Gnomes do a little of both, but are somewhat lacking in Strength and Stamina. This reduces access to bigger armor and drops combat survivability. Note: Small armor weighs less. Thus, races that can wear small armor (Dark Elf, High Elf, Dwarf, Halfling, Gnome) have a large advantage in Strength because full plate will weigh 5-10 pounds less.

Statistics:

Prime Statistics: Stamina, Strength, Wisdom

Strength: On the obvious side this is the statistic that determines your damage cap and adds a little to your chance to hit. More important is that Strength determines your maximum carrying capacity. As a cleric, you are going to run short every time. You can wear plate at 75 Strength but you would pretty much have to pass on looting and summon your food and water in small amounts. All of a Cleric's equipment is heavy. Their armor is heavy, their shields are heavy, and their weapons are very heavy. You can never really go wrong with extra points in Strength, even if you don't plan on doing much melee. The carrying capacity is key.

Stamina: This determines your hit points as well as the rate at which you tire. Some warn against putting points into Stamina, but with heavy plate armor, each point of health gained from Stamina is much more effective because of the additional protection the armor provides. Other classes do not have this advantage. It is also important because of the weight of the weapons that clerics are usually swinging around - they will tire you quickly if you neglect Stamina.

Wisdom: This one is a bit obvious. Wisdom determines mana gained per level. It also provides better resistance to spells and is checked on a failed skill roll. This is the last statistic you should put points into, and only after you decide what type of cleric you are going to play. Having a large pool of mana is not the end all be all of effectiveness. It really only helps in emergencies and to some degree at high levels. If you have 25 points put into Wisdom, and you're playing well enough that you do not drop below one bubble, you may have made the wrong choice on statistics. I (a Combat Caster) personally put only as much as I feel is absolutely necessary because I feel that the points better serve main combat survivability rather than providing me with extra mana that I rarely use. Support casters should probably go maximum wisdom.

Recommendations:

If you're planning on playing a Support caster, recommendations are for high Wisdom (more mana), and a bit into strength, so you can get some decent armor layered on. Armor is more important than agility now. A Cleric in Chain with 95 Agility will not last as long as a Cleric in Plate with 85 Agility. If you can, go for the Strength to wear Plate. We calculate that you'll need about 90 Strength for full Plate. However, you can go partial Plate and Chain for probably 75-80 Strength. Keep it in mind.

If you're playing a Combat caster (with a Dwarf, Halfing, or something else), we recommend that you diversify point allocation. Combat Clerics need strength for Plate, good Stamina for combative ability, and Wisdom for spell-casting. Dwarven Clerics might consider adding a bit to Agility, but that's not a requirement, because of their enhanced Stamina.

The most important statistic for a Combat Cleric is Dexterity. This is the statistic that determines your ability to join the melee without ending up with dead party members. Dexterity affects your ability at Channeling. With a high dexterity, Channeling allows you to cast faster and occasionally through attacks by an enemy that is actively attacking the Cleric. Do not ignore this statistic.

Additionally, Stamina is now a very important statistic. You will see a return on investments into this statistic that are comparable to Agility. This depends also on your choice of Cleric 'type'.

Level 4: Channeling
Level 8: Meditate
Level 15: Dodge

There are only a few notable Cleric skills. Having to wait until level 8 for Meditate is painful. Levels 6 and 7 tend to drag on. Group for those two levels, then go back to soloing. It gets bad again around 12-14 because Healing is no longer effective against the amount of damage you're getting hit with. At level 15, a cleric gets dodge. It helps a bit, but Cleric Dodge caps out at Good, and thus it functions more as an augmentation to Channeling. It increases your chance of getting spells off, but won't increasing your combat abilities that much.

Specialization doesn't work as of yet. Down waste any trains in it. It is completely non-functional and the official word is they "forgot" to turn it off in the guild masters. Should it become enabled, it might be worth investing in Alteration or Abjuration a bit so your heals are more effective and faster, and your buffs are a tad stronger.

As a side note, all Cleric skills are skills that only need a single point each. They will all improve from zero with use. This leaves a large number of points to devote to training weapon, language, or trade skills at some point. Don't be surprised to find your self sitting on hundreds of unused points when you get to higher levels.



Spells
Healing:

Healing is good. Efficient and powerful, it allows everyone else to do their job during combat. It is basically the Cleric version of indirect burn, and has the efficiency corresponding to Wizard burn (in fact it's almost exactly the same damage/mana ratio at each corresponding spell rank, if not slightly better). It keeps your tanks in the battle longer allowing them to deal more damage and have a greatly increased chance of finishing the enemy(ies). More obviously it keeps people alive. People like that.

Level 1:

Minor Healing

  • Mana: 10
  • Health: 10
  • Eff: 100.00%

    10 points of healing for 10 points of mana. It is useless when you reach level 5 because you (coincidentally) receive the next healing spell at that point. It's not a bad idea to keep this spell memorized for a while, since it may save you in a situation where you are caught with critically low mana and need just a tad bit more health. You really don't have a whole lot of spells to choose from at that point anyway.

    Level 4:

    Light Healing

  • Mana: 20
  • Health: 26
  • Eff: 130.00%

    It is a little bit more efficient heal/mana and much more efficient based on heal/time. It's a big help at low-intermediate levels, but really starts to go downhill at level 11. At this point the enemies hit for much more in a round than you can heal for. Clerics heal the 'weakest' in this level range compared to their usual ability. This clears up as you enter level 14. However, the period from 11 to 14 is rather rough.

    Level 14:

    Healing

  • Mana: 60
  • Health: 88+1/level above 14
  • Eff: 146.67%

    This spell is the beginning of your more mana efficient big effect heals. This one has very little problem getting you to the next level of heal. At Level 23 you will be fighting creatures that can do 90 in a round but their damage will average closer to 40. It will seem a little weak near the time you are about to get greater heal but nothing terribly crippling like light healing at level 13.

    Level 24+:

    Greater Healing (~300 for 120)
    Superior Healing (~600 for 250)
    Complete Healing (L39)
    Word of Health (Party area affect heal)
    Word of Healing (Better version of the previous)

    Your healing gets exponentially better as you go up. The complete healing spell is amazing, but generally will not be as mana efficient for you as superior healing except on the truly obscenely healthy tanks (1200+ hps). The Word of Health spells aren't amazing in combat (unless you're fighting something that has area affect damage as it's prime mode of attack) but are good for speeding recovery in an efficient fashion.

    Cures:

    Cure Poison
    Cure Blindness
    Cure Disease

    These spells cure you of the corresponding affliction. These first level versions of this series may require multiple casts to remove the adverse effect. Poison and Disease are the most common afflictions.

    Buffs:

    Clerics have the best defensive buffs around. Resistances, Defensive armor, and even Statistic assistance all fall here.

    Defensive Buffs have two effects: They increase health, and they decrease enemy attack effectiveness through AC bonuses. This allows Buffed characters to stay in combat longer and thus have a better chance of finishing the mob they are attacking. Buffs also ease your job of healing during combat. Always buff your group before a fight - it's well worth it.

    Level 1:

    Courage

  • Mana: 10

    This is the first in a series of spells that add hit points and a small amount of ac. Even this first buff on a level 15 character will make a difference.

    Divine aura

  • Mana: 10

    This spell and it's later versions are not so much buffs as they are a panic button. They render you and at the high levels your group invulnerable for a short period of time. While invulnerable you cannot attack or cast spells. It does allow you a better chance to make zone or for your group to finish the creature that is attacking you while you are protected. It is good, as well, when you are in the middle of combat and are just short on mana for a heal. In this case you can go invulnerable and meditate in the middle of the fray for a short time to get enough mana for that life saving heal. It is also good for when your health drops below the 40% mark causing enemy frenzy. When your health drops below 20%,you can also go invulnerable giving your group about four rounds to finish the creature on you (if they won't be able to do that, attempt to zone.)

    Flash of Light

  • Mana: 10

    Blinds a target for about 10-20 seconds. Prevents spell casting as well as being a general pain in the ass. Primarily a PvP spell, but has some specific applications during normal hunting.

    Reckless strength

  • Mana: 10

    This provides a target with a longer, though decreasing, burst of strength. This first one will provide the target with a 20 point increase that will decay down to -10. This spell is great for when you are fighting alone with a tank around level 10 to 14. They will be able to finish the mob quicker because of the increased damage. In general though this spell is not terribly useful because of its short duration and the fact that it goes negative. It is useful for soloing as well as around 10-14, since the increased damage extends your limited solo ability a few levels further. This spell also has higher level versions of it.

    Yaulp

  • Mana: 5

    This is a personal ac and strength buff that lasts for roughly 18 sec. It is extremely useful early on when you are soling but is worthless after level 12 or so. It provides a nice burst of added ability but after level 10 you really are not providing enough damage in melee to warrant the mana expenditure and the exposure time. You're already casting spells (healing), and I doubt your tank is going to be very amused if you are out of mana when he needs a heal because you Yaluped through combat. This is the first in a series of spells. The upper level ones rarely get used either.

    Level 5:

    Holy Armor

  • Mana: 20

    This is another first in a line of buffs for you as a cleric. These spells increase AC as well as reduce the amount of damage the target takes.

    Level 9:

    Center

  • Mana: 40

    This is the upgraded version of courage. This one adds 44 + 1 per level above 9 and a small amount of AC. It also reduces the damage taken from each attack similar to Holy Armor but to a lesser extent.

    Endure series:

    Level 5:

    Endure Fire

  • Mana: 20

    Gives a bonus to resistance of fire.

    Level 9:

    Endure Poison

  • Mana: 20

    Gives a bonus to resistance of poison.

    Endure Cold

  • Mana: 20

    Gives a bonus to resistance of cold.

    Level 14:

    Endure Disease

  • Mana: 20

    Gives a bonus to resistance of disease.

    Level 19

    Endure Magic

  • Mana: 20

    Gives a bonus to resistance of magic.

    These spells give you a bonus to the corresponding saving throw. They are also part of a series that is improved in higher circles of spells.

    Level 14:

    Symbol of Transal

    7 hps per level added.
    This spell begins your third series of buffs. The symbol spells stack with the armor and courage spells, providing more health to burn, but require a material component (about a 2.5 gp gem). This first spell usually makes people angry because it seems useless. It can be a huge bonus for the tanks, adding an effective 2-3 levels of health. This, combined with decent healing (Duh, you have a Cleric) makes this spell a big help for dungeon hunting. Your tanks just have that 50 extra damage that can easily sneak up on you in a dungeon. It is circumstantial, but can be funded easily with the rusty weapons your group would otherwise toss away (medium levels).

    Level19:

    Daring

    This is the third in your line of courage spells. Bigger hp bonus, bigger protection.

    Spirit armor

    Holy armor v 2.0. More protection, more AC, more damage absorption.

    Burn

    There is a dark side to healing, and that is the taking of health. Clerics have some reasonably priced 1:1 mana/damage burn. It's nice for finishing runners. It is also good for when something needs to be finished immediately. Clerics deliver damage in decent amounts but the mana cost is too high to use regularly. This is of course with the exception of the undead damage spells. These spells are on par with spells a wizard of the same level would have.

    Level 1:

    Strike

  • Mana: 12
  • Damage: 8
  • Eff: 66.67%

    8 damage for 12 mana. Think of it as a way to draw creatures to you. It is never really any better than that. By the time you can use it well (i.e. have meditate) it is too weak to be worth casting. It is the beginning of a series of spells. They are not terribly mana efficient but they have good damage and are not often resisted.

    Level 5:

    Ward Undead

  • Mana: 30
  • Damage: 41
  • Eff: 146.67%

    This is the beginning of your undead burn spells. They are very mana efficient and extremely helpful in the undead dungeons, as well as for those occasional targets of opportunity.

    Stun

  • Mana: 30

    This is not so much burn as a debuff. This spell will stun a creature for 2 - 3 rounds. Excellent for fighting something that is doing a lot of damage with a group. It is with a series of spells. Later versions of this will have a bit of damage in addition, as well as longer stuns. Note that with any debuff, resistance is much more likely.

    Furor

  • Mana: 20
  • Damage: 19 max
  • Eff: 95.00%

    This is an upgrade of strike. While it is not the most efficient spell it is still extremely useful. It allows you to quickly apply a bit of damage without committing too much mana.

    Level 9:

    Word of Pain

  • Mana: 47
  • Damage: 29
  • Eff: 61.70%+

    This is the start of another series of spells. These are point blank area effect spells, meaning they hit a lot of baddies in the near vicinity (4-6 character widths away). Note that because this is an area affect spell, it can actually become very mana efficient if you hit 3-5 enemies with it.

    Level 14:

    Expulse Undead

  • Mana: 60
  • Damage: 88 initial
  • Eff: 146.67%

    This spell is Ward Undead v2.0. Very nice. [Equivalent to Shock of Lightning L12 Wizard]

    Smite

  • Mana: 70
  • Damage: 74 initial
  • Eff: 105.71%

    Strike v 3.0. This spell is good damage, bad efficiency. Still it's good to have in your arsenal, though most time you will beholding the mana back for heals.

    Ward Summoned

  • Mana: 30
  • Damage: 41 Max
  • Eff: 136.67%

    This is again, first in the series. It is a version of the ward undead spells that only works on summoned creatures.

    Level 19:

    Word of Shadow

  • Mana: 95
  • Damage: 52
  • Eff: 54.73%+

    Basically an upgraded version of word of pain. The mana investment for this spell is significant, so Word of Pain might be the superior choice for a while (1-3 levels).

    Holy Might

  • Mana: 60?
  • Damage: 29
  • Eff: 48.33%

    Upgraded version of Stun. It provides a touch of damage as well as stunning the target for 2-3 combat rounds. Really makes a mess of enemy spell casters. Badly.

    Summoning:

    Summon Food
    Summon Water

    Summons one food or drink that will disappear when you log out (or if you go offline in any fashion aside from momentary LDness). With the time it takes to summon up to 5 of each it quickly becomes a large annoyance. It is still worth the effort for two reasons. First you have little else to raise your conjuration to an acceptable level. Second it saves you a bit of money over time. Since clerics are the most expensive class to play any bit of change you can keep around will help.

    Hammer of Wrath

    The Summon Hammer spell series starts here. This spell summons very nice magic hammer (damage 6, delay 32). It is fairly light and will not drain your Stamina nearly as much as other standard Cleric weapons. The best part about this weapon is that is strikes creatures immune to normal weapons. This saves you a tremendous amount of money around L10, as you are not required (as most classes are) to buy a Combine or magical weapon. Clerics are not required to do much damage to their opponent (they're in a group with a fighter usually), so this weapon is more than sufficient until you find a nice weapon, or get to the next Hammer spell (of Striking).

    Other summoning spells:

    Abundant Food (4 food at once, merely a time saver)
    Abundant Drink (4 drink at once, time saver)
    Hammer of Striking
    Hammer of Smiting
    Hammer of Requital

    Utility spells

    Most people don't know that Clerics are part enchanter (I think that is because most people haven't ever played an Enchanter how they are supposed to be played). Clerics have 3 series of spells designed to control the numbers before and during battle.

    Lull

    This is the first of a series that allows a cleric to pull individuals or small groups of mobs from an area that contains a number of creatures that would over run their group easily. It also lowers frenzy on a mob so it allows someone to back out of combat when they are lower than 40%. This makes it an absolutely vital spell for attacking camps, especially in dungeons. (See strategy section for more details.)

    Lull is not any where near as effective for this purpose as later variants of this spell.

    This first spell is not terribly useful though, for its dreadfully low success rate. It is resisted, by even blue creatures, nearly 50% of the time and even when not resisted only about 60% time is it going to hold them in their position if another is attacked near them. Lull is better than nothing, but Soothe is exponentially better. As a note, this spell does not work on the undead. Only Necromancers get the Lull the Dead type spells.

    Spook the dead and Fear

    These are your fear line of spells. This is your second line of defense against overwhelming odds. When cast, these spells cause the targeted creature to run away from you until the spell effect ends. What this does for you is turn, for example, a 3 on 3 fight into a 2 on 3 fight for a limited amount of time. Putting your group at an advantage for this time should allow you to finish one of the creatures who has stayed without the added damage and interruptions that the third mob would have caused. Fear spells should never be used in dungeons except under controlled situations. The fleeing mob will make anything it runs by immediately go aggressive on the caster of the fear. This quickly turns a bad situation into a frenzied run to zone, leaving dead bodies in its wake.



    • Solo Combat Tactics
    If you want a good shot of how boring EQ can get, soloing a cleric is your gig. Clerics are a group class almost exclusively. Just like every other class they can solo to level 13 without too much problem but after that point, you begin to see exactly how little damage you are doing to the creature. As the enemy's health and damage increase, fight duration increases as does recovery time. Basically it gets to the point where you have two choices. You can either melee the creature for a long period of time and heal during and after the fight. This allows a somewhat shorter recovery but fights take sooooo long that I am often AFK while fighting from boredom. The second option is using your burn with melee. This finishes the mob somewhat faster and is my preferred method but makes for long recovery times because of the mana inefficiency of the damage. Once you get past level 12 these methods become so time inefficient that all but the most anti-social will realize that Clerics are a group character.

    You may suggest that Clerics should solo undead after this point. This would be a great idea, if it worked. You can solo undead that will give you decent experience, but you will be running back for your corpse often. 95% of all the undead at your level will be in dungeons (or outdoor micro-dungeons and ruins). This invariably means you are going to be fighting multiple enemies which is a losing situation, regardless of whether you win, or die. Even if you win, the recovery time will make this method of gaining experience inefficient. And dying... well, that's never good. Usually, if you manage to find a location with a single undead opponent of the correct level, you still have to deal with little green annoying stuff (we call it chafe or trash) that will attack you while meditating. Once you hit that meditate button, you will have every piece of green chafe within two rooms all over you. You don't get much recovery done when you have to fight a rat down to half and then chase it down as it runs at full speed around corners and through doors that you can't burn through. No fun.



    Group Combat Tactics
    The group is your home. You are a group character and thus you improve a group exponentially by your addition. When you are there, your group fights more effectively and safely. Your duty: to keep everyone alive.

    This is where the fun of the cleric lies. Your spells, your weapons, your armor, and even your very HP are the tools that you have to use to keep everyone alive to fight another day.

    Clerics must keep a cool head under the most extreme situations. A cleric who panics gets people killed. You, as the cleric, have to be willing to ride your health down to under a bubble to keep the tanks in and damaging. You must also have the trust of your group so that they will not get spooked and try to bug out when they see the entire party at half life and none of the opponents dead (but a few are close). When things get ugly and the odds are not in your favor, everyone has to have trust in your ability and the group as a whole.

    The first thing you must learn is when to heal and when not to heal. If a Cleric runs out of mana, combat becomes 2-3 times as dangerous as it was. Clerics must conserve mana to keep your options open as combat progresses.

    Healing most often is a 60% health twitch response. However, a 60% twitch heal may be premature. The enemy can be naturally drawn off it's initial target by a more dangerous foe (Monks draw enemies off fighters almost 100% of the time after 5-10 rounds). If you twitch heal the primary target, then you're wasting that mana on a target that is no longer in danger unless they can manage to draw the enemy back onto themselves.

    This sort of healing intuition depends to a great degree on your party composition, and as a Cleric becomes comfortable in a party, he knows how combat is likely to progress and who will be in the most danger as the fight goes on. Healing in most situations is a judgment call, and panicking and responding to somewhat low health will only be more likely to hurt the party than help it.

    Additionally, a good Cleric becomes amazing if the group knows how to distribute damage by 'taunting' with different effects (Burn, special attacks, skills, etc). Please read Grouping 101 and 201 formore information.

    Sometimes, a good group in a tough fight will often let people ride down to about 40-50%. This may seem risky and dangerous, but it effectively makes Healing a dual effect. Cleric healing can be used as a taunt to pull an almost frenzied monster back off a Tank (which is not attacking now) and onto the Cleric. Then another tank steps in and takes the enemy off the Cleric. Remember, if you're going to do this, everyone had better know this is going on, otherwise your group may panic and bug out.

    Someone who is somewhat familiar with Clerics might say that only bad Clerics use Direct Damage. They should instead be saving mana for healing. This is over simplifying the role of a Cleric, and is a discredit to the class.

    Simple fact: Burn can be as much of a life saver as your healing. If an enemy is going to dish out more damage in a slightly extended combat sequence than you can easily or safely heal, it's time for the group to 'light up the enemy'. Basically, all the casters go into nuke mode, chain casting burn at the enemy trying to reduce it to slag before it does the same to a party member. In many situations, this will include the Cleric! If this is a part of a multiple enemy engagement, the Cleric must be a bit more careful as there still will be a need for Healing later in the sequence. Lighting up an enemy can become very addictive for groups. This is a very inefficient method of combat, as it incurs a severe recovery time, and thus should only be used when the party is in extreme danger.

    The most common usage of Cleric burn is as a taunt. Light application of burn can turn enemies off of one player and on to you. This is a form of healing in itself, as you prevent further damage to a party member. This works the best on intelligent opponents (greater undead, and most demi-humans) as they are most likely to notice the cause of the 'ouchie'. Burn should be used sparingly but it is not without its place.

    Aside from their very effective anti-undead arsenal, Clerics have amazing point blank area affect damage in their Word of Pain series. These PBAOE spells don't do a lot of damage (about 3 damage per level of caster on average) they have one very important effect. Area affect spells draw enemies onto the caster like flies to raw meat (think about it, it's very apt). For a normal caster, that's deadly, but as mentioned above Clerics can take the added heat because of their armor.

    When the group is rushed by a few targets, the enemies will all lock onto the first one that strikes the group. If this is a tank, the rest of the party has a little while to get their act in gear and pull a few of the enemies off. If this is a pure caster, the party has only a few rounds to get the enemies off before they frenzy and kill the character. In either situation, the Cleric has the advantage in being able to launch WoP (or later versions), which will pull any enemy not already being attacked onto the Cleric. This can be a life saver. No one character can survive being attacked by many many opponents simultaneously for very long, but those in heavy armor will obviously last the longest.

    At this point, the Cleric has the option of just tanking the enemy(ies) until the party has dealt with the previously engaged enemies, or having another tank pull the enemy off of the cleric. The latter is the safest, as it gives the Cleric free reign to cast other spells (like healing!).

    Clerics are more than just health reserves. Their enchanter spells make them able to control the pace and difficulty of combat. These are the lull and fear spells that clerics have available. Both of theses series of spells even the odds for the your party in a fight. The soothe type spells are for situations when there are more mobs in an area than you care to try at once. By soothing some you can effectively divide and conquer your enemies. The fear and spook the dead series are the cleric's second line of defense against overwhelming odds. Fear spells will send one creature running for roughly 30 to 45 seconds for the first versions. This will save your group that monster's damage for the time that he is running. It effectively neutralizes them for several rounds of combat. This is a life saver, though a somewhat subtle one compared to clerics other abilities

    Soothe - Cast it on a few of a group of enemies, then target a different enemy and attack. This should have the effect of leaving the Soothed creatures behind. They will not assist. Pull the aggressive ones out of range quickly because Soothe doesn't last forever.

    Fear - Cast it on an extra enemy (usually a big one) during a multiple enemy engagement. It will run off and you can finish the remaining monsters more easily. Be wary for the Feared enemies return as Fear does not have a set duration.

    The Cleric as a Canary? Ok, bad old reference to the birds used by miners to detect gas pockets (and thus death). The Cleric is likely the best judge of how well a combat situation is going (or not going). Good clerics are likely to know very early how much longer the party can sustain the abuse that it is currently receiving. If your Cleric calls "RUN!", your party had best be heading towards the nearest zone border in a hurry.

    When you have two Clerics in a party (assuming they are both competent), each must be careful to take into consideration the other Cleric when analyzing the situation. Mana reports during combat become vital to maintain awareness of 'heal status'.

    It's better to call RUN early and have everyone live, than later and have some of the party limping to zone (or walking back from their bind point).



    Miscellaneous
    Armor

    Clerics are one of the few classes (others being Bards, Fighters, and Paladins) that can wear plate armor. This armor is just truly amazing, providing immense AC as well as the best damage absorption in the game. Upgrade your armor to the best you can find, steal, or purchase as often as possible. It is more important than some of your spells. Even if you are playing in a Support Cleric, the fact that you can stand up to damage if an enemy turns on you is extremely important. Your group needs you in combat and healing, regardless of the number of enemies on you. You are not a Magician, Wizard, or Enchanter which needs serious protection during combat. A Cleric that begs like a caster for protection is going to get his group killed. A Cleric that refuses to cast while engaged in melee is even worse.

    Money

    If you're a Cleric, you're broke, that's life. The combined costs of armor, weapons, and spells can seem overwhelming.

    Don't expect to ever really have more than you can spend hand to mouth until very high levels. Many times you will find yourself borrowing money for armor and spells. You can reduce the amount you spend somewhat in a supporting role by skimping a little on armor but still you will feel the pinch when you need to buy that next circle of spells. The only way you can really keep up in the lower levels, if you do not have a group to support you, is to sell your services. There are always warriors willing to shell out little money for some healing or others willing to pay for their disease to be gone. It won't get you fortune or glory but most of the time it's a lot less boring than fishing for 3.5 hours for one low circle spell.



    Conclusion
    Clerics are all but a necessity in every party. It is possible to play without one but I would not want to try it. Even using another healer (Druid or Shaman) will not be enough as you go up in levels. Their healing spells are very helpful and a welcome addition, but lack the potency at medium to high levels to keep a group alive (especially against foes like Hill Giants).

    Clerics are not for everyone, and in fact, may not be for most people. They don't solo well and they pretty much don't do damage. They do, though, allow a party to fight well beyond their normal limitations. They provide pulse racing fights and epic tales instead of runs to zone and much more forgettable demises.

    If you're into the adrenaline rush you feel when rushing headlong into an impossible fight, you've found your calling: Cleric.

    Good luck, and good hunting!
