JC is one of the easiest and cheapest skills to raise (no offense to JCers):
- you can buy all the components you need from the same store
- components stack, so you can buy a lot at once
- only two components per combine, both vendor bought - to really appreciate this one, I suggest you try Misty Thicket Picnics. Just the final combine for MTPs requires eight components, all of which require subcombines.
- buyback prices on final products cover components cost (minus vendor margin, of course). This is rarely the case with newer recipes in other tradeskills.
- success rates will be high, because you can always choose recipy with trivial very close to your current skill level.
- requires no other tradeskills to do subcombines

quote:
...pottery was nerfed to oblivian...

I can verify this statement first hand.

I was right at skill 200 when the pottery nerf patch came through. I watched as my learning rate went from 1 skillup every 5-10 combines to roughly 1 skillup every 70-120 combines. Albeit, being higher than skill 200 will slow down the learning rate, but an examination of the cost involved shows that 70-120 attempts to get ONE skillup is well beyond even vaguely reasonable.

Opal Encrusted Stein (Trivial 222): 20pp average cost

70-120 attempts to gain 1 skill up = roughly 2200 attempts to gain 22 skill points.

2200 attempts at 20pp each = 44,000pp

Star Ruby Encrusted Stein (Trivial > 250): 70pp average cost

250-222 = 28 skill points to gain = 2800 attempts = 2800 x 70pp = 196,000pp

196,000 + 44,000 = 240,000pp to get from 200 to 250 pottery skill.

I know, I know, all you blacksmithers out there are about to say, "THAT'S CHEAP!!" But remember that pottery doesn't produce +51AC steins or +40AC greaves. The best thing a potter can produce is a range slot item.


Btw, all of the above numbers are based on a character with 186 wis. Yeah, I know that getting wis over 200 will help with the learning rate, but the nerf that took place harmed the little guy far more than people realized. It's like all the testing that took place used a test character with 250 wis or 250 int, but failed to realize that people below level 50 often don't reach that peak. Result = little guy screwed.

If you are an enchanter, I would recommend Jewelcraft as your tradeskill of choice. You don't need to hunt for any dropped components, and there are lots of good cities where the gem merchant and the metal merchant are side by side. If you're high enough level for KEI, you can probably invest 5k or so, which you won't really even lose, it's just vastly easier to be able to buy full stacks of gems and metals at a time. You can also enchant the metals yourself, and with KEI the mana will come back quickly; this makes the resulting item sell back to the merchant at a better price, and you might even sell some of the things you make to other characters if you just /auc now and then what you haven't sold back to the merchant yet. With JC you just park yourself in Thurgadin or Shadowhaven (not sure yet if there's a jewelshop in PoK) and click click click your way to 250, moving up the gems as they get trivial, and up the metals when the gems get too expensive. Any failures to combine will be about covered by the slight profit you make for selling back enchanted jewellry. If you have money to throw away, it would go even faster if you don't bother enchanting. And at the end of your efforts, you have a skill that is still of some vague use, because you never know. JC also has the advantage that it covers one of the steps in doing the trophies. Enough pottery skill for the other step can be learned incredibly cheaply and quickly if you feel independent.

And remember, hold down control to pick up one thing out of a stack, you are going to love that shortcut!

Greetings, all!

Just a comment from someone who has made exactly one serious attempt at mastery of anything: JC. I originally was very intimidated by JC because so many people told my about the huge expense of materials, the horrible loss of plat with nothing to show for it after failures, and the mind-numbing amounts of time needed to get anywhere. I finally began it despite all that when my chanter hit 30th and was able to enchant everything but platinum and velium.

So, the objections, in order:

Huge expense. Like everything else in EQ dealing with money, "huge" is a relative term. My personal (and very recent) experience is that, if you are planning to make money along the way in order to defray the expense (selling in the bazaar, hawking your goods around town, whatever) that you are likely to triple or quadruple the amount of time it takes--while not a mathematician, it seems to me that the money you save is in direct relation to the time you spend playing merchant. That said, I have to admit that I did not need to try that as I am part of a regular group (not a guild....yet) of dedicated (read: addicted) players who are supporting each other's attempts to master (and later Grandmaster) a skill. As a consequence, I made 1.5k between my own avatars and them borrowed a further 1k from my friends. With that combined 2.5k, I went from 43 in JC (previous desultory practice with whetver was handy, not a concerted effort) to 187 JC...and I still have 500p left. The main reason for this apparent defiance of the odds? RECYCLE! I never bothered to actually count, but I am fairly certain that I have spent that same 500p about 10 times now. IN actuality, it would not surprise me to discover that I have spent on the order of 15 - 20k...but it's the SAME original 2.5k I started with.

Loss of plat through failures: you bet, and it still makes me shudder. HOWEVER--if you take the time to enchant your own metals, assuming you can, it is possible to recoup (over time) better than 80% of your losses, and after 150 the failure rate is abysmally low...thank the gods. This, by the way, is from a human chanter with an int of 153.

Mind numbing amounts of time: Ick, nothing you can do about this unless you can really boost your own stats. Still, I managed those 140 skillups after 4 sessions of about 3 - 5 hours each, and while they were indeed mind-numbing they were anything but difficult. I parked my chanter midway between the vendors in Qeynos, and except for standing to cast the enchant metal spells I didn't have to move in order to get everything I needed--I didn't even have to stand up to buy and sell. I should also point out that my method involved buying/enchanting lots of metal bars, but buying the gems in stacks of 5. Yes, I bought a LOT of stacks of 5, but at later levels those gems become frighteningly expensive and my way has left me having very few leftovers...which are almost always used by the next round of trivials when a new metal is achieved.

Now, having said all of that, there is one very important thing I want to mention to all you would-be JC masters: SAVE YOUR GEMS. Part of the reason this has only cost me 2.5k so far is that, having long ago decided that I was going to do the JC thing, I kept every single gem that I found that had ANY relevance to JC (lists of relevant gems can be found in several places; I recommend EQTraders, linked above). This means arranging with friends and pick-up groups both that any gems found are your part of the loot, or should at least be rolled for. By following this policy, I went into that shop in Qeynos already having full or nearly full stacks of 75% of the gems I was going to need. When it game time to start buying jade, emerald, and (shudder) peridots this was an immense help. I still think that I could have done it with the same amount of money, but the extra time spent recycling might have driven me nuts.

So, there's my 2cp worth. After less than 3k spent and 4 days of moderately intense effort, my chanter is half a level away from enchanting plat, has a full gem cest, and is 187 in JC. If any of that sounds attractive to you, go for it--as with all questions involving the addictive time-sink we call a game, I finish up by saying persevere--it CAN be done.
