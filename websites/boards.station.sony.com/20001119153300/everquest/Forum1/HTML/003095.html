The Voodoo 5 is indeed a great board. Depending on your system it may be your best bet. Something to note however is that EQ's Direct3D implementation is currently undergoing an overhaul. I've tried out some of the new upgrades on Test, and the look and performance on a Geforce 2 is now better than the V5 (sharper and cleaner mipmapping with very steady framerates).

If you have a Celeron, P2, or P3 and a motherboard with an Intel BX or later chipset and an AGP slot then go for a card based on the Geforce 2 GTS. If you don't want to spend $250 to $350 then go for a budget card based on the Geforce 2 MX.

If you have an Athlon or K6-2 based system, get the Voodoo 5. The performance will still be strong and the Voodoo has generally better compatibility, especially with non-Intel products. (/nod CoffinDancer) If you're budget minded here, you can pick up a Voodoo 4.

If you have an older system, or a name-brand system that lacks an AGP slot, get the PCI flavor of the Voodoo 5 (or 4 if it's available).

Below is my quick rundown between the two (both cards are in my household). Note that some of the differences are driver-based and could change in the future one way or the other:

The Voodoo 5's advantages are Glide support(still a biggie), superior full scene antialiasing(2x2 is the only truly usable setting on either card and 3dfx's implementation looks better), broader hardware compatibility, and better looking dynamically compressed textures(Quake 3 is the signature example).

The Geforce 2's advantages are superior performance(fill rate and T&L), better 16 bit visuals involving multitexture or transparency (3dfx's hi color dithering pattern drives me up the wall), and better mipmapping (which EQ will soon exploit).

Each also sports a few features the other lacks(certain per-pixel effects, depth and motion blurring, etc), but these tend to be mere showpieces for tech demos and aren't likely to see a lot of use.

My preference is for the Geforce 2, but you really can't go far wrong with either choice. ATI's Radeon is supposedly a worthy contender, but I haven't seen it in action, and ATI's history is still a little shaky. I'll put more faith in them once they have a product that stands the test of time.

Congratulations if you read this far, I hope you enjoy EQ.

A Voodoo 4 is the same as a Voodoo 5, but with only a single VSA-100 chip and attendant bank of memory. Thus it has no accumulation buffer effects and only half the fill rate.

Distinctly unlike the V3, the V4 does support 32 bit color, useful texture compression, etc.
