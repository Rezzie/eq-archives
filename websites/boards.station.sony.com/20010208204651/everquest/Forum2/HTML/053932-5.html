This exception states that you can sell the copy you bought as "the owner of a particular copy ... lawfully made under this title"... "without the authority of the copyright owner..."

Here's my take on where I see account transfers being legal. See if these steps make sense...

1.) I buy the physical game Everquest.
2.) I now own that copy of Everquest.
3.) I log onto the servers and pay a lease to use those servers. I do not own the servers and therefore do not have any expressly given right by copyright law to sell the information on those servers (still a question if Verant has the right to prevent that sale.)
4.) I do have the right as directly stated in the copyright law to sell MY COPY of EverQuest.
5.) In order for me to sell MY COPY it must be in working order.
6.) In order for MY COPY of EverQuest to work the buyer must have access to the servers.
7.) Therefore, it is CLEARLY stated in the US Copyright laws that I am allowed to sell MY COPY of the game which would include access to the servers.

My Conclusion: It is perfectly legal on my part to sell my physical copy of Everquest AND to transfer my login and passwords. To prevent this sale is a violation of the US Copyright laws as they protect the consumer of a particular Copyright. This means that if I were to delete all of my character information and sell only my physical copy of EQ AND my access to the servers it would be illegal for Verant/Sony to prevent this.

As far as the information that is stored on their servers that is still something I cannot find a solid legal argument for or against.

Two mistakes, Wahoo.

"5.) In order for me to sell MY COPY it must be in working order."

Nope. I can sell a car without an engine. I can sell a record that has been broken in half. I can sell a MacIntosh that no longer works. People will buy for parts, to fix it up, as a collectible, or for no reason at all. This is not a valid requirement.

"6.) In order for MY COPY of EverQuest to work the buyer must have access to the servers."

Just told you, it doesn't have to work. The buyer may not want it if it doesn't work, but the law does not require the MANUFACTURER to guarantee it works when you sell it.

I can sell you some old online games that the manufacturer no longer exists: bankrupt.

I can sell you AIX operating system 3.0 for which IBM will no longer support it.

I can also sell you AOL and you won't get a working account with it. You will have to sign up for your own account. You don't get someone else's for free. If you don't sign up the AOL software doesn't work. (As much as EQ doesn't work.)

If you sell your EQ program:

- It doesn't have to work to sell it. The law doesn't care if you took a match to the underside of the CD.
- Verant doesn't have to honor the new owner. They didn't sell it to them and did not give you reseller rights. (Most software distributers must have a reseller agreement. Check the shrinkwrap agreement.)
- Verant can require you to start your own account.
