Here's an idea. Make failures not destroy EVERYTHING, or have a CHANCE of not destroying everything.

Example: Tailoring. You failed to make the silk wristband because you tore the pattern pinning it to the silk. You throw out the pattern and keep the silk to try again.

Example: Smithing. The water is always consumed. Forged 2 handed sword takes water, sheet of metal, pommel mold, hilt mold, heavy steel blade mold. The failure is because you broke one or more of the molds. You get at least the metal sheet back, perhaps as an "unforged sheet of metal", and up to 2 of the molds. An unforged sheet needs to be reforged with a flask of water, turning it back into a sheet of metal. Making bricks, blocks, etc. of ore, you lose one or more of the components, but not all.

The "Folded Sheets" should be able to be upgraded by forging (ex.) 1 medium quality folded sheet, charcoal and water -> High quality folded sheet. A failure gives a low-quality folded sheet, which can be upgraded to medium by the same route. A failure on the low-quality would end up with just a regular sheet, which cannot be upgraded.

Baking: Screw up the sandwich, you lose the bread OR the meat: Either you screwed up slicing the bread, or dropped the meat in the coals.

Containers should NEVER be consumed by a failure, EXCEPT if that is the only item consumed. Ex: poison: you dropped the vial while trying to open the container of the suspension.

Jewelcraft: You screw up the bar of metal trying to form it into shape, creating a "lump of <insert metal here>" (non-stacking), and you get the gem back. This is combined in a forge with a (consumable) "bar mold" to re-form the bar. Perhaps this can dispel the enchantment (at least you get the bar back to be re-enchanted). Or, you shatter the gem setting the points. In this case, you get "an enchanted silver ring" (no stats, cannot be worn), for example. Combining this with one of the gems that WOULD have made a ring results in the correct ring (success), the unfinished ring and the gem is shattered again (failure), or the lump of metal that must be re-formed into a bar (critical failure). Maybe the bar mold can be a craft item as well, using a small block of clay and any bar of metal, combined in a pottery wheel. This cannot be failed, and you get the metal bar and the bar mold back. This does not need to be fired.

I have several issues with trade skills.

----------
1) There are not enough items that are valuable to the gaming community. Too many of the items produced by crafts are inferior to those found on creatures, or have absolutely no use at all.

Why do things like Small Bowls exist? Has anyone ever bought a forged weapon? What about Reinforced leather? Why make Studded leather if it is exactly equal to Raw-hide armor that drops? Do you own any pieces of Electrum Jewelry? These design oversights tend to undermine the players motivation to do crafts.

----------
2) The cost of producing items is too extreme. The cost of creating usable goods is prohibative, even in Jewelry.

Granted, Verant didn't want every PC to have a crafted Yak at 3rd level, but why do Yaks (and Ghoulbanes and Thulian Claws, etc.) continue to drop? Look at some of the twinks in a newbie zone these days.

----------
3) The destruction-on-failure mechanism is very unrealistic, and serves to artificially inflate the cost of items. Players often fail to take this into account when selling the useful items they make.

For example, I have kept pretty accurate records on my expenses for Jewelcraft. With a current skill of 190, I am just over 6,000 pp in the hole. I am a very active salesman, and do good business. However, I doubt I will ever make up this loss. In real life, this kind of expense without return would cause a business to fail.

----------
4) Verant has not seen a reason to release the exact statistics affecting crafting skills. This leads to unneeded controversy within the player base. Verant's reason to withhold this information: to keep the mystery in the game.

Shouldn't the mystery in an RPG come from the story line and world events, rather than the mechanics of the trade system?

----------
5) The time invested in the skills is not very realistic or satisfying. The trade skills are click-fests. It should be possible to mass produce many items, especially in baking and brewing.

In a game that requires as much bandwidth as EQ, the network traffic generated by crafting skills should have more value to both the gaming community and to Verant. Otherwise, this is a waste of bandwidth/money.

----------
6) Crafted weapons and armor are inferior to quested or dropped items at the same cost range. I just made a Seafarer's Cutlass -- a very nice 7 damage, 23 delay 1-hand slashing weapon.

If I were to try to sell this to recoup my investment, I would have to ask a minimum of 25pp (not including cost of failures). A more realistic asking price (given my known costs of a known set of failures) would be about 65pp. For this same amount or less, I can purchase a wide variety of equivilent Magical weapons.

----------
7) Sales outlets are too time consuming. Since the only viable mechanism for selling to other players is via the /auction channel, this requires a large investment in the player's time to sell an item. An alternative method for selling to other players is needed.

A pawn-shop type system would work well. The players could place ONE item in an NPC's shop and set an asking price. The player would recieve the money, if and when his item sold. The NPC would keep 10% of the sales price. This would allow players to sell to others without the enornomous waste of time required to sell something.

----------
These are a few complaints about the existing trade system. There is not likely to be a remedy within the current incarnation of EQ.

Such changes would be a waste of time on Verant's part because the player economy is already so far out of balance. The only way to fix this would be a complete item wipe of all players, re-itemizing each and every zone, rebuilding the NPC system, etc. The player base would not stand for the item wipe. So, craftsmen must wait for another game including EQ2 and its competitors for any hope of salvation.
