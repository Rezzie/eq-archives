>>For example, the 10 things you have to do could be made so that they have to be done in a certain order, not just randomly do part 8, then part 4, then part 7 etc...

Doesn't change it though. You still end up with an assembly line. A goes to camp B. You now get a new A. B moves on to C. A moves to B, new A. The max that the quest could be completed is the spawn time of its longest component. With an item like J-boots, if it was 20 1-hour camps, everyone would do it. Everyone. Because the time commitment wouldn't be there to discourage.

Is it fair to casuals? Not really. Are there solutions? Sure, but all have problems of their own.

Having the AC spawn anywhere (as in any zone) is one. However, this does remove the "questing" from the quest. Instead of you doing a quest, you now rely upon luck. Basically, instead of going out to get the parts so you can complete the quest, you get the parts and then decide to complete the quest to get the loot. It's a subtle difference, but there. Think about the warrior that goes to permafrost to get his crafted... as opposed to a ranger that happens to be hunting in perma that gets the quest comp and just decides to complete it. The one is a goal, the goal of the quest. The other is just something that happens. Perfectly acceptable for some items, but not so for others.

Having the results be subpar (compared to the best item of that type) is another. If you can get an item that is better with some camping, that is what those with bigger time commitments will go for. This works for quite a few quests, like langseax and short sword of morin. Both are weapons regularly weilded by more casual warriors/rangers.

A third option is to offer both a camp and a quest. For instance, if you could camp the SMR or get lucky with 5 random drop items from 5 different zones... well, then the casual player could eventually end up with an SMR just due to hunting in those zones.

But when they want to limit the number of items that enter the game, it'll be reduced down to a simple thing like the AC. 1 element is easier to control. And as for casuals not having a shot, considering the SRO AC is considered free-for-all, you've got the exact same chance as anyone else in the zone. Just because you have to do it 2 hours per night as opposed to them doing it in 8 hour stints, the end chance is pretty much the same.

There is SOME way to do these things so that BOTH camps are happy, one group because items are only getting into the game at a certain rate, and the other group, because the time chunks required are small enough to be accomplished by a casual player.

What I think needs to be changed first is the mentality that an item should only enter the game at a certain rate. Say, 5 of the items a day, and instead, the focus should be on how long a player spends on a quest, 20 hours, 25 hours, etc...

You might end up with an assembly line, but so what? Its still 20 hours (or however long) of that persons time to do the quest.

Total randomizing isn't the answer because (as someone else pointed out) a quest involves: Finding monster A, defeating Monster A, getting item B from Monster A (which may be the prize)/or returning item B to NPC C to gain the prize.

For "roleplaying" purposes, Quests and quest monsters need to be there.

However, the need to "limit" how often something comes into the world is a poor design that punishes the players for playing.

This broken philosophy is what has led to "rare" spawns that "rarely" drop the "rare" item needed to complete a quest.

The Glacier Bear, or Grimrot, Bilge Farfathom, for instance are all symptoms of this incredibly broken notion.

Let me remake the JBoots "quest," in order to make it a (1) "workable" quest that rewards the player for undertaking the quest, (2)allows the player to make progress on the quest in his limited amount of "casual player" hours, (3) reduces Twinking and (4) negates camping.

Hasten Bootstrutter, who roams around Rathe Mountains gives the quest information. He says that he needs 2 items to make the boots. (1) 3250 gold pieces (2) Magically endowed leather. To help, he gives an "Idol of Wolves."

Now, remember the boneheaded Vox/Naggy cap? No one over a certain level can attack now? That means the means is in place to check player level. Hasten, when asked about the boots, says: "The Journeyman boots are only for those brave enough to travel the land and obtain the items. If ye do not have yer surname, you be not able to wear the boots!"

If they already have boots (or the Idol, or the Essence of Wolf), then he says "Bah! Ye have a pair! Be not wasting my time!" And he does NOT give them his assistance, or the Idol.

I didn't get my Jboots from Drelzna until I was 29th level. So a 20th level minimum on obtaining the Jboots is not bad. Whatever the minimum might be is irrelevant. The point is that by building such a restriction -- twinking is eliminated. By refusing to help them if they *have* boots, the Idol or the Essence, means that they can't get a powerful person to go get the items for them, nor can that person go get them for one of his alts. Again -- twinking is defeated.

To make the magic leather, the player must go to a magician guild and buy a "Combine Kit", he then puts in the kit (1)A Shadowed Rapier (2)The Ring of the Ancients (3)A High Quality Lion Pelt (4)an Essence of Wolf Potion.

The Combine Kit, key, and all the items needed are, of course, Lore and No-Drop. The Magically Endowed Leather is too. This means (1)no twinking (2) the character *must be on hand during the battle* to get the items.

Here are some ways, when hunting for the items, to have the items always be available, without stupid camping or ultra-rare spawn / ultra-rare drops.

(1) Shadow Rapier. This is mini-camp, just like now. But, you see, there are about a dozen Shadowmen camps throughout Norrath. So you spend some time there and eventually you'll get your rapier. Of course, the problem with it being no-rent etc., remains, but that's ok

(2) High Quality Lion Pelt. Well, this comes from lions in the Karanas. About level 20 is when people start exploring in Karanas, so that's ok. A little nerve-wracking, but not too bad. And it introduces the young character to the Aviak house and Paw. The lions roam around and are constantly being spawned. Since the lions are constant, it's OK to have the High Quality be a semi-rare drop. Few players will complain that they have to run around hunting, chasing, killing monsters if they know that eventually they'll get what they need. We *DO* complain when we sit on our asses for 45 minutes at one spot...kill a stupid placeholder...sit for 45 minutes...kill a placeholder...sit for 45 minutes...kill the mob but find something OTHER than what we're after...sit for 45 minutes...kill a placeholder.

(3) Essense of Wolf. This comes from the wolf pit in Najena. The player must fight his way to the pit which is in the back where the goblins and stuff are. There will be many battles with the elementals, guards, goblins, and spiders as he works his way to the spot. Most likely, he'll need a party to help him---but if he's high enough *HE ISN'T FORCED INTO A PARTY* by the Vision (tm). The party descends into "The Pit" where they are confronted with an altar. Using the Idol, that they click on the door which teleports them (much like the TP pads in Sky or Howling Stones to a "mini dungeon" within the same zone. Again, because they must have the Idol to get in, and because it's a Teleport and not a door, no high level can come in and help or KS them.

Here, they may have to defeat a few more monsters (or not). When they are finished, the see a statue or gargoyle (neutral faction...it's merely a prop NPC). They give him the Idol, he gives them the Essence of Wolf.

There is a teleport pad (ala Howling Stones) that they step out to be teleported out of The Pit or Najena entirely. Once someone steps on the pad, however, the room instantly repops. So if a group is working together, they'd better all have their Essence of Wolf and they'd all better hit the pad and get out....or fight the wolves again.

Of course, the Idol is "soul-bound" like the keys to Sebilis or Charsis, so if they do die, they're not locked away from their bodies. Because they gave the Idol to the gargoyle -- *THEY CAN'T GO BACK for potential twinking.

Furthermore, because the room repops when they leave means that someone WILL have to fight again to get to the Gargoyle. The room can't be "held" clear because as soon as someone leaves, the room repops. There will be fighting going on. Most likely a group "on deck" will set up lines of communication with a group "going in." When the first group zones out, they'll likely be asked to send a TELL letting the 2nd group know. The 2nd group TP's in..and the fight is on!

The beauty of this, you see, is that they don't sit down and wait for something to appear, only to be disappointed. They fight to the spot, they fight the guards -- THEN THEY GET THE PRIZE AND MOVE ON!

(4)Ring of the Ancients. What a great idea to have it come from the Ancient Cyclops. Really it was. What a great idea to have the Cyclops spawn in different zones. Really it was. What *STUPID* idea to make people have to wait 20+ hours to hope to see him! Really, it was. Solution? Look above. Either put him somewhere and make him always there and always with the Ring--but guarded(like the gargoyle for the Essence), make him always up with the Ring (even if in different zones), make him frequently up but only occasionally with the Ring (like lion hide)

Finally, it's all done. They've gone to the Mage guild and bought the Combine Kit. They've gathered all four items, they hit the Combine button and presto! They have Magical Leather. Now they go to the Bank, get their gold pieces.

Now the player lugs the new gold and the Magic Leather back to Rathe Mountains, hunts down Hasten (who is *always* up)...(unless you want him to say "Bother me not at night." and despawn him at night when you spawn the undead in Kithicor). Once they find him, he turns in the items to Hasten and...they get Journeyman boots!

Now it is possible that someone without boots might do all the quest stuff and turn in the Magic Leather while letting the other fellow just turn in gold. This is not *great* but it's ok. Most likely, however, this won't happen because (a) the high level probably has boots of his own..so can't and (b)the quest is a LOT OF WORK to do for a reward that you're just going to give to someone else.


But look at what has been accomplished. The quest is long and not exactly easy. All the components are available at all times...just not simple to get in hand. Twinking is gone. EverCamping is gone. More importantly, the "casual" player, who only has an hour or two a night can log on and *MAKE PROGRESS* toward the goal. He may not get the entire thing from start to finish. He may not get half of them. He may not even get ONE of them. but he can end his session having made some progress in the quest instead of another "EverCamp the NeverSpawn" that is the norm now. Some things he can do solo..some things he does with a group. Because the quest is so long the Boots won't "flood" the world....but if they do *SO WHAT*? It's one set of boots per peson and not transferrable.

Want another example? Here's how you can put decent Class-specific armor in the game, still prevent twinking, camping, waiting.

Since I play a Cleric, this will be done from his perspective. With a bit of imagination (a challenge on your end, I know), it can be done similarly for all classes.

By battling, etc., etc. we finally come up with a set of bronze armor in our single-digit levels. As we hit our teens, we're given "quests" by our guilds. If we bring a bronze armor item, a regular iron weapon (not rusty and not Fine Steel), and Cistern Asp skin, it can be crafted into ... Steel Armor! Better AC for our upcoming battles. (Verant..Clue: New armor should prepare us and offer protection for our "upcoming" fights...not finally be adequate for fights we did 10 levels ago!)

So we have our Steel armor. When we hit 20 or so (but not before...our guild master deems us "unproven" yet) We can turn in our Steel item, a Fine Steel weapon, and an Aviak Charm and we can get "Fine Steel" Armor. Better AC..maybe a stat boost!

About 30's we're told we can trade in our Fine Steel, a number of Rubies, and "a certain item" and we can get "Rubicite Armor". The "certain item" would vary depending on the armor item and is what gives each item a particular boost. A beholder lens, for instance, with a ruby and a helm, would give a Rubicite Helm with a WIS bonus. etc., etc.

At 40, we are sent to the Temple of Ro. There we learn that by collecting the Icons and Idols, and by turning them in *with* the right Rubicite piece, we get the appropriate Gold armor..with BETTER STATS THAT WHAT CAME BEFORE!!

At 46, we can hit the planes. There, instead of getting the armor like we do now, we get a "Gem of Innoruuk" or some such. By combining them with our Gold Armor, we get the corresponding Etheral Mist piece WITH BETTER STATS THAT WHAT CAME BEFORE!!

We hit 50's and it's time for Donal's. (Donal's..great AC, but piss-poor stats. Fire whoever dreamed that up!). Again, by collecting the right items from the mobs, we can trade in our Etheral Mist for Donal's Armor of Mourning WITH BETTER STATS THAN WHAT CAME BEFORE!!

Post 50's: We take the Donal's and upgrade it again. Perhaps here, we even get to customize it a bit. By combining an armor item, the right mob drop, and a Star Ruby..we get red "Velious" armor. If we put in a Star Sapphire..we get blue armor. Fire Emeralds give a green piece. Black Sapphire gives Black..Topaz gives Gold...etc., etc. All the same stats, but we actually (gasp!) get a choice!

Of course, all the items are Lore, No-Drop. If you have an Etheral Mist Helm, you cannot get another Rubicite. You upgrade and that's it.

Now...look at what this creates. Ongoing quests. We now have something to work on while were IN a particular level range..and something to look forward to in our next range. Lore / No-Drop means no twinking. Level-Keyed means no 5th level Rangers in full Rubicite. No downgrading means no farming. Definite status symbol armor, as you CANNOT have the armor unless you've earned it. The "extra" items are always available, even if sometimes difficult to get to. And item "input" is regulated because when something comes *in* you know that something else is going *out* due to the concept of "upgrading."

------------------------------

Long post, I know. But there are your solutions. It uses mechanics that already exist in the game. Verant will have to do a bit of work to implement them (instead of the lazy "dragon level" approach). But when done properly you will have a game that works, not a game that *is* work.
