There's no real way that I can think of to disable twinking in the game other than to make all quest items no-drop or to try to enforce an anti-twink policy, and I'd prefer the guides and GMs spent their time helping players who needed it rather than enforcing something that doesn't adversely effect game play for most of us.

I dislike twinking -- I've had higher level characters pass down minor items to me (rings, etc.) for helping them with some minor task or combat, which works for me because it makes sense within the game, but I've turned down outright twinks from other well-meaning players. People handing down rubicite or lambent to low level characters is just pathetic -- I've been known to power level one or two bumps to the next spell group, but the idea of power leveling to 50 in as fast a time as possible is like reading the last chapter of a novel, first -- you're missing the point. The "wealthy parents" analogy sort of works for me, but ... personally, I think it would be embarrassing to run around with equipment that I hadn't earned in some regard.

But what to do with the equipment and cash of a high level character you no longer intend to play? If I had a high level character that I didn't want to play anymore, instead of twinking a low level friend or alt, I think I'd have a death-bed event for my guild or friends where I read my will and passed out my equipment and cash, maybe giving a few items to a trusted executor to pass down to a "younger relative" as an inheritance. Makes more sense in the game world, and works for alts that aren't the same class. You could also bequeath armor and items to guild members or friends, or donate them to the guild for quest events.

Personally, the most enjoyment I think would be possible to get out of EQ would be from player-, guild-, or GM-led events (sadly, too few of these last on Povar). The machinations of the game make the built-in quests a little weaker than they could be (anyone who's seen a dozen or more people crowding around the same area in a dungeon knows what I mean), so in this respect EQ is the perfect backdrop for PC-led events. The idea of passing on loot from a stale character this way could make for a really unique event ... almost makes me wish I had a high level character I didn't want to play anymore.

Someone brought up MUDS as an example of where "twinking" was considered "cheating". Having run a MUD for 4 years I can tell you why there are very distinct differences between EQ Twinking, and MUD Cheating.

First, and most obvious, is size. My MUD had anywhere from 70 (off-peak) to the maximum of 200 during peak. We had anywhere from 5 to 10 "GMs" on at one time. And since only a very small percentage of people were ever really a problem, we had ample coverage. EverQuest, is a different ballgame altogether. On Mith Marr, it's 600 too 2600 players, probably 5 to 10 Guides and/or GMs. It would be VERY difficult to monitor 2600 people with only 10 people.

Second, MUDs are highly impacted by small changes. In other words, SoandsoA gets this uber equipment, and passes it to SoandsoB. SoandsoB goes around killing other characters to loot. He has a huge impact on game play because he alone can affect such a small population to a great extent. On EverQuest, if someone "twinks" a character, he's a very very small face in a massive crowd. And because of certain implementation details, the only REAL advantage he has is appearance. He "looks" better than the other players.

Third, most people that twink other characters are not doing so just to play barbie doll. They usually have a desire to play that character. Mules are a different story. Mules, in fact, are the #1 reason why "twinking" was not allowed on my MUD. They stored the limited drop equipment so no-one else could get it and overpower them. Not all MUDs had limited drop equipment, most were probably like EQ. Mules have very little impact on the game... The equipment they have will still be dropped, if the equipment has been removed from the game, then there isn't enough of it on any Mule (or mules) to have a huge impact on the game.

Fourth, and probably most obvious, is the business aspect. I've worked my way through 54 levels. I started with a newbie robe, a newbie dagger, and my free food just like everyone else. My desire not to repeat that is understandable to most that have worked through 54 levels. This is what keeps me coming back. I can start new characters and have them enjoy the blessings of the work I put into my main... the fruit of his labor, so to speak.

I don't "overtwink", probably because it's pretty useless to put in that much effort with all of the level based tuning the EverQuest staff has done. A level 5 warrior with an executioner's axe isn't going to deal very much more damage that a level 5 warrior with a fine-steel two-handed sword. So, I didn't bother equipping my warrior with such things. I couldn't solo things that the non-twinks couldn't, no advantage there either.

My caster twinks, well, they have a slight edge in that they probably have a bit more mana than a comparably leveled caster. But, I'll tell you it's hardly noticeable, except for the downtime I have waiting to get full mana. The intelligence bonuses at low-levels is neglegible.

What I do oppose, however, is an uber-guild getting a newly created character in their guild up to 50 inside of 2 weeks. But, banning twinking isn't going to stop this. The equipment isn't what makes the character, it's more often who he has to help him, or what other out of game advantages he has (i.e. a level 54 druid on a separate account to power-level his characters on another account). If you had a level 54 druid to babysit you, you could go to your mid 40's completely naked, save for a rusty weapon.

Bottom line, it isn't hurting a game of this scale.
