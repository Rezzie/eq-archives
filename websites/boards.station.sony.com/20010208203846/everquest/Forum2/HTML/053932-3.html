This is largely a cut&paste from another post of mine, but I don't feel like rehashing the first part. It's in reference to EULA's and shrink-wrap license agreements.

http://www.avault.com/articles/getarticle.asp?name=eulapt2

Adrenaline Vault article about a Washington Supreme court decision:

http://caselaw.lp.findlaw.com/scripts/getcase.pl?court=wa&vol=677964maj&invol=1

...in which the court ruled in favor of the software company because the construction company knew of the terms of the EULA. Much like EQ players being well aware of Verant/Sony's terms.
http://www.law.emory.edu/7circuit/june96/96-1139.html

1996 court case of ProCD v. Zeidenberg. Software company sued Zeidenberg for using a database which they owned (much like verant owning everything on or about your character), for his own "for pay" website. Again, much like ebayers selling software, bit and bytes of info, that they do not own, for their own profit.

Precident has been set.

These precedents are upheld by article 2-606 (of the UCC) which defines acceptance:

http://www.law.cornell.edu/ucc/2/2-606.html

(1) Acceptance of goods occurs when the buyer

(a) after a reasonable opportunity to inspect the goods signifies to the seller that the goods are conforming or that he will take or retain them in spite of their non-conformity; or

(b) fails to make an effective rejection (subsection (1) of Section 2-602), but such acceptance does not occur until the buyer has had a reasonable opportunity to inspect them; or

(c) does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership; but if such act is wrongful as against the seller it is an acceptance only if ratified by him.

You could claim "I didn't know!" but courts don't take ignorance as an excuse. (As per the Washington case mentioned above.)
