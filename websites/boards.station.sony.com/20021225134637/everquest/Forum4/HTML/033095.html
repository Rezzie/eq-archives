Daraquells, I wasn't going to respond, but I read your post about how your parents feel you are not living up to your potential, and it brought back memories of my schooling.

quote:

You are absolutely right. I am a highly eccentric, intelligent-but-doesn't-live-up-to-my-potential 14-year-old girl.

I'm sure you've heard it before, but I just want to caution you to be careful who you let know that you are a young girl. The less information you give out the better. After all, better safe than sorry.

quote:

Oh, and I found out why my parents had initially been so harsh. For the past 3 years, I started out with a good year. As the year progressed, I began to slack off a bit. I started with a few minor things, but eventually ended in an unretrievable poor grade. My parents thought with a bit of "shock therapy" early on, that they could prevent that from happening this year, because it now counts for college.

I drove my parents to distraction in grade school and high school. It was pretty obvious that I was intelligent enough to excel, yet my grades often suffered because I failed to turn in homework (sound familiar?). I always did well on my tests, so I just kinda of blew off the homework as pointless.

What I didn't realize, was that doing homework serves multiple purposes. For some students, it offers a chance to reinforce the instructor's lecture material. However even if you don't need the reinforcement, homework serves one *critical* purpose: it serves to give students the good work habits necessary to succeed in college.

I breezed through high school, doing little homework, aceing my tests. I thought I was invincible. I was a genius, there was no subject that could challenge my intellect. Boy was I wrong. I found out that once I got to college, I was actually going to have to *work* to pass my classes!

This was really hammered home when at the end of my first year, my GPA was only a 2.45, and I was in danger of losing my National Merit scholarship. Being put on academic probation was my shock therapy.

After that, the pressure was on. I had a second chance, but if I blew it, I was done (stick a fork in me). So after that, I worked diligently. Unfortunately, your GPA becomes increasingly difficult to raise as your college career raises. Worse, classes become *MUCH* more challenging after your freshman year. It took me until my last semester of my senior year to finally get my cumulative GPA above a 3.0.

In the end though, it was *WORTH* it. I keept my scholarship, and graduated college with a BS in Computer Science, and *NO DEBT*. Most of my friends that graduated with me, have $20000-$25000 in debt. If I'd not busted *** after my freshman year, I would have lost my scholarship, and ended up in debt, if I graduated at all.

quote:

I must say, that sheads a whole new light on things. I find that I can no longer feel angry at the mere thought of them. They were doing what they thought to be best, trying to protect me from past, repetitive mistakes that ultimately leads to a poor end-of-year report.

You should hug your parents. They obviously care a great deal for you. If anything, this should prove to you that your parents love you. Take strength from that.

I don't know if you are a US American, but if you are, I have some advice for you: Make sure you take the PSAT your sophomore year, and do well!

The PSAT is also the National Merit Scholarship Qualifying Test. If you score high enough, you'll become a semi-finalist.

Once you become a semifinalist, you'll have to write an essay, and take the SAT. If you score well on the SAT, and your essay is well-written you should become a finalist.

You'll have to pick a first-choice college, who will be notified of your finalist status. Many colleges will give you a scholarship just for becoming a finalist.

Get your guidance counselor to help you. They'll know what forms you have to fill out etc.

If you are are also living in Missouri, you should be aware of the "Bright Flight" scholarship. I don't know what the current requirements are, but when I was in high school a 30 or higher on the ACT was the minimum to get the scholarship. Bright Flight is a scholarship that pays $2000 a year ($1000 per semester) for up to 5 years, provided that you remain a student "in good standing" at your college.

Finally, I would encourage you to explore your creative abilities. Writing stories is a wonderful way to focus your mind and have fun. The Harpy's Head Tavern message board on this site is the perfect forum if you wish to share with us.

Take care, and enjoy your time in Everquest.
