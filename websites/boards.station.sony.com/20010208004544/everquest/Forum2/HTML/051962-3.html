After a week of new experience system, and much consideration on the weekend about this thread (pointed to by Caster's Realm, which is the only way I'd still remember it), I have the following comments:

1. I think I understand the original poster's point. The experience system fix is because Verant realizes at a big picture level that the classes are MOSTLY balanced at high levels. If the outcome is considered roughly equal, then the cost to get there probably deserves to be equal too. The only other experience system worth considering would cost more when the relative benefit is higher but still equal out in the end. That means that warriors and rogues would go faster until about 40th or so and then hybrids would go faster (they start falling behind in melee abilities and to a certain extent in battle contribution due to the caps on weapons, offense skills, and defense skills.)

2. The poster believes that referring to this general change as an 'improvement' for hybrids is roughly equivalent to removing one of several major obstacles to equality and declaring the problem fixed. Imagine if we just said that 'separate but equal' in schools was wrong but allowed blatant discrimination in the work place. The problem would not be perceived as 'fixed' just because you addressed one aspect of it.

3. There is a begrudging acceptance by the paladins and other hybrids that the new system is fair to 'up and comers'. It is hard to accept that the additional experience in their 'exp bucket' is somehow not earned and somehow not worthy of the reward of additional levels. Most realize the disruption to the game system and many realize that the exp in that bucket was often taken from other party members, but it still feels like 'theirs'.

4. Although the classes are MOSTLY balanced, Verant's vision for the ATK and AC stats unbalances warriors, rogues, and monks by allowing them too much melee damage v. their hybrid brethren. Verant needs to demonstrate to itself that relatively small differences in skills make too huge a difference in combat. It makes since that a warrior does, say, 25% more damage than a paladin, but it doesn't make sense for a warrior to do 100% or 200% as much damage. By the same token, it makes sense for the rogue to do 25-50% more damage than a wizard (manaless DOT with somewhat higher risk of being IN melee), but not 200% or 500% or (as some believe is the case) 1000% more damage. That imbalance has pushed Verant to make some Kunark and some Velious monsters EVEN MORE powerful, and therefore unbalances casters and hybrids even further.

I've been banging this drum for a year and more but what Verant REALLY needs to do is build static models of various player strategies for each class v. all levels of monsters. These need to be coded up so that Verant can SEE the effect of additions like manastones and moss-covered twigs on downtime and on combat efficiency. It is becoming increasingly apparent that Verant is depending on the user base to tell them how things 'really work' instead of being pro-active and KNOWING how they really work. If analytical tools are being used today, they either are weak or are not sufficiently thorough to detect problems before they become complaints.

Another way of saying this is that you guys can run analysis logs too. And given that you can 'pop' a monster in anytime you want, you can capture results either in the server or in a separate model and log-analyze the results. The players aren't lying when they say that stats seem 'subtle' and that rogues do a LOT more apparent damage than wizards, especially at high levels. These things present a sense of imbalance that is more overwhelming than any mechanisms that are in place to manage that imbalance (such as the stamina bar having relatively little impact on combat v. the mana bar--heaven help us if you make us throw that invigor spell even more often, though.)

In my opinion and many others the experience penalty removal was indeed an enhancement, but, and there is a big BUT...the experience penalty removal was NOT in anyway more beneficial to the hybrid community.

First, there were an abundance of classes that had a penalty of some sort. One of those was the INT casters. They had a 10% exp penalty.

Second, they also had classes that had experience bonuses.

Third, they are penalizing the hybrid classes and bard and monk with greater death penalties with the new system. The troll SK has the greatest death penalty and this class doesn't necessarily benefit that much overall from the exp penalty removal.

The experience penalty went hand in hand with the need to have the hybrid classes have 40% more to gain a level then those that weren't getting the penalty. As a result, even though the hybrid was taking more experience from the group with the penalty in place, they needed 40% more to level. On top of that the penalty was setup in such a way that the hybrid didn't receive 40% more, so they actually levelled slower then the other classes. It's all in the math. 12% gain due to the exp penalty and 40% more needed to level to next level then the non-penalty classes.

Upon removal, the hybrids received 40% exp tacked onto the end of their already given experience for each kill. But, and the big BUT here is that the hybrids still need 40% more to gain the next level.

So, in reality the gain is 0. The hybrids get 40% more and need 40% more.

The bonuses they get are ONLY from the ZEM and from the group bonuses. Nothing more. The end result is that they get little additional experience due to the removal of the penalty due only to the fact that they require 40% more to level.

The non-penalty classes were receiving 12% less experience. There is a 12% (this is my estimate) average of exp taken by a hybrid during any kill. So, adding multiple hybrids to the group and you can see that the non-penalty classes were getting hit hard with multiple hybrids in the group.

So, if you add the exp sucked up by the hybrids during the kill and you had many hybrids the exp drain could have been dramatic. So, removing the hybrid penalties gives that much back to the non-penalty casters.

In the case of the INT casters, they had a 10% exp penalty.

The monk had 20% and the bard had 40%.

As you can see, the removal of the exp penalty did not, in anyway benefit the hybrids more then anyone else.

If we look at it from the perspective of the new system, the non-penalty classes get a percentage more experience then they did, plus they get the additional bonuses for ZEM and for the group. Removing the exp penalty gives them more experience overall without having the additional needed experience to level from the experience tables.

In other words, they didn't have the experience penalty, thus they didn't have to have more exp to level to the next level, and thus their experience gain is pure and unadulterated.

The experience penalty the hybrids had meant that they had to gain 40% more. In a solo situation the hybrids had the full 40% experience penalty and they had the 40% more to gain to the next level. All this did was remove the 40% experience penalty, and with the experience system change they gain 40% more but still have the 40% difference for each level thus negating the penalty, giving them a net of 0.

So the non-penalty classes get a bonus on top of the ZEM and group due to the removal of the penalty and they can play and gain exp as if they never played with a hybrid before. But the hybrids are gaining 0 extra except for the ZEM and group bonuses.

The hybrid also receives a death penalty. This penalty requires that they loose 40% more experience upon a death then a non-penalty class. Verant saw 40% as a large number so they shaved 10% off that making it only 30%. This isn't a big problem except that if they don't get a rez then they loose 30% more then a non-penalty class would loose upon death.

So, the hybrids get only the ZEM and group bonus and receive a death penatly if they can't get a rez. This is hardly a greater benefit to the hybrids vs. the non-penalty classes.

um, it seems to me that the xp penalty was shared with the group equally before. The removal simply made _groups_ with hybrids DING faster. Which is a good thing for hybrids, because they are _always_ in a group with a hybrid, as opposed to non-hybrids who ain't. . .

Ok. You've piqued my curiosity. I'm gonna work out if there is any situation (ignoring death) in which a Hybrid could advance slower now than he did before.

Now, how about this situation:
Troll SK.
Baseline Cleric

in a group?

At L 2, Troll SK has 1680 xp to a cleric's 1000 xp.

Kill a 100 xp monster.
Before:
Troll earned 64 xp. 1.25 % of the xp to DING
Cleric earned 38 xp. 1.24 % of the xp to DING
(difference due to round-off error, really)
After:
Troll earned 71 xp. 1.4 % of the xp to DING
Cleric earned 51 xp. 1.67 % of the xp to DING

So, Trolls SKs DING faster in a group of two.

...

1 Troll SK
5 Halfling (Rogues or Warriors, the one with a .9 mult)

Halfling mult before was what, .855?

On 1200 xp, the group got 1296 xp.

Before:
At L 2, the total group xp was 5955
At L 2, the troll had 1680 xp

After:
The xp totals are the same, but the group earned 1440 xp, and the troll got 336.

Less xp.

Yes, there is a situation in which a Troll SK can earn _less_ xp after this patch than before.

Not a likely situation. And, if you are grouping with non-halflings and non-warrior/rogues, it won't happen. But it can happen.

I doubt this was intended. But it happened.

Similar things happen to a lesser extent in any large group with 1 racially penalized race in it. They are earning slightly more usually, but everyone else is earning _much_ more.

In small groups, not counting death, and for those grouping with hybrids, this has been a boon for the Hybrids. In large groups, counting death, all the xp patch did was make people stop avoiding the xp vampires.
