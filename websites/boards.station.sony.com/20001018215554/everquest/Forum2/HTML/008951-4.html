hmm, after re-reading my post above, it may be a bit extreme. My main point was that if the overall image of your CS by your customer base is poor, then regardless of how you feel you are doing or even may be doing, something is wrong. Whether the problem lies in your own perception of the quality of your CS offerings, or whether its the nature and frequency of your communications to your customers, or whatever else you think could help, it needs to be done, assuming you want an excellent rep in this area. A basic fundamental principle of CS is that people want to feel like they have received extra special attention and havent been ignored. In reality it doesnt take much to make this happen, just some extra time and energy exerted towards these efforts. The longer your quality is perceived to be substandard the harder it is to change peoples minds. If you are short staffed or dont have the manpower required for such efforts, you have to ask yourself whether it will be more profitable in the long run to bite the bullet and get more staff (or pay them more or for longer hours), raise the rates and deal with the flames from that, or sit and wait. I suspect that if you raised the rates say $2 a month and made sure to notify all the customers that it was to improve customer service, you would get far less grumbling and many who would applaud you. Assuming that the perceived (by the customer base) CS improves, this will have been a good thing. I humbly suggest that you consider what your players AKA customers are saying about the CS in-game (specifically guides/GMs) before other games as good as EQ get released to the market or could see a hit your customer base. IMHO, the complaints about GMs/guides should be the exception, not the rule. Instead we see just the opposite, and this post about how excellent a GM has performed is the exception, not the rule.
