Let me explain. This isn't just a Verant thing. In software testing and QA, the word "intended" means something very different from what normal human beings think it does. :->

When the programmers write the code and get enough of it to the point where they can hand it to QA for them to start testing, normally bug list is kept. It's mostly (sometimes exclusively) the QA testers who are allowed to add bugs to the list, update the status and description of bugs, or 'close' bugs, noting them as either fixed, or unfixable, or whatever.

The programmers are supposed to keep tabs on all the bugs in sections of the program they are responsible for.

The exact way this works varies from company to company and even project to project, but... If the programmer says a bug on the list may have an adverse impact on the user of the program, but they can't think of a reasonable way to get rid of that bug because it would ruin some other part of the program, most of us would call that bug 'an unintended consequence' of the design. In the software world, it's called 'intended'. Because we <b>intended</b> for the program to have these other features and for the features to work in this specific way and there is no way we are going to get rid of them, and it isn't our fault that there is the unavoidable consequence of XYZ. So, the bug list is updated, the bug is 'closed' and the reason for closing the bug is noted as "intended". Some companies call it "as designed" or whatever, but it's still the same thing.

Sometimes it isn't just that nothing can be done about the bug unless you mess around with features you don't want to mess around with. Sometimes, the features and the bug really are mutually exclusive, and there is no logical way whatsoever to fix the reported bug without hurting the features that management has determined must exist in the program. In the real world, more often it's a case of it just being too hard or time consuming, or it would require the software engineers and QA testers to tell management "the program design you gave us is stupid". Engineers and testers who tell management they have stupid ideas tend to not stay at the same company very long. Testers being considered more expendable than software engineers, if someone tells a QA tester that a bug is 'as intended' or 'as designed', most testers aren't going to argue and start a dangerous crusade.

I'm <b>not</b> trying to imply something cynical took place with this UI (User Interface) bug that Suicide20 and Zerion reported. Just filling in the rest of the big picture for people to use for future reference.

Another weird and important aspect of the big picture is that once a bug is determined to be 'as intended' on the bug list, that means it almost certainly won't get thought about when the next version of the program is being designed and developed. Yes, the designers and developers do start with a list of all the open, unfixed bugs from the previous version so they can try to fix things in the next version. But, the stuff that was closed obviously doesn't come up when you print the list of <b>open</b> bugs now, does it? The list of open bugs is already plenty of work to tackle on the next version, without starting to dig into the list of closed bugs. And thus, these bugs are institutionalized and probably remain through many future versions of the software.

As long as you don't get ulcers or something, it's pretty funny. And yes, even the software that runs the air traffic control system or runs space shuttle missions is developed this same way.
