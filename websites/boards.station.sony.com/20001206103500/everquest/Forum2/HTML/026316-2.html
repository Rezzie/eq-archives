I'm not sure of the database (or lack thereof) technology employed to track the guilds, but this does *not* need to a processor-intensive implementation; in fact, it is quite possible they can simply tack on another, very small box (say, a Linux box), which can access the data on the other servers for periodic updates to, say, a web-server.

I/O contention can be addressed by process prioritization. Disk access can also be addressed in the same manner. Memory? That's for the dedicated box to sweat.

If they're using an RDBMS, each has it's own database-sharing technology to permit acces by another box. If it's flat files (or similar), an NFS or similar implementation addresses that.

Further, this can be run, say, when the user count drops under 20% of maximum.

Will it be 100%? No. Updates that occur during the read would not make it, but how often will significant numbers of guild invites/removals be occurring when the server's mostly empty.

However, if this were performed, say, weekly, it would be better than what we currently have. One week for a box to cycle through the servers, read the data during low times, and ship the data to a website just isn't that difficult.

Condiering Verant is bringing in $3,000,000 per month, and the Operating costs are $1,500,000 (by recent reports originating from Sony), that's a lot of money which can be used for a one-time equipment cost (likely much less than $50,000) and a miniscule maintanance cost (say, worst-case, 1 FTE at $7500 per month) ...even after the money which goes into R&D and related areas.

It's just a shame that this topic has been black-holed. It's the guilds and other social aspects that, even by Verant's own acknowledgement, are the underpinnings of their game.

My (extended) 2cp's
