Buff ordering -- and by that I assume carefully arranging at least your first buffs -- is one of those things like the fire department: Most of the time you don't need it, but when you do, you REALLY do.

With the prevalance of NPC casters in the game increasing as you level and them appearing in great quantities in LDoN, the odds that someone will hit you with a dispel is relatively high. By having one or more "junk buffs" in your first slots, you are more likely to just lose those (most dispels still hit your first available slots, despite recent changes to make it more random) and even if you can't immediately repair them with an instant-buff item (which would still be a wise investment), you've protected the important ones for at least a little longer.

Really advanced people routinely dump one of these before going into combat, so that they can have magic-based effects cast on them dispelled without losing buffs as well, since the effect will just go into the first available slot.

It's certainly possible to set up this way AFTER it's happened to you, but why wait? It's easy to get into the habit of being prepared from the start, and it makes a huge difference.

My top three buffs are always Allegient Familiar (undispellable unless all slots are full), Frostreaver's Blessing (off Coldain ring 10) and Ultravision (from the Eye of Innoruuk). When hit with a dispel, I hit 6 and 8 on page 3 of my hotkeys, and instantly those two buffs reappear, and my KEI, Virtue, PotG and all the rest remain safe, even as my groupmates are suddenly finding themselves more vulnerable and less able to keep fighting.

It's definitely worth the time investment, and the sooner you get in the habit of doing it, the more benefit you get out of it.

Assist is a skill you will be expected to have learned at least by the time you are 20.

It is a command used while grouping. What it does is target whatever the "Main Assist" or "Main Tank" is hitting on. By using assist you do a number of things. One is you don't break mezzes and roots cast on other mobs. Two you tend to kill stuff faster and have less deaths in the group by concentrating on one mob at a time.

The way to do it is to target your group member -- you can click on them OR use your F2-6 keys (F1 always targets yourself, if you have a pet hitting F1 twice will target the pet), and then type /assist or use an /assist hot key.

The Thread Macro 101 will tell you a lot about different types of hot keys, different uses for them, and how to set them up and use them.

http://boards.station.sony.com/ubb/everquest/Forum4/HTML/001332.html

I have 4 chat windows on all of my melees. I filter my hits/misses to one, hits/misses against me to another, and all other combat spam to a third, and leave the other for conversations and skills and such.

The problem with doing this is that the messages are kinda long, and I'd prefer one line in the window for each attack. I can reduce the text to "slash a drolvarg rager for 67" or "67" using hitsmode, thereby reducing the space requried for the window to look nice. Organizing the incoming information so I can at a glance see how things are going helps me to gauge how to act in battle.

Originally, hitsmode was needed to split up combat spam at all, not just to change the formatting (now the UI can be used to do both, you don't need the text command).

One thing that a lot of poeple don't pay attention to is which slots they put spells in.

There are 5 ways to cast spells:
-Click on the spell gem
-Make a hotbutton for a spell gem and click the button (a macro with /cast is the same as this)
-Make a hotbutton for a spell gem and press the corresponding number key (a macro with /cast is the same as this)
-/cast <spell number>
-Alt+Spell gem number

I usually use a combination of the third and last, but a lot of people don't--they have their own styles.

If you click on spell gems to cast spells, and don't want to change, then put important spells (heals, evacs, mez, etc) in a place that the mouse can easily be moved to. Similarly, if you press some combination of keys to cast spells, make sure that the spells you cast most often involve a simple keystroke. If you use the right hand to move hte mouse, that leaves most people with their left hand on the keyboard; my left hand hovers over Shift, A, 2, and 4, and it is easy for me to adjust my hand to cover Alt instead of shift, but is a starin on my wrist to keep it that way. Because i rest my hand that way, hotbuttons 2 and 4 are my most important skills, with 1,3, and 5 being other common ones. Spells 2 and 4 are stun and heal, respectively, with 1 for yaulp and 3 for secondary stun.

Arbitrarily placing spell gems and skill buttons can make it hard to use important skills when you need to in a hurry.

Thats all I can think of to offer players above and beyond the standard command help...

Press "D" to duck and interrupt your own spells, unless you're on a horse. I felt this was worth bringing up, as I've explained it to several level 60+ people in LDoN pickup groups this week.

When you're pulling, and the mob is or may be a caster, hit it from a distance and get out of it's line of sight, i.e. around a wall or corner.

If you know you're going to be fighting an enchanter type mob, and you have a low level DoT spell in your arsenal, cast it on yourself before the fight. It will break the mez the enchanter may cast on you.

Once you've learned how to assist effectively, get into the habit of staying behind the mob, on the opposite side from the Main Tank, any time you melee. Certain situations may prevent this, such as when it is necessary to wall a raid mob, but use it otherwise. Makes life much easier.

It's ok to turn autoattack off sometimes. If you're not the MT, and you get aggro, stop attacking for a sec (unless the mob is very, very close to dead) and let the MT get it back. DO NOT RUN AWAY!.

Seperate topic. DO NOT RUN AWAY! If you wear tissue paper for armor and get agro somehow, take the time to mez it, root it, stun it, whatever, and step back while the MT or off-Tank gets agro back. Running around just insures that you'll probably die.

If you're the MT and you're going to attack a mezzed mob, taunt it first. If you can, break the mez with a stun spell, or Bash it if you don't have a stun spell. Otherwise, the mob will rush the enchanter as soon as mez is broken.

quote:
baking, brewing, fishing
Brewing ties to other tradeskills. High level brewing and baking produces stat food and drink which is very much in demand.

Almost all low end trade skill products are a loss as no one wants the stuff. The days when you could tradeskill and sell back to the merchant for a profit are long gone. With careful planning and development of your tradeskill character, you can break even on some lower level tradeskilling.

Halas Heaters are brewed ingredients used in one mage researched spell. Halas Heaters requires brewed vodka and spiders legs. Both brews has the same trivial of 135. Vodka requires foraged vegetables. Having a forager in the fleet with high skill keeps the cost down.

Fishing and baking work together. You use the fish you catch for baking. There again a forager can produce stacks and stacks of fishing grubs. Bookworms are foraged in Pok which give a +2 to fishing. So you can fish for free although the bait you buy from a merchant is only a few copper. Fishing was popular when people had to wait for the boats and during the long ride.

I'm just scratching the surface when it comes to the complexity of tradeskills and how they relate to other tradeskills and quests.

One thing I have found useful:

Make a separate page for your situational hotkeys. By that I mean:

1) A page for Charm solo hotkeys (I am an Enchanter)

2) A page for standard group hotkeys/messages (using /gsay as the chat "channel)

3) A page for raid hotkeys (/rsay for messages)

4) A page for buffs/buff messages (if relevant)

Then you don't need to reconfigure on the fly if your CHeal or pull message is set to /gsay during a raid, or vice versa.

Another useful tip, for me, is customized spell sets. Seems obvious but lots of people don't use them. I have a Charm set, travel set (invis, gate, various illusions, Benevolence, etc.), buff set, standard group set, raid set, specialized group set for when I group with Coyotee (our tactics are sometimes eccentric). When you wipe, you can be on your way back (if no res) faster and better prepared.

If you are an Enchanter, Berserker Strength series of spells are a componentless Rune that will absorb a lifetap/harmtouch painlessly. When mana is not an issue, I try to keep it on the MA. It is also nice to "patch heal" a player until heal lands, although the Rune is sort of small.

When facing caster mobs, dispell is your friend. Strip the SoW off that mob, or remove his thorns. Keep it in mind when facing casters.

Lots of classes can pull effectively if mana isn't an issue. Pacify is a great line of spells.....

If you can adjust to the bobbing effect of lev, it is nice to have up. Running across a gap when a mob has to go down one side and up the other can give you the time to land a root or mezz.

These are just a few of the less obvious tips I have found useful.

For Sense Heading, or Forage:

Go to your Options hotkey, go to Keyboard, go to Macros... See how it lists yer new hotkeys? There are some dashes after said hotkeys. Click on the hotkey you want to macro, then click the keyboard key you'd like to use to macro this skill. Instead of the dashes, it will now list the Key you just pressed.
Example: When you're brand new, try tying your Sense Heading Hotkey to your Right Arrow key. Every time you use the Right Arrow key to turn right, you'll automatically sense heading.
