It must be clearly said that in this case they are wrong and you can explain it politely to them .
Here is why .

The Rules of Conduct tell in §12
"You will not exploit any bugs ... that grant the user unnatural or unintended benefits in game"
Further it goes "You will promptly report ..."

Independently from that there is an obligation for a seller of products or services (f.ex software) to guarantee the performance of said products and services within a reasonable time .
Here it must be said that a bug (or dysfunction) is ALWAYS the definition of the buyer and not the seller .
Concretely a seller can not pretend that that or that function of his product is a "bug" if there is no complaint from a user .
Of course there is always a reservation which enables product modifications by the seller BUT (and that is a big but) the buyer cannot be held liable for using the product as it was delivered .

Provided , again , if there is no complaint relating to this use .

Seen in this light , there are serious flaws (or at least non valid provisions) in the §12 of the Rules of Conduct .

As an example let's take a (theoretical ) case of a user fearing a monster whose path may lead it in a wall where it would be blocked as long as Fear holds .
This user would abide by the second part of §12 and promptly report this fact because he feels that it MIGHT be a bug and he likes to improve the quality of the game he is playing .

As he considers that this situation doesn't lead to any unnatural benefit (the Fear duration is the same with or without wall) and more importantly he knows that he isn't hurting any other user , he plays his game further .

This same user comes 1 year later to the same place and as he likes it , he begins again to hunt .
Sometimes the monster goes in the wall and sometimes not - so the situation has not changed .

As the situation was reported 1 year ago (remember ?) there is no way to suppose that this feature was "unintended" .

But this time a GM appears , warns the user for "bug exploiting" and perhaps goes so far as to forbid the user to hunt in this zone .
One could even imagine that he could threaten with banning .

Well for you all who read me , if that theoretical case happened , you can politely inform the GM that you are using a known feature and that his point of view must be incorrect .
As that theoretical GM has more important things to do , he will be on his way and wish you good hunt .

Bah, it isn't an exploit, and it may or may not be a bug. It may be a design flaw.

It's not an exploit because is isn't without risk. Fear will wear off, and you can be killed. It just keeps you from chasing the mob around.

In programming, there are 3 things.

What is intended.
What is designed.
What is coded.

A bug is where a designed feature is miscoded.

A design flaw is coded correctly, but the design doesn't get the intended result.

If the pathing was mis-designed on paper to go into the wall, it is a design flaw.

If the pathing on paper isn't supposed to go into the wall, but it is coded so that it does, it's a bug.

There is no way to tell which case this is, so it may or may not be a bug.

There is some confusion here, this should clear things up a bit.

>> You will not exploit any bug in EverQuest and you will not communicate the existence of any such exploitable bug (bugs that grant the user unnatural or unintended benefits in game)

Here is the part of the RoC that applies.

The problem is that people don't know the definition of a bug, and they are misusing it badly. You can only exploit bugs, design flaws are not bugs. You cannot exploit a design flaw. See the quote above. People need to get their definitions straight, because the RoC is badly worded. Bugs ONLY apply to code.

Design flaw:

The intent is to add 2 numbers and display the correct result.
The design is to add 2 and 3 and display 4.
The programmer codes 2+3=4.

THIS IS NOT A BUG! The code does exactly what the spec requires, THIS IS A DESIGN FLAW!

Bug:

The intent is to add 2 numbers and display the correct result.
The design is to add 2 and 2 and display 4.
The programmer codes 2+3=4.

THIS IS A BUG! The code does not do what the spec requires!

The definition of a bug is not arguable any more than the defition of 'addition' or 'subtraction', it's very clearly defined. And was defined long before VI came along.

Per the RoC, design flaws cannot be exploited, period, only bugs, it's right there in black and white.

If bad pathing is a design flaw, then it cannot be exploited. It can only be exploited if it is a bug.

What we lack is the information to determine wether it is a bug or a design flaw, because you need the EQ pathing spec to tell the difference.

If your going to argue a point, please use the correct definitions and applicable rules to do so.
