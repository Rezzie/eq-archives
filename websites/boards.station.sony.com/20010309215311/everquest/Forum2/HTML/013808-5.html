Aspell,

The maxim that "The Customer is Always Right" has gone out the window in the general business community and especially with businesses deaing with large numbers of people. (This is a general statement not specifically about Verant). With client databases and the information that they track, companies are taking a closer look at their customers. They're finding that not everyone is worth keeping. Some people are just "too expensive" or "not profitable" enough to keep, so corps are looking at "firing" customers, i.e. getting them to go away somehow -- perhaps to a competitor. Consider the rule of thumb about how 10% of the people are 90% of the work (feel free to substitute your own numbers - they're just made up anyway - I think the point is made). Businesses are now cluing in to this fact and they've got the tracking info on customer purchases, complaints, etc. to figure out who is profitable to sell to and who isn't.

Now, as for Verant, I don't think they've necessarily gone draconian on this, but I do think that they've been straightforward about their philosophy: people will be warned, suspended or banned based on their behaviour's impact on the game. If "the customer is always right" there wouldn't be any bans for bug exploits, selling accounts or inappropriate posts.

New world order time (sounds vaguely X-Files conspiracy put that way) our actions are far easier to track these days and life is changing. We all pay ~$10US/month. Consider how much it costs to handle problems and it doesn't take much for someone requiring personalized attention to quickly eat up a year or two's worth of dues, i.e. to become unprofitable to do business with. Everyone here can draw their own interpretation of the implications of that situation -- I chalk it up to the reality of living in a free market economy.

Instead of "The Customer is Always Right" I think the reality is that most customers are reasonable and hassle-free, a minority are wrong, and a subset of the "wrong people" are actually right and mislabeled. At $10US per person per month, figure out how much a _for profit_ business is able and willing to do to figure out which of the latter two categories an individual person belongs.

To The Question of "Is My Post Going to Get Me Banned?"

Place yourself on the other side of the virtual customer service counter and consider: if Person A walks up threatening to have you fired, calling you an incompetent <insert adjectives and nouns>, etc. etc. while Person B calmly and clearly articulates a problem and solution, who would you be predisposed to believe? Another aspect is how quickly (and therefore cheaply) can each of these concerns be handled?

I guess what I don't understand is how some posts have been made in this thread that people (and do I think they are sincere) don't understand how to evaluate whether a post would be inappropriately offensive or derogatory. First off, you're posting to other people you don't know and (with I'm sure a few exceptions) Abashi/Gordon Wrynn is one of them. Show people on these forums the same civility you would show someone you met at work. Look at your post and (Golden Rule Time) really consider if what you wrote would be considered offensive if it was directed at yourself. If you want an acid test, consider whether you'd say what you type to Abashi's face if your Grandmother was standing next to you. (If you're feeling especially paranoid)

In a game this big and this successful, dividing Gordon's job makes sense. I like Lum's suggestion.

Thoughts about a reorg of the position:

People who need to be at the front of the company, who need to be known and speak *with* the players:

1) Company spokesman. This guy announces actions such as new releases, patches, changes. He can answer the questions about how the changes are intended to work. He understands the game. He understands the company's objectives.

2) Player ombudsman. This person collects feedback, considers it, and presents it to the development team where warranted. This person engages in discussions with players about what benefits the game and what doesn't. He plays the game anonymously, and has no in-game powers. He has no authority to change the game, but can eloquently present design issues.

3) Product support lead. This is the ultimate person to whom to address all customer support issues. The uber-GM, who enforces the rules and makes sure the game is working as intended. Not a game designer, although he may present game design issues to the development team, as they affect customer satisfaction.

Kind of like: Mayor, City Council, and Chief of Police/Superintendent. A citizen needs to know where to go!

My opinion on EQ.

It's a business:

VI is a business, I owned an ISP and I hated chronic whining users just as much as VI does. At the ISP I threw them off my system because they were trouble makers and generally wasted more of my time because they'd hose their system every 2 days and we'd have to spend 3 hours a day listening to them complain that they didn't like something while we were trying to help them. After a point in time I asked them to find another ISP and on several occassions did more than ask them to leave. This was < 1% of the subscriber population and in any business you need to deal with it. If I was at VI and someone started spamming me every other day with "you suck" I'd refund your $10 and tell you to buzz off. (nerfed for the younger viewers)

The ISP I owned was the most successful in the area and because nobody could compete with me a national ISP decided to give me double what it was worth to get me out. I know how to run a business, I owned 4 and sold 2. Making good decisions based on the good of the whole user population is what makes a good business, not making a rash decision based on the level of crying done in the forum about a particular topic.

Gameplay:

It's a game, same as PacMan or Starcraft. If you play more than you do any other activity then you need to reflect on your life. I play EQ daily but I also have a job and a family, some people like gardening for fun, I mez monsters. Don't make this game your life, it's just a game.

Like life, there are parts of EQ that are less than great.

Like life, there are things that are unfair.

Like life, there are classes (ie careers in life) I would never be. (I had a Wiz ask me last night why I would ever plan an Enchanter because it looked "boring", I just look at him with amazement and pity but I kept my comments to myself trying to be nice. If you gave me an oracle robe at lvl 1 on a brand new server I still wouldn't play a Wizard. My .02)

Like life, dying sucks, deal with it. If I couldn't die I wouldn't play the game. This is what separates newbies from the smart people and adds excitement to the game.

I hate getting nerfed, 3 of my favorite characters were necros a year ago and then VI started making things "better" because other people complained that we were too powerful. I'll probably never play a necro again.

You're not forced to play:

It is your choice if you want to play. VI will listen to your comments but after spending a good amount of time this morning trying to weed through the "you suck" messages I just chuckled as streams of people whine while they're licking their envelope (metaphorically speaking) to send their money to Verant. I thought I had a good racket when I owned ISP.

Does Verant realize that people are mad at them? Probably. What can VI do to help it, the same thing I did at the ISP. Get 5 more people to help Abashi do his job. Respond honestly to people's posts, don't sugar coat it and explain in minor detail your response. If someone posts "My Bard Sucks" don't ignore it, post a message from VI that says, "We think your bard is fine so we're not going to do anything about it. Have a nice day." At least people will get a response, even if they don't like it. If someone intelligent posts "My Bard Sucks because of these 512 reasons" then read it and post, "We think that your bard is fine for right now but we appreciate the detail you went into and we will talk about it at our Bard strategy meetings."

Economy, or lack of:

The economy system needs major consideration. There is no player run economy besides the /auction type random shouting. This is not economy. An Economy is supposed to run itself, even if you're logged off. I'm not talking about a UO type economy where there are houses popping up all over the place, which is irritating and stupid but that's not EQ so I won't ramble on about UO. I used to play a MUD called Moral Decay where we had a simple economic system that went like this. A player would kill a monster and pick up the corpse. They would sell the corpse to a guild owned corpse processor for cash (the cash had to be supplied by the guild so it was important that people actually logged on to do guild business). The corpse processor would then sell processed corpses to a guild owned distiller. The distiller would then make barrels of alcohol of different strengths and sell them to guild run bars. The bars would then sell different strength drinks (basically healing potions) to the players.

This is a perfect example of a player run economy that had nothing to do with the GMs. Prices were set independently based on competition and capitalism was encouraged. With the money the guilds made they could purchase items for their guild, like a guild teleporter. I knew people who would log on for hours and never fight a single monster. They would just build their guild standing in the economy and because of the integration of the economy with the fighting system (via the sale of healing potions) the players depended on this guild businesses to function in order for the game to be more playable. I can go into more detail if anyone cares but my point is that it is possible to integrate a real working economy into a game like EQ.

Balance:

I believe that trying to balance the game always ends up in nerfing and that trying to make each class equal is detrimental to the game. Classes are supposed to excel in their own way. Complaining because a class can kill a monster solo when you can't is dumb because odds are that other class that solod the mob can't heal themselves. It's a group game. My latest character is an enchanter and that class is not a soloing character. I've read time after time how the mightly enchanters charm one mob and and kill another and then kill the charmed mob all nice and solo. What that person forgot to tell you was the 4 times they died that night because the charm wore off and both of them turned you into a pile of goo. That or they fight 3' from the zone point. Either way Verant don't "fix" the enchanter because our DD spells are bad or because we can't heal. That's what other classes are for.

Overall:

I started playing this game pretty much since the first day it was released in the stores. I quit back in April for a few months because a few new games came out and I grew bored of EQ. I came back again a few weeks ago because there is nothing out there that is currently better than EQ. EQ will be obsolete eventually but for now it is the best game out there.

-Bilan
