Aradune
Station Member 		posted 01-20-2001 09:34 PM           
Once upon a time (early 1996, to be more precise), Steve Clover and I drew the world of Norrath on a white board in the old Sony Interactive Studios building. We added rough names, including most of the starting cities I think (Steve is really good with fantasy names, and he also came up with the name 'EverQuest', btw).

Then, a few months later, Bill Trost came along, and he and Kevin Burns began to fill in a lot more detail... mountain ranges, lakes, oceans, and other regions, as well as dungeons and such.

They did this for all five continents, and after working on the map for a while, they started to flesh out the points of interest, writing up documents describing them.

Some areas we meant to include in the game, and some areas were meant as context and background, similar to maps included in many fantasy novels, depicting areas the protagonist may or may not one day travel to, but implying a vast world and a deep history.

And, as actual development of the game progressed and we learned what could and could not be done, and how long it took to make a zone and all of the content associated with a zone, we narrowed things down a bit and came up with a list of primary areas of Norrath we wanted to cover.

And then, a little later, we decided, after experimenting with zone density, that it would probably be better to include less continents with more zones, as opposed to more continents with less zones. So we axed Kunark and Velious, hoping one day we'd get to do those too, but realizing it would have to be after launch.

The good news, of course, is that slowly but surely, due to the continued success of the game, we've been able to fill in other Norrathean points of interest; for example, the Hole, Paineel, and the Temple of Solusek Ro, and over the next year, the Warrens and Stonebrunt Mountains.

-Brad

Regarding crowded zones of our youth:

Oasis, High Keep, Lake of Ill Omen, South Karana. They're similar in that they're always packed.

Why? It's pretty safe. Outdoor zones are generally good hunting (HK has guards instead).

But take a look again at that last producer's letter. Read the part about finding good, well-trained dungeon hunters at level 50. Now ask yourself how much training you get going to these crowded outdoor zones?

Get a group together, and head into one of the dungeons that just go an XP bonus. In the lower levels you can head to Kerra Ridge (save Odus!) or Runnyeye Citadel. Higher up, go to Permafrost or Solosek's Eye (Sol A.).

Find people you trust. Learn to hunt in deep, dark, dangerous dungeons, without sow! Hone your dungeon skills now, while it's still relatively easy.

Remember - EQ gets harder as you gain levels, not easier!
