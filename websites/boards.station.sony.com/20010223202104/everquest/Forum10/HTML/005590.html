Grouping is this games greatest strength. In a solid group you can do a lot more then 6 times what you would be able to do alone. It’s just another case of the whole being more then the sum of its parts. There are many issues with grouping, but as they arise just ask here for info on the best way to handle things.

Basically you need:

1 Cleric: They should Never nuke, unless to stun a runner or caster. Always keep the entire group buffed with their best Daring spell. Always keep the main tank and Enchanter alive.

1 Enchanter: They should also never nuke, they should keep breeze on the casters, and haste on the tanks. Their main job is to Mez Any additional mobs that come.

1 Warrior: The puller, they should be the main assist person, his job is to taunt nasties off the enchanter and cleric as well other casters. He must select a target as quickly as possible and stick with it until its dead.

1 Wizard: Deliver the killing Blow and stop runners before they have a chance to call for help. If a Wizard only casts one spell the entire fight and kills the runner then he is doing his job. At later levels its also the Wiz/Dru responsibility to Evac.


This is the foundation for a solid group. They are the primary classes. You can add any other 2 classes to the group depending what’s available. Bards are one of the best classes to add to the group, but also one of the hardest classes in the game to play correctly. Mage or Druid can replace the Wizard at lower levels, A Mage AND a Druid can replace the Wizard at any level. (One for the damage one for the Evac) and Rangers, rogues Pallys and SK’s can replace the warrior to some extent. Later on the Monk becomes the best puller once they get FD. At this point the Warrior switches roles to taunter and assist person.

When you make your own group, first start with a cleric and chanter. Use the following method:

This example will assume the group leader is level 25

/Who all 22 28 Cle

Then send tell to the people who are NOT in a dungeon. Anyone who is not in a dungeon is worth a tell at least. Keep hitting the up key and just change the Cle to whatever class you need.

Also do a:

/who all 22 28 LFG

Again the best people to send a tell to are the ones in Town or areas that they can’t get experience. For example you find a level 28 cleric in East commons, and you know they are not in the middle of a fight. You will need someone who can port the group sometimes when you find people that are far away from your current zone. People may not like getting a tell asking for money or items but in most cases they do not mind getting a tell for a group invite.

This can take some time to master and the entire group can take 20-30 minutes (or more) to put together. But as you find someone that you like as a person and a player don’t forget them and next time you log start with them. There are many things in this game to master, but making a group is one that will always be useful and is a skill that is great to master.

This is just a foundation for getting groups, there is a lot more to add or ask about so I opened up this thread for just that reason.

If the tank is tanking properly, the cleric/healer should have no more work to do than if there was only one tank. Judicious application of the 'taunt' skill should keep it so that only the main tank is taking damage. This becomes extremely important at higher levels when the cleric can Complete Heal the main tank. If everyone is taking damage, it takes a lot more of the cleric's mana to keep 'em healed using the smaller (and less mana-efficient) heals.

A great class to add there is a ranger. Rangers get the taunt skill (I believe), and have spells to taunt as well. With a warrior as main tank, and a ranger as secondary/caster rescue, a group can tear through mobs like no tomorrow . When a mob gets agroed on a caster, the ranger goes into action (leaves the primary target mob) and taunts the mob off of the caster, taking the hits for a minute while they finish up the primary target.

Of course, most of my experience is grouping *without* an enchanter, so we have a lot of standard tactics like that. If you have a good enchanter, you'll rarely need to taunt mobs off of casters. I've noticed that clerics and chanters usually work closely together to keep mobs mezzed... when one mob comes unstuck and heads for the chanter, the cleric pops a heal on the chanter (changing the agro to the cleric), the chanter mezzes it again, and everyone's happy. The tanks usually don't even notice .

Forming a group based on specific classes ultimately prevents players of non-specialist classes from grouping. Furthermore, it makes finding a group extremely difficult, since you may not be able to locate a member of a specific class. Therefore, I like to form my groups based on roles. The core of my groups are as follows:

1. Primary Tank - this character's task is to hold the attention of attacking MOBs, and needs to be of a class with high hit points, high ac, and the Taunt skill. Warrior, Paladin, and Shadow Knight do this best; at low to mid levels, a Ranger will also suffice.

2. Secondary Tank - this character also needs to be capable of handling the attentions of attacking MOBs. Taunt skill is a plus, but not required. Warrior, Monk, Rogue, Ranger, Paladin, and Shadow Knight all do this well (though each does it a little differently); I even sometimes use a strong pet in this role.

3. Primary Healer - this person's job is to keep everyone alive. Clerics do this best, but in many cases a Druid, Shaman, or even a Paladin can fill this role, though this may depend on the class balance and tactics employed by the group.

4. Primary Nuker - this person's job is to accelerate the rate at which the group kills MOBs. He needs to be capable of dishing out large amounts of damage quickly. Wizard, Magician, and even Druid do this very well; Enchanters can even fill this role at low level, as can Shaman or Necromancer (OK, a good DoT may not do its damage quickly, but it does it reliably).


The other 2 slots can be used in a variety of ways. They can be used to shore up weaknesses in the primary roles (e.g. I could only get a Paladin for primary healer, so I want somebody else with some healing), it can be used to add a crowd control expert (such as an Enchanter), it can be used to add a generalist character who adds a little of everything (like a Bard or Druid), it can be used to add an extra class with abilities especially suited for what we are hunting (e.g. an extra Paladin, Neromancer, or Cleric for dealing with Undead, or an extra Druid for dealing with Elemental creatures), it can be used to add an extra Tank if I feel it's needed, it can be used to add a class with excellent pulling skills (such as a Monk, Shadow Knight, or Ranger), a mana-regenerator, etc.

Something to keep in mind that Zandar is a self-confessed spelunker (translation: he spends most of his time in dungeons). It doesn't take any work at all to put together a group that can do the snow cougars (which I absolutely love for great xp, constant action, and low risk), in fact I know from experience that just a 35 warrior and 36 cleric can do it with zero downtime . However, I have used Zandar's technique many times, starting around level 25 when I was trying to put groups together for the Mistmoore zone line. (Note: he meant "shift up-arrow" to bring back the "/who cleric 20 30" so you can backspace and substitute some other healing class after discovering that there aren't any clerics in that 11-level range on the entire continent of Faydwer. Secondary note: I actually included bards in my quest for healers, since if you have a mezzer the zone line mobs aren't so tough that you need healing during the battle and they can do both. Tertiary note: bards are the weirdest, funkiest class in the game, completely unclassifiable. )

Dungeons are special. It is difficult to control pacing, because you usually have to rest someplace where mobs spawn or wander (only enchanters, bards, and necros can speed up the healers' mana regen, and necros can only do it by giving their own away). It is difficult to pull singles because you can't see around corners, there's no room for shifting direction and pulling it off to the side past the one that spawned behind you, harmony doesn't work indoors, and lull causes aggro when resisted (only enchanters, bards, and necros can mez adds). MR becomes a major factor, because you are often forced to fight casting mobs that see invis, so a single successful fear or charm could result in a nigh-unretrievable corpse, not to mention a dead group (warriors not only have a steadily-increasing level-based hp bonus, they also have a steadily-increasing MR bonus -- with my gear I'm up to 81 unbuffed at level 36 -- and bards, enchanters, shamen, clerics, and druids can all buff MR). You *cannot* let runners get away (finishing them off in one blow is the most efficient way to accomplish that, and while many classes get good nukes, only wizards can be sure of having a recent one to which the mobs in any given dungeon are not overly resistant). Finally, many mid- and high-level dungeons can be harder to get out of than to get into; some literally have one-way entrances, with the exit past the boss mob (only wizards and druids can evac the group).

When I put together a group for ordinary xp hunting, it's just "Tank, I am one, check; healer, of course Genamorfie is here, check; ok, we can kill stuff now and anyone else who wants to join can". When I put together a group for a dungeon crawl, it is very, very different. I agree with Zandar's basis for a group that can take *any* non-raid-scale challenge appropriate for their level (and having a warrior is absolutely non-negotiable to me -- since I am one ).

I completely forgot to mention why clerics are necessary, because it goes without saying: revive/rez. If anyone should happen to die while the group is already past two locked doors, or where the nearest spot for melee binding is a 20-minute run away, there's no substitute in terms of time saved and risk negated for being able to just cast a spell and bring them instantly back to their corpse. Yes, even the melee binding problem creates *risk* for dungeon groups, because 15 minutes just to get back to the same zone is long enough for respawn; most non-casters will need someone to meet them to invis them back to the group, so the group will have to either evac out and rebreak the spawn, or else meet the respawn while at least two people short of full. Plus, there is absolutely no substitute anywhere in the game for getting back some of the xp that you lost when you died. Even in easier dungeons where clerics aren't needed for things like complete heal and AoE heal, they will still be needed just for revive/rez.

My own dungeon resume: Blackburrow, Befallen, Crushbone, Kurn's, Highhold Pass, Unrest, Mistmoore, Upper Guk, Solusek's Eye, Inner Paw, Dalnir, Permafrost, Runnyeye, Frozen Shadow...umm...dang, I'm probably leaving some out. I just can't seem to stay in one place for long.

So, now for the big question: why?

I mean that there are things to consider before even starting to put together a group. The Powell Doctrine, named for our esteemed Secretary of State, is helpful here. First, you need a clearly defined goal. Is it loot? XP? A specific item? exploration? the cheap thrill of constant risk? The answers to those questions will determine which dungeon you choose, where you go in the dungeon, the pacing you set, and even fundamental things like whether you stay in one spot and camp a single room or wander around slaughtering as much as possible.

Second, you need an exit strategy. What is the recognizable criteria for when to leave? How are you going to get out when that time comes? Is everyone going to have to leave when one person leaves? This determines whether having a druid or wizzie for evac is a necessity or just a nicety, and also whether you invite people who can't make a commitment of a few hours at least.

Finally, you need communication and consensus (I'm not sure that's part of the Powell Doctrine, but I think it is). Everyone needs to know and agree on the first two considerations. It is impossible to have a successful group if the members have diametrically opposed goals; for example, the best way to get xp is to not die, so someone whose primary goal is leveling will have severe disagreements with someone whose primary goal is crazy adrenaline-pumping action. Also, it does no good to make sure you brought an evacer along so that the melees and hybrids wouldn't have to fight their way back out, if she just gates and camps at the end of the night.

I love this thread, since I love dungeons. If I help even just one person have a more rewarding dungeon grouping experience, then it was worth the time of writing this.
