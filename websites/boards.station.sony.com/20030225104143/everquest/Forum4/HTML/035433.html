We do.

"Applications" sums up the info we need (it's amazing the people who would ask to join the guild without even mentionning their class or level, or if they knew anybody in the guild already, or what their goals are in the game...). It allows the guild members to quickly review the new members and speak up if something is bugging them about the guy.

Do you go "Sure, come in!" when anybody ask to join your guild? At some point, you get tired of letting bad apples contaminate the lot, and you put up an application form. Doesn't stop all of them, but it does bounces back enough to be worth it.

Think of an "application" as a presentation letter. If you can't be bothered to introduce yourself, I don't know why a guild should welcome you...

Likewise, we have a "probationnary" period. This is to help alleviate the problem of people jumping from guild to guild, getting free stuff and moving on.

Well, KB, I'm not on your server, nor is my guild high enough (yet) to offer you a worthwhile experience for grouping and raids; but I can tell you that some guilds are simply pickier than others. That includes mine, and for some really good reasons.

We're an offshoot of an old guild on an old server. During my time in that guild, I went from newbie player invited as a RL friend of a friend to a dedicated player to an officer who helped out when the GL was busy.

We invited people who seemed like great players: friendly, generous, quality players who had a lot of devotion to the game and guild. Some of these people turned out to be real menaces -- to the game, themselves and the guild, let alone a raid. And after booting those people out (sometimes with a lot of trouble, since you have to catch all their toons in-game), some of us realized that we had an even bigger problem with our GL. So there was an exodus due to the powertrips and lack of ability to communicate effectively.

Having been through all of that, we're even pickier than what was a pretty selective guild. That means you don't join us unless you play with us first, and people who ask to join before we've played with them enough to know if we want them get probationary status. It also means that we consider our little (and likely to remain little, since we're choosey) group a family, a group of friends we trust as players and as people.

We still have to boot people out sometimes, still have some bad fits, but we're all the better for it. The people who have been with us are people I know will stay, and they bring new friends to us who are assets to the guild.

The opposing example comes from some (SOME) of the guilds that have begun to dwarf the servers by virtue of size alone or the ones that started last week because someone decided they wanted to start a guild -- just because they could or because they couldn't get anyone else to take them; the ones that shout to the zone for recruits and accept them all, with few rules of conduct. If someone asks, or even looks at them for a second longer than normal, officers invite them in, no questions asked. In my experience, these are the people who end up being the bad apples, because good examples were never set, rules were never established and their loyalty to a name is more than it is to the people involved.

It's a long way to justify having an application process. No, it's not a job interview, but people spend a lot of time in this game, building friendships and guilds, maintaining guilds. It's important to like the people you associate with and to trust they won't ruin your reputation or your experience. And having all the information from the start makes roster management a lot easier.

You really have to look at why you want to be in a guild. Is it just to facilitate grouping? Is it to have friends to share information with and chat with in-game and out? If it's the former, you may wish to put off getting into a guild for a bit longer (you've waited this long), and just focus on playing with people you like and trust. If it's the latter, then finding those people is the core of finding a guild that you'll be pleased with.

As for why you're having a hard time finding a guild -- IMO, anyone who, as an uberguild, rejects a lvl 59 chanter as too low is either way too focused on the letter of some rule they made (relatively recently, since lvl 60 hasn't been obtainable for all that long) or very short-sighted. If they'll want you at 60 or 62, they should be building a friendship with you now, if nothing else. If they don't want you, they don't want you and should say so.

Either way, I'd reconsider any guild that's that picky about levels and try to find one that really wants you as a player. Know also that some people are wary of high-level toons that aren't guilded, as they could have been booted out of every guild on the server or purchased yesterday on eBay. Play or talk with people you like, even if they're a little lower in level, and see if they don't have guild connections in a guild that might be a good fit for you.

IMO -- a good overall fit, a friendly atmosphere, in a guild is vastly more important than the exact levels of the players or even being in a guild at all. People will come and go from EQ; some players will level faster than others; and eventually you'll find a group where you're happy. In the meantime, focus on finding good groups, or even consider the dreaded server change to find a guild that will make you happy with the kind of people, the kind of players you'll be playing and socializing with.
