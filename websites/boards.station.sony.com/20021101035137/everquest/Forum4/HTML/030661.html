Greetings, all!

--well, interesting to me, at any rate. And by the way, as the title implies, this post is intended for a different sort of newbie: those of us who are new to the endgame (50+). Not restricted to them by any means, but more relevant to the middle years (30 - 49).

So, to begin: although I have been playing for slightly more than a year now, I have never gotten an avatar past 39th. This is mostly because I have too many alts on too many server (full dockets on 2 servers, scattered twos and threes on 5 others) and also because I really enjoy exploring the beginning of the game with every class/race combo I can find even a little interest in. Sadly, though, I have hooked up with a really great group of people (not a guild, just a group) who have pet avatars that they insist on playing on a regular basis.

All of which means that I had to pick one of mine to do the same, and--unsurprisingly--I picked my druid. We are now all well past 30 and heading for the higher levels (new territory for all of us) and as a result I have a new perspective on the game re all the patching and balancing going on in the last few months.

In essence, it boils down to this: the patches and balancing do absolutely nothing to make the game itself good or bad. To use a possibly dubious analogy, like guns there are no good or bad games, only good or bad people--which is my clumsy way of saying that the game is what it is, and the perception of the individual is what colors it.

What brought this thought on is the realization that, since I do have avatars of every race/class combo that interests me, practically every patch or balance affects me (or will affect me in the sweet by and by) one way or another. At first, I really worried about them--I mean, why waste time leveling up an avater if the class was so heavily nerfed that it was going to be useless? Then, like a bolt from the blue, I realized that all of the changes to the end game--and I mean ALL of them--have, in reality, absolutely no effect on me whatsoever (IMHO, natch).

Why?--I hear you ask. Well, good question--which leads me into a side anecodote concerning a phenomenon I call The New Movie Hoax. This involves the hype put out by the movie-making media concerning all new movies, to the effect that you ABSOLUTELY HAVE TO SEE THEM WHILE THEY ARE NEW, and the implication that if you don't, you're some sort of loser. Some years ago (approximately at the time the theaters started charging more for a movie and snacks than it costs to by a filet mignion dinner with all the trimmings) I came to a stunningly simple conclusion concerning those movies that has saved me thousands of dollars since: any movie is new to me the first time I see it, no matter how old the movie actually is. What you are actually paying through the nose for is the experience of seeing it in the theater--an experience I don't particularly enjoy (at least not at 15 bucks a pop minimum, assuming I'm not out with someone) and therefore can do without.

The relevance of this anecodote to 30+ endgame newbies is simple: the endgame is new to everyone the first time they get there, no matter what has been done to it prior to their arrival. Does that mean no one should whine--er, pardon me, justifiably complain--about changes? Not at all. To those that are already in the theater, having the air conditioniong shut off really sucks. What it does mean is that, to anyone who has not yet arrived, none of these whines--damn! Sorry, complaints--should put you off or deter you in any way.

Keep these thoughts always in your mind as you grind your way to the 50's: nerfage cannot affect patterns of play that you have not yet developed. Nothing done to your class at a certain level can affect you before you achieve that level. By the time you achieve the nerfed level new patterns of play will have evolved to compensate and/or take advantage of the most recent nerf (keep an eye on your favorite spoiler site).

Above all, keep in mind that this game, like all of the others (I hope) is not a static thing that was once perfect before it was nerfed; it is, instead, an ongoing process during which mistakes will be made by the designers--mistakes that, one assumes, are the results of trying to improve the game instead of maliciously making it worse (because we all know that Verant really, REALLY wants to make the game so bad that no one will pay them 5 million dollars a month to play it).

That's all for now, got to actually get some work done so that I can contribute my part of the 5 mil to play this lousy, maliciously nerfed game.

I started playing EQ in November 1999 and I have yet to get to the "endgame".

My highest level character (obviously a druid hehe) made 50th about 18 months ago. She is now 52nd. Below her I have one in the mid-40s, three in the 30s, a couple in the 20s and half a dozen or more in their teens. They are spread over 6 or 7 servers, too.

I might get to the higher levels one day. I am not actively avoiding it, but I just find the lower levels are still far too much fun to move on from. As for leaving Lake of Ill Omen and South Karana behind, I don't even stop hunting there with my 40+ and 50+ characters!

If you aren't interested especially in raising the level of a particular character, then yu don't "outgrow" zones. I personally love all the plains of Karana and spend hours roaming around them. One of my main motivations in getting up as far as the 40s and low 50s was to have characters who could roam freely in the zones I loved and take down pretty much anything they felt like.

It's a minority choice of playstyle, but it's not completely weird. There were some figures released a year or two back on players with multiple mid-level characters and it was something around 15 to 20 per cent of the population.

The key to enjoying EQ is to make the game fit your playstyle, not to change your playstyle to fit in with the game. If EQ can't be used as raw material for the game you want to play, then it probably isn't the game you want to be playing in the first place.

It's a very flexible, versatile environment - I've played all the other big MMORPGs and some of the smaller ones and not one is as open to interpretation in how the player approaches it as EQ (UO comes closest, probably).

At all costs, don't get sucked into the "must level - must get to 60th - must have Epic Weapon - must have Uber equipment" syndrome. If those are natural goals for you, then reaching them won't be painful. If it feels like more work than play, then you have probably taken on board someone else's goals - take a step back and choose some of your own.

I don't think Llanwe ever said that 30+ is endgame. She said she was in the 'middle years' (30-49), and a complete newbie with regards to the endgame (50+).

I do have a couple of comments with regards to the recent "nerfs", and how they affect the players. A few have implied that some 'nerfs' are OK in that they are just part of the ever changing nature of EverQuest, and other nerfs are not, since they change something that has been there since day 1.

As an example of this, Aandaie suggests the 'manaburn' nerf would likely fall in the first category, while the change to monks is of the second type. Here I disagree. While monks have had their defensive ability since the beginning of EQ, the armor and equipment available to them is a more recent (and gradual) addition. It is only recently that a significant number of monks have surpassed the AC 'soft cap'. This, in combination with high +hp gear has allowed the monk to surpass plate-wearing classes in the traditional role of main tank.

If you consider this in combination with high damage output, as well as class abilities like feign death, and mend, then you can understand why the situation had to be fixed (read: nerfed). There is no point to a warrior if another class is superior in virtually all respects.

How does this change affect players that have got used to their monks as they were? Obviously there is a significant adjustment to be made. Does it make the monk obsolete though? Definately not. How does it affect lower level monks, that weren't in the habit of tanking 'endgame' mobs? It doesn't other than as a perception issue if they were looking forward to that role.
