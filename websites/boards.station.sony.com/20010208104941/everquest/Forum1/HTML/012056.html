Following up my research on people crashing when zoning, sound problems, and all the other issues in another thread here. I've been looking into the serious bouts of packetloss that people on DSL and Cable seem to be getting.

I've been able to duplicate the problem on my system, but I have not been able to isolate the cause. Its almost as if EQ is overflowing the TCP/IP stacks under some conditions. I won't go into the methodology of my testing to spare a 4-5 page note. However, I will tell you the configuration that solved my problems.

I spent the better part of a week in my spare time tweaking every possible setting I could find in Windows 98/ME in regards to TCP, etc. Then, to make it easier for people, I spent more time, trying to find tools so the average user can do this. Here is what I found to be the optimum tweak settings to play EQ on Cable/DSL with max speed, and no packetloss bouts(that I have found).

1) Set your datarate.txt file in the EQ directly to 9. (ive found 9 to be most suitable for most faster connections.)

2) Download a program called "Tweak-Me" from: http://www.tweak-me.de/frameset-tweak.htm Install it, and goto the "Internet" settings, and click on the CABLE/DSL box to enable optimal RWIN settings, etc. In addition, click on the small box that says "Enable other settings to improve speeds." Save and exit.

3) Goto Start:Run and type msconfig, goto the startup tab, and unclick everything. The only thing you really need running under most conditions is Explorer. For me, I also need my Force Feedback joystick TSR running. Thats fine, but don't leave too much in here. Msconfig is a handy way to clean up your system on boot.

4) Reboot system with tweak-me settings and msconfig settings saved.

5) Next, download Dr.Tcp from: http://www.dslreports.com/front/drtcp.html save this to your desktop, click on it, and run it. Select your NIC card in the pulldown, then turn "MTU Discovery" to YES.

Load up EQ, and enjoy. I think this MAY solve alot of problems, but without trying it on more machines, its only my best guess. In addition, I am unsure if Dr. TCP saves the MTU settings everytime you re-boot. I will check this out tonight before bed.

Loki,

I have found that the MTU setting is best when matched with that of your ISP's.
I know I did this fix 7 months ago when playing EQ.

What happends is windows (98 at least) defaults the MTU setting to 1500. This I have found to be way too high.

By matching that of your ISP's you will send out single packets to EQ instead of Broken packets (what I like to call twice as many packets as what are needed to go out.)

ie. My default is 1500 my ISP it defaulted to 1320. That means my puter sent out a packet of 1320 and 180 to make up the difference instead of just sending out the 1320.

Best way is to send fragmented pings to your ISP.

Starting at 1500 and segrading in blockes of -10 each time. Say 1490, 1480,1470 and so on until it goes through without an error.
Then you set your MTU setting to that number.

It will not cause all the lag to go away but it will allow you to recover faster incase of linkdeath. After changing this setting I only went Linkdead about maybe 1 per month due to raids or massive problems on my ISP's end or on Verants end.

Loki,

Please let me explain the datarate.txt file.


The Datarate.txt file can range in numbers from 1 to 10. This translates to the size of the packets you will receive. The larger the number, the larger the packet size. It normally defaults to 5.0. For strong fast connections many players increase the number to increase zoning times, and speed in the game. If you feel you have a good connection, you may try 7.0 first to see how you like it. Some people go all the way up to 9.0. With a high number, if you ever have packet loss due to connectivity problems while playing you will lose more packets. With a large amount of packet loss you might see a lot of ghosting. This is where things will appear to be in a spot that you see on a screen but are actually somewhere else according to the game. If you feel you have a weak connection, you may try 3.0 first to see if it helps. This reduces ghosting but can increase zone times.
Loki, when you recommend 9.0 you will affect many of the 28.8 and 56k users who should be around the 5.0 setting. It does help with DSL and Cable connections, but some people still need to stay at the lower setting.


For people using ICS, we do not directly support ICS. We do not make ICS programs and they can be rather complex. We would like you to do some research for yourself to learn how to use ICS.
Please go to www.dslreports.com and do their tweaks for high-speed connections until you have a healthy connection. This will work with cable modems also. PLEASE PLEASE PLEASE do NOT try to tweak your registry yourself. One wrong number or letter and you might need to reformat to get on.
Then go to www.practicallynetworked.com/index.htm (Go through this site for the kind of network you have) there is a file there you can download that will change your open ports if you are using ICS. This is the key point here you have to have port open through at least 9000. Then run the thorough patch checker. This has worked for people. You might also want to go to www.modemhelp.com

Key points
Need ports 1024 through 9000 open UDP.
Need TCP port 7000 open.

I've got some tweak instructions for experts only. Others I would strongly recommend you consult someone who knows who can talk you through it over the phone or come over or something. This is for people having connection, packetloss, sudden modem freakouts on EQ. We've done this on 3 system now, and it really makes your system smoke! On to the good stuff..

This works with every connection although the values I entered are for my Cable setup.
Two important values. The RWIN and then the MaxMTU. The RWIN you should just dl a reg patch and merge it onto your reg. But for the MaxMTU I found that the value has to be discovered on an individual basis. To do that follow this: Go to Run and type Command, you'll get a DOS window.

Type this:
C:\ping -f -l [value] [your ISP]

I started like this

C:\ping -f -l 1420 www.pacbell.net

And kept going in increments of 5 until the message read that the packets needed to be fragmented. So the idea is to input the highest value before the data gets fragmented. In my case it was 1426, and to the highest value you then add 28, so I reached my ISP's MaxMTU of 1454 and that's the value that I entered in every instance in:

HKLM\SYSTEM\CURRENT CONTROL SET\SERVICES\CLASS\NETTRANS

When you get there and expand the NetTrans key, you'll find a lot of keys like this 0000, 0001, 0002, etc, in each one of them the value MaxMTU is present and you need to right click on it and choose Modify and then enter the value you discover to be the best for your ISP The values you pick up in websites are good but generic, so you need to find the value where the data doesn't get fragmented and at the same time you are sending the max amount of data b4 that occurs.

It's pretty simple really and that way you'll have the optimal value for your connection :-)

Here's some rather dry reading for anyone interested in "Path MTU Discovery."

ftp://ftp.isi.edu/in-notes/rfc2923.txt

A little nugget from it:

===================
TCP Problems with Path MTU Discovery

Description
A host performs Path MTU Discovery by sending out as large a packet as possible, with the Don't Fragment (DF) bit set in the IP header. If the packet is too large for a router to forward on to a particular link, the router must send an ICMP Destination Unreachable -- Fragmentation Needed message to the source address. The host then adjusts the packet size based on the ICMP message.

As was pointed out in [RFC1435], routers don't always do this correctly -- many routers fail to send the ICMP messages, for a variety of reasons ranging from kernel bugs to configuration problems. Firewalls are often misconfigured to suppress all ICMP messages. IPsec [RFC2401] and IP-in-IP [RFC2003] tunnels shouldn't cause these sorts of problems, if the implementations follow the advice in the appropriate documents.

PMTUD, as documented in [RFC1191], fails when the appropriate ICMP messages are not received by the originating host. The upper-layer protocol continues to try to send large packets and, without the ICMP messages, never discovers that it needs to reduce the size of those packets. Its packets are disappearing into a PMTUD black hole.

Significance
When PMTUD fails due to the lack of ICMP messages, TCP will also completely fail under some conditions.

Implications
This failure is especially difficult to debug, as pings and some interactive TCP connections to the destination host work. Bulk transfers fail with the first large packet and the connection eventually times out.

These situations can almost always be blamed on a misconfiguration within the network, which should be corrected. However it seems inappropriate for some TCP implementations to suffer interoperability failures over paths which do not affect other TCP implementations (i.e. those without PMTUD). This creates a market disincentive for deploying TCP implementation with PMTUD enabled.

===================

What does this all mean?

Well, if you've followed the steps outlined in this forum, in an attempt to solve you connection problems, but are still having trouble, then it is quite possible that your firewall (or your ISP's firewall) is not setup to allow ICMP messages. If this is the case, your system will not receive these ICMP messages and, as a result, PMTUD will fail, which in turn will cause TCP to fail... The result being that the connection is timed out, and you are disconnected from EverQuest.

For those of you running Win2k here some tweaks for DSL/Cable for you. BE WARNED THAT THESE REQUIRE REGISTRY MODIFICATIONS!

Cable Modem & xDSL Speed Tweak (Windows 2000)
Category: Network & Internet
Use these settings with your Cable Modem or DSL broadband Internet connection, to boost network throughput performance with Windows 2000.

Open your registry and find the key below.

Create the following DWORD values, as most of these values will not already exist you will need to create them by clicking on 'Edit -> New -> DWORD Value' and then set the value as shown below.

DefaultTTL = "80" hex (or 128 decimal)
Specifies the default time to live (TTL) for TCP/IP packets. The default is 32.

EnablePMTUBHDetect = "0"
Specifies whether the stack will attempt to detect Maximum Transmission Unit (MTU) routers that do not send back ICMP fragmentation-needed messages. The default is 0.

EnablePMTUDiscovery = "1"
Specifies whether the TCP/IP stack will attempt to perform path MTU discovery as specified in RFC 1191. The default is 1.

SackOpts = "1"
Enables support for selective acknowledgements as documented by Request for Comment (RFC) 2018. Default is 0.

Tcp1323Opts = "3"
Controls RFC 1323 time stamps and window scaling options. Possible values are: "0" = disable RFC 1323 options, "1" = window scale enabled only, "2" = time stamps enabled only and "3" = both options enabled.

TcpWindowSize = "5ae4c" hex (or 372300 decimal)
Specifies the receive window size advertised by the TCP/IP stack.

GlobalMaxTcpWindowSize = "5ae4c" hex (or 372300 decimal)
Specifies the system maximum receive window size advertised by the TCP/IP stack.

Exit your registry and restart Windows for the change to take effect.
