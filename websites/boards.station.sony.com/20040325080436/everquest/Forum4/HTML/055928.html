As a newbie myself, here are the tips that helped me out the most:

- If you have the patience for it, try out several different race and class combinations up to LVL 6-10. I have tried seven different combinations and found the one that was right for me. Not only do you learn what other classes are capable of, but you get to learn some of the town geography as well.

- If you are a caster class, make sure to practice low cost spells on yourself to keep your skills high for less chance of a "fizzle".

- Many people advise putting off tradeskills until later, but I have had a lot of fun and success working on tradeskills under LVL 10 with only mob dropped items.

- The newbie armor quests are your friend. I can't count how many people have assumed I'm a twink because my Barbarian Shaman has a full set of newbie quest gear. Some of these quests are quite hard, but it is good practice with great rewards.

I put myself on a no-posting ban because I was getting... well... cynical and snotty.

However, for you I will break the ban. Although most of what I would say has been said already.

Dubyah mentioned remapping your A key. That should already be done for you.

Go ahead and ask people for help if you need it. Shrug off any rude responses. But don't ask for money, gear, or weapons. And until you know your way around, don't ask for buffs either. Learn to do what your character can do.

Do your newbie quests. There are a lot of people who say they are junk or whatever, but for FREE and for learning the game they are great. Some are harder than others. HOWEVER, If you make a Qeynos character that needs giant field rat whiskers or a Felwithe one that needs Giant Sylvan Bat furs, drop that character for later. Two of the most frustrating sets of armor. Also, regarding the newbie quests, the armor is better than most everything I had before I was 20 when I started the game (but back then people actually wore bronze armor as an upgrade).

While we are talking about armor, DO NOT BUY ARMOR from NPCs until you know what you are doing and/or are paying less than 15 plat per piece. When I started I didn't understand the auction abbreviations and bought ringmail from vendors for a very high price.

If you have the PoP expansion, do your shopping in the bazaar. Expand the /bazaar window so you can see all of it and put in your race, class, slot you wish to fill, and price range at the bottom and compare items.

If you don't have PoP, probably the cheapest way to get all expansions (except LDON) is to get the Evolution set, even if that means you double up on an expansion or two. Just remember when you install it that you are updating NOT doing a new install.

Don't be afraid to try things. There is basically no penalty under ten for dying (except if you are a spell caster you have to rememorize your spells). Don't be afraid to try different characters. Some home towns are easier than others. Play what interests you, even if you get the "difficult class" message. A lot of people recommend starting something basic like a warrior or any of the hybrids (ranger, beastlord, paladin, shadowknight) to learn the game because there is less to do in the beginning.

It takes a lot to learn. There are so many things that we don't always remember. Like to autorun press the numlock key on your number pad. Then all you have to do is use your left/right arrows to turn. A lot easier on your hands then mashing down the up arrow button.

Read the hints and help topics. At least the first time. Three years playing and I keep the MOTD (message of the day) on and sometimes I learn stuff from it.

As Immortal Brother said, visit us on the TNZ chat channel, that is type /join serverwide.eqlive:TNZ to listen, and then if you want to talk just go /1 and say whatever you want. A lot of the time we are pretty quiet, doing our own things in comfortable silence. Sometimes we get silly. Other times we actually ask and answer questions.

OK, Ice going back into board exile until after the New Year... but remember, it's a game. Games are supposed to be fun. When it isn't fun, take a break. It will all be there when you get back.

quote:
Can you fine people please explain what it means to aggro, mez, do crowd control? I have been playing solo and would like to begin grouping with my level 6 human ranger but would appreciate knowing what these terms mean so that I am learning to do the right thing.

Aggro is hate. The more a monster (mob) hates you, the more aggro you have. You want the tank (the plate wearing classes, Paladins and Shadowknights and Warriors generally, although at lower levels most melee classes can tank to some extent) to have the most aggro, as they have the best AC (armor class) and HP (hit points) so can take a beating better. It is the job of everyone else in the group to not generate more aggro than the tank. This means not nuking (damaging spells) until the tank has enough aggro generated to overcome that of those spells. Even the healer, despite possibly never casting a spell on the mob, must manage his aggro. Healing someone the mob hates generates aggro too. Ping pong aggro, where someone gets more aggro than the tank, can lead to your group getting killed. The mob runs over and kills the cleric, say, and in the meantime the wizard has been nuking to try to save him, so the mob then turns to him, and so on until the tank is fighting alone. The tank needs a few seconds to gnerate aggro, and then the group must make sure to never exceed the aggro the tank has generated. It takes a bit of practice, but good aggro management will place you well above many players in the skill department, and help you be welcome in a group or even possible a guild.

Mezz is a line of spells/songs that make a mob just stand there "hypnotized". It will not take action until mezz wears off, UNLESS IT TAKES DAMAGE. Thus, if you are fighting a mob, and another comes wandering by and jumps in to the fight, a mezzer will mezz it, so everyone can finish the first mob and then turn their attention to the second. This is where /assist is important. Everyone should use the /assist command to see what the tank is fighting, and then everyone should fight the same one. Then the mezzer should select the other one and park it. It is important to not break mezz until everyone is ready, and generally only the tank should be breaking mezz.

Crowd control is the process by which people manage the adds (extra mobs) if you try to pull one and 2 or 3 or more come. Mezz is the most common way, as even casters cannot cast spells while mezzed. You can also use root spells, which make the mob stand in place, but this does not stop casters from hitting you with spells. You can also offtank, where another melee class engages tha add or adds ontil everyone is ready for it, but this can be risky and inefficient. More damage, more healing from the cleric, etc. Thus, classes who can mezz are usually the preferred crowd controllers.

Enchanters are the main crowd control class, generally, as they have the best and most varied line of mezz spells in the game, and can mezz almost anything (giants, for example, are all immune to mezz). Necro's can mezz undead mobs, so can also serve as crowd control. Bards can also mezz. I don't play a Bard so cannot explain the finer points of their strengths and weaknesses as a crowd control class, however. And as I explained above, any class with root spells can do crowd control to a point by rooting adds away from the fight until you are ready for them.

Hope this helps!
