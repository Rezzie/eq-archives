Regarding the tracking bugs...this is caused by one of two things:

1) Simple lag. As far as your machine knows, the critter really is ahead, ahead, ahead...until it actually gets and updated position from the server, when it can tell you, oh, it's behind now....

2) The Z-axis. If you're tracking in hilly areas, things can get very screwy. Sometimes it works great, sometimes it's as unreliable as a Ranger's AC.

Ah, tracking. beautiful tracking, wonderful tracking...


Hokay.

Tracking is a great skill - and I figured I'd condence what the folks above have said and a stack of rangers have learned over the last three years:


Tracking Skill reflects the /distance/ at which you can pick up an enemy/mob/player - /and/ how long you can go before you 'lose the trail' and have to reacquire it. Using NK as my reference point: At tracking 50, I could track from the spires out to.. somewhere not very far. By tracking 100 I could pick up the druid ring from the spires and some things beyond it, by 125 I could track to the Guard Tower and Gypsy Camp, and now at 200, I can track from the spires to about three steps short of the corrupt guard in the very northeast corner.

Tracking is /most/ reliable in the center of a zone, and fades off rapidly as you approach the edges. Tracking at higher levels and in certain zones (Stonebrunt, Gfay, WK) actually tracks as though the zone were a cylendar - and at the edges, you can track things off into the nonexistant lands past the mountains you can't climb over.

If you track someone who subsequently becomes invisible (and you can't see invisible) you will track to the spot at which they vanished, and you will circle that spot. The same applies if you track someone who teleports or camps, or a mob that despawns, but you will simply /lose/ tracking if your target zones normally.

Tracking also improves as you get closer to the beastie (as others have said here) due to lag and where your computer thinks something is relative to where it actually is.

Tracking PC's is less reliable than tracking NPC's.

The standard tracking list is sorted by 'spawn' - that is, how long ago did the beast set foot on Norrath? The higher on the list, the more recent the spawn - /very/ useful when hunting a specific, rare-spawn beast, like the heretics in stonebrunt. Get close to where they appear, and keep throwing up tracking...

But rangers can also sort by distance (wonderful when travelling or pulling), con (EXCELLENT for pulling in dense zones), or any number of other filters. See your eqmanualsuppliment.txt file for full information.

You CANNOT track a player's corpse unless: 1) He was visible when he died. 2) He was part of your group. 3) He has not reenetered the zone since dying. 4) You were in the zone when he died, and have not subsequently zoned.

Tracking ignores walls, pathing (or lack thereof), trees, hills, impassible gorges, and the like. It's more akin to radar, but ground effect doesn't do much to 'em.. this makes it less than ideal in dungeons, but fantastic for paying attention to what might kill you around the next corner (if you're a ranger) or whether the named harbinger of doom has spawned lately (if you're a druid or bard.) Rangers do have it better.

Finally, you can track your group at /any/ range, as long as you're in the same zone.

Enjoy!

on tracking players:

tracking players grouped with you is as accurrate as tracking mobs and npc's. Additionally, you can track them anywhere in zone regardless of distance.

tracking players *not* grouped with you is more difficult because their position is not updated as often as mobs, so if they have moved you will be directed to the place they left. ie if player A zones into south karana and runs to the spires, you will be directed to the bridge until their location is updated to reflec thier new position at the spires. I recommend tracking casters rather than melee when trying to find folks that have been at a spot for a while, as they are most likely to be in the same spot long enough to have been updated

All times are PT (US)
