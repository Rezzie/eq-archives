>>The Essential Enchanter: We should look into why an enchanter is considered ‘essential’ in a Kunark group, and perhaps change that.

The answer to this lies in the cause.

The cause is downtime. We all hate it. And nothing drops downtime like an enchanter. A bard comes a close second. Both classes significantly increase mana regen rates. And both are therefore highly wanted. If enchanters didn't have clarity and wizards did, people would suddenly learn they don't 'need' us but you can bet there would be a wizard in every group. And if every group had a wizard, then AE's would become more popular... and you'd end up with people complaining about how you had to have a wizard.

The reason enchanters are more wanted is simple. It's bards. They don't mez as well and they are limited in how many songs they can have up at once. An enchanter can keep 4 mobs mez'd, have the party hasted, the casters claritied, and the mob currently being fought slowed, while having stuns available to stop gaters. The bard can't match this. They get a few bits of compensation. Their mana regen song is faster and heals as well, for instance. They can melee while singing. But in the end, the enchanter is more effective.

This is compounded by the fact that bards are relatively rare. The nature of their playstyle tends to make most people abandon their bards at an earlier level. An additional factor is that the bards only recently got a bit of their mez problem fixed and most people are not aware of how well their new mez song works.

People look for enchanters because when they are 50 or so, the enchanter is the best for where you are going... you have to have him for his ability to control the big pulls, his stuns, and the complete enchanter package. At that level, you need the power of the specialist. Once people become fixated on this, they don't change. They had to have an enchanter then, therefore they have to have one now. They fail to accept that because they are now 3 or 4 levels higher, they can get by just as well without an enchanter now.

From my viewpoint, the best way to solve this is with two changes:

Nerf all forms of primary mana regen (clarity, bard song, lich, the new druid one). Nerf them all to be less effective. And then up both standing and sitting mana regen rates. At 29, a person with clarity should be as effective medding after the nerf. At 54, a person with c2 should be as effective medding as before. Although some enchanters will complain about being nerfed, the end result will be a reduction of downtime for people in general. The gap between a person with clarity and a person without is too great. You want an enchanter because clarity makes the game more fun. It makes you more active. Active is fun. Sitting around doing nothing isn't.

The second part of the fix is to make bards better mezzers. There is nothing wrong with the idea of mez. So fix up the bard mez'ing ability to bring it a bit closer to enchanters and they'll be a better option. Bards are pretty close to where they need to be mez-wise, but I think they need just a bit more of an upgrade. Improve the duration of screaming terror a bit too.

With SoV, Verant took the completely wrong approach. Instead of thinking why enchanters are so wanted they tried to make us not wanted and figured we'd still be balanced because of our buffs. The reality is that our buffs is what makes us wanted and by making us unable to do what we do, they've inflicted tedium upon us. People will always want us because even if we can't mez... clarity, haste, and lack of bards.

>Illusions and Link Death: Can we make it so that Illusions remain up during a link death (not during zoning, though).<

I've seen posts both pro and con here on dropping illusions during zoning, and it just seems to me from a usability and personal responsibility standpoint, they should not drop when zoning.

Scenario #1 (as it is now) - If I am walking through a zone (especially one that has no visible zone entrance but where the zones change in the middle of wide open spaces) and I zone, my illusion drops and if there is a guard of some kind there, I'm immediately attacked before I can bring the illusion back up.

Scenario #2 (if illusions didn't drop) - I am in a zone under illusion, and I gate back to my home point, forgetting to drop my illusion first and am attacked by my own guards.

In the 1st scenario, the risk is almost entirely random, as is not tied to choices made by the character or to anything under the player's control. If the player zones, the illusion drops, and if anything happens to be there, then risk exists.

In the 2nd scenario, the risk is directly associated with the player's actions. If the player zones without choosing (remembering?) to drop their illusions, there is risk involved. The player has direct control over whether or not to take the risk. If they fail to drop their illusions, and die because of it, it is a result of their own actions (or inaction).


Final thought: I've seen several posts here saying that people don't care about spells that don't directly affect combat, but I think EQ is about more than fighting things. Sure, that's a big part of it, but so are quests, social interaction, and FUN.

Spells and abilities should help us in combat, help us help others in combat, and help with quests... but, there should also be FUN spells too. Things that just add to the social aspect (like emotes); Things that add to the flavour of the class; and Things that are just fun (like voice graft, and mages being able to summon "party packs").

Obviously, these shouldn't take precidence over other issues, but I just don't want to see them overlooked in the run to turn every class into "lean, mean, combat machines". If that's all there was to the game, to be honest, I wouldn't be playing it.


Thanks again for taking the time to start these threads, and spending the not inconsequential amount of time needed to read them all (I know, because I've read every post myself).

Cheers,

Absor said:

"I like the idea of characters having to deal with their religious choice, even enchanters. But, well, they aren't anymore because by now every single new enchanter is being told by their mentors to restart their characters as agnostic. That's something that I don't like at all."

I just wanted to point out the source of this problem:

"CONSIDER RELIGION (OR NOT) AT CREATION

If you wish to use your spells for secretive reasons, you should consider being agnostic. For example, even with the use of faction-improving spells, you could get into trouble with good city guards if you were the follower of an evil god, or vice versa."

-- page 88, EverQuest: The Ruins of Kunark

I think I just saw someone backpedal into a dumpster.

Seriously. As someone else already mentioned, this is a problem that probably exists across the board. I’ve made agnostic bards, rogues, enchanters, mages (although I didn’t keep all of them).

Why? When it comes to playing evil races “it seems” like there’s nothing offsets the bad vibes you catch at good-aligned zones. After coming to terms with the fact that my bard would never get close to Nagy even though she worshiped Veeshan, or get to run errands for dragons, I deleted her. It’s a prevalent mindset from the writer/programmer point of view that evil gods don’t care for their subjects. While this is true at some level, any god worth his salt knows that he’d better keep an eye on things if s/he wishes to maintain his/her influence mortal affairs. This is true for both evil and good gods. It makes absolutely no sense for an evil cleric to get treated like a passerby on her god’s home plane.
