It's all about choices.

You make the choices not to spend the time or energy. You have to accept the consequences for those choices. If you choose to play in such a manner that you can't pull 30+ people together to go on a raid, that was your choice. You COULD have played a different way. If you don't have the organizational skills to make a 30+ guild work, that's your choice again. You can choose to go learn those skills. If you choose not to organize your life around Everquest and spend 8 hours per day playing... that's your choice. It has consequences.

An analagy: I'm going skiing next weekend. The resort I'll be at will have a variety of different runs. I happen to be a bad skiier, so most of those runs will be "content" that I will never experience. Why? Because I don't make the choice to become a better skiier. Even if I skiied casually for 30 years, I might never be good enough to go down the really really hard runs. I might have to spend 8 hours / day training if I wanted to do that, and the way I choose to live my life doesn't allow for that. However, I don't blame the ski resorts, skiing in general, or God (or fate or whatever) for that. I COULD choose to pursue skiing. I simply don't want to enough, and I have to live with that choice. The equivalent of this thread would be me requesting all ski runs be tailored to my level. Or the level of 90% of the people who ski (who also will never train 8/hrs per day). Why deprive those people who can and want to excel of content that is meaningful for them? What's wrong with accepting that there are things you will never achieve? The top ski competitions are a lot more interesting when the competitors go down slopes I could never manage. You COULD have them run on Blue-Square runs and not fundamentally alter the competition.

The same goes in Everquest. A challenging monster or encounter is only challenging because not every player can do it. For something to be challenging, there has to be the possibility of failure. For something to be REALLY challenging, there has to be a lot more possiblity of failure than success. This could mean two things (More or less abritrary numbers). 1) Every group/guild(no matter what level) that attacks a monster has a 5% chance of winning. 2) 5% of groups/guilds have a good chance of winning, while 95% of groups/guilds have no chance of winning. There is a spectrum between the two, but obviuosly in a stratified game where you gain power, or skill, or whatever (like both Everquest and skiing), it is weighted towards 2. If there is a challenging run in skiing, for instance, it usually doesn't mean that I have a 5% of going down the run without falling down. I have a 0% chance of going down the run without falling down at my current skill level. I probably have a 0% chance of EVER going down that run without falling down. Maybe 5% of skiiers would have a 95% chance of getting down that really challenging run without falling down. But I'm not going to say there shouldn't be runs that are this challenging. And I'm not going to say that there shouldn't be monsters that aren't that challenging in Everquest. In my opinion, there should definitely be there. That's what makes a challenge in the game.
