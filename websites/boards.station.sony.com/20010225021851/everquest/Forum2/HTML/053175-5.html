I personally think that to make a game where inflation wont be too bad, they need to do one of two things (not both):

1) Make the inherent power/skill of a player or class NOT come from items at all. When a level 50 buys a suit of armor off the rack and a sword and shield off the rack, FEAR THEM. Beyond level 5-10, players shouldnt have problems picking up this kind of gear. Magic items would be limited to non-combat effects such as a light spell or a true north spell. That would make the game all about grouping, exploring, fighting, learning new skills, and mastering the game, but uber treasure wouldnt help your character become overly powerful.

2) If you are going to keep magic items in the game and keep it where a large percentage of the power or effectiveness for a player CAN come from items, then a different approach is needed. They need to set the scale of power that a player will EVER be able to reach, and never exceed that. Then, they need to provide redundant magic items with similar effects. In other words, set level-based damage caps, AC caps, stat caps, damage-over-time caps for different levels and enforce it in code behind the scenes. Make sure that 75% to 80% of those caps are gained inherently by the class, so that uber loot wouldnt help you.

Example for #2. Maybe you design a game where a warrior should never do more than 4 * his level in damage in a combat round. OK, so then make sure that his innate skills let him do a minimum of 3 * his level in damage. Then, the magic items he gets can add all they want in terms of damage, but at any time if they excceed the innate cap of 4* the level, the extra is lost. Then hide damage messages by making them non numeric. Then make sure that there is PLENTY of equipment to achieve the difference of 1 * his level to make up the extra damage, such that at any given level there might be any number and variety of items to make up this difference, so there was no uber equipment, but plenty of interesting equipment for your level to quest for.

Of course all this is moot as far as EQ is concerned.

Those dragons back in those days were not as powerful as they are today. Since bronze, banded and fine steel was sort of "uber" back then, VI tuned the dragons knowing that people would be fighting with those kind of equipment.

The problem was, when people started killing nagafen and vox more and more, it became routine and pretty simple and people just get better and better gear (ie Cloak of flames). So VI started buffing up the dragons to challenge those elite players without thinking about the casual players who are still in bronze and banded. But that wasn't enough. The elite player kept Pummeling the dragons to a bloody pulp and VI just kept buffing them up more and more. Because of that the casual gamers in bronze ended up fighting dragons that was meant to challenge the elite players decked out in the best gears. Later on, with the kunark expansion, people were able to exceed past level 50 and on to 60, and onces again those dragons had to be beefed up more to compromise.

This cycle went on for a while till someone at VI finaly came up with a good idea. They let the dragons be able to pick who they fight. That means that people beyond level 53 (I think) could not fight nagafen or vox. I think that idea went a long way to help reduce the mudflation problem.
