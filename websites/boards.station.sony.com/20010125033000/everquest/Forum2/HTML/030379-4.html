How's this line of reasoning:

You own the right to use that file.

The file was written to be read by and used by the EQ client software to communicate with the Verant servers.

You have every right to do that with it. That's what it is, what it's for, why they put it on your drive.

Here's another example: at work, we have a $1,200.00 software package that inventories the machines on our network. It generates .dbf files (dBase format) to save the data. It integrates with Crystal Reports to present the data.

I was going to import part of one of the databases into Access to create some labels. Guess what? You can't. You don't have permission.

Did I kick and scream and let these people know that for $1,200.00 they better not infringe on my right to my data? No. I accepted that this was not how they ever intended the program to work, and I will have to do a little data entry.

I probably could have cracked the data somehow, but I will have to be content with generating a report of the data and entering it into another program.

So, accept that you're supposed to read the data in the .dat file using Everquest, the program, and that you are supposed to find out the information in the file by obtaining the spells, casting them, and observing their effects. And yes, every licensed subscriber of EQ has EVERY RIGHT to use this method to read that file. =)
