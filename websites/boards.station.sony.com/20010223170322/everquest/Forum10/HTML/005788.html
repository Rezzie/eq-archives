1. My suggestion is to play around with a Dwarf Warrior for a short time to get used to the interface. That's the simplest class, and they start in a pretty tame area. When you get the hang of that, play a caster for a while; I recommend a Halfling Druid or High Elf Wizard. By the time you get used to the magic system, you should have a pretty good idea what you really want to play.

2. When you make your character, you get to choose the look from a small list. After that, your race and items have a preprogrammed look when you equip them - but you get to choose your equipment.

1) TheSpike forgot to add, play the tutorial first. Also, you can download Ellegon's newbie FAQ at www.ellegon.net/faq.txt

The problem with shaman for new players is that you either start as an ogre, troll, or iksar, in which case you are killed on sight by the guards in most cities in the world (unless you spend countless hours doing good deeds to become accepted); or you start as a barbarian, in which case you're nightblind until you reach the level where you get the Serpent Sight spell, and it really does make it difficult at night in the outdoors (in dungeons there are plenty of walls to reflect light from your Greater Lightstone or whatever). Just so you know.

2) You get to choose from a small number of heads for your race/class combo. After that, what you wear will determine your look; starting out, and on corpse runs, you'll look just like every other newbie of your race. Int-based spellcasters (wizards, necros, enchanters, magicians) get the short end of the stick there, since most of their gear has no graphic; they are all about the spells.

Looks actually play a very large role in EQ for some people, since there is such a wide variety, and it can be fun to make your character look good. After you've played awhile and amassed some wealth and power, it is possible to put together some really sharp outfits with the right mix, especially if you're playing a plate-wearing class (you can fill out various slots with custom colors using player-made and -dyed fine steel plate). Even spellcasters have some leeway; for example, I once read about a necro who had a Mithril Quill for a weapon, and in the offhand an int-boosting item with an open-book graphic; she looked like she was writing people's names into the dead-book.

In general, for each race there is one "standard" look for leather, one for chain, and one for plate; standard cloth has no graphic, although of course there are "cloth" items with their own graphic (such as silk or magical robes). Also, old-world and Kunark helms have their geometry set by race and armor type, and the human plate geometry is atrocious; Velious helms have custom geometry, so that a particular helm will actually look the same on each race, instead of just having the same color scheme.

I'm sure you'll have plenty of other questions once you start playing. We'll still be here.
