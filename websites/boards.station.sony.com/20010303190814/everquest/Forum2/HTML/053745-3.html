Bards don't get double attack, instead they get haste. But that haste doesn't work with an enchanter around. Hence all the requests for more melee damage, because without that haste, bard melee is unbalanced.

All bard effects stack. This is so universal that there is special code that says if caster = bard, stack. That's why cantana will go up when C2 is up, because ALL bard songs stack. Enchanters don't have the same code, and C2 is 1 mana/tick weaker than cantana so it won't overwrite, thus C2 bounces.

Haste should stack as well. Ah, but what about shamans? Their haste doesn't stack. Well shaman resist buffs don't stack with other resist buffs. Shaman str buffs doesn't stack with other str buffs. Shaman ac buffs don't stack with other hp buffs. Shamans have a X^2 curve if all they ever do is heal (or nuke), all the other effects are there to help the class make up for the weakness it has in healing (or nuking), not to keep to the X^2 curve.

Bard haste IS there to keep the bard on the X^2 curve. And when an enchanter is around, it doesn't exist.

I honestly can't think of a single argument against haste stacking, yet there are literally dozens of valid arguments for it. I'm curious why there is such resistance to the idea. What is the argument for having bard haste, of all the bard abilities, not stack? Why is it the exception?

If you haven't seen it, check out the history of haste stacking and some "what if" math at
http://www.afterlifeguild.org/Thott/haste_stacking.html.

Okay... folks, the issue is balance.

The broken aspects of our class are annoying but not really a part of the balance topic.

Mechanical issues, such as auto-twisting are also outside of the scope of this discussion.

We need to focus on what changes would bring us to parity with the other classes.

OVERVIEW: It's fairly obvious that we're doing okay through the first 20 levels, with a few notable exceptions. From 20-32 we slowly decline in comparison to the other classes as everything we can do is eclipsed by someone else. From 32-50 the decline increases, making us little more than a mana battery for caster. Then from 50-60 we seem to become more of a liability than an asset from all I can tell.

As it stands, the most useful position for a bard from mid-levels on is to find a fighter or two and run support for them. As soon as a real caster or two joins the group, there's hardly any use for the bard. I don't think this was or is Verant's "vision" for the class.

The overwhelming reason most of us choose barding as a profession seems to be the unusual aspects of it. We enjoy surprising those around us with what we can do, and like doing what we do in a different manner than everyone else. This should remain a defining aspect of the class.

SPECIFICS:

UTILITY SONGS: Our utility songs are fairly useless due to the short range of effect. We cannot reliably shepherd groups across the world, since our speed, levitation, and invisibility songs have too short a range to allow a group to move. Either increasing the range, increasing the duration, or a combination of both would alleviate this problem, and since these songs don't aid in experience generation, I really can't see how this can be honestly argued against.

PROBLEM: Limited range and duration makes them nearly useless in real play.
SOLUTION: Increase range and/or duration of non-combat songs such as levitation, invisibility, fast travel, etc.

COMBAT SONGS: The majority of our combat songs are markedly inferior to similar spells, occupy the bulk of our time, and are resisted an insane amount of time.

As an experiment I spent a couple of hours last night in Crushbone at level 31 playing lullaby for orc centurions. They resisted between 40 and 50% of the time... these are creatures 20+ levels below me, and I cannot use a sleep song to keep them sleeping? How ridiculous.

Any effect whether spell or song should be resisted with the length of effect taken into consideration. If a spell can be cast 6 times in a minute, then lasts for 5-10 minutes when it finally works, then why are songs which last for 9-12 seconds being resisted at the same rate? If any caster were restricted to only casting three spells during an encounter, they'd never run out of mana and spend most of the time wandering around bored. We have a frenetic task of keeping at most three "spell effects" active during a battle, yet cannot get anywhere close to the same reliability or effect of a spell, whether from caster or hybrid.

PROBLEM: Resists on vs. enemy songs are too high considering limitations of singing mechanics and extremly short duration of effects.
SOLUTION: Decrease resistance on songs, or severely increase duration.

As for our combat enhancement and combat utility songs, such as hastes, chains, and mesmerize spells, resistance is not the only problem. Virtually all of these conflict directly with caster based spells that are more valuable to a group. Even the lower hybrid spells available to rangers, paladins, and shadowknights are more desirable versions of effects than the bard's songs.

The simple solution, it seems to me, is to let the effects stack. Overwriting and spell/song conflicts have destroyed the usefulness of the vast majority of our songs. Chains interferes with snare, any of our haste songs interferes with speed, our slow songs interfere with slow magic, even our pathetically weak damage shields interfere with other damage shields. I say, let them all stack... every last one. Let us be a desired asset to a party... rather than a liability.

Looking back, specifically at haste/slow stacking issues from the past, the problem seems to be that haste spells, plus item haste, plus a bard's haste made some groups too fast too quickly... I've never seen a haste item, but from what I can tell they're rather high-level items. Caster haste spells also work a heck of a lot more powerfully than anything a bard can do. So to fix this problem, the lowest component was made so it couldn't work in concert with the others?

I cannot believe that across the land, overhasted groups were ruining the game. This adjustment to correct what seems to have been a specific high-powered problem, filtered all the way down to the level 1 bard song... making the vast majority of our combat support songs relatively useless for anything but partial groups and solo efforts. Parity can only be achieved here by either boosting the haste effects to rival spells (which I doubt you would ever do), or letting them stack.

PROBLEM: Any non-damage song a bard might sing in combat is useless, because it conflicts with more effective efforts by casters and hybrids and does more harm than good.
SOLUTION: Let songs stack with the spells, across the board. The effects are much lower than spells, and the limitations of only being able to keep two or three active are sufficient to keep this from imbalancing the game.

ADVANCEMENT: Finally, as casting classes advance, they get much larger mana pools, numerous mana-recovery options, and an enormous array of spells to choose from. Bards continue to get a small array of songs, which are not drastically better than the ones preceding them. A high-level caster can use more spells in a combat than they could at low levels, yet the bard can still only use three non-conflicting effects. Since melee abilities don't significantly increase either, bards are left behind with regards to effectiveness.

Perhaps allowing songs to increase duration as a bard advances, letting high level bards twist 3, 4, even 5 songs at a time if they wish, would compensate for this problem.

PROBLEM: While other classes can accomplish much more at higher levels, bards are limited to the two or three effects they can "twist" together.
SOLUTION: Increase song effect duration with level advancement to allow more songs to be twisted at higher levels.

I hope this helps somewhat... I realize I'm looking for the simple solutions to a complex problem. However, I think that balancing the class in this fashion would restore bards to the role of desired group member, without really affecting other aspects of the game.

Taelenmor Summerpsalm

The thing that limits Bards is the time it takes to sing a song and the short duration of the songs. At level 3, we can sing 3 songs at once. At level 60, we can sing 3 songs at once.

At low levels, casters are hard-pressed to keep three effects active on an entire party, and they're not all that great. Why?

1. Small mana pool.
2. Slow mana regen.
3. Short spell duration.
4. Low strength/mana ratios on spells.

At high levels, casters are quite capable of keeping six or more effects active. This includes buffing all relevant party members, sticking long-lasting (e.g., longer-than-the-fight) debuffs on target MOBs, spot healing, damage, snaring, et al. Why?

1. Larger mana pool.
2. Much faster mana regen (meditation, clarity, mana theft, cannablize, et al)
3. Much longer spell durations.
4. Much better strength/mana ratios on spells.

Bards get none of the first three advantages. Our songs never increase in duration, so we're always stuck singing three.

If Bards had no melee component and were just casters with a different way of measuring their casting ability, the fact that casters go from being able to keep 2 effects active to being able to keep 6 effects active, and that those effects are better, when balanced with the fact that bards never get to increase the number of active effects, means that, in order to keep balanced, the power of Bard songs should be vastly superior to anything that they are replacing. They should more than triple in power. And given that our melee contribution is at best 30% that of a high-level melee character, they should still be better.

I am speaking here of high-level Bards.

At low levels (1-20) I would suggest that Bards are actually overpowered, except that they do not have a very large song list to choose from, and those levels don't last that long.

At mid-levels (21-40) I think the 3-song twist vs the caster mana pool works very well. The 20s are a little too hard, the 30s a little too easy (due to the songs we get at various points), but it balances.

In the late pre-Kunark stage (41-50) Bards get weaker, but not to the point that it's intolerable. They're not as much fun, they're pretty limited, they're not overly useful except in specialized circumstances, but they're not terrible.

Post Kunark (51-60) though, all other classes see almost a tripling of total power per character. Bards see very little of this. The songs that scale up do so linearly. So while other classes are doing 3x the output at level 60 as they were at level 50, we're doing 20% more.

And the replacement songs just don't cut it. You are still balancing those songs as if casters can still only do three things at once. In addition, you have added a lot of specialized songs on the theory that the Bard can still use their lower-level (non-scaling) songs if they so choose. They currently can't, if they want to be at all effective.

You need to SERIOUSLY THINK about Bard balancing post-50. This class is VERY underbalanced. You haven't heard all that much about this because there aren't very many Bards out there, but that does not make it any less true (in fact, the dearth of high level Bards just about proves the point).

1. You need to significantly increase our haste effects on higher level songs or make them stack. Hastes should be about 30% stronger or stackable.

2. You need to significantly increase our buff strength at high levels. Stat buffs should be about twice as strong.

3. You need to significantly improve our slowing capabilities at high levels. Slows should be about twice as strong or stackable.

4. You need to moderately increase our overall damage output, perhaps 25%.

5. With the exception of slow and the uselessly weak mana drains, our MOB debuffs are good.

6. You need to fix all the stuff that doesn't work right, and not call that a class improvement. We should have had that 2 years ago.

Kenross Cantorfojado, 56 songs

Okay, so a lot of people are complaining about the Bard class. The summary seems to be 1)that they are nerfed/weak 2)Seem to have few original group strengths, aside from transportation. So, I was thinking about that, and had an idea. Bards are primarily geared toward grouping. What is the most pervasive problem encountered in grouping that NO class can currently prevent? Trains! When a train is made, they will aggro until the target leaves the zone entirely. This is a real pain in the ass in a deep dungeon. My solution to alleviate this problem, while at the same time strengthening the bard class, would be to introduce a new, unique-to-bards song. This song, when used, will de-aggro the group, as if the group had zoned, and will return the spawns to their origin. Keep reading, it gets more detailed and I attempt to deal with problems that are obvious in its abuse. 1) The song must be in effect until the mobs are completely returned to their point of origin. If the note is missed before that, the effect is broken and the mobs will continue to aggro as before. 2) The song would of course have a chance of failure, per mob. If the song is failed for a given mob, it will immediately aggro onto the group. This would result in some interesting tactics and cooperation to keep the bard safe from having their spell broken. The remaining mobs must be killed, or they will interrupt the song, which would result in the rest of the mobs re-aggroing, possibly with reinforcements! 3) One immediate and obvious abuse of this would of course be the run of the mill, high-level spawn farmer. The spell could be used to give an even greater advantage to high-level item farmers, a grim prospect for those of us who get angry at farmers who will not let us of appropriate level have a crack at getting, say, our SK quest armor, for example :P This abuse of the new skill would be alleviated by manipulating the rate of success for the song based on nearness to the bard's own level. For example, if a mob or set of mobs were within 5 levels of the bard, the song would have a much higher chance of success than if the mobs were more greatly removed, be that Higher, OR Lower, from the bard's own level. Therefore, a bard of appropriate level for the item would have a greater chance of pulling off the success of the strategy than even a much higher level bard. This would also have the nice side effect of being useful to exp-seeking groups. Since we tend to hunt within our level, this effect would be a boon to a group hunting for experience in dangerous areas (i.e. those dungeons Verant's been having people play in due to the difficulty? Sound familiar? Hmmmmmm...) The programming would be simple for this effect as well. Simply have the success rate set per level comparison, then if successful, the mob treats the entire group as being zoned. Simple. The mob returns to its point of origin. Anyways, I thought it was a really great idea I had, and wanted to share I hope others think it is worth looking at as well.

A couple of issues about balancing bards; it seems like Verant still consistently misunderstands what a bard is, and how to balance them.

MISCONCEPTION 1: "A Bard is a Jack of All Trades"

There are 5 "trades" in the game:
- tank (taking hits)
- fast (combat) healer
- damage dealer
- buffer/debuffer (includes regen)
- crowd control (can include root)

Most classes either dominate one area, or do well in a couple. The classes closest to being a 'jack of all trades' are the druid and the necromancer; not the bard.

Don't use the a bard is a 'jack of all trades' argument when balancing bards; it isn't currently true.

--------------

MISCONCEPTION 2: "Bards effects should be weaker to offset the fact they are not limited by mana"

The effectiveness of bard effects should be based on the number of simultaneous effects a that can keep up over time, as well as how many can be done at once (i.e. 1000 damage in 1 sec, then 99 sec of downtime is signficantly better than 10 damage a sec for 100 sec)

One thing that is not often pointed out, is that a for a bard to use a debuff, he has to drop 4-6 buffs... that means any debuff is 4-6 times more 'costly' than a buff.

All bard effects should be balanced at each level with the equivilant mana cost for a caster's spell.

For example, if a enchanter regains 18 mana every 6 seconds... (3 a second), and casts a 450 mana spell that lasts 1 hour (3600 seconds), this would result in (3600*3)/(450) == 24. This means a this spell take 1/24th of an enchanters continual maintenance.

For a bard, it is reasonable to manage 3 combat buffs (full time) and 1 non-combat effect (half time). In a 6 person group, I will assume that 4 people benefit from the melee buffs, 2 benefit from the non-melee (i.e. mana song). This means a bard has (3*4*1)+(1*2*.5) buffs active... or 13 total..., this means each of a bards buffs take 1/13th of his continual maintenance.

In this example, it would imply that a bard is roughly 55% as effective at maintaining a number of buffs over time. Despite the facts that a bard's songs are 'free', an enchanter comes out ahead beacuse what he 'pays' in mana is more than offset by what he 'earns' in meditation.

The higher efficiency, longer lasting, post-50 buffs (even post-40) abiltiies really weaken the use of a bard within a party. These abilties need to be re-evaluated in the context of how many effective buffs are maintainable.

-------------
3. MISCONCEPTION 3: 'Bards are Group Friendly'

Ok, maybe an overstatement, but not by much. One thing that makes a class 'group friendly' is specialization. A rogue does not need to tank if a warrior is there; and a warrior and rogue together are better than 2 of either.

A Paladin is less 'group-friendly' than a Warrior primarily because he is less specialized. He can play *more* roles, but any particular role he is less good in. This makes Paladins well suited to smaller groups where the flexibility ise useful, but less valuable for larger ones, where specialization is better.

Almost anything a bard can do can be done better by another class. This makes bards less useful in a full group, where specialization is key. What catches bards is their effects are not driven by the number of people in a group, so the effciency described above goes *down* in less than a 6 man group.

Another way of statiung this is: what that makes a class group friendly is synergy. A warrior with a cleric can benefit greatly from a cleric's buffs; a paladin, less so. In a party, only the best effects really count... a warrior might be a (10-tank), a paladin might be a (8-tank, 4-healer, 4-buff), but with a cleric (10-healer, 7-buff), his skills don't help. A cleric/warrior group might be a (10-tank, 10-healer, 7-buff) group, where a cleric/paladin would be a (8-tank, 10-healer, 7-buff) group.


Looking at the above, it seems that bards are best in large, far from optimally balanced, groups, which is certainly what my experience is. In a large reasonably put together group, in a multi-group situation, or a small group, bards are usually *not* the best choice. That's not exactly what I call party friendly.

--------
ADDITIONAL COMMENTS:

Bards debuffs and crowd control are weakened by the fact that they lose 4-12 effective buffs to do these. This makes them comparitively less usefull in big fights (when it really matter!).

Bard buffs are far less reliable. Stuns, Link death, real death, lag, being spread out, or just being AFK cause them to drop.

Bard buffs have much less scope. They can't effect pets, nor can they help multiple parties.

Bards need to be evaluated in light of these limitations. There should be a corresponding benefits for these problems.


SUMMARY:
Bards need to be balanced in terms of sustainable
effects. Generalizations like 'it doesn't cost any mana', or 'you are a generalist', 'you are better than most classes in parties' should really be examined.

It may turn out on this basis if you plug the actual numbers in (I just invented some) that bards are *too* strong at points. Balancing works both ways... just make sure you are balancing based on real comparisons, not on unverified assumptions.

---> My feeling is that for a long time (~35ish) bards are fairly well balanced. At lower levels taunts should possibly be decreased, and maybe add more defensive abilities (like songs) earlier.

----> My feeling is that 35-50, bards start to slowly fall behind other support classes as they get longer lasting, more effective buffs; and they get more access to +int/wis gear that boosts maximum burn effectiveness. (i.e. a caster with 200 int can do more in a single fight than a caster with 120 int, even thought hey can't do more on average... however, a lot of the game begins to revolve around occasional rare-spawn tough fights.)

----> My feeling is that post-50 bards fall behind significantly, as other caster spells become massively longer lasting and effective.
A level 50 bard doing a mana song is not noticably less effective than a level 55 bard doing mana song (which occupies much of our lives). A level 55 warrior seems to be much, much tougher than a level 50 warrior.


SUGGESTIONS:
a) reduce the taunting of songs under 30th level (after that, you do crowd control and mez them...)

b) increase lower level dodge caps, and add parry as a lower level skill.

c) Upgrading bard songs 35-50 to be slightly more effective

d) significantly increase the effectiveness of bard songs post-50 to bring them in line with 50+ caster effects. This would involve changing the formula's for bard so effectiveness to be non-linear after 50.

e) add some combo songs that increase the number of effective buffs a bard can have going, keeping resists the same as the single effect songs (i.e. the combo should always be better than the single effect.) This effect should scale with the increased duration of caster effects.

f) make bard songs stackable with other caster effects. There is no point in having a group class that doesn't play well with other group members.

One other thought on class balancing:

Some Bard songs (party buffs, regens and travel songs) increase in power in proportion to the number of people in a group. E.g., singing healing costs the same in time spent whether a Bard is singing healing for one or singing healing for six, but in the latter case, he will be doing up to six times as much healing for the same cost.

It is not reasonable to balance these songs by looking at a Bard in an ideal 6-person party in an ideal location and then ensuring that the Bard effect is no better than the effect that another class can give. That is a sure-fire way to guarantee that Bards won't be balanced, both because: a) they won't always be with six people, b) they will rarely be in an ideal location, c) they will almost never have an ideal party makeup.

Absor's comment on the 5% haste add from the level 50 song ("[It] could easily be argued that a 5% haste, in the right party, could provide more damage than the bard's blades") illustrates this point. I can imagine a party where a bard putting away his instruments and singing a 5% stackable haste would result in more melee damage. But: 1) other than an almost-pure melee party, I can't; 2) a pure melee party would benefit much more from the bard continuing to melee and singing some alternative; 3) songs are SUPPOSED to augment party performance, not just be barely better than break-even in a very skewed maximum-sized group.

The level 50 haste add is laughable. The level 60 haste add is debatably useful perhaps at level 50, but not for a level 60 song, especially given that others in the group are going to be max-haste already.
