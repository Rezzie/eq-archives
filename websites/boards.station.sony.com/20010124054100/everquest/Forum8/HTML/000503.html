I don't think Verant will do this, because I think they are under the misguided notion this would somehow take away from the experience of exploring the world. We know they did not approve of the in game maps available in EQ-Macros so we pretty much know this will never happen.

But, in a lot of ways I think it would be a good idea. Not only would it be interesting for RP reasons, but it would actually incourage exploring. After all who goes around running into unknown territory without a care. You want to know that if you get aggregoed you can find the zone fast enough to get away! This game does NOT incourage exploration simply because of the fact that it is terribly difficult to escape high level mobs whey they are on you. If something is significantly higher than you, you don't have a chance to make it to the zone. Even if it isn't, you have to run WAY before you are going to die, because almost everything runs just as fast as you and just keeps hitting you hardly missing a beat (you'd think a running man would be harder to hit) and once your health gets to a certain point you slow WAY down. This is exacerbated by the extremely wide diversity of the mobs levels and the fact that they often tend to roam pretty much anywhere. It's true that they spawn in different locations which follow some sort of a level progression from one end of a zone to the other, but when they start wondering about that sort of system just goes right out the window. Anybody who's played on a server that isn't so old that you run into a 50th level player ever thirty seconds will recall the threat of the Griffons in East Commons. Those Griffons do NOT belong in that zone IMHO. It is mobs like them, air elementals, gouls, Dorn!, ect... that thoroughly DISCOURAGE exploration until you are high level. Of course, by then it's a mute point. By the time you are high enough level to survive a Griff attack, (much less kill it) you have long since known the layout of EC. You have to learn at the peril of your life, because you have to fight in these areas with these higher level mobs that can and will kill you. The mobs you will get experience off of are in these zones. It almost doesn't matter what the zone is, there is usually a mob or two that are much higher level than all the rest, but only a few of them. This ensures that nobody bothers camping them until the server is crowded, and even then they're not always killed. So there are a LOT of things that discourage exploration, and not too many that incourage it. Heck, I play a 19th level rogue and I hardly know anything very far from Freeport. I've been to West Karana once, Ferroot once, Across the sea to Kelethin maybe three or four times. I've visited a total of four cities included Freeport, and only explored Freeport. Why? Why not? Why should I risk my neck to explore the world? Almost everything I want is in Freeport, and when it isn't I run go get it, and then come back. I've been in two dungeons so far. I went a little ways into Befallen (which seems much too low level now). I don't care for Befallen. I find undead kinda boring. Only thing I found interesting was locked doors for me to pick, but everybody just gets keys there, so I'm no help. And, I went to LGuk to get the Mask of Deception. I think I'll do some hunting there soon if I can get a group. That's a much more interesting dungeon, although Frogloks are admittedly silly. Maps would definately encourage exploration!

Okay, enough with the justification for maps argument. Of course there is the whole point about the maps on the internet. This is both a plus and minus for cartography as a skill, IMO. First of all I think that Verant is fooling themselves if they think they are denying maps to anybody and forcing them to play the game according to the supposed "vision". (Personally, I wish they'd forget the vision and just give us what we ask for. The gameplay in EQ is very narrow, but the games appeal is very broad. This results in many players becoming disgruntled with the game and the company since the game does not allow them to enjoy themselves as it should. If Verant were a little more open minded, this would be less of a problem, but that's just my opinion.) In this way the online maps should be posative, because whatever Verant thinks they can achieve by not having in game maps is defeated by the online ones anyway, so they should give it up. There is a negative side, and it's been mentioned. Why go to the expense and trouble to build up this cartography skill when you can print off the maps online? Well, I think there should definately be some in game uses for maps. I don't know if I exactly agree with the ones mentioned, but something should be devised. I'll think about it more later.

What I really want to talk about is implementation. There have been lots of good ideas here on implementation of cartography, however most of them would have required it to have been a part of the game from the beginning. And many also just wouldn't uhhh jive with the way Verant has put the game together. So... I got some suggestiongs. First of all it could either be a diddy do nothin' little skill like fishing lures, or it could be a full blown trade skill. I'll discuss my ideas for the full blown trade and everybody can deduce simplified systems based on it. Keep in mind I just brainstormed up some ideas on this, it's not very well thought out.

Personally, I wouldn't make this skill class specific. I think just about any class should get the skill. Any race too I suppose. However, it could be available for certain classes at different levels. I like the idea of Bards and Rangers being cartographers. Personally I also especially like the idea of rogue cartographers as well. (Perfect skill complement for Hide/Sneak.) It may be a good idea to have the skill cap based on level, in fact I suggest it. Why? Well so you don't get some level 5 making maps of LGuk and selling 'em for lot's of cash to higher level players. However you want to cap it, doesn't matter that much to me, just as long as level figures in there. The skill of course would be largely int and wis based, so the dumber races will have dumber maps. It might even be a good idea to have a read maps skill that everybody else using the map would have to develop. It could be tied to or even simply be Sense Heading. (I'd make it a unique skill for many reasons, one of which is that simply spamming SH will cap it fast.)

Let's get to the mechanics, however. Most of the suggestions so far have been pretty vague, and some have suggested things that would require a great deal of work to implement. One suggestion was to make it so that the cartographer could not map out areas of the zone they had not been too. This definately would be a good idea. Otherwise sombody could map an entire zone just by sitting at the zone border. I believe that players should have the oppurtunity to use maps to aide them in learning a zone (not so detailed of maps that they wouldn't have to learn it at all), but cartographers shouldn't automatically be able to map an entire zone just because they were in it. Still, how do you implement this? Keep in mind we cannot modify the zones themselves... or rather we do not wish to. The more work this skill is to implement the more expensive it will be to do so, the longer it will take to do so, and the less likely that it will get done. (...when pigs fly anyway, but I'm talking hypothetically) Okay the mechanics... just some ideas, not really a fully conceived system. Obviously we start with the Cartography Kit item... whatever it shall be called, I don't much care. Do some research, find some name that's historically accurate to something remotely similar and go with that. How exactly the kit is made/obtained is not that big a deal, but then I don't think it should be terribly difficult. I also don't think it should be as easy as getting say, a sewing kit. This should be a reasonably difficult skill. (We don't want every other person in Norrath to be a cartographer do we?) If we're following the Verant tradition, then this trade should be quite a time/money sink, with little reward. Okay no problem, it already has to compete with online maps you can print.

Okay, so you got a high enough level for your class, trained 1 or more points into the cartography skill, and got a kit, now what do you do with it? Get components. Components should be simple, pen and paper right? Well... You need a quil, right? Maybe this item could be bought from a couple of merchants, perhaps it could also be tinkered. There might be different quils for higher quality maps too, which would be more expensive. Ink, *cough*dye*cough* sounds like this could be done in a very similar fashion. I think I'd actually make ink production a bit simpler. Let's say make ink doable via alchemists and rogues via mortar and pestle. Don't make it too tough and make the ink stackable. (hint - you're going to use a lot) Obviously, if you want to add diversity to cartography you could have different kinds of ink, possibly even enchanted ink. (mwahaha I'm getting evil with this, no wonder trade skills are hard, designing them is too fun!) Third major component, of course, is something to write on. There could be one, all encompassing item for this (parchment?) or it could likewise have different items that have different effects on the map. Imagine involving skins, pelts, parchment, getting tailors involved, ect... (This is also addicting, I'm just a hair away from making up a whole component and recipe list. I'm just crazy enought to do something like that.)

Well, that's the simple stuff. Not really the mechanics. Here's some ideas on the mechanics. First of all for every attempt pretty much everything but the quil is destroyed. You get to keep the quil, even on failures, but they are expensive. Maybe on rare failures you loose the quil too. (pure evil) It could in fact be a tinkered only item, that would be plane mean, but... Hey, look at it this way, if we're going to implement some of those nice map features talked about before then we gotta make sure there aren't too many cartographers. (...improves sense heading, ect... I would NOT have it increase movement speed. We don't want to cut into SOW's territory or Shamans will hate cartographers.) Now as for making sure Mr. or Mrs. Cartogropher actually explores the zone and only maps what they know. I don't think this would be all that difficult, actuall, but it would be somewhat less automatic. Here's my idea. Cartogrophers buy (or make) stackable sketch paper, err parchment... whatever. Small, stackable, not particularly expensive stuff. You put one standard ink container, one quil, and one sketch paper into kit and combine. This does not give you a usable map. heh Instead, you have created a sketch of one particular aspect or detail of the zone. (Provided you passed the skill check, of course.) This actually mimics what a true cartogropher (given the level of technology) would do. You are essentially taking notes, before actually attempting to map the zone. The sketch itself is of a particular feature, but you can't use it like a map. Oh and it's no drop. It would be worthless to other cartogropher's in real life, and same here. Why all this? It forces our cartogropher to walk the zone, sketching as they go. Nobody will be mapping an entire zone without taking more than ten paces into it. How does it work? Simple thing to include. Basically this part of the skill would do a /location when you make the attempt. Each zone would be split into areas and the skill would mathmatically deduce which area you are in based on your location. This would require a table of areas and coordinates for each zone, but that wouldn't be too difficult to create, and wouldn't modify any existing files. Each area could have a different difficulty to map it. For instance sketching the tunnel entrance in EC would probably be pretty easy (mainly because it is both prominant, and an entrance to another zone), but the druid thingy would be more difficult. Why different difficulties? Different recipes for the map's themselves my friend. You see, there's a second step. In the second step you need a large piece of parchment, or a skin, or whatever is goint to be used. You may need better or more or more kinds of ink as well. So you will put the large parchment, your ink, quil, and several sketches into the kit and poof. Heh, guess what happens when you fail? You get the quil back. Succeed, and you got a quil and a map. But each zone get's perhaps three different levels of quality on the maps. Low quality maps only require the low quality sketches, higher quality ones require higher quality sketches in addition to the lower quality ones. Like I said, the sketches are no drop. Now this as is requires the creation of hundreds of items. That is the weekness so far. It would be easy to scale back to only one map per zone if necessary. Also some zones may actually be unmappable. For instance the planes of fear, and hate, ect... Perhaps these are unmappable since they are not of the physical realm... *shrug* Whatever exscuse you come up for would do. Certain zones may be deemed too complex to be mapped, or certain areas, ect. There are also the secret areas. Perhaps only rogues can map the entrance to the rogue guild, and it is somehow notated in a graphic version of thieves cant. Perhaps the Dark Elves can also make such secrets. It could be that the secret maps are simply unreadable by certain races and or classes, or it might hinge on your language skills. Parts of the maps might be hidden to the non rogue who reads a map with the rogue guild marked. However you'd like to do it. It would be easier for the whole map to either be legible or illegible, but if you build in a system of hiding certain details depending upon the player trying to use the finished map, then you could also bring in a map reading skill. A higher skill would allow you to make more sense out of the map. Heck somebody with a low skill might try to read the map upside down. *grin* That would be fun. But let's keep it simple. No map reading, no hidden item within a map. You either read the map or it makes no sense to you, because of your background. We still have a whole set of sketches for every zone that have to be unique. (After all it wouldn't make sense to be able to use a "First Inn" sketch from WC to make an EC map.) That's a lot of freakin items. I've got a suggestion, lot's of people won't like, but it would work. Make most of the sketche items pretty generic like that, so that there are many, in several different zones. You would have things like "Tunnel Entrance", "West Trail", "Mountain Pass", "Docks", "First Inn", "Third Derv Camp", ect. Now these are just sketches, they are notes. In way of an excuse you simply cannot create the entire map from the mere sketches, but likewise you cannot do it without them because you can't see every detail of a large area at once. Let us say that the sketches are really very temporary things, like something you skribble on a post it note on the way to the store. If you aren't reminded what context the sketch belongs in sooner or later you will forget, and it becomes worthless. (Do you see what I'm leading up to here?) Basically, what I'm saying is that if you zone you loose your sketches. I myself don't like it, but it is the simplest solution. If you loose them every time you zone, then you must make all your sketches without leaving that zone. This means you could never have sketches from two different zones in your inventory. Eureka! Hundreds, possibly thousands of unique sketch items have just been reduced to a couple dozen. Considering the kit can only be 10 slots maximum, and you will have to use at least three slots for ink, quil, and large parchment, the most sketches a zone would ever have is 7. You could pretty easily divide each zone into seven distinct areas then come up with generic sketch items for all those areas. Most sketch items would be used by multiple zones. Of course, if we wanted to add some complexity you could have to make intermediate maps from the sketches then combine those to make the entire zone map. I would suggest this, because the seven sketch upper limit is assuming we only use one container of one kind of ink. If we want to do anything with that we will reduce the slots available for the final combine to make the finished map. Keep in mind that we wanted three different levels of quality on the maps, dictated mainly by the how many and which sketches are used. These intermediate maps could be readable, though. Wouldn't that be a kick. Or it could move in stages. You could make a low quality map, then put it, ink, quil, and more sketches in, and try to make a higher quality map. Fail, and oops, there goes your map! Lot's of options here. As far as the zoning thing... well, I don't like it either, personally. So here's another idea, but it might be too difficult to implement. We still want to force the player to only work on one zone at a time, but allow them to go through other zones if need be. So (one way or another) we have to destroy the sketches from one zone to start mapping another zone. Well, let's say we do it this way. Let's say that you have to start a map layout type of sketch first. (This would be just vague outlines and showing the locations of the detail sketches in relation to each other and other landmarks IRL.) It would be simple, but large. So it would be something like, ink, quil, large parchment or skin. You hit combine and poof, "East Commons Map Layout" is created. We want to make sure it is in inventory every time a sketch is made, and it becomes an actual component for when you combine the sketches. (Probably the easiest way to make sure it's in inventory while sketches are created is to make it a component for each sketch attempt, but returned like the quil.) The key is that whenever you create a new Map Layout, it destroys any sketches you have (as well as any layout you may have). Plus you must be in the zone pertaining to the Map Layout you have. A much more complicated solution, but it would let you zone out, go to the bank or whatever, zone back in and continue mapping. I don't much like that one either, honestly. I think it would probably be better to make sketches vanish on zoning, but let cartogropher's make partial maps from just a few of the sketches, then combine those for the full map. Or go the quality levels way and make it easy to make the Low Quality map, then you add sketches to it to bring it up a level. (This would actually be fairly accurate as a real cartogropher would make a fairly vague map, then add in details in waves, the finest details would be the hardest to do, and would be last.) You might need different inks and/or different quils for each level as well. (Again, fairly realistic finer details require sharper points and smother flowing ink.) This also adds the possibility of having different cartogrophers pass a map around in order to finish it. The newbie cartogropher passes it to a friend who's skill is higher so that he can add in the details the lower level guy would fail on. Then you could make certain details class specific for secret/specialty maps. For instance a druid or a ranger could add interesting insights to certain maps. Iskar might be able to add interesting things to Kunark maps, and Barbarians might have some insights to add to maps of their native lands. As well, Dark Elves may choose to obscure maps of their home, and rogues may wish to secretly document certain things. A master fisher might add some details too, it need not be specifically race and/or class based, but skill as well. Of course, it would be practical to keep the number of specialty maps to a minimum to keep the item count low. One problem with maps is they will take up lots of inventory space. Even if you make maps of a single zone stackable (and I probably wouldn't just because I think that's the way Verant would go with it) there would still be at least one map for almost every zone. That is a whole lot of maps. If you get lemons make lemonaide I say... in other words. Allow master cartogrophers to make map books. Something like "The lands of Kunark". You would have to combine several HQ Maps, plus some other supplies (binding, cover material, book making stuff). Fail, and you ruin a bunch of GOOD maps, so I would make sure there were plenty of items to get your skill pretty high without even trying this. But, you are still going to loose some maps. Succeed, and you have a book. Each page you turn flips to the next map. If you made these light enough to carry, and they did have some gameplay effects (perhaps through enchanting) then I would imagine the books would be fairly rare and sell for quite a bit. Individual maps would be much more common, but you could only have so many before you started really wasting inventory space.

Even if it didn't make much profit, it would be a lot of fun. I also think it would actually make the game more fair. As is people with printers and/or multiple computers have a huge advantage over players that do not. The ability to look at reference material from websites while playing is invaluable. This wouldn't go away, but it would make available to those underpriveleged players the ability to consult a map without logging out of the game.

Okay, I'm sure there are tons of things I didn't think about, and there must be lots of other ways to do things. I just thought this stuff up on the spot, mostly. But, anyway, let's discuss it. Whether or not Verant ever does this, I had fun just coming up with ways they could. Hell, I'd voluntere to come up with the new item lists and new recipe's personally. I'd even wander around the zone's using the /loc command to set up tables for all the seperate areas manually. heh (They would have to give me a GM login to do it, my poor rogue would not survive!) Hey, I said I was crazy. Well, I'm sure that won't happen, just saying I'd do it is all.

BTW Sorry for bad grammar and/or spelling in this. I didn't intend it to become an essay!

My thoughts:

1) Maps should be a trade skill, available at lvl 1 to any player, like fishing.

2) Mapping should require that a quill be held in the primary hand (like a fishing pole), a "map" held in the secondary hand, and automatically use components (ink), just as bait is used when fishing.

3) A blank "map" is a simple piece of parchment. Existing maps can be augmented to add detail for the area where the mapping occurs.

4) When a player hits the "map" key, a skill check occurs and the map is either improved (or not) for that area of the zone. Failure should result in the loss of the map ("Your ink runs, rendering the map useless"), much like a failure in fishing results in a broken pole. Not improving is not necessary a failure in this sense.

5) Maps should be trade-able, and not stackable unless bound into a book. Binding should be a 1-time operation, like combining potions.

6) Only complete maps could be combined into books.

Implementing cartography this way would be fairly easy to imlement as there are only as many items as there are zones, and each zone could have a fixed number of bits in the code for a completeness mask (the programmers know what I mean here). The item database doesn't have to grow much this way, nor does the character database to keep track of the skill.

Some of the realism is sacrificed in this way, but a nice new trade skill is added for those that enjoy that sort of thing, and folks that just want to explore can see some pay off for that effort. Guild could offer map books of different regions to junior guild members. Lastly, we could see some standardization of place names by how the maps were implemented ("Derv camp #1" becomes something more in line with the roleplaying aspect of the game).

My thoughts for what they're worth.

Hmmm... a lengthy response. I don't see that sketches are fundamentally any better than the scheme I proposed. That's just an opinion of course, but one I'm entitled to.

There is a difference between "items" and detail. Pelts are all generally the same item. Because a pelt can't be both bear and cat, LQ and HQ, it requires fewer bits to save that data. Only one graphic with different colors is required for all the various pelts.

In the case of maps, let's take 8 distinct areas of a zone. That requires one byte of data to store. If you had mapped areas 2, 3, and 5, it would look like this in the bit mask: 00010110

If there are less than 255 zones in the world (I have no idea off the top of my head), then you could keep track of those in the item database with another byte of data. If they use the graphic for parchment (add scribbles for a map and track that with one extra bit in the data type), they wouldn't even have to add an item, just expand what they already have. There are many, many book types, so adding another type is trivial.

Buttons vs containers, equipping vs not. I don't really care which way they do it, but a button is the way they do fishing. Like fishing, there is no reason you would *have* to break out of hide to map. that is handled by a function call, which they have complete control over. Would you be vulnerable to attack while mapping? Yes, but then, what cartographer with his map spread out before him is ready to jump up and engage in battle.

No skill will ever be the way everyone wants it, but the scheme I described is eminently programmable with minimal effort and achieves many of the features you described. I am a programmer, I can see how they might have programmed the other trade skills and modelled my suggestion on how I would probably implement this feature.
