"Only the person playing /gems sees it. It has nothing to do with whether or not you are roleplaying."

That I must reply to in the interests of game design, as an industry developer for over a decade now.

Every single caster in the game, which leaves out maybe 3 classes, has this available to them, in-game, in-environment, and if it goes live it will be in-conversation. Just the existence of it on Test means that it has invaded the meta-game of EQ on these discussion boards, taking us further away from a cohesive fantasy environment.

The screen environment we play in has nice borders, class-specific graphics on our info screens, period costuming, and spells/effects with appropriate names.

The world we inhabit has swamps, deserts, mountains and forests populated by a wide variety of mythical and mundane beasts, castles and cities, caves and dungeons.

All elements should have purpose, and add to the fit-and-finish of the player experience. Inconsistancy glares, tearing attention away from the experience and towards the ridiculous oversight or mistake.

When we see "Bait Masterson" and like-named in-game personalities it is a slap in the face that wakes us up from the pleasing fantasy of an exotic locale.

And now, to compensate for grievous and over-bearing down-time meditation wherein all casting classes must sit and stare at a book, we are given TETRIS. A modern arcade game embedded directly into our experience of EQ.

A glaring, attention ripping artifice attempting to make up for extremely boring gameplay for low-level casters.

I always leave a suggestion, in an effort to be part of the solution and not yet-another-gripe-artist:

1- Make meditation separate from the spell book, which is used to inscribe or memorize new spells.

2- Disallow actions other than cancel-meditate selectively based on level. eg. a lvl 5 caster can just sit, while a lvl 10 caster can visually check his inventory, and a lvl 35 finally can do all he can do now while sitting and auto-meditating.

3- Let people see what is going on, but tone-down the "sampling" of the world outside, dim the 3d part of the display, muffle the sounds, based on level.
