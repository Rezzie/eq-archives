For those of you that are interested, go to this website and read the RFC's that pertain to how the internet backbone is made up www.faqs.orq Some very interesting reading and very technically orientated towards the rules and standards on how data packets pass through the internet.

For those of you that run DSL, cable, and other conntections remember the connection for your dsl/cable is ONLY to the point of presence that your provider has to the internet, it is not a direct route to the eq servers. So while you have it(and I am one of them with DSL), it only gets you to your provider faster, it is still subject ot the vagrances of the internet backbone routers.

For those of you that say well 200 of us, or my whole guild just went LD, it is a problem with the servers. Maybe, but not a solid statement. Lets take the following senario.

Players 1-100 - live on the east coast of the US
Players 101-200 - live in the southeast of the US
Players 201-300 - live in the midwest area

All 300 of the players are in different areas and the chances of them using the same ISP is minimal. They all connect to everquest via thier dsl, frac t1, oc3, dialup, cable modems. Playing along with no problems.

Now, even though they are in different locations of the US, using different providers, different forms of thier initial location. They all have something in common with their connection - what is that? Easy, they all have to pass their data through the internet backbone. Now the their data route may start off different, but because of the structure of the backbones, at some point in route the will ALL pass through the same router, maybe it is the last hop to everquest, or 3 hops back, it is the same router.

All of a sudden, this shared router has a problem or is taken OOS for service(happens often in the early morning hours). Suddenly all 300 of the players get a massive lag spike, go LD, disconnect.

Given the above senario, it is true that no matter where you are, who is your provider, what your connection is, you may all be affected by a single point of failure on the internet. This will not always be the servers, but a common router in your datapath.

Lets face it, the current makeup of the internet was not designed for the traffic that it is being hit with nowadays. Not much that can be done about that, arpnet, the base design for todays internet was created to pass data back and forth between military computers.

Something to think about
