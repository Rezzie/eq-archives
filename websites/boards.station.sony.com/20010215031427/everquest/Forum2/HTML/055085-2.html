This is a good point.

There's a rule of thumb in much of software development called the 80/20 rule. You try to write your software to handle 80% of the cases pretty well. It's likely that the remaining 20% of the situations will be FAR more complex, harder, or something and prohibitevely difficult/expensive/time consuming to do well. But you put most of your effort in making sure you do 80% of the situations well.

Applying the 80/20 rule to EQ, I would suggest Verant spend most of their time making sure they handle 80% of the players well. The remaining 20% (including the 10% ahead of the curve and the 10% behind the curve) you only do things for if they don't mess up what you did for the 80%.

A very good discussion...

I have thought for a while on this and feel that the uber/non-uber disparity within the game is now irrepairable.

There is no real solution to it apart from a complete revamp of many of the basic game coding. Unfortunalty, this is not a commercially viable solution for Verant/Sony, it is simply not worth their effort.

The only way that the development of the game can continue is with an injection of revenue, ie, an expansion. It is all well and good to talk about what is best for the game etc. but we must never forget that we are dealing with a commercial product and that profit is the bottom line.

That said, lets look at what we can do with an expansion.

The introduction of new interfaces, graphics etc. is an easy one to add... most expansions are expected to include these.

So it comes down to content. First, set your boundaries. You must create content up to the highest level.. and a little bit more. This is to 'advance' the game. It must be only slightly higher than the current highest level in order to allow room for your next expansion.

This is where we hit a major problem. The origional top guilds have had many months to build up equipment from the very highest levels of the game. Areas that once took 50+ people to sweep clean now take 25. This is not only due to equipment but also the very high level of skill and coordination that well drilled uber guilds can attain. This kind of level is already out of the reach of the general population.

A slightly lesser guild might well be able attempt the kind of 50 people raids that the uber guilds are now finding straightforward but are unable to do so with an regularity due to the constraints on spawn times. They are trying to 'catch up' but unfortunatly, cannot match the current uber guilds in sheer zone dominance.

So, the game designers have there 'upper boundary' to work from. The best loot goes to the very top of this boundary. The organisation and skill required is also set from this upper level. Remember that this upper boundary is slightly higher than what the designers think the uber guilds are capable of. These zones are created to be uber deadly.

The lesser guilds are practically excluded from them due to the dangers of complete character loss there. The uber guilds are keen to get in there, more for prestige value than anything else.

The developers begin to work downwards from the upper boundary and create lower level zones. They set the zone difficulty based upon the first ones they have created, the uber+ zones. These zones are more difficult than the current ones that already exist for the same level as the developers expect eveyone to eventually get better (better equipped, more skilful).

The problem is, the general population doesn't want to work harder than they currently do for same reward. Even the lesser guilds have constraints on time/people that force them away from the new zones. All we are left with is an uber playground.

Eventually, the top guilds begin to explore and recover loot for the very top level zones. Increasing the gap even further.

They also begin to reap the rewards of the slightly easier zones. The 50-55 zones are so difficult that they require equipment from the old top level zones even to survive in... these zones are now straightforward to the now uber+ guilds (new term.. uber+ = no zone in the game requires more than 30 members to reap).

The other 95% of the population is left to scramble around in the old zones trying to attain a level that is forever beyond them.

Is there a solution to this ?? Quite frankly, no. It is a vicious circle. Now that this disparity exists, it can only increase as the developers try to 'advance' the upper level of the game. 'Advance' in EQ means more hitpoints, more resistant, more damaging, more, more, more. It will eventually reach a point where you require certain loot from a MoB before you can defeat it !!! This will be the ultimate outcome.

So the future of the game has already been decided. There will be an elite cadre of guilds who can access 100% of the game. The rest of us will have to settle with whatever is left after these guilds are finished with it. Future expansions will also be no longer viable. The customer base that will buy them will be too small to justify the investment.

These problems were created from the very roots of the game. With all the good intentions in the world these problems are practically impossible to avoid. MuDflation is not a game problem, it stems from human nature and is no ones 'fault'. It's too easy to ignore what is happening to the game in the early stages and agree to 'look at them later'. Once the concrete has set... thats it.

Eventually, as new games begin to take over EQ's throne, similar problems could develope in the next generation of games unless they are VERY careful. This will require a much more long term view of the systems they are producing, and I think this is beginning to happen.

When we are playing our total immersion games in 30 years from now and commenting on how perfectly balanced they are remember that EQ has taught us many lessons.

Just my 2cp

One of the greatest culprits that I've seen which promotes the disparity between the 'haves' and 'have nots' is truely in large part the game engine.

Though it is too late to change the system now, let me express a possible solution for fixing this problem.

The "David and Goliath" scenario should be possible. Why couldn't an aimed shot find gaps in armor and pierce the mere mortal underneath? Certainly a determined 'nobody' with a fair amount of skill, determination, and a little luck can overcome the greatest of champions; though currently this isn't the case. A level 40 will perpetually defeat a level 20 without breaking a sweat.

I'm not saying that the playing field should be totally even, but rather that level should almost solely improve *skills* that promote your survival rather than simply making you 150 times as hearty as the peon just starting out, allowing you to absorb hit after hit while the lowly drop like stones. The notion that one should avoid being hit altogether seems to not even dawn on some who develop these sort of titles!

With all certainty, this system would promote it's own problems to work out before implementation: Would one critical hit, say a major wound to the neck, kill always? Would the mage, capable of igniting to cinders his unfortunate victim without flinching, be assailable?

Though I've not pondered further in this direction, I'm certain a think-tank out there somewhere can conjure an answer or three. I'll just leave it to the reader for the present.
