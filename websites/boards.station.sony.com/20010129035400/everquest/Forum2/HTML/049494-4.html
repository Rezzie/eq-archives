I posted in the first thread, but there's a point I feel needs to be made, so I'll post again.

I keep seeing the words 'roleplaying' used as a means to justify upgrades to existing abilities and new abilities altogether.

Now don't get me wrong - I'm a roleplayer and have been for 25 years now. I've worked in the RPG industry and been heavily involved in scenario and character design for tournaments. As such, I've seen many a player try to justify things that are massively unbalancing in the name of roleplaying.

One of the things that bothers me is the idea of a non-aggro charm animals spell. Yes, it makes plenty of sense to be able to 'befriend animal,' but, in my opinion, this would be horribly unbalancing. Where's the risk versus reward? No risk charms of NPC Mobs (which are far more powerful than pet NPCs) is just a free damage dealer/meat shield pet for a class that already has no problem soloing. If a charm animal breaks now, druids can easily run away with sow and snare it, so even tho' it's not 'a proper roleplaying scenario,' there isn't that much of a risk involved.

Compare this to enchanters who don't have the sow to escape when the charm (their only means of soloing) breaks.

Roleplaying is all well and good and the basis of any well written class, but game balance has to be taken into consideration as well. For example, a well known and classic 'trick' of mind controlling (charming)magic is to cause the victim to fall on his sword and kill himself.

But if you gave enchanters a /pet suicide command, it would be far too unbalancing. Perfectly within roleplaying parameters and in line with the type of spell, but not balancing.

Since the issue at hand is balancing druids, try to keep that in mind when making any changes to this or any class.

My .02

SG

Most everyone I know feels that there should be more than one viable healing class in the game and that shaman and druid should fill that role.

Maybe a shaman can in some cases fill that role, thanks to slow, but in many more places they still cannot. There are almost no cases where a druiid can fill that role. The game would be much more fun for everyone, clerics included, if more things could be done, albeit less efficiently, with a shaman or druid as healer. These days just about anyone will take a jerk cleric over a nice druid or shaman, even if they are friends with the druid and dislike the cleric. Need I say how messed up that is!

If both classes are increased to be 66% to 75 % as efficent as a cleric, then we'd be in shape, though shaman would still be preferable due to slow, which is fine.

If not though, at least show some caring for the druid class by giving them something that is as generally desirable to groups as slow is for shaman. IE not THAT important, since the enchnater can handle it anyway, but still pretty darn nice to have if possible.

All druids REALLY need is something that will make groups want them, healing would be the best way to do it, as it would reduce the frustration that results from a single class having a chokehold on all high level endeavors.

Ask a hundred high level raid organizers they will tell you that druids, as a class, "suck" and are not needed. Many guilds have closed membership to future druid apllicants due to the fact that they are nothing more than a taxi cab class. Two druids cannot fill the primary healer role even close to as well as one cleric.

Perhaps a good way to deal with this would be to simply give druid and shaman a celstial heal type spell at 44, leave sup at 53 and make a BIG accross the board reduction to post 50 fizzle rates.

Just give groups a reason to want a druid. Every class should be wanted. Yes, some should be a bit MORE wanted. But since this is a game designed around grouping... Ultimate balance, and player satisfaction is going to hinge on everyone being in the same ballpark(roughly) when it comes to group desireability.


Not everyone for everything. Having some encounters where a certain class is strongly advantageous, is GREAT. Having some encounters where a certain class is particularly weak is ok too. Some classes should have more cases where they are less desirable, due to other benefits of their class. But this narrowing of the game to the point that basically anything other than the ideal cleric/warrior/enchanter/rogue lineup feels like a waste, just isn't good for the game, or fair to the players.

The post 50 game is where people will ultimately be spending their time, and having some classes that are 90 times more desirable/less desirable than others at this point isn't healthy to player morale, or fair, or fun.

Group desirability in so group-centric a game is the only "power" a class has that matters, and is the key to any effort to finalize class balance in any real sense.
