Tonight I will show you what I intend to do with the data. If any statisticians want to jump in, feel free. My statistics is rusty, so realize that I may have some errors in what follows. But I don’t think my reasoning is fundamentally faulty.

I am assuming that trade skills follow a binomial distribution with a probability of success that depends on the trade skill level, wis/int/str and the difficulty of the Combine attempt. This is called the “null hypothesis”. It is what we have to disprove for Lirolar’s hypothesis to be accepted. So, what I want to know is this: if I run a test and get some data, what is the probability that I would get results at least as good, if the null hypothesis were true. That probability is called the “significance level.” Often 5% is considered a reasonable significance level. That is, if I would need to redo the test 20 times to get results that better match the null hypothesis, then the null hypothesis is brought into question.

If that made sense, I will go a step further. Let’s say the probability of success on even wis/int/str is Pe and the probability of success on odd wis.int/str is Po. Now say the difference, Pd, is Pe-Po. Now say that my new hypothesis (H1) is that Pd = x where x is 0% or 5% or –5% or anything else. My new question becomes, for what range of x, is H1 significant. We can make a “confidence interval” that is 95% likely to contain the true value of Pd. The more data, the tighter the interval. So here is a purely made up exercise:

Let’s say I make Heady Kiolas (HK) with wis of 100 and a wis of 101 and that I am trivial in HK. I get 48HK of 50HK at wis=100 and 42HK of 50HK at wis = 101. (These are made up.) What range of x is 95% likely to contain the true Pd? The mean successes were 0.96 and 0.84. Their standard deviations are (for binomial sqrt(N* (P-1)*P) where N is the number off attempts and P is probability of success) 0.1960 and 0.3666 or 1.3856HK and 2.5923HK. If I subtract the distributions, I get difference in means of 6HK per 50 attempts with a standard deviation of 2.9394HK (s.d. = sqrt(variance) and when adding/subtracting distributions, variances add.)

Now, assuming that the binomial distribution can be represented as a normal distribution, and plugging into Excel with the NORMINV function with probabilities of 0.025 and 0.975 (95%), mean of 6, s.d. of 2.9394, I get in 95% confidence interval of 0.24HK to 11.76HK for each 50HK because of the difference in wis. Since 0HK lies outside this range, these data would be not be inconsistent with a claim that there is a difference between even and odd wisdom.

Notice that there are several places where there are potential problems. This is why it is easy to lie with statistics. I could make the claim that the difference in wisdom is the important factor rather than the fact that is even or odd. More tests would be needed. I could claim that one run of HKs was done one day and the other another day, and that is the important factor. More tests would be needed. I could claim that since 50 were done in East Freeport and the other 50 were done in East Freeport, that is the difference. I could claim that the results were selectively tested only after the tester saw that they might support the hypothesis—sort of like flipping a coin and testing if it loaded after a streak of Heads and then using that streak to show that the coin is unfair.

Tests are done. Thus EQ science marches on.

My point is not to dazzle people into believing something. It is to show how you might support Lirolar’s hypothesis. You need math to turn anecdotal evidence into empirical evidence. And you need to account for other forms of bias over time.

So next I went to the Intel website and downloaded a couple of Intel Pentium Architecture manuals from http://developer.intel.com/design/pentiumii/manuals/243190.htm and 243191.htm.

I guessed that the reason for the use of the FPU was just more bits than the standard IMUL and MUL. So I found the description of FIMUL. It states that the FIMUL converts the integer to an extended real which is an 80 bit floating point number.

So, it is converted to floating point! It’s sort of a trivial floating point because the integer fits into the mantissa of the extended real. My guess is that since they have the big multiplier for the FPU anyway, they just make use of the already existing hardware and package the integer as an extended real. Becoming a floating point number is just the price of admission to the big multiplier. A sort of fake ID to get into the transistor party.

So why use FIMUL instead of MUL? Is it faster? I guess OutlawPhantom is right. I can only speculate. But in the end, the particular implementation and Intel’s hardware design techniques do not change the fact that the RNG algorithm is most likely an integer based algorithm with no FP rounding problems. Note that the conversion to FP is hardware specific. Other processors may not do that.

However, I find it hard to accept that one character's stats are used to define the seed or the random number generator itself.

Think like a programmer for a second. You have the tasks of defining the probability for success, and providing a random number to check against it. The success formulas would likely be a function of your skill, your stats, and the trivial level of the item. The random number generator is already available in the language/compiler provided for you, so you use that. Why would you go through the extra effort of customizing the generator with character stat values that are already factored into the success formulas?

There is also the question as to whether there is a single server wide psuedo random number generator, or lots of independent generators created by zone, or by character. Is it a psuedo random number generator software routine, or a look up table?

Excellent, Discordia. Your name doesn't fit your presentation It appears that the even numbered attempt did have a slightly higher success rate. But is it significant? Just looking at the results, I can guess the answer is no. But, let’s do the analysis and put some numbers on it. (I’m not sure if the tabs are going to line up right, but here goes)

Trials Attempts Success p q npq s.d.
1 28 25 0.892857 0.107143 2.678571 1.636634
2 28 24 0.857143 0.142857 3.428571 1.85164
1-2 56 1 0.017857 6.107143 2.471263

p = Success/Attempts
q=1-p
npq = attempts * p * q
s.d = sqrt(npq)

The ‘1-2’ trial adds the attempts, subtracts the Success, adds ‘npq’ and takes the square root of npq for the standard deviation. Now, assuming the binomial distribution can be represented as a normal distribution, and using the Excel NORMINV function, I get a 90% confidence interval of –3.06 to +5.06 (NORMINV(0.05,1,2.471263) to NORMINV(0.95,1,2.471263)). That means that the actual number of increased successes per 28 attempts is 90% likely to lie between –3.06 and +5.06.

Since 0 lies within the confidence interval, it is safe to say that the test does not disprove the null hypothesis that there is no effect on even/odd.

Lets look at the skill point increases for kicks. Assuming that you can increase on a failure even though it wasn’t observed, we get:

Trials Attempts Success p q npq s.d.
1 28 3 0.107143 0.892857 2.678571 1.636634
2 28 1 0.035714 0.964286 0.964286 0.981981
1-2 56 2 0.035714 3.642857 1.908627

I get a 90% confidence interval of –1.14 to +5.14 increased skill points per 28 attempts with the stats mentioned in Discordia’s post. Again, 0 lies in the confidence interval, so it is safe to say that these data do not support the hypothesis that even differs from odd.

It would be interesting to know how many samples would need to be done to notice a significant difference in combine success if the even/odd effect showed 1 in 28. For a 90% confidence interval, the answer is about 463 at each wisdom. For a 95% confidence interval it is 657 combines at each wisdom. When the difference is larger, not so many are needed, so don't give up hope.

Since there are "lies, damned lies, and statistics," lets look at some of the questions we should have.
1) The methodology was designed to eliminate streakiness. Perhaps an even/odd effect has less to do with probability than with maintaining streaks of success.
2) Could there be an effect from the skill increases themselves?
3) Is a normal distribution a reasonable approximation for a binomial distribution?

As for the Einstein-God argument, there may be a real effect of believing in an even/odd effect if one doesn’t exist. Some people may be leaving off significant items of jewelry because it makes their stats odd. They may be having extra failures because they aren't wearing their jewelry.

Better, Sterma, but still not really testable. In order to demonstrate the even/odd hypothesis, you need to remove other effects. If you do 400PP worth of combining at an even INT and get 2 increases and then 400PP at an odd INT and get 14 increases, you haven't eliminated at least one important source of bias. Maybe you got more increases because you crossed some number where skill point increases come faster. Maybe you would have gotten about 2 increases in to first 400PP at odd and about 14 in the second 400PP at even. If you were to have switched back and forth between even and odd several times during 800PP of making malachite and gold, then you would have tested the hypothesis.

So, making my own judgements...the data is not quite sufficient to show anything despite the additional information you provided (400PP in Malachite and Gold).
