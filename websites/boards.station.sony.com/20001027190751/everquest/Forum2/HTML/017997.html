Well, I looked all over my desk, flipped through the EQ manual, and even looked in the EQ box, but I couldn't find a copy of the EQ server source code anywhere. I guess I'll just have to make some assumptions as to how the data storage is handled, and you'll just have to get someone from the dev team to tell me why my idea won't work.

You have stated before that NO RENT items cannot have a timer attached to them when a person goes linkdead due to the lack of a place to store the data. Well, a thought occurred to me today as to how this could be handled. I have noticed that buffs like Arch Shielding stay on me after I camp. When I log back on, buffs don't always last the same amount of time. Sometimes, they last only a few seconds, sometimes several minutes. It seems as though a 30 minute spell will run for 2 minutes after logging back on if you had it active for 28 minutes before camping. Now, given that there are hundreds of spells, each of the 15 "spell effect" slots must have 16 bits allocated to them just to denote which effect is present there. The "timer" effect that I have witnessed would indicate a minimum of 8 additional bits. This means that each of the 15 slots have a minimum of 24 bits of data storage that is held even after camping. If this is the case, then I would suggest adding a new "spell effect" that is immediately placed upon players when they go linkdead. This spell effect would be placed in the bottom (15th) spell effect slot, overwriting any buff if necessary. The "spell effect" would contain a timestamp of the date and time of the linkdeath. When the player logs back on, the current date and time is compared with the "linkdeath effect", and if it is more than 15 minutes or so later, any NO RENT items are destroyed. As for pets, well that might be more complicated but perhaps pet data could be stored in an additional spell slot for pet classes. Now, as for data storage....

(Assumptions of data storage method)
16 bits -> index of spell effect type
8 bits -> effect duration timer (minutes?)

The 16 bits could uniquely identify 65535 spell effects. I doubt there are this many effects in EQ. If you use up the MSb as a flag to denote LD timer value (set to 1) or spell effect (set to 0), then that still leaves you with 32767 uniquely representable spell effects. If the MSb is a 1, then EQ identifies the remaining 23 bits as a date and time stamp. With 23 bits, representing day and minute, having 1440 minutes per day and 365 days per year, 23 bits represent values from 0 to 8388607, allowing the timestamp to be valid for up to 15.96 years.

Basically, my point is that if there are at least 3 available bytes in a single spell slot or in ALL spell slots combined, I believe players would not mind being stripped of some or all buffs during linkdeath to have a chance at keeping NO RENT items and possibly pets too.

So, Abashi, what do you think?

My word, clambake, this seems to have hit a sore spot with you.

Actually, I tend to disagree with you. Bugs cannot usually be fixed on a daily basis. I happen to think having a patch every two weeks is pretty good for a software product.

It's debatable whether the issue at hand, losing items to linkdeath, is even a bug, per se. Though it'd be nice to have it fixed.

However, speaking as a software engineer, when it comes to bugs, there are degrees. There are the "Oh %$^&, we have to fix this as soon as we can" bugs, the ones that you fix in the next scheduled release, and there are even ones that you don't fix - sometimes no-one's complained, sometimes it's a rare bug which would take too much effort to fix, sometimes there's a satisfactory work-around and you'd rather spend the effort on developing new features/products.

Just because people who read these boards have suggestions for quick bug fixes, doesn't mean that it'd actually work in EQ's case - we don't know how things are configured. Or maybe they have their own reasons for not hot-swapping out drives - it's probably a lot more reliable to take all the servers down together and patch them all at the same time. Yes, this is more downtime for us and as a British player, it eats into my prime playing time.

Is it possible to fix bugs quickly and apply the fixes almost immediately? Maybe, but that doesn't mean that it's feasible or even desirable to do so.
