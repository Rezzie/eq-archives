Actually dogs are not colour blind in the true sense of the word. (if you were truly colour blind, you would only see white (all light) or black(no light) reflected off the object).
Dogs (and most colour blind people) actually see in shades of gray, depending on the colour of the object (absorbed/reflected light) and the condition of the surrounding area. That is the 'dividing' lines between objects of similar colours blur, and the objects are seen as one shade of gray.
If the Colours looked at are VERY different, the the colour blind person, will see each object as a definate shade of gray. If the colours are very similar then the person will see some of those objects as one shade (or similar shades) of gray, and find it hard to distinguish between two or more objects.

Well thats my scientific answer to *nudge* the post.

But there are also cases of colour deaf, as well.

For example, my father is colour deaf. He can pick out all the colours of the rainbow, tell them apart, etc. I showed him a chart of states yesterday from CNN that had states coloured green and orange, he could tell them apart a glance; my colour-blind friend could not.

But he is colour deaf; he'll put together two or more colours that clash terribly and not be able to understand why people thing it's ugly; conversely you can put two things that "go" together, and he won't understand why.

But there are also different degrees of colour blindness: I wore a green sock and a blue sock on a date once, the shades and tones were similar, but the colour blind date could tell the difference right away.
