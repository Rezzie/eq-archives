heh heh, I had Dungeons of Daggorath on my Trash-80 Coco. :P

It was basically a ripoff of Wizardry from the Apple][ a couple years back, only a much lighter game because Wizardry was pretty complex for a dungeon crawler.

Daggorath was neat though because the SOUND was cool. You had two hands (duh), and you could "Pull Left Torch" and "Pull Right Sword" to pull these items from your backpack so you could see and fight.

The sounds were way cool in Dungeons of Daggorath. You could track a monster in 3D throughout the maze (a 3-dimensional maze with multiple levels, deeper being harder) by the sound alone. This game was sort of a primitive turns-based game much like the 1995 title "Stonekeep" was.

As a matter of fact, except for the primitive graphics, Daggorath was just about identiacl to Stonekeep in many ways! But basically Wizardry was the Grandaddy of all of these turns-based 3D graphics computer games, I guess even EQ owes Wizardry a tip of the hat.

My first RPG: ADVENT - or Colossal Caves. You're a spelunker who is exploring the Colossal Caves in Kentucky, when you start finding treasure, fighting dwarves and tracking pirates. This came out in 1976 for the PDP-10 mainframe, which is where I found it when I went to the University of New Hampshire in 1979... Everyone there was caught up in the game. It was called ADVENT by everyone because on the PDP-10, file names could only be six letters (plus a three letter extension).

The electrical engineering department had a Vax with another RPG on it called Dungeon, inspired by ADVENT. That one took us a couple of months to work through; then it was expanded and released as Zork I a few years later.

Chris Caldwell was the local computer genius who wrote the first multi-user "graphical" RPG I'd ever seen, called Caves. We were all avid D&D players. The graphics were just ASCII graphics on terminals.

We could never get ROGUE working for some reason, but that was another RPG out around then. Later released commercially in an excellent adaptation for the Atari ST.
