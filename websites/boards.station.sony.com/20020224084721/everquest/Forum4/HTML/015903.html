I have never counted when trying to twist songs.

I start song one and wait for the pulse (you'll see the spell effects and the text message on your screen). Those are what I use as my clues that it is time to stop song 1 and start song 2.

Eventually you'll not even need too look for the text or the spell effects, you'll just get a "feel" for when it is time to stop one song and start the next.

There is a definite rhythm to twisting. Just keep practicing and soon you'll be doing it without even giving it a conscious thought.

IMHO: Don't use Macros to twist your songs. Keep up with what your doing. If your song misses, you macro misses a song. Plus, manually switching allows you to adjust your twisting on the fly.

You mentioned instrument usage. Use Instrument switching thru your hotkey pad. For example: Have your Primary, Secondary and Inventory slots as hotkeys. Your fighting and want to take off running quick... your 3 clicks away from cruising to the beat of your drum. (grab Primary weapon, switch with drum in inventory, and switch drum with your shield). Use the same method with other songs that excel with instruments.

.... oh, and make sure you stretch your fingers out as much as you can. A Bard's Carpel Tunnel comes quick and often.

If you use a macro such as the one described above, you usually can twist faster and better, with more ability to chat with your group too. You do NOT get stuck if you miss a note; by the time you miss the note, the macro is long over, and you can manually start the song over (I usually use the Alt keys in this case, but sometimes I click instead). If your system is fast enough that you don't need to insert a /pause 1 line in between the /stopsong and the /cast #, you can usually get away with just hitting the macro key again after missing the note.

Been playing this way for 52 levels... no, that's not right; I started using those macros somewhere in the 20's (when they first put the /pause command in) on my old server, and got to level 35 there before I started my bard over on Test. She's 52 now. And I tell ya, I get a LOT fewer "you have not recovered yet" messages, and I can twist a LOT faster (because I'm not waiting until I'm pretty sure I wont get that message) with these hotkeys.

I don't recommend any macro that waits until the song has pulsed to do something. Make sure the very last thing it does is /cast (whatever) (unless you want a line of text in there, like /g CHARMING %t). If you try to put more after the /cast, like pausing until it pulses and then doing another song, then you can almost never use the chat bar and, as mentioned, you can't recover from missed notes or stuns very well at all.

BTW, mine reads slightly differently than the one above, it is just:

/stopsong
/pause 1
/cast 1 (~8)

I've never had it NOT stop the song with only one time issuing the command, but if you have a lot of PL it may be a good idea to repeat it.

Macroes could work for common situations, but keep them in a seperate bank, and do keep your spell gems hotkeyed in case you need to make some situational twists.

Twisting 3 songs is about as good as you can consistently get. If you can do that at your level, you are doing quite well at becoming a good bard. The next step is to add instruments to the equations. Hotkey your weapon slots (yes, you can map item slots to hotkeys) as well as at least one inventory slot (in which case it should contain a bag that you will ONLY use to keep your instruments and primary weapons). Keep the bag open while fighting. When starting a new song, switch to the hotkeys, swap out your current weapon or instrument (later you will have to decide on intrument twisting or singing only as they are quite hard to combine) and put in the one that boosts the song you will be singing. Rinse and repeat for each twist.

After that, the real reason so few good bards make it to the high levels comes into play. Knowing WHICH songs to use WHEN, and when to focus on song support and when to dual wield for some melee damage are a cience and an artform in one, and what truly distinguishes the mana song bards from the good bards. I pretty much mothballed my own bard when i got intimidated by the sheer volume of choices, so i can't help you much there, but learn exactly what each song can and can not do, when they might help and when they will make matters worse. What intruments they require and wether or not you can sing them.

If you know your songs well, you can respond to the changing situation on the battlefield and use those techniques and songs that will do you the most good in that situation. If you do that well, groups will not only want you, they will actually fight over you when the time comes for raids .
