My issue with downtime is the same as my issue with monster balance, class balance, and similar topics. And this is this: Verant doesn't know when to stop.

Let's look at monster balance. Because monster AI is not as creative as player brains, says Verant, the monsters need to be compensated. I can accept that. But look at the list of compensation:

1 - Have many more hitpoints than a player of the same level.
2 - Hits harder than a player of the same level.
3 - Hits more often than a player of the same level.
4 - Can cast spells about 3 times faster than players.
5 - Can cast on the run.
6 - Can cast around corners and through walls.
7 - Can Gate from combat and return to home point...HEALED.
8 - Can summon.
9 - Can have no weapons but hit players while the player is told the monster is "out of reach."
10 - Can mix classes. I've seen "Cleric" monsters who multi-hit for over 100 points damage! Meanwhile, my cleric is ecstatic if he gets over 30 with his Flail!

I'm sure there's more, but I'll stop there. I have no problem with a monster getting a couple items to compensate for being stupid...but more often, the monster doesn't get 2, or 3...but ALL TEN. That's not compensation at all.

Now, with the downtime issue, we see a similar situation. When you die, there should be a penalty, yes. But look at "the" penalty.

1 - Body with equipment falls to the foot of the monster you're fighting. You reappear at the bind point naked.
1A: Most "corpse recovery" tools are on the body and thus "lost".
1B: Corpse recovery must now take place while essentially naked. I don't know about you, but the juggernauts are hard enough to manage when fully armored.

2 - Corpse Recovery:
2A: Melees are now, potentially, miles away from that body.
2B: Even with Summon Corpse, melee types must now run (naked) from town to the dungeon to be summoned.
2C: Now we have this ridiculous "dragro" issue in SoV making corpse recovery even more difficult. It may be difficult to imagine, Verant, but some of us *do* play solo sometimes and don't have an legion of assistants at our beck and call. A wrong turn, or run into the tail end of a train in KD, and it is not just tough, but *impossible* to get your body back. With Woushi in Wakening Lands...same thing.

3 - Experience points are lost. Far more are lost than the monster would have given.
3A: Even with top resurrection, experience points recovered fall short of the experience points lost. Some remain gone.

4 - After corpse recovery:
4A: If resurrected, we have Res-effects.
4B: If not resurrected, all lost exp is still lost.

5 - Groups must reform, party must refight *back* to the point of death -or- groups are disheartened or it's too late and raid dissolves.

So you see, we have the same situation. We do not have *a* penalty, we are saddled with multiple penalties all which apply. Over penalization.

I don't think the game should be a "gimme" but neither do I think that "challenge" is equal to "impossible." And I believe that death is penalty enough without heaping insult on top of injury.

There are solutions, most quite easy, that can be used to fix these and still keep the "challenge."

1: Melee binding. Put safe spots (really safe ones, unlike some we've seen) in each, or 'every other' non-dungeon zone where melees can bind. But make them work for it. To bind, they have to go to their home town (there's a trek) and buy from their guildleader a ring of binding. This ring is lore, no-drop, and reasonably expensive (that's reasonably, not outrageously). Now, the melee, if he seeks to rebind, must then get to the binding point (say one of those obelisks we see here and there) and use the ring to bind there.

2: Experience point loss: The monster only takes as much experience as he would have given. Or you do not lose exp, but incur a "exp debt" (similar to AC) in which your stats are somewhat (reasonably so) until that debt is earned back. Cleric Resurrections then remove 50%, 90%, 96% of that debt.

3: Corpse Recovery: I played UO for a while, and when you died there, you were given the option of instant reanimation or playing as a ghost. To aid in corpse recovery, we could be given options as well. A box could pop up that gives us some options to ease part of the pain while still maintaining some challenge. Some of the options could be:
3A: Corpse stays where it is, you go back to dungeon zone-in.
3B: Corpse goes to zone, you go to your bind point.
3C: You go to your bind point--with all your gear, except a few items which remain on a corpse where you died (ala AC)
3D: Body stays where it is, you go to your bind point and land with a lore, no-drop, ring of corpse summoning which you can use, once in the zone, to summon your body to yourself. This helps those of us who occassionally get caught without assistance. The ring is *not* no-rent, because LD's happen, but (in order to keep people from trying to "save" one) once you start looting your body the ring vanishes.

Those are just sample ideas, and some can be tweaked to keep "balance." For instance the Ring-of-Recovery might seem to make Necros useless, but that can be balance by making the body non-resurrectable, so the exp debt must be worked off. If you don't adopt the exp-debt, then perhaps a 96% Revivisence is "too powerful for your target" and the body can only be res'd with a 90% res instead.

There are ways to actually achieve balance without over-penalizing the players.

Brilliant Idea #1
One of the biggest problems with travel is that you have to meander through zone after zone to get where you truly want to go because of the way zonelines are set up. Take a page from BG II for a second. When you zone, you get a world map with places that are adjacent to where you are now that you want to go. Instead of having all zones have one zone in to one zone out, give us the ability to pick which adjacent zone we want to go to. That alone would cut some serious travel time.
Brilliant idea#2
Automate boat transport. Utilize the follow comand when targetting the boat/shuttle to automatically transport you from said shuttle/boat, to any connecting boats and then to drop you at the LAST dock. This will allow people to still use boats as they are now, jump off whenever/whereever they want AND it will allow those that just want to get to that destination to get up from computer, and when they come back be where they want to go.

Brilliant idea #3
Make the boats on a set in-gametime schedule. Post that schedule in the user manual, online, and at each dock. By knowing when a boat will be at each stop, this will allow players to plan trips a heck of alot better than they can now. The sound idea of when a boat comes in is neat, but by the time that you get to say Butcherblock docks, that boat is long gone. Better to know ahead of time when a boat will be here than wait for a sound.

Brilliant Idea #4
Make boat trips useful. Place vendors on them that will allow you to stock up, get drunk, practice a tradeskill, or just generally do SOMETHING. What makes you bored? Doing nothing. What are the vast majority of the complaints about downtime based on? Being bored by doing nothing. By coming up with a way to make downtime fun, you can minimize (not eliminate) boredom. If placing vendors on the boats is a technical nightmare then load them buggers up at the docks. There is no reason why you can't have every kind of merchant easily accessible by every dock.

Brilliant Idea #5
Make boats connect at more ports. Butcherblock to Odus, Oasis to dock on going to kerra Isle. Make Odus a viable place to go to, and want to go to. Not only does Tox forest need a redo, but give it more access and quicker access. By sending a boats to more places, you increase different starting points on each continent. You want the thrill of running from FP to Qey? Great, knock yourself out, but the boat should take you from BB to FP to Qey. Raft should go from Oasis to Tox forest to Tim deep. Open up where one can go safely, and faster than a overland route. That's what boats are for.

Change how binding works. First of all, make it so that all characters have bind-point limitations, such as only in cities.
