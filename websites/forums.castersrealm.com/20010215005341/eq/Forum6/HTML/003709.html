I think Petoffen sums it up nicely. The resentment comes from the apparent waste of our "new stuff points", and not just the lack of power in the spells. Most classes got actual improvements and new abilities with Velious. The DoTs don't actually qualify as such. So what did mages get, then, that's actually new and useful?

It's kind of like when Kunark came out, and everyone was getting new and more powerful spells -- and mages got to summon cake instead of bread. Other Kunark spells were actually better than just that, but it caused some resentment at the time, too. If they thought mages were powerful enough, they could have just said so, and instead of wasting effort on DoTs that are the same as the DDs we already had, come up with something completely different. Bristlebane's Bundle may be useless in a combat sense, but at least you can play with it. The DoTs are a combat spell; there's nothing to do with them but use them in combat, and they offer no improvement there. Useless combat spells are far more useless than useless "fun" spells.

Such things also shake my confidence that anyone at Verant thinks about these things at all. It's not exactly a triumph of analytical ability to realize these DoTs serve no apparent purpose, and are weaker than existing mage DDs. Everybody was posting about it on the boards about fifteen seconds after the new spdat file came out of the parser. Why even design such a thing? How could the spell stats make it through even a rudimentary design review? And if there is some factor there not apparent from the data parser (extra-low aggro factor, maybe), all Verant had to do was tell us what it was. But there was no such justification forthcoming. Are they awake? Is the mage class really of that little concern in the halls of Verant? Such maldesign raises lots of questions.

Finally, adding in a sudden new capability like DoTs just earned mages a bunch of undeserved resentment from the traditional DoT classes (necros, druids, shaman) for infringing on their territory -- like we had anything to do with the choice. That's class-envy hassle we didn't really need, and it comes back to haunt us every time some real mage problem comes up. "Well, you got our DoTs, you can't have that, too." "You got new DoTs, shut up". And so on. There's a social/political cost involved with getting anything new, and if the new thing isn't actually effective, that cost is a net loss.
