It's all in the numbers, or should be. Even fixing the mistakes.

Risk vs Reward. Good idea. If applied across the board.

Every mob should have a difficulty index (range). Every item should have a value index (range). They should be matched up mathematically on a table, not by programmer guesswork. (Too mistake-prone).

All these risk vs reward issues should be automatic and math-based. It keeps the player experience FAIR. That's what the word MEANS.

Joe Programmer decides to create an item? Great. The item stats should produce a value index and that item should then be suitable for a certain segment of the mob population through simple risk index matching.

"Learn your math kids, it's the key to the universe." Still, mistake will happen.

Here's a thought... leave the items exactly where they are and adjust their stats to the risk involved. Retroactive. That's right, retroactive.

You see, in this case people have already gone through the risk, that's DONE. It's the rewards that need balancing. Here's how it works...

I hate to say it BUT... if the 'twig' is too powerful and it's unbalancing groups (it is), nerf the 'twig' to its correct stats and leave it where it is. That includes every twig in the game... every twig.

You get what you earned. For a short time you had something better than you earned, good for you. Now, it's time for the 'twig' (or whatever) to get real. Sorry, but be glad for the time you got to enjoy the advantage.

If some item in VP is too weak, leave it where it is and strengthen it. Retroactive. Anyone who already busted their butt to get it, bingo, better stats and they now have what they earned.

Verant thinks they found some mistakes (broken items). Some too strong, some too weak. Okay, simple... fix the items.

Not everyone will be happy, that's why it's called a 'mistake'.

IMHO, the more DIRECTLY these mistakes are dealt with, the less negative fallout Verant with experience, and the less confusion created in the player population.

And one more thing... better start matching those rewards to the risks a bit more tightly, eh?
