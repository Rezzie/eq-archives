I strongly feel that Verant's idea of "adventure" is far removed from the general populace's. A quest, for example, should be one of the most adventurous aspects in the game. A quest should not only involve danger, but also excitement. How fun and exciting is questing when you are required to wait around, literally for hours, until a particular creature spawns? The adventure in getting a quest piece should be in *getting there*, and defeating the mob, rather than the mind-numbing and boring hour after hour camps. Place the "quest mobs" at the bottom of a dungeon, and severely increase the frequency in which they spawn, and you'll find a lot more people having fun with this game.

Verant places "quest mobs" on ridiculously insane spawn tables in their goal to limit the flow of quest rewards on the server. This is ludicrous. They feel that the pride that comes with owning an item comes from rarity and uniquity. This may be true to some people, but the majority could care less. The pride that people get from owning a particular item is that they succeeded in their quest. The only feelings that players get from enduring a long camp is relief and satisfaction through perseverance. In truth, I actually *pity* players that I see equipped with a rare item simply because I know how long they most likely had to camp for it. Place quest items for "uber" rewards on an appropriately leveled mob, and place that quest mob in an appropriately dangerous dungeon. I feel *absolutely* no pride in the fact that I camped a quest piece for my Robe of the Lost Circle for many many hours. I feel pride in being sufficient level to attain the quest piece, and having adequate factions and resources to do the quest.

Blah I'm getting off track. Bottom line: NO ONE WANTS TO CAMP. Verant is needlessly subjecting questers to insane camp times due to their own warped "Vision (tm)" and horrible game play design. /em makes a rude gesture at Verant.

People are failing to realize that most of EQ is NOT scripted. Such scripting (story line) was the LIMITATION of past games. EQ is an open environment.
People desiring a story line, beyond those presented as quests, are simply people that require greater structure in their lives. A quick way is to avoid taking the easy way.
Instead, refuse twink-items. Use self-restraint. Resist the urge to purchase you quest items. Instead, let them be possessions that you earn. What you work for, you will appreciate, more.
If you want storylines, then you will simply have to accept new rules, as well, which define the structure. For example, we have the 8-level grouping rule. You can expand this to apprenticeship programs, where your characters must follow a predefined path for advancement.
Twinking would have to cease. Low level characters would be unable to use high-damage weapons, equipment or even possess platinum pieces. NPC pickpockets must be introduced to relieve rich newbies of their excesses.
High level characters would be unable to use low-level weapons (just as a man, strong enough to swing a ten-stone sledge, would be unable to do any damage with a toy hammer, regardless of his strength). That's right. Damage caps, usage floors (e.g., rampant regulation).
With predefined story lines, players would be left, either guessing where to go next (think MYST, and ZORK). Or, NPC's would have to send them on a neverending, succession of quests. The quest items would have to follow the same guidelines as newbie weapons. They would be NO DROP, NO SELL, so characters could not purchase the items. They'd be forced to get the items, from NPC's, alone.
In EQ, MOBs are one strength, whether attacked by a group, or an individual. Readjusting MOB's strength, based on number of attackers present, within range to attack, may be a way to lessen dependency upon camping. That way, a player could, conceivably, solo the entire game.
Truly, what is a quest? Isn't it more of a scavenger hunt? Go get these items and you get a reward? Have you ever considered that locating items to practice your trade skills constitutes a quest? There are many paths, and your skills are improved in the process.
Many EQ players are either naturally introverted, or physically isolated (e.g., live out of town). EQ is an excellent opportunity to spend time with friends, without needing be concerned with Real-Life limitations. It helps people overcome their limitations, and have a fuller, more robust life.
In EQ, sheer force-of-personality comes to bear. You like, and are liked, based on the content of your character, as you choose to display it. Spending time, together, working on goals, together, constitutes a PROCESS. Goals provide a direction. The fun is not in achieving the goals, but in the ongoing process.
A person that is spending time camping & levelling needs to be playing somewhere else. Such a person hates EQ for its strengths.
