It has its uses.

The mod rod is a mana tap spell. It's a little different from the necro version in that it does damage to both health (when used) and mana (when summoned). It's a net loss on mana, and more so when you consider healing the wound, so the only utility it can have is for when you need to transfer mana to someone else. It's not hard to think of emergencies where that can come in handy.

The rod has one advantage over the necro spell, and that's that you can "time shift" the mana cost. In a raid situation (which is the only time such things see much use anyway), you've got plenty of time to set up, and can med the mana for a bunch of rods back before the battle. The cost at time of use then is just the health cost. Your overall downtime is greater, but you can spend some of it before the battle, so that you can have a bit of extra mana during the battle.

There's one big conceptual flaw with mana tap spells, though. It should shine through in the posts from ardent defenders of the mod rod. The flaw is the notion that it's a good idea to sit around feeding some other class your mana, at a loss. (You'll see necros complain about this mightily; mages are so unused to having any utility for others they don't tend to notice as much.) Think about the design statement here. The existence of such spells basically says that mage and necro mana isn't worth anywhere near what cleric mana is. Even transferring at a loss, you're better off with the cleric having the mana than the mage or necro. The int casters can't make effective use of it, so spend it all to give a fraction away to a valuable class. If you think about it, you'd be better off just taking another instance of the valuable class along in the first place. If you need cleric mana, two clerics are better than one mage and one cleric. The second cleric can "transform" their mana into clerical mana at a 1:1 rate with no damage at all, twice as efficient as a mod rod.

I don't think I'm the only one that thinks perhaps a better design would insure that all the classes had some use for their mana, rather than contriving ways to routinely pump it to another class. Occasional use in emergencies is one thing. Keeping that rod in your back pocket can be handy. But mana transfer of entire bars of mana as a standard tactic reveals fundamental problems with class balance in EQ.
