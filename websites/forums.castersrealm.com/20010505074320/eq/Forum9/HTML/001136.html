Foreword:

The purpose of these tests was to determine how effective enchanter haste spells really are, and, more importantly, which spells are most effective in combination with haste items (FBSS, Mithril Two Handed Sword, etc.). After reading Baelish's editoral on haste spells, I noticed that the article was entirely theoretical, and gave no concrete numbers (except for hypothetical ones). I ran these tests with the idea of just plugging numbers into Baelish's theory and thereby supplementing his article. What I found out, though, was that Baelish's article is wrong in many respects, and the interaction between haste spells and items is a lot more complex than many people think.

Methodology:

Simple. I loaded the test subject with four different configurations:

  • a 50 delay non-haste weapon;
  • a 50 delay non-haste weapon plus an FBSS;
  • a 21 delay non-haste weapon;
  • a 21 delay non-haste weapon plus an FBSS.
For each configuration, I measured the attack delay in between swings using each of the following spell configurations: no haste spell, Augmentation, Alacrity, Celerity, and Swift like the Wind.

I then tabulated the results in a format that displays both the absolute attack delay figure (in seconds), and the relative attack delay figure (relative to the base attack delay with no haste items or spells). Note that all spell effects are from a level 50 caster (and used on a level 50 test subject), and that at lower levels the spells have a lesser effect.

Analysis:

Obviously, haste items and haste effects do not combine in just an additive or multiplicative fashion, as most people believe. In other words, if augmentation has a 25% haste effect, and an FBSS has a 20% haste effect, when you combine them you do not get 45% haste (25% + 20%) or 50% haste (125% x 120%). This is the fundamental mistake, I think, in Baelish's article and in many of the posts I have seen discussing this issue.

In actuality, Augmentation plus an FBSS will automatically take you straight to the maximum attack speed. This may also be true for other haste items (e.g., Mithril Two Handed Sword), but I did not have time to test them.

Also, there is no single maximum attack speed or "haste cap" in terms of percentages -- rather, it varies depending on the weapon used. The maximum attack speed seems to vary between 170% and 175% of the base attack speed, at least for the two weapons tested. The actual maximum may be tied to the base attack delay of the weapon tested. Also, the effect of a haste item (unlike a haste spell) may be scaled according to the type of weapon used.

Conclusion:

If your tank has an FBSS (all do by level 50), Augmentation ROCKS! There is absolutely no excuse in choosing another speed spell over augmentation, since all of them will get you to the exact same haste amount. Augmentation provides additional benefits: 30 agility, extra AC, and stamina regeneration. Augmentation also consumes only 90 mana and lasts 27 minutes, far longer than any other haste spell. If your FBSS-equipped tank refuses augmentation, tell them they're idiots and show them this study.

Some respond by saying that with a fast haste spell, the tank can remove his haste item and use a better item instead. This is a rather silly argument, though, for three reasons: (1) the added benefit of swapping in a second item will be so marginal as to be undetectable, (2) in some cases, the haste effect is built into the weapon (e.g., Mithril Two Handed Sword), and most importantly, (3) unless your tank is using Wonderous Rapidity from a level 60 enchanter, the haste spell alone (without a haste item) will NOT result in the maximum possible attack speed, by a fairly large margin.

These tests also cast serious doubts on the usefulness of the ROK haste spells. Given that Augmentation gets you straight to the haste cap anyway, one wonders why Verant even bothered making the level 56 Augment (upgrade to Augmentation). Since the added haste effect will be irrelevant, will anyone really want to spend over double the mana just for a tiny AC increase? Doubtful. A group version of Augmentation would be much more useful than Augment.

And of course, all of the other ROK haste spells (Aanya's Quickening, Wonderous Rapidity, Visions of Grandeur) will be utterly worthless for your party members -- a joke really. The only use of these spells at levels 50+ will be to haste NPCs (charmed and player pets), and of course to powertwink newbie Iksars. What was Verant thinking?

Not sure what you mean by a bug. It's just a matter of what formula Verant picks for combining two effects. Many people assumed it's just an additive function (i.e., X + Y), but that was a baseless assumption. You can't automatically say it's a bug just because it doesn't really work the way you assumed.

My suspicion is that the formula is more complex, possibly some sort of exponential function. Perhaps if there were two very low level haste effects, for example a level 29 enchanter's augmentation, which should be 20%, and a low-level haste item, the total effect would not reach the maximum. But at level 50, an FBSS plus an enchanter's lowest-level haste spell will reach the maximum.

As to whether this is good or bad -- I think it's a mixed bag. On the one hand, this system devalues the role of enchanter as "haste buffer", since shamans (and probably bards as well) can also provide the maximum haste effect to any tank who has a haste item. On the other hand, this system allows the enchanter to use a single low mana spell to maximize the offensive potential of the party's tanks. Overall, I'd say it's a good thing.

Timmok, to answer your first question, I measured the attack delay by having both parties keep a log, then analzyed both logs to determine how many attacks were attempted in a given period of time (usually 2 minutes). The logs were in most cases identical down to the second, except for a very small number of cases where the timestamps were 1 second off due to rounding error, and in those cases I averaged the two figures. For a two minute test period, a 0.5 second (maximum) variation would not change the figures very much. Of course server lag might affect any individual result slightly, but if you look at the numbers as a whole you will see a pretty consistent pattern that cannot be explained away by server lag.

As to your second question, re-read my methodology: I used a non-haste weapon, since obviously a weapon with a haste effect would render the results meaningless.

Several RoK-beta testers, most notably Kendrick on the safehouse has hinted that haste caps WILL change in the expansion.

Sorry, but 99% of the information I see from players is utter nonsense (actually, closer to 100%). EQ has been out for over a year, yet with all the supposed player testing/rumors, until this morning no one has even come close to the truth regarding the effect of haste items and spells (you want the truth, look up).

What happens is that someone invents a rumor and spreads it around, and suddenly people take it as gospel even though it's totally made up. Players are lazy and they would rather believe a rumor than spend an hour doing an actual test. I've seen players post about the "60% cap" or the "65% cap" as if it were one of the Ten Commandments, yet my simple test above proves any such notion is nonsense. The fact that this Kendrick "hinted" at it and didn't say it outright makes the whole issue even more dubious.

So unless Kendrick shows me a scientifically sound EMPIRICAL test which proves that the haste cap will be raised, I'm not going to believe it. Also, if you read my conclusion above, you'll see that a higher haste cap will likely not matter anyway, since the real issue is not the haste cap per se but rather the function used for determining how haste spells and items combine. Since the function results in a haste figure much larger than a simple additive or multiplicative function, haste spells above augmentation are essentially meaningless where a haste item is involved.

The haste cap *is* raised for players post 50th level, and to varying degrees based on class.

This isn't heresay, speculation, or rumor, it is a fact.

The cap was increased to add value to the higher level enchanter spells and bard songs, differentiate the pure melee classes a bit more than the hybrids in combat power, and to add value to higher level haste items.

The test above is pretty damned accurate, by the way, though it does, as someone else pointed out, overlook one factor, that being there is no cap on special attack refresh speed at this time.

In other words, SLTW and a FBSS will get you faster backstabs/bashes/flying kicks, etc, than Augmentation and a FBSS.

All that being said, I still ask my enchanters for Augmentation.

Overall, I find the added duration, reduced workload on the caster, the very nice AGI bonus (Agi does more than most players will give it credit for), and the stamina regeneration are worth much more than the ability to use a special attack a bit more often.
