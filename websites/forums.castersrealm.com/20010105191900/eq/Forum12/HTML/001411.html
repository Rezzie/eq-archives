This is a very poorly run test.

1) There is no control. Greater kobolds range from level 32 (or 33?) to level 37, and there will be a wide variation in the amount of damage they do based on their level.

2) This test is not indicative of the typical type of mob a level 50 ranger would be killing. Greater kobolds are all green at level 50, with the exception of the highest level ones which would be blue. I'm not saying the test subject should have been level 50 (that's not indicative of a typical mob either), but something in the range of 40-45 would be much more appropriate than something in the 32-37 range.

3) The number of data points is not listed. This makes a calculation of the statistical relevance of the results very difficult.

That said, I would agree with the conclusions of the test. There's a strong negative linear correlation between AC and damage taken in that test (R=-0.84), with m=-0.044. In other words, if the relationship is presumed to be linear (not necessarily the case), there would be a -0.044 reduction in damage taken per second for every 1 point of AC increase. Further analysis on confidence intervals and such would require more information, such as the number of data points.

How many times do we have to have this silly argument? Want to know how useful stat buffs should be? I'll let you do this little thought experiment:

Given that Verant likes balance, stat buffs aren't going to be grossly more efficient when it comes to converting mana to damage. Now, there is a range for mana to damage conversion which reflects a wide variety of factors, so I will compare a stat buff to another way a shaman can indirectly inflict damage -- though attack speed buffs.

Alacrity: +40% attack speed, 115 mana, 8 minutes duration. Doing some math here --
this is 14.38 mana to get a +40% damage for 1 minute. (115 mana) /( 8 min). Consider this a simple normalization.

Using this as a baseline of what Verant considers fair, a stat buff isnt going to have a much bigger effect. Lets look at a stat buff:

Strength: +67 str, 100 mana, 63 mins. Or doing the math: 1.59 mana / min for 67 str. Ratioing this to the alacrity results gives (1.59 / 14.38 ) * 40% = 4.4%. Therefore, if strength has the same efficiency as alacrity, strength should increase net damage by 4.4%.

While the actual performance of stat buffs might have additional factors in deciding efficiency, it is pretty easy to see that the effect can't be large and still have a balanced spell. If +67 str gave +10% damage, it would be hugely unbalancing. Sorry if you think it should, but frankly it can't without making tanks ungodly relative to other ways of doing damage.

The proper test would compare level 20 shaman buffs on a level 20 warrior against level 20-appropriate targets (whites, yellows), just as the proper test for the 50+ crowd would be a 50+ warrior with 50+ shaman buffs against typical 50+ targets (almost all of which will be low to medium blue).

Testing a situation which will never occur outside of a controlled test is, well, pointless.

In any case, with both tests you are going to get the same results -- the effect (if any) will be drowned out in the noise of the random number generator, or will be statistically insignificant.

Given that Verant likes balance, stat buffs aren't going to be grossly more efficient when it comes to converting mana to damage. Now, there is a range for mana to damage conversion which reflects a wide variety of factors, so I will compare a stat buff to another way a shaman can indirectly inflict damage -- though attack speed buffs.

dwilgar, efficiency is only one factor in determining the usefulness of a spell, because mana is not finite. Magnitude of the effect is the other major factor. If I gave you a DoT spell at level 60 that did 100 damage over 2 minutes for 1 mana, no one would argue that it is about 20 times more efficient than your other DoT spell. But would you ever use it? No, because the magnitude of the effect is miniscule, making the spell utterly worthless.

The good spells are the one that combine decent mana efficiency with a strong effect. For example, in my groups tanks are usually fighting at least 80% of the time. Augmentation, a 90 mana spell that lasts 27 minutes, increases their haste from around 20% (depending on what haste item they have) to 75%, resulting in an absolute 45% increase in damage over time. A good tank will deal 2,000 damage per minute, and if they're fighting 80% of the time in those 27 minutes, I have netted approximately 20,000 points of damage over 27 minutes for 90 mana, i.e. a mana efficiency of roughly 222 to 1.

On the flip side, look at the debuffs. If tugur's insects slows a target to 35% of its regular attack speed, and it's a typical level 50 mob that hits for a maximum of 140 and an average of about 100 per 3 seconds (including all special attacks), and takes about 2 minutes to kill, one casting of tugur's insects will reduce the damage taken from around 4,000 to 1,400. Mana efficiency is 2,600 hp for 250 mana, over 10 to 1 -- better than a complete heal even.

Compare that to a strength buff. How much damage do you think +67 STR adds over the course of 63 minutes? I assure you it's not 20,000, or even 2,000. Maybe 300 or 400 -- that's just a figure I'm throwing out based on what I remember from my old testing. So while the mana efficiency is decent, it's not even as good as the above-referenced spells, and you will never even notice the effect.

In other words, useless.
