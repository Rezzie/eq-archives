After a couple marathon (11+ hour total over 2 or 3 days) gaming sessions for mods that shouldn't have taken more than 6, a discussion sprung up over at the Online Game Day yahoo group about what kinds of things you can do to make the online gaming experience go more smoothly, both as a DM and as a player. I thought I'd repost some of the suggestions here, because I thought a bunch of them were really good advice, even for people not playing online.

The group, and so some of the suggestions, are related to RPGA-based games and module structures, but I think there's plenty of stuff that's applicable to people playing any type of game. I didn't make any of this stuff up, so I acknowledge the source at the beginning of each quote.

First, a post by Ken Jenks


Quote:
If there's a rule question or debate about one player's action, skip
that action until the player looks up the appropriate rules. Then fit
the action in to the combat later. Take debates out of the main
window to a private chat window, but don't neglect the main window.

At the end of each round, give the players a quick summary of what
happened in that round, like, "Okay, that completes round 4, where
Rintrose dropped the goblin leader, the Ogre stepped on top of the
table, and Charlamak is still unconcious under the table, trying to
stabilize. The goblin sorcerer has three Mirror Images left."

When you are preparing your scenario, pre-roll Hide, Listen, Spot and
Disguise checks and pre-cast any long-duration buffing spells for
your bad guys, so when the good guys encounter them, you spend less
time rolling dice for the bad guys. This can also help you visualize
where the bad guys are before the encounter, so it's more clear when
the good guys get a Spot check to find a hidden or disguised bad guy.

When the players are clearly off-track, looking at the plot the wrong
way or searching the wrong area for clues, they meet an NPC named Red
Herring. He'll start to drone on and on about nothing in particular
and the players always figure it out right away. Sometimes it's a
bartender, sometimes it's a cleric, His High Priest of Perpetual
Pompousness, Herring the Red.

When I ask a player what he wants to do on his turn, I'll sometimes
ask which of several likely paths he'd like to take, like, "Do you
want to keep attacking the troll or take a five-foot step and grapple
the wizard?"

I always discourage splitting up the party or letting one group get
far ahead of another. That often leads to combat disasters and always
leads to player boredom, but splitting up the party also takes more
real time.

I have the monsters surrender or flee from combats when things look
bad for them, especially if the party does something dramatic and
intimidating. This can greatly decrease the amount of time spent in
combat, and it usually lets some of the monsters live to fight
another day. Not that this has any campaign effect in LG, but it's
more realistic than every monster fighting to the death.

Make sure you are familiar with each encounter at the APL selected.
Don't assume that the encounter as written is the same as it was when
you played the game; your DM may have screwed up, giving you a false
impression of how an encounter "should" go, and you may be DMing at a
different APL than you played. Read the flow chart, if one is
available.

Take the time to copy/paste all boxed text into a Word document (or
whatever) in paragraph-sized chunks that will go through AIM so you
don't have to take extra time to select/copy/paste during the game.

Make any maps available on a Web site, then give the URL of each map
at the appropriate time during the game. This avoids the delay of
launching e-mail, checking for new messages, downloading attachments,
etc.

Make the maps large enough scale that players can print them out on
standard paper and plunk the map down on a battlemat and use
miniatures. This saves oodles of time.

Don't volunteer be a DM unless you really have time to play out the
whole game. Don't DM in one window and play another game in another
window -- it's rude to keep your players waiting.

Announce a schedule of breaks then stick to it. This will help
everybody get to the fridge and the potty or whatever without
interrupting the game with too much AFK time.

Another tip for saving time when you're the DM: have a player look up
spell durations and other details. When an NPC cleric casts a Ghoul
Touch on a player character, ask the player to look up the effects
and duration. Keep track of the duration on the during your end of
the round summary so everyone can see it.

Even when you're playing on-line, it may be more efficient to work
from a printed scenario, where you can pencil in notes in the margins
and use a highlighter for the encounters of the correct APL.

For NPC spell casters, it's a good idea to pencil in a little table
showing the DC of each spell level the NPC can cast. So if the NPC
sorcerer has a 16 CHA and Spell Focus: Evocation, I'd write a little
table in the margin of the printed scenario like this:

Lvl | DC | DC Evoc
----+----+--------
0 | 13 | 15
1 | 14 | 16
2 | 15 | 17
3 | 16 | 18
4 | 17 | 19
5 | 18 | 20

For NPC fighters with multiple weapons and/or multiple attacks, I
check the math in the scenario. It's often wrong, or it includes Rage
bonuses or other situation-dependent bonuses that might or might not
apply. Then I pencil in a little table like:

Main Longsword +9, 1d8+6
2nd Dagger +8, 1d4+4
Iter Longsword +4, 1d8+8

This is a good place to note situation-dependent bonuses, like Ranger
favored enemy or Rage modifiers.

You should have a pre-generated scratch paper for each combat, with
information on each NPC, including hit points, spells, potions,
scrolls, doses of poison and maybe the tables I mentioned above.
You'll need one for each APL because you may not know before the game
starts exactly what APL you'll end up DMing.

And if you really want to save time, play via teleconference using
free/cheap minutes on cell phones and chained three-way calling.
Telecon games take about half the time of an AIM game and are much
more fun. See Y! group LivingTelecon for details.


From Mike Sitts

Quote:
GMs:
-- know the mod. sounds silly, but that extra hour of prep will pay
off, likely saving 2-3 hours, plus, make the games run a LOT more
smoothly.

-- take 20 minutes to a half hour at the beginning to copy and paste
and perhaps print out everyone's basic info. Knowing PC names,
general stats, etc. helps speed things up, and gives the game a more
natural feel. Not everyone's AIM name matches their PC name. It's
cooler if you can maintain that feel throughout the mod.

-- sometimes, when it's running long and people are frazzled or
frustrated, do some creative editing. Is it obvious that the PCs,
who aren't particularly hurt and have dispatched two-thirds of the
baddies without a problem? Wrap it up. Don't start fleeing and
spring-attacking and such if it's not integral to the mod. All it
does is bog tables down. That extra hour of meaningless combat
doesn't do anyone any good and can be very frustrating.

-- Recap frequently. Give Sit-reps. Especially when there aren't
any maps to hand out, it's pure visualization, and feeling completely
out of touch bogs the game down with incessant questions and makes
people frustrated.

PLAYERS:
-- Be ready! Know what your character is going to do, pay attention
to what others are doing and preferably have it typed out just
waiting for you to hit 'enter'.

-- If you have questions about a situation, ask them in a concise
manner. Indicate what you'd like to do, if you can do it, and
alternatives if you can't.

-- have the die-bot in your clipboard, so a Paste will set it up.

-- keep track of who's in front of you once initiative is rolled.
That way you can be alert and ready.

The long games usually end up becoming an exercise in endurance. By 9
hours, people are burned out, usually frustrated and annoyed and
become increasingly careless with their PCs, which can lead to
unfortunate results. "I don't care, I just want SOMETHING to happen,
so yes, I charge into the middle of the Ettins with Barbarian/Rogue
levels!"

With a bit of work, prep and attention, things can be brought down to
reasonable levels and the enjoyment of everyone will increase.
