omg, Nenjin, are you serious?

Okay, picture your typical dragon versus adventuring party. Dragon strikes first, winning a surprise round. One breath of superheated steam to start things off, and all the non-rogues have taken some pretty serious damage. Next round, dragon moves in - boom, claw-claw-bite-wing-wing, chances are a party member just died. Now the dragon retreats and flies high up into the air, casting a healing spell on itself to replenish any damage it took. Circling over the party once, it delivers a flyby attack on the most wounded person it sees, then comes around one more time and breathes again.

At this point, chances are at least 2-3 party members are dead or down, and those remaining are either low on hit points or considering their chances against said dragon alone. As if that wasn't enough, this is a sea dragon - which has a swim speed and can fight underwater, and it uses its Control Water ability to fill the room with water. Remaining party members are now drowning, failing their swim checks, moving half speed at best, and taking penalties on their attacks AND their damage, and worst of all having a visibility of five feet, whereas the dragon is using blindsight (and thus can see perfectly for 300 feet) and is making swim-by attacks on relatively helpless PCs. And this is using an environmental variable as simple as H2O, the most common of resources on the planet.

How about a Xorn's burrowing ability to make attacks through solid rock? Formidable, sure. But a Xorn with levels of assassin? Suddenly, the party's cleric falls over dead, but no one can see what happened. What do you do?

A simple sixth-level thief and his gang get targeted by the PCs for a purge. But what are the PCs going to do when the thief reveals that he carries a stone on him that renders all magic inoperative within thirty feet of him in every direction - and the thief has an intelligence of 18, with a booby-trapped lair and six layers of contingency plans. Sure, the PCs vastly overpower him in resources - but a rogue with maxed tumble at that level is never going to get hit in melee unless they can catch him, and with no magic in a 30' radius only a high-level monk even has a chance to get close - and the monk is then within range of the thief's bolas, poisoned caltrops, blinding powder, and tanglefoot bag, all of which are totally nonmagical.

Or a ghost with the Sunder feat who hated magic items and lives to destroy them right off their owner's bodies?

A trio of bad guys who can body-swap in midfight, as per the Keeper ability? Run up on the wizard, suddenly he's the warrior, kicking your ass - and they all look identical.

A battle with six grimlocks in a room where no light can shine.

A talented assassin targets the PCs, cleric first - and wears a ring of misdirection so that any divination spell cast on him rebounds upon the caster. The assassin carries scrolls of Trap the Soul with him to prevent resurrection of the fallen cleric, then starts culling the herd.

A group gets teleported away from their magic-users, who are then slapped with dimensional anchor and beset by magic-immune golems.

These are all examples of seriously dire challenges, all that use plain, ordinary spells and abilities from the player's handbook and monster manual. Basic cleverness can account for a lot, and the element of surprise can account for even more. Giving an NPC a unique special ability is nice on occasion, but the more often you do it, the more you give the impression that you just want your NPCs to be more powerful than the PCs.

Furthermore, if you feel the PCs are too powerful as a well rounded group, maybe you should try to exploit that - split them up, charm them, or make them face other adventuring groups that work just as well together. Make them face intelligent opponents that target their healers and magic-users first, preferably with one-shot-kill attacks.

The hierophant with a level of sorcerer who wears boots of speed, hasting himself and casting quickened True Strike and ranged Harm, followed by magic missile.

The Dragon-riding wizard who follows a burst of his dragon's breath weapon with Power Word, Kill, taking advantage of depleted hit points to allow no saving throw.

The lich who puts a Gate into the doorway to his throne room, which actually leads onto the Negative Energy Plane, where mortals accrue negative levels simply for existing every round.

The rogue who wields a +1 flaming frost screaming shock corrosive shortsword and deals 5d6 damage of sneak attack in addition to the 6d6 from his weapon, and uses his ring of Change Self to pretend to be the party member he just decapitated, claiming it was a doppleganger.

The fire giant who coup de grace's victims of his cleric wife's Hold Person spells.

The drow fighter whose cloak surrounds him in a continual fog cloud of Id Moss Mist and Insanity Mist, reducing int and wis by 1d6 each the first round and 2d6 each a minute later, while the fighter himself is completely immune due to his periapt of proof against poison, while the fighter attacks away with poisoned swords that reduce his opponent's CON scores.

Ghosts that cause any human within 60 feet to suffer 1d6 points of permanent STR, DEX, and CON drain, and then blink out of existence while their shadow hound pets attack the weakened party.

The permanently hasted high-level monk that uses his fistwraps of energy drain to bestow 5d4 negative levels per round, using only touch attacks - and has Spring Attack so that he's never within the attack range of anyone he fights.

The psion who feigns death then continues to use his componentless, wordless, gestureless powers while motionless on the floor.

There are dozens of ways to provide interesting encounters with basic material. Just be creative.

The idea was for them to find this guy, realise they could not beat him, and then go to find some other way to stop him.

They ended up sneaking into his camp, trying to kill him in his sleep, and getting forcibly thrown through the planes.

Enter Planescape. The travelled to a few different worlds seeking powerful items and growing in personal power, then returned almost a year later (in real time...) as a group of 19-20 almost epic characters to free their people, who had been enslaved by these super-goblins.

Dueling this monstrosity of a goblin one-on-one ended up being the event that gave the level 20 paladin what he needed to become an epic hero. (and even at 20 he would not have won if not for the fact that he had basically and artifact holy sword given to him directly from the hand of his god, a shield artifact from his gods first paladin, armor that was an artifact from another realm of existance, and a number of other VERY powerful magical items.)

NOTE on that: I don't run epic as 'you get x XP, you ding 21'. To become an epic character (level 21) the character must complete some VERY special task. In this case the group was about to be slaughtered by a horde of beasts and the Paladin challenged the goblin leader to a man to man combat for final victory. The paladin became the first memeber of the group to hit 21 although 4 memebers of the group technically had MORE than enough XP saves up for it.
