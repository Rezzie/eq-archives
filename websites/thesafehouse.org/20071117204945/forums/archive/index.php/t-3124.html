Quote:heh and remember, vampirism = a magical affliction that turns you chaotic evil.

Not always. I have seen a character play a lawful good vampire and do it WELL. Basically this characters history was RPed well enough to show that his indomitable will was enough to fight back the evil and choatic tendancies. Because of this our GM allowed him to skip the alignment change, although the fact that he was forced to drink blood did weigh on his consience constantly and he had to deal with it.

It all depends on a number of factors.

1) Is it his gear that makes him too powerful?

If so, take some of it away. Even before the game begins. Whatever event brings this character to meet the rest of the party could strip him of his items. Be it a magical portal the moves only flesh, or if he was captured and the group rescued his naked body with nothing else. There are many ways to separate a player from his stuff.

2) Is it the vampirsm that makes him too powerful?

Use it against him. Have them be attacked constantly during the day and have people hate him even if he tries to be 'good'. Have a local vampire hunter find out about him a decide to add him to his trophy list. These are things called 'story hooks'. I use them a lot to remind 'special' characters that they are special.

3) Is it his level or EL that makes him too powerful?

Here, the player has to know that the game is set for a specific level. Figure up his EL (vampire level + class levels) and let him know that he can only keep x number of levels.

Hope this has been helpful. Personally I enjoy running high level / high power games myself, and I have learned that there are many 'tricks' to control powerful characters.

Ask me sometime about my campaign that consisted of 4 34th level characters, each paragons of thier race, gifted with deific power. Now THAT was a power trip.

Uh oh, time to get all theoretical on your azz.

People play games like D&amp;D to primarily, yes, build power for themselves. But note, it's about building power, not getting it. For a character to walk into a new game with an old, stacked character, what does that do to the rest of the party? Think, the lvl 9 thief reminisces about that time he fell into the pit trap of sleeping black widows, and almost blew his MS/HIS roll trying to sneak out. But he did find his +3/type-e poison short sword down there! Or the level 10 Paladin, who remembers volunteering to be geased so the temple would ressurect a fallen comrade. Little did he know it would end up in him becoming a semi-avatar of his god, including his ultra-special deific armor and weapons!

Building power is supposed to give players something to remember, it gives their character's history, personality, and it creates a sense value in the levels they have and the equipment they get. What is worth more to you? Getting a +3 Longsword of the body of an ogre you guys killed(among the 8 total you encountered), or getting that +3 longsword after exploring the depths of the catacombs, slaying the guardian, and solving the puzzle? Dumping a character into the world that has stuff and powers you didn't give him leaves him only with the memorys and appreciation for his stuff that he got in someone else's game, under someone else's ideas.

Maybe that doesn't strike you as bad, but again, consider this. That paladin that has been in your world for a year and a half, he is used to policing himself and his actions because he status and position rely on his good or "in-character" behavior. So he won't be threatening NPCs to make the plot move using his powers, he won't bash down the walls of the legal merchant to see the map he has hidden in the shop, he won't just slaughter NPCs out of character and on a whim, he has things to lose and he knows how they will be lost.

One of my favorite "people actually play in character?!" moments: The party was going.. somewhere. A river was in the way. On the shore, an old man was sleeping in a fishing boat. When the party woke him up, he was belligerent and refused to let them borrow his boat. Instead of either trying to haggle or just moving on to try to find another way, one of the players started trying to just take the boat. The old man fought him as effectively as a feeb can fight, and the player responded in the manner of an experienced rollplayer - lethal force. Instead of sighing with relief and getting in the boat, or simply complaining, the very Good oriented party subdued him, turned him in to the city watch for murder, and then continued on the adventure.
