Clairvoyance and any power that lets a party avoid walls (teleport, dimensional fold, stone to mud, super-tunnelling, or whatever) are always encounter wreckers.

The way I dealt with them was either to disallow them entirely, or to make countermeasures realatively available (though expensive)

One thing I did was make a house rule that precious metals (Copper, Silver, Gold) were magically inert. It is part of what makes them valuable as currency, that they cannot be produced by magic. A sufficient thickness of them (100mm of copper, 5mm of silver, or 1mm of gold.) wold block magical effects. A magic barrier for an "inner sanctum" measuring 5mx5mx3m would require (4*5*3)+(2*5*5) 110 square meters of precious metal plate.

(assuming 100 coins to the kg, and 1:10:100 gp/sp/cp relationship)

0.11 m^3 of gold (2123 kg) (212,300 gp)
0.55 m^3 of Silver (5610 kg) (56,100 gp)
11.0 m^3 of copper (99000 kg) (99,000 gp)

Now, I didnt want this much money falling into the PCs hands too often, so I made another rule (that took care of tunnelling too) that natural stone still rooted in the earth (ie unworked, unmoved and still connected to bedrock) has a similar magic resistance if it is at least a meter thick. If a person takes the time and effort to chip his stronghold (or part of it) out of a natural stone outcrop, he can avoid prying eyes/teleporters and such by installing high quality precious metal door/shutters over any openings.

Anyway... None of that helps you in your current situation. I like Telurinon's idea.

Disallowing entirely isn't something I did a lot. Tunnelling was about it, as it made the whole "castle" thing not work. If someone can move through earth, or even solid stone, at a running pace and leave a tunnel behind them, it becomes way too easy to undermine structures.

Your friend was right that a level 12 Necromancer is no real challenge for a full group of level 6's. The ease of the mission was partially that, and partially the players making a good plan that worked well.

I used to kill off players whenever the dice said so (I run mostly realistic games where no matter the player's level of power a single blow can fell them). But now I only let them die if they do something they know is high risk and/or act foolishly.

In my opinion teleportation is just too useful. The game gains alot of romanticism when you actually have to ride to deliver a message/or get somewhere on time.

In one of my recent games the gamemaster decided that when you teleported you were actually travelling, just on another plain where nasties roamed. So every TP was a risk.

If overpowered items are a problem there are several solutions:

1. Pit them in adventures where their items are of less use. Their +5 vorpal blade will do little good when on a diplomatic mission.

2. Items can be sacrificed to the gods. Maybe the players have been cursed and items sacrificed to the offended god will clear the curse.

Another option is that players can sacrifice items to gain boons. Sacrifice items to the god of wisdom for information on something.

Here's one possibilty. After the players return to town to collect the X thousand gold piece reward for killing the ogre, dragon or whatever, they find that one of the party has already been in town to collect the reward. After some investigation it becomes clear to the players that this person was a doppleganger. At some future point this same doppleganger does other thing against the party or (better still) is linked to past events the players were never able to solve.

After dealing with the mastermind doppleganger a while drop a hint about sacrificing items to the god of wisdom for information. If this doppleganger is enough of a mastermind players will be sacrificing some good items. When they finally face the doppleganger make the victory sweet for the players so they don't feel robbed. Perhaps he's been impersonating a crown prince or something. Maybe he escapes to lick his wounds but his plans of becoming king of X country are foiled.

3. The players meet an item merchant (a wandering one they won't find again) who sells odd items (nothing normal).

This is basically giving the players a chance to trade items that give them raw power for items that give the players more interesting abilities that aren't found through normal spells or items. Edited by: Iscin at: 8/17/03 6:20 pm

A sack of weak items doesn't equal a single powerful one.

What I was thinking is that the wizard, when casting teleport, has to hold forth the item that is to power the spell. The single item. A +1 dagger might move you 100 yards. A sack of +1 daggers will move you 100 yards a whole lot of times, though you will have to recast teleport a whole lot of times too. If you want to jump across a continent in a single bound, you will need a single item of considerably more power. After the spell is cast, the item doesnt just "poof", it is simply drained of all of its magical power. A +1 dagger would become a normal dagger, a wand of eensyheals would become, basically, a stick, etc.



Or... to extend your component box idea, have one of the ingredients be a bit of matter (or what have you) closely associated with the destination. If one wants to teleport from Seattle to Miami, one will need a bit of soil from Miami. And where, exactly, in Miami the teleport deposits the wizard depends on just where in Miami the bit of soil came from. This will retain a lot of the usefulness of teleport for quick travel, but will remove a lot of its usefulness as an assassination tool. Unless you can get someone to give you something from the Necromancer's bed chamber, you won't be able to teleport there just as the Necromancer is drifting off to sleep and assassinate him.

Many other possibilities present themselves if this component need is adopted. Thieves become pathfinders for teleporting wizards. They sneak in and steal something from where the wizard (and anyone else he is bringing along) wants to go. Quite the trade might develop in dirt, too, as high level wizards amass carefully labelled collections from around the world. A nasty trap for those wizards might be put in place when some rival of theirs manages to secretly replace the contents of one or more of his dirt vials with dirt from someplace much less pleasant than was previously in it. Its a nasty shock when one thinks he is teleporting to Peoria to see his sister, but, in fact, shows up in a dragon's lair. Woe unto the wizard who doesnt collect his own dirt, for he doesnt really KNOW that it comes from where he is told it comes from!


If you want to be even more restrictive, have any matter that has been magicked lose its previous associations. So the mage teleporting from Seattle to Miami cant bring any Seattle dirt with him to teleport back with, or a group that has manages to steal something from the Necromancer's bechamber might be able to teleport in, but wont be able to teleport back out. Unless their thief snuck in a second time (!) bringing something along that has an outside association.

Line of sight teleports, would, of course, require no destination component. (though clairvoyant LOS probably shouldnt count)
