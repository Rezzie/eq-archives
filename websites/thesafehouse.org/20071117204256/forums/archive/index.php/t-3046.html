As to when you want to reward experience points is entirely up to you. Now I know you are asking where we as DMs stand as far as Roleplay Experience, well, my methods are similar to yours.

If the character (PC) really plays his character, I.E. has an intelligence of 5 and is a Barbarian, and he's making all the correct decisions, then he's using Outworldly Knowledge and is not Roleplaying. Now, if that same character, sits there and talks about wanting to grow his potato farm, and goes into a violent frenzy every time he smells blood, then I would award experience, for him Roleplaying that character.

If the Rogue just sat there and just "diced" his way through that character, then that's not really Roleplaying. If he actually acted it out, and truly played his part, then award the experience.

Now, when I award experience, I don't sit there and calculate the exact experience from each kill. I keep a list of what was killed and just award a lump sum, depending on what was killed, how many were killed, etc. Then I award for Roleplaying the character. Playing obviously out of character should be an xp penalty.
Playing in character in easy circumstances is worth a small bonus.
Playing in character in moderate circumstances (ie: you are a paladin and you group wants to torture info out of a captive) gives a moderate bonus.
Playing in character when it's difficult (ie: that same Paladin being forced to accept the surrender of someone who murdered members of his church) is a large bonus.
Playing in character against impossible odds (ie: The Paladin being faced with some great evil, yet being honorbound to do nothing to it at the moment) is a huge bonus.

That is how I do things. I use a paladin as the basis because they have the largest number of roleplaying constraints placed on them (and if you ever find 2 game masters who expect the same behavior set in a Paladin, I'd be shocked.) Frankly, nearly ANY activity a player can come up with can be justified as playing their character properly if the player is clever and cheesey enough. It is only through the players exploring their characters weaknesses that roleplaying bonuses are really useful.

In my current game, I am a Rogue/Bard. Among other things, I was cursed years earlier when I broke into a tower which was inhabited (unknown to me) by a Lich. I survived the encounter only through blind luck (this is all backstory material) and now the character is pretty much completely useless in the presence of undead...or even in places where undead MAY be found like crypts. Even a simple skeleton moving it's arm or the moan of "Braaaiiins" from a zombie is enough to send him into a state of near catatonia. This "weakness" is one of the things I base his personality around and when it comes into play, I generally end up with a roleplaying bonus.

Being stupid and getting rewarded for it (the ooze example above) just sounds like a bad situation. Were it me, I'd ask that rogue to go scout ahead, and then run the other way. People like that WILL get others killed and need to be abandoned as quickly as possible if you want to live a long life.

My group, whether I'm DMing or someone else, usually has a period as we wrap up for the night where you can sue for exp. Typically this is where outstanding roleplay or innovation is rewarded. 90% of the time, people do not sue for themselves. They sue for other members of the party. If the DM feels that a reward is meritted they'll give exp. Often a smaller amount of exp will be awarded to the person doing the suing as well.

Things that are typically sued for (these really happened at one time or another):

"I sue for Joe for figuring out the maze."

"I sue for Megan for using perform to distract the angry mob"

"I sue for Kevin for roleplaying the encounter with the mage and averting combat"

"I sue for Drew for staying in character, even though he actually figured out the plot point before anyone else"

"I sue for Del for putting style above results" (it was a superhero RPG)

In most games, the rewards for these types of incidents range from 25-100 exp each. Depending on how well the group was playing we've had as few as 2 and as many as 15 things sued for in one session. - Nymm
