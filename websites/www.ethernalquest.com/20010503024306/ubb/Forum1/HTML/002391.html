1) Having a background zone loader running, it would require almost twice as much memory as EQ, and a considerably faster proceser but would load zone data into a seperate section of data, the date that is loaded and the amount of it that is located would depend upon your reletive position on the map. ie the closer you get to a zone, the more of that zone is loaded compared to the other possible zones that could be loaded. Or you could do a variant ...

Each of the required files for a given zone are all in all, typically anywhere from 5 megs to 20 megs in size. The largest zone (by size) that I can think of is East Wastes, which measures in at a grand total of 10 megabytes on the hard drive.

Now, if you load any adjacent zones into memory in the background slowly, starting with whichever zones are the closest (and keeping the zone you just left in memory), you might at most consume another 30-40mb of RAM. I can't think of many zones that have a lot of adjacent zones. East commons has west freeport, nektulos woods, north ro, and west commons; east wastes has iceclad, kael, great divide, and crystal caverns; west wastes has sirens grotto, temple of veeshan, and dragons necropolis, etc ...

Once the files are in memory, you could do some pre-world building steps on each of them, working with a limited amount of memory. I don't know much about what is in EQ's package files, but some things that you may want to try and do is have an arranged set of textures, have the appropriate pointers associated with the textures for surfaces, etc, get everything ready that you may need, but only do it when you have extra processing time.

OR ... you could just convert all the package files to a new format in a pre-world built fashion, so that when loaded up into memory, it's a few short steps to build that world.

But then, if I was going to go that far, I'd just export all the meshes into 3ds format, export all the map geometry, import it into the Unreal engine, and try to use a REAL engine for making a game.

-Phantal
