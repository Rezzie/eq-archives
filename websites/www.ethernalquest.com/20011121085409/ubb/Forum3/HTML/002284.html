One thing could be the network segment you have assigned to your laptop.
If you are running DHCP for your IP address at work and home, then this does not matter.
But, if at work, say, you have you laptop with an ip address of 192.168.1.5 and your work PC has an IP address of 192.168.1.10, then when you get home, your home pc has an IP address of 192.168.0.10 and your laptop is still on 192.168.1.5, then you will have problems.
Assuming you have the subnet of 255.255.255.0.


Hope this makes a little bit of sense.

Basically there are two ways of doing this:

1) Have 2 nics in a linux machine and make it your router -- everything goes through it.
2) Have the linux machine just be on the network in promiscuous mode.

What I'd do is plug "computer A" (eq machine) into the hub, then plug the laptop "computer B" (linux machine) into the hub (or if laptop is eq machine, regular computer is linux - doesn't matter)...

Then plug the hub's upstream into your cable modem.

Unless @home does some funky limitations on DHCP, they should both get addresses.

This is by far the easiest way to set it all up.

Make sure they are set up for DHCP. If they are set up for static you may wind up having to mess around with route tables, DHCP is so much easier.

To sniff a network from linux doesn't require the machine to have an IP address or to be able to talk to the net.

ifconfig eth0 up
tcpdump -i eth0 | grep <ip of EQ machine>

Surf the net from the windows machine and watch the screen of the linux machine (must be root to do this). As long as you see more than just ARP or BROADCAST going by on the screen then you are seeing the traffic and can run SEQ to sniff the EQ packets.
