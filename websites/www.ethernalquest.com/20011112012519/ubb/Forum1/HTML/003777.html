Zombie 		posted 08-24-2001 07:02 AM            
ram manufactures have nothing to do with mobo models.

If your mobo supports say, 512 megs PC133 unbuffered 3.3v SDRAM 168 pin DIMMs, no matter who makes the mobo, anyone who makes ram of that spec will work. Be it generic or top of the line, there is no reason it wouldnt work wither way. Anyone who tells you that your board only supports 'name brand' ram is just trying to rip you off. Now if the say your board only supports RIMMs (rambus) or DDR SDRAM, then yes, you need to get the proper type of ram, but again, this has nothing to do with who makes the ram.

casey
Zombie 		posted 08-24-2001 08:47 AM            
all my current systems are amd based, and i've yet to encounter incompatibility issues.

Also, even with generic ram you can have some level of assurance that it will perform (in addition to the lifetime or 1yr replacement warranties a lot of ram carries), is in the way they do the QA testing. They'll fab a lot of chips, and run tests on a sampling at 133Mhz and if they pass, they are shipped and sold as PC133 sticks. If they fail, they are tested at a slower speed of 100Mhz, and if they make that cut, the lot gets sold as PC100 ram, etc. Processors are also tested and marketted in such a fashion. Of course there are going to be bad apples sometimes (single particle of dust is enough to screw up the silicon during fab), but for the most part, your going to get a working part.

I myself am a skeptic of cheap ram, but my wife just bought 2 sticks of 512 megs for $32 a pop, and they work like a charm (and are warrantied just in case), so i'm beggining to lighten up my views on 'cheap ram'.

when they say you need a mobo that can handle 'high density' ram, that is just referring to the max capacity, again, this goes back to my statement that any ram to the mobos spec will work. performance wise, it is actually better to install pairs of sdram. most BIOS's allow you to specify interleaving of some sort (2 way, 4 way) which is basically similar to having raid 0 on your ram (but nothing like it...). it can help performance cause it can write to one chip, and write to the second while the first recovers... and so on... course, not saying i'm descriping it well :)

old ram DID have to be installed in pairs at one time. 72pin simms needed it. the reason was (and i might have my bits wrong) that the memory path was 64bit, BUT the chips were only 32bit, so you needed two of them (identical) to work.

course, this hasn't been true for several years now... but you can't expect a sales whore to know that :)
