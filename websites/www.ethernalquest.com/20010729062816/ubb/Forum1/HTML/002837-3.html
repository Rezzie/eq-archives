Thrust is only created by pushing something away from you. Ever heard the phrase, "Every action has an equal and opposite reaction"? This is the nature of thrust. I am still not terribly interested in talking to you. Go read some books on determinism and think carefully about it.

Here is a synopsis.

I assume that, at any given time, the current state of the universe, and *only* that state, *fully* implies the immediately-following state of the universe. All effects *must* come directly from causes, so nothing will happen in the immediately-following state which was not caused by the current state. Therefore, one could examine the following state (from outside the box, so to speak) and fully reconstruct the current state by retracing all causes back to their effects.

In short, state T implies state T+1, and state T+1 implies state T. Slide T around the timeline all you want, there is no time at which it isn't true. Make +1 as small or large as you like, but the "following" state is still fully implied by the current state.

There is no infinitely-branching tree of choices. At any time, there is only one choice, one state, which may immediately follow the current, and it is fully implied *by* the current. No matter how many times you try it, you will always get the same following state out of the current state.

Now replace the word "implied" with "determined", which you should, and determinism will be your daddy.

I couldn't find any of my books that described this, and it's been well over a year since I've thought about this, but if I remember right...

As an observer A (that has mass) approaches the event horizon, the force needed to remain outside the event horizon approaches infinity. It is something like trying to climb a wall by applying force horizontally while the wall is sloping upwards more and more to the point of becoming vertical. Inside the event horizon, there is no straight line in spacetime (keeping in mind that the path light takes defines a straight line) that leads back through the event horizon; and since A cannot travel as fast as light, his path in spacetime is restricted by the "light cone" formed by all paths light can take passing through A's location, he cannot pass back out of the event horizon either.

From the reference frame of an observer B that remains outside the event horizon, A never makes it to the event horizon. Instead A appears to slow down, and light reflected from A arriving back at B becomes more and more redshifted as the light has less and less energy (since light travels at a constant speed, rather than losing kinetic energy when travelling "against" gravity as an object in freefall with mass would, it loses energy by having an increased wavelength). As the time passed for B approaches infinity, A approaches the event horizon and the light from B approaches zero energy.

Some other very odd things are predicted by our models, but I probably shouldn't attempt to describe them without reading about them again.
