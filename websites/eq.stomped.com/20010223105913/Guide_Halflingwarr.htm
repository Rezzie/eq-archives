    Halfling Warriors

    By Tanlen Bosenberry

    The Warrior in EQ

    Warriors are made for combat, period. They are made to be able to wade into the thick of battle able and withstand more punishment without being healed than any class out there. Many refer to them as "tanks" because of their staying power in a battle. As a warrior you have more of a chance to survive when things go very bad than any other class. When I say, "things go very bad" I'm not referring to a losing fight and where people need to run. I'm referring to the unexpected situation where multiple "reds" are around you at close range. I've hunted in Unrest and had a huge train come by and wipe out everyone in my party except me. Warriors have more hit points and a higher AC than any other class out there. They also get all the best defensive skills and at levels before any other class. Warriors can wear all forms of armor and have the best armor at higher levels. As far as physical offense (with a weapon) or defense they have no equal. The ability to absorb or even completely duck damage is the warrior's number one advantage and asset in EQ.

    So now that we know what the warrior's strongest point is we can cover the second best thing that they are good for, (and the reason why most people play them) melee offense. They get to use the biggest and best weapons out there. They can deal more damage with a weapon than any other class. The problem is, most people have an ideal in their head of what a warrior "should be" but it usually doesn't match up to what they are in EQ. Most people don't think "ability to survive in combat more than 60 seconds", they think "Conan the Barbarian, slicing through anything that stands up to them". If you want to deal raw damage you might be more at home with a caster. If you want to deal the most melee damage, you may want to think about a monk. But if you want to be in the "thick of battle" where all the action is at then a warrior has no equal! At this point in time, Warriors are not the masters of melee offense in EQ, at least not the way that most people set them up (I'll get into this later). Maybe one day that will change, but for now they are a strong second.

    Mages do much more instant damage at those higher levels. They can finish creatures faster thus equaling shorter fights. Problem with mages is they usually come out of a battle with full health or none at all. Older mobs will tear a mage to shreds if it gets in close enough for melee. So what do we know? Mages have better offense later in life whereas warriors have better defense and can actually survive after 60 seconds of melee combat. Everquest is definitely group oriented. Mages need tanks to keep the mobs off them so they can destroy the mob, while warriors need mages to keep the fights quick and to finish off fleeing mobs. This means that a warrior's strongpoint later in life (in a group is defense) What's important to defense? AC, agility, stamina, and defensive skills. We will get more into these later.

    Halflings

    Halflings are about half the size of a human thus the name. Many people don't want to play Halflings because they don't look as "imposing" or "cool" as many other races. A lot of people have a short complex also. There are a lot of advantages of going with a halfling. They are extremely agile and dexterous. They naturally have the ability to hide, which can keep you out of a lot of hurting. Hide is excellent when you need to sit down and heal up or are in a dangerous area and need a minute to get your wits about you. Halflings also get the innate ability to sneak. Basically being able to move without a mob noticing you. Another excellent skill for getting out of potentially bad situations. Let's say you are deep in a zone and get cut off from your party. You are too weak or injured to solo in this area but you can sneak your way out of the zone or to where you think your party is. Another plus to halflings is that they have infravision. Infravision helps you immensely at night. From being safer running around to finding stuff to hunt, it always helps if you can see what's in front of you. Another good thing about Halflings is that they can wear small armor, which is cheaper and lighter. Finally, the last really good thing is their starting city, Rivervale. I'll cover Rivervale later.

    Halflings as Warriors

    Where are they? You don't see many about. You see a lot of barbarian warriors. I've read about five different warrior guides and have never seen they words "halfling warrior" mentioned once (at least not in a positive light) I see ogre, troll, and barbarian mentioned in almost every guide. I guess because they have the highest strength, and stamina. Plus they look real tough and powerful. If I asked you what a halfling looks like in platemail, chances are you don't know or it would take you a few minutes to conjure up a picture. If I asked you about an ogre or barbarian, chances are you could picture it right away.

    In my OPINION halflings make the best warriors, and I'll tell you why. The reason is defense and offense. Higher defense means getting hit less and when you do, taking less damage. That means, if you have a healer in your group they don't need to spend as much mana on you as they do on the clumsy barbarians, ogres, or trolls who seem like they have a big bulls-eye on their chest. More mana usually equates to more buff spells for the party, more mana to heal other members, and more mana for offensive spells. All that equals less chance of someone or a whole party ending up dead. Survival or Victory. Whatever you want to call it. Usually around now someone's screaming, "yeah but I have a lot more hit points then a halfling". Yep sure do. When you get older and mobs start whacking you for over 100 points a shot, every other swing because you can't get out of the way via dodge, parry, or riposte those extra hit points get sucked up pretty fast. If the party can't take the mob down as fast because they need to heal your damage receiving arse, who cares if you have more hit points? I'd rather not get hit in the first place, or when I do not for as much. If the tank dies the party usually follows if you are fighting formidable foes. The next argument I hear is "Yeah but I hit for more damage than halflings at older levels". That's the biggest misconception there is. A halfling maximized for offense will destroy the large sized warrior later in life (I'll get into this more later). Also the mages do a lot more damage than warriors. Warriors will not be able to take mobs down as fast as a mage at older levels. If you want to deal out the most damage in the group than be a mage. Why try to be the offensive hero when you can't compare to the mage standing behind you? The main reason why halflings have such great defense is agility. If you maximize your agility it will be 125 if not 100. Another nice thing about having a high agility is finishing ability. You notice that when you are down to your last bar of health your agility starts dropping like a rock and your trying to run while getting whacked in the back? I can't imagine what it's like to have a low agility to start with and then lose agility on top of that. Doesn't give you much hope of survival. Taking this into account I can usually stick it out in a fight a little longer before I'm forced to run or back out, and if I do run I usually have a better chance of escape.

    Statistics

    The people at EQ say that the most important stats for warriors are strength, stamina, and agility. In my opinion that statement is wrong. I believe the two most important stats are agility for defense and dexterity for offense. Dexterity is the most under-rated stat there is for a warrior. I bet you 90 percent of ogre warriors out there have a 150 strength and a 147 stamina because they dumped all there points into those stats. I'm telling you that after level 30 a halfling warrior would eat that ogres lunch in a duel if certain conditions were met. The conditions being 1. Similar equipment on each, 2. Both being of the same level, 3. The halfling with a maximized dexterity of at least 180 or so (not hard to do if the warrior game planned for a high dexterity) 4. an agility of 120 or so, and 5. The halfling wielding 2 one handed weapons with decent procs (special effects that randomly go off based on dexterity).

    Here's what a 40th level shaman posted on the subject of warriors in regards to agility and dexterity:

    "My opinion is that all classes start to shine after level 30, and this includes small race Warriors. MOBs really do get tougher at 30+ and if your agility sucks, your healer is going to notice regardless of how many hit-points you have.  At 40+ it's even more important to have a good agility. At this point most casters have SEVERE down times due to mana recovery. You will want a Bard or Enchanter in your group, or use a manastone/Cleric combo. And at this level the secondary priests (Shaman/Druid) do not cast Greater Heal for recovery. You get Chloroplast, then you have to sit and wait for your HP. GH just makes a dent in ANY race Warrior at 40+, not just large races.  Last, but not least, at 40+ Warriors will be dual wielding proc weapons in most battles. You will want a high Dex for that. The picture of a big strong Warrior wielding a shiny 2H silvery axe is romantic. But the fact is that the agile and dexterous Warrior is just as valuable at 40+. And ANY Warrior at those levels is always welcome in a group.

    Snowbeard Blackprophet"
     

    Ok back to stats. One last thing about dexterity that most don't know. It influences the rate at which you learn combat skills. Now lets touch on the other stats. Your strength can be buffed pretty easy by group members and is high enough, as it is to comfortably wear plate mail. Adding points in strength aren't going to add as much damage as getting some good proc weapons that go off a lot more because of your high dexterity. Stamina is a waste if you have the mindset of not being hit in the first place and you will have plenty of hit points with your starting stamina as it is. Intelligence, wisdom, and charisma just aren't worth the points. As far as stat placement I recommend to either dump all the points into dexterity (for offense) or all into agility (for defense) or a balance between the two.

    Using Training Points

    I cringe every time I read a guide that tells you at level 1 to go put training points in skills that go up on their own. In my OPINION there are 2 ways to use training points. First way is to develop a trade skill or a skill you wouldn't use much, which would be expensive or hard to develop otherwise. Second way is to learn the basics of a skill (by spending 1 point ONLY on it) you do not have such as a language your trainer offers you or a skill you obtain once you reach a certain level (such as dodge at 6).

    Choosing your weapon(s)

    Ok this is very important. Your choices are 2handed slashing, 2handed blunt, 1handed slashing, 1handed blunt, piercing, and archery. You should work on keeping EVERY weapon skill there is maximized for your level. As a warrior you can use any weapon. You'll use archery for pulling creatures from a distance and finishing fleeing ones off. You'll use two-handers when fighting mobs with damage shields. You'll want to be able to use any one hander out there because there are nice blunts and piercing weapons out there too and you may come across one that's better than the one handed slashing weapon you are using.

    Starting Location

    Halflings start out in Rivervale, which has a great hunting area for levels 1-10. Rivervale is a one zone, easy to get around in town unlike Freeport or Qeynos. Rivervale is probably the most central location in the world that you could start in. If you want to go hunt in Runnyeye, Beholder, Befallen, Highpass hold, Kithicor forest, West Commons, the Karanas, or Paw they are all close. Rivervale also has one of the best lower level quests for warriors, the squad ring quest. I'm not going to spoil it so you'll have to figure it. It's definitely worth doing. That's all I'm going to say about Rivervale. There are good city guides out there if you want more info.

    A Couple Group Basics

    A few things I wanted to mention about fighting in groups.

    1. Learn the assist command. This is especially important if you are fighting with an enchanter or bard who are mesmerizing the extra mobs. Nothing is more frustrating to the player who takes a mob out of the combat equation only to have you put them back in.

    2. Learn to watch your cleric or casters in combat. If the mob gets on them and they can't get off spells then you need to step in. Do everything thing you can by attacking and taunting to get them off the caster. A warrior who watches out for the casters and healers are very appreciated and not the norm.

    Closing

    One thing I wanted to mention but didn't want to do a section on was etiquette. If someone helps you out try to give him or her a tip. Most people won't take tips but it's the thought that counts. You want a faction increase between you and your fellow players? That's one way to earn it. Don't bug people about buffs, especially if they aren't in your group. Help people out when they don't expect it.

    I didn't go into group strategy, hunting areas, or a lot of other things that warrior guides usually cover because there are plenty of resources out there for that stuff. There was a gap in the warrior guides concerning halflings. The average person thinking about playing a warrior whom reads one of them guides may think I need to decide between a barbarian, troll, or ogre. I wanted to make sure halflings get their fare shake. The people who designed this game aren't stupid. Many come from a long time mudding background. They know what works. They understand balance. They didn't make any bad race/class combos.
