I found it interesting, heres just a bit about guilds:

Are there other ways in which your 'Guilds' are different?

Yes. A few of the more notable changes include :

• Guilds can swear fealty to other Guilds (even organizations of other types,) thereby forming allegiance trees and setting the political landscape. In this manner, the 'Guild' can grow over time, as new organizations are pulled into the fold. Eventually the concept of the 'guild' blurs, and the organization begins to look more like a Nation.

• To further this distinction, Guilds are bound to territory within the game. With enough power and resources, a hierarchy of guilds can conquer a city-state to use as a home base, an open marketplace, a military outpost or a mercenary stronghold.

• As more city-states are taken, the guild becomes a Kingdom. Still more will fashion a Kingdom into an Empire.

• Guilds may declare War, leading to an entirely new type of game play. Rules of engagement can arise, and intrigue and diplomacy become a valuable commodity.

• Mercenary armies can be formed, moving between guilds to appeal to the highest bidder.

• Guilds support rank structures - so Guilds can set their own recruiting policies, and players can be promoted up through the organization over time.

These are all novel ideas, and the implementation may even be succesful but I see several issues with such a structure.

When you allow a group of players to "dominate" a "city-state" you create newbie havoc. For lack of a better reference let's refer to a "city-state" as a EQ Zone.

A group, or guild, or several guilds, decide to band together and exert influence over, say, Qeynos Hills. Anyone in the group receives the obvious benefits of a safe haven for themselves. Unfortunately, anyone not in that group is now excluded from the maximum enjoyment of that zone, the "don't belong". Get enough such groups dominating other areas and suddenly you have a world full of "city-states" where if you don't belong you could very well be screwed.

While in an ideal situation this could be great, as it would create many small player-controlled nations. But taken too far, and from your explanation I think you do, and it would give the ability of a "clique" of players to make the game highly unenjoyable for other players.

In my mind the thing EQ does right is minimized the ability of other players to ruin your enjoyment of the game (yes there are still issues but not many). This means that folks unwilling to partake in guild activities (and no matter how you slice it a majority of players in EQ don't participate in guilds) would be excluded, and hence not enjoy, a large portion of the game world. This happens because you give players control over a piece of land/property/object in your game that other people use as well. Whereas in AC such influence trees only dominate members of that tree, in your world the influence trees dominate more than the members of that tree.

You get the idea, this would allow players to indirectly affect others enjoyment. I certainly wouldn't want to be a newbie in your world after it had established "dominant" landscapes. Talk about intimidating.
