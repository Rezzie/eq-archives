The Meaningless Words of Everquest

by Loral on September 13, 2004

Jargon plagues Everquest. We find meaningless words or phrases everywhere. Everyone has an idea, everyone has a complaint, and everyone has something about the game they want to talk about. Instead of finding ways to properly communicate what our problem is, we fall back to generalizations and buzzwords that may have meaning to us but lose that meaning the more they are used and misused. These words don't help us communicate with SOE. Instead of articulating a problem, they muddy the conversation and cloud the real problems.

Five terms in particular get used dozens of times each week on the various forums. These words add nothing to our conversations and don't help SOE find and fix the problems we gloss over. If we eliminate them from our vocabulary and speak in detail, we increase SOE's ability to respond. Lets take a look at these meaningless words:

Uber: The German word for “Over” describes high end characters, guilds, equipment, or abilities. We hear about uberguilds and ubermobs. We use the word as insult or praise when a friend acquires a new shiny item. Uber is anything more powerful than you. Some 65 paladins with 300 AA points don't consider themselves uber. Level 25 rangers tear through Crushbone like a tornado and everyone in the zone considers them uber. There is no specific use of the word that helps describe a particular state of the game, guilds, or players. In almost every Mobhunter article I wrote for my first three months, my editor, the uber wizard Azile Ciricbane, had to edit out my use of “uberguild” and told me to replace it with something more specific.

Casual: Casual is the most misunderstood and misused phrase in the Everquest community. There are casual guilds who raid the Elemental Planes. Some casual players have hundreds of AA points and play 40 hours a week. Everyone has a different definition of a casual gamer. Sometimes it means low level. Other times it means non-raiders. Instead of asking for changes for the casual gamer, we need to describe exactly what it is we desire. Do we need more short-duration events? Do we need less locked content? Do we need higher power equipment available to single LDON / Ornate equipped level 65 groups? Avoid the word casual, speak in detail.

Mudflation: This ancient term gets used any time someone sees the game progress and doesn't like it. Mudflation means little but has a built-in wrapper of negativity that lets us label progression like a package of spoiled meat. Few describe exactly what they mean when they use the term and fewer can describe how to fix it. The game gets larger. The high-end zones get higher. Level limits increase. Equipment grows in power. That isn't mudflation, that's progress.

Timesink: This is one of my favorites. Timesinks describe any action in Everquest that someone doesn't enjoy. Everquest is a timesink. Reading a book is a timesink. I know one perfect way to avoid timesinks. If you aren't enjoying what you do, do something else. Before you begin telling me that you HAVE to camp shards to get intoVex Thal or you HAVE to get your Ring of Scale faction up, remember that this entire game is voluntary and the world is big enough to find a million other and often more enjoyable things to do. If you find a particular event or hunt that bores you to tears, instead of labeling it a timesink, just tell SOE what you don't like about it. Speak in details, not overused and meaningless generalities.

Class Balance: Classes, by definition, cannot be balanced. Every class is good at some things and bad in others. There is no way to balance the wide variety of skills from one class to another. How does one compare the blasts of a wizard to the slows of a shaman to the healing of a cleric? With limited combat interaction it becomes difficult to make every one of the sixteen classes unique yet not required for a group. SOE can tweak class abilities and help support those unique roles. SOE can also tune encounters so they work well with particular class archetypes as they did with knights and the Ikkinz trials. There is never a clear solution for class balance and there is no conclusion. Classes cannot be balanced without ruining the unique skills that each class has to offer.

Every day players interact with SOE and every day players use these terms to gloss over deeper problems or recommendations. When you find yourself using one of these terms when writing a message board post or sending feedback, ask yourself what you are really saying and replace it with detail. Instead of asking for more casual-friendly content, ask for more single-group events that don't require raid gear. Instead of complaining about timesinks, ask for more Ring of Scale faction options. We love our meaningless terms but our communication becomes stronger and SOE's ability to respond becomes greater when we say exactly what we mean.

My idea of timesink:

A "timesink" is directly connected to the fun in a game. A personal, subjective concept but that can be transformed into something objective.

Basically a timesink is a part of the game that isn't really present. It's a "hole" in the fabric of the game. A timesink could be the travel time when during the travel nothing really happens. Or a player waiting a bar to fill.

A "timesink" is exactly a design strategy that replaces the void left by the gameplay with a time-sink. What should have been covered with gameplay is now hidden by an expense of time or a repetition of an action.

And the repetition is the link to the fun. Because the fun directly depends on what is felt "new".

Comment Posted by: Talaen on September 14, 2004 08:50 AM

I think the distinction between casual and non-casual gamers is something that merits quite a bit more discussion, and really something that SOE needs to get a handle on if they want to seriously move forward with the game.

In my view, there are several different areas that all work together to determine how someone plays the game:

- Time to play. Some people can play for 80+ hours every week, others for only a few on specific days.

- Ambition. Some people are driven to progress their character and obtain new items, spells, levels, and so on - and others are not so driven and are just out there having fun.

- Immersion. Many people see the game as nothing more than a hack-and-slash fest with a chatroom. This is why we see silly character names and conversations in game. Many others are deeply interested in the integrity of the game "world" itself.

- Activities. Some people like raids, some people don't. Some people like outdoor zones and others prefer dungeons.

- Challenge. A great many players are content with very little in the way of challenge - other players completely thrive on it and grow bored quickly if not presented with insanely difficult areas to conquer.

You can't define "casual" gamers based on any one area - to do so ignores a lot of other things that factor into someone's enjoyment of the game. I know many players and no two of them are exactly alike in how they approach the game. Some have lots of time but little ambition, others have lots of ambition but really don't care about the story, and others still have moderate amounts of time, but hate to raid and constantly seek out the most challenging things they can do.

Comment Posted by: Talaen on September 14, 2004 09:11 AM

I have to also disagree on the term "mudflation".

I spent a few years writing for MUDs and they suffered the same problem that everquest does - an inflationary cycle of player power and creature/item power that occurs as new content is added to the game.

Most people think of progression, or expansion of the game as the game having more to do. New places to go and explore, new things to fight and conquer, and new treasures to find and use. It goes without saying that in a level-based game these items and places will have to be more powerful than existing ones in order to entice players to use them.

The cycle is this - the original game is more or less balanced for players of all levels. The game expands, and more challenging areas are added. Encounters become tougher. To compensate, players are granted more power in order to lower the difficulty of these encounters. This is a normal, healthy cycle for any online game.

Mudflation is an ambiguous term. It can refer to the cycle of expansion and progression, or more commonly it refers to a power bleed effect. It's a given that expanding content will trivialize older content for a percentage of the player base. That's expected - after all, that's why the new content was added. The folks at the top will go do the new stuff, and the old stuff won't really challenge them anymore. However, mudflation occurs when the additional power given to the players in the new content gets used by lower-level players in lower-level areas. The best way to recognize mudflation is to look at the impact on the parts of the game that were not actually expanded. For example, if all of the new content you added to the game was for levels 40 and above, yet suddenly, your level 20 characters are having a much essier time of things - you have mudflation.

Everquest is not alone in this problem. Most online games have struggled with it and very few have overcome it. In my experience, there is only one way to really prevent this from occurring and that is to be extremely strict when it comes to itemization and new content. If you add a new level 30 dungeon, then it needs to be no more or less challenging than any other level 30 dungeon. The weapons you add to that dungeon need to be equivalent in power to the weapons in other level 30 dungeons, and just as difficult to obtain. This is a challenge for most area designers, as they are trying to come up with things that would entice players to try out the new area.

If you have a game with a history of this problem, the only way to really address the existing damage is quite simply to rewrite the older areas of your game. The balance of things can be restored if you recognize that you have significantly raised the bar on player power. Old areas will have to be redone, old items will have to be upgraded and replaced to correspond to the new "standard" of player power at all levels of the game. For a pay-for-play game like Everquest, this is something that players will appreciate, but that won't really earn the company any more money in the short-term. Long-term, it creates a healthier game which retains players, but I can count on one hand the number of business and marketing executives I've met who would really understand the importance of something like that.

Over the years the various members of the EQ Live team have all struggled with this, and they've made some visible attempts to try and rein things in - I think we can all agree that the recommended and required levels on items were a good step. Unfortunately, they didn't do enough to stop this early on, and as a result there's a lot of content in the game which is simply not used even though there are still players to use it. While it remains to be seen how much worse this gets, in my opinion there's already a very large "cleanup" project that should be done.

Comment Posted by: Candymang on September 16, 2004 02:42 PM

I will have to disagree with a couple of the top five most over-used and misunderstood terms that you listed. Here are a couple terms that I see all the time, and a summary definition of them.


leet or l33t (n). A bastardization of the word "elite" in "hacker speak". Similiar to "uber", it is not quite the same thing but often can go hand-in-hand. While uber is best used to describe and item or anything else tangible (ex an uber breastplate), l33t is more apt to describe something intangible such as skill, knowledge, etc. Of course, sometimes they are mistakenly inter-changed since often people that have the most skill are able to acquire the best equipment, and vice-versa people with the best equipment have the most leverage to display their abilities. Of course, this touches upon the entire class balance issue, because a melee class that is l33t but not uber won't do you much good in a tough zone/encounter, and as much can be said about a cleric or enchanter that is uber but not l33t. For example, I can take the most talented warrior with 5 kazillion AA but is only ldon and EP geared and he will get owned in Ikkinz. On the hand, I myself as a cleric that is ldon/minor time/KT geared have been able to pull out wins that would have been CRs for some a$$-clown cleric that has rode his guild's coat-tails and is Time+ geared.


Zerg (n). Zerging can mean a lot of different things as well. Zerging can be a strategy for a particular encounter, or it can be an entire raid philosophy, or it can simply mean max-dps at a certain time in any given fight. Allow me to clarify: Corinav, the "boss" fight in Plane of Water, is a zerg fight. Once he becomes targetable, the entire raid goes maximum dps to drop him before the timer for the event boots you out. Some guilds, however, use the zerg mentality for every fight. Taking 150+ people to Terris Thule and just throwing people at her with minimal strategy and crushing the encounter with pure numbers when the event could be done with a very skilled and tight raid of 35 people is the most popular, and negative, conotation of the term zerg. It's the reason that GoD in my mind has so many instanced events that only allow set numbers of people to join in order to progress. Zerging is also used to denote the point in the fight at which all damage classes are supposed to use all of their damage disc and casters fire off their nukes in order to effectively "burn" a target down.


Own or pwn (v). To own, or the hacker bastardization "pwn", is to display obvious dominance over someone/something else. A player can "pwn" another player in terms of gear/AA/etc, or could simply be able to display dominance in a duel format. While your 72-man Time-geared raid could walk into Vex Thal and "pwn" the entire place quite easily, they in turn would probably be "pwned" trying to walk into Txevu without massive planning and leadership to guide them through.
