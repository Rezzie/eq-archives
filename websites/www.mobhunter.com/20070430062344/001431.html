Now for our last bit of news, the Lost Scrolls of Moorgard!

In the late hours of the evening, when the blackness of of night was absolute, I found myself hunched over my writing table scrawling out this article on sheets of parchment with a large quill. A whisper brought my head up from my words, slipping through the ancient halls of Castle Mobhunter. I picked up my candelabra dripping hot red wax onto the oak table, gathered my monk's robes about me, and with trepidation, followed the strange whisper.

The whisper led deep into the lower vaults of the castle, revealing halls and rooms I had never before seen, halls lost to the current residents of the castle. The whisper traveled further until it led to a huge oak door banded with steel and marked with a great M. I pushed on the door and felt an ancient latch break free. The door swung open.

A room filled with dust and cobwebs held nine large pots. I lifted the heavy lid off of one of these pots and felt old stale air escape for the first time in centuries. Inside were sheaves of lambskin bound and tied in thick covers of leather. I untied it, feeling the twine turn to dust under my fingers, and read the first page. Suddenly understanding dawned on me. I had found the Lost Scrolls of Moorgard!

From September 25th, 2000 until August 16th 2003, Moorgard wrote over four hundred articles for Mobhunter. These articles entertained and informed thousands over the years. They helped steer Everquest into a new age. Now these articles, once thought lost forever, have been found.

These articles document the history of Everquest from the times of Kunark to the times of the Planes of Power. Follow Everquest back through its history from the eyes of Everquest's best editorial writer.

Comment Posted by: Loral on October 25, 2005 01:38 PM

"What really ticked off the few players of AC2 was they had just released an expansion for it in August."

I went over and visited their closing thread. It made me pretty sad. To see the core of a community, even a small one, broken up like that was pretty brutal.

A lot of people were upset about paying for the expansion, thinking that the company squeezed out a few drops before tossing the game aside. That probably wasn't the case. Expansions do a lot to bring new players and returning players back into the game. It was probably a last ditch effort to get new players into the game and fund continued uptime.

As far as squeezing it out, expansions run about $30 and we play the hell out of them for about six months (well, some of us do). Today I'm about to go buy a copy of Metroid Pinball for my Nintendo DS. I seriously doubt I'll get the same sort of playtime out of Metroid Pinball that I will get out of Depths of Darkhollow. Even two months with an expansion is usually enough time to justify the cost.

The problem we run into is the idea of "investment" (I wrap it in quotes to keep it off of my shoes). People think of their time in an MMO as an investment but don't think of the ten hours they spend playing Shadow of the Colossus as the same sort of investment. When they're done with Shadow, they stick it on a shelf and happily go after the next game. A game like EQ or WOW, or EQ2 or AC2, however, they expect to be able to play it for six years (and some of us have). Yes, we pay a monthly fee on top of our $30 for the expansion, but its the cost of two movies and less than the cost of a DVD and we get a LOT more play time out of it than the typical game.

Still, its understandable to see the anger on that forum. Regardless of the lack of a financial or time investment, people have an emotional investment. We make friends, set up goals and accomplishments, build up our character. In most games, we're done when we decide we're done. In MMOs, some of us will be done when the company yanks the plugs. That's a disturbing reality for this type of game.

Comment Posted by: Vamirez on October 26, 2005 04:25 AM

I think the time needed to loot your corpse and equip your gear is an interesting game detail.

You all know the following situation: A pull went awry, you died. You think 'Oh my god, now we wipe!' Right after zoning, you type 'Are you alive?? Hello??' A rezz window appears, you click immediately and arrive in a mid-combat situation. It looks critical. You loot your corpse like mad, hoping you will be armed in time and able to return to the fray and help turn the tide of battle before it is too late.

The same mechanisam applies when many people have died in a raid. You see them put their armor on while bitter fighting is going on around them, and I think this actually adds quite a bit to the situation - increased tension and increased difficulty (the situation has to be endured longer before rezzed people can help again).

I don't think the argumentation that its too much to right-click your thirty items does stand up against that. It reasonably takes a moment to reequip all your stuff. Removing details such as this imho make a game more bland.
