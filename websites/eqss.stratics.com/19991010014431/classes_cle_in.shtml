Playing a Cleric - Intermediate Strategies

 Overview   Highs & Lows   Creation   Playing   Formative Years   Terrible Teens   Group Play   Hints 

Overview
So you want to be one of the healers of Norrath. Listen well and heed my words faithful one, for the journey is long and hard - but infinitely rewarding! Clerics are one of the most exciting and playable classes in the land of Norrath, possessing healing and offensive spells that make them a cornerstone of any well-balanced group, as well as being able to wield blunt weapons and wear almost any armor they please. These unique abilities allow the Cleric to hit hard as well as take a beating, and live on to fight another day. In addition, Clerics are granted abilities that allow them to take on undead foes at levels that would have a fighter at the same level fleeing for his life! Whether your style is group or solo play, the Cleric is equipped to deal with the perils of Norrath like no other class.

Highs & Lows
To be honest, a Cleric is not as effective at solo play as the heavier hitting pure fighter classes. This is because round for round, a Cleric is going to have a harder time dealing consistent damage to kill high level monsters, as well as having fewer hit points to survive the vicious counter-attacks. Clerics can dish out a beating with their spells, but their most powerful damage spells take a while to cast - leaving them vulnerable to a well placed hit from their foe - and drain large amounts of mana. Take it from me, the one time you're guaranteed to need a quick heal is right after draining all of your mana to blast your opponent down to an inch of his life! Additionally, while there are plenty of blunt weapons that do a fair amount of damage, only the most powerfully enchanted mace is going to keep up with a two-handed claymore in the hands of an experienced barbarian warrior. The one caveat to this is the fact that Clerics are able to wear all kinds of armor, including full plate. Find yourself a powerful set of armor and wade into the fray, well protected from harm. This is an advantage none of the other spellcasters can claim!

As with many other classes, Clerics come into their own in a group. With just one fighter to distract the monsters and keep your hands free for healing and support, you'll find that your tank can live well 'nigh forever battling against monsters far above your own levels. And your offensive spells are potent enough to deal the decisive kill at the end of the battle.

One drawback to the Cleric is they have to deal with the worst of the money situation of both fighters and spellcasters. On the one hand, you need to save all the gold and platinum you can to afford the spells you want in the next rank. On the other, if you don't get some protection covering those scrawny bones, your gonna be soup. So it's especially important to keep a tight fist on your money supply, and never waste a single copper chip. Come level fourteen you're going to want to spend 50 platinum to get all those great spells - and if you're not careful, you'll be broke as a milkmaid!

Creation
If you've played EverQuest before, you know that race plays a minor role in your character's strengths and weaknesses. The beauty of EverQuest is that any race/class combination, if played smartly and creatively, can be successful. Blame this on 989, not the players - they have designed a system that enhances character creation and allows you to play what you want, instead of forcing you to pick the "best" class in order to keep up. That said, the races that miss out on Clerics are the Barbarians, Half-Elves, Ogres, Trolls, and Wood Elves (Ogres and Trolls should check out the Shaman class).

Wisdom is the driving skill for Clerics, as it determines the amount of mana you get. Strength is also important, especially if you are dreaming of your character crusading against evil with platemail, tower shield, and war hammer. You can't wear the armor if you can't carry it! Otherwise, adjust your attributes as you see fit and wade into the fray!

Playing
Playing your character is all about exploration and experience, mistakes and victory. Nothing's going to prepare you for adventuring and honing your skills like Norrath proper, but the next section will help get you off on the right foot.
 top


Formative Years
If you've played one character under level four, you've pretty much played them all. Life for a rank newbie in Norrath is pretty much the same no matter what class or race you're in. For a while your existence will consist of killing everything you can find in the newbie gardens (usually snakes, bats, rats, and other unimpressive fare) and selling whatever loot you can. You will start out with Strike and Light Healing spells - scribe and memorize them immediately, and begin practicing your magical abilities. Use Strike to finish your opponents off, which will give you the practice you need for timing your spells between your opponent's attacks. Cast light healing as necessary - you will find that with your healing abilities you will last longer on the battlefield than your non-Cleric friends! The five practice points you acquire for completing your guild quest will be best spent in one-handed blunt, as that's what you'll be using to do the majority of your damage for quite a while.

When you can afford it purchase Courage. When cast, this will boost your AC and HP, and at low levels is a tremendous boon. Unless you absolutely MUST have one of the other Rank 1 spells, save your money until you get to level five, which has some more powerful spells.

Clerics gain new spell rankings slower than Magicians and Wizards. This is to balance the Clerics' ability to use blunt weapons and armor. Therefore levels one to four are more of the same stuff. For maximum experience you'll want to stay away from parties until at least level three and depending on your personal inclination even until level five. At level five you'll want to purchase Light Healing, Holy Armor, Gate, and Ward Undead. Your prowess on the battlefield will have improved quite a bit and these additional spells will help. But just because you have Light Healing, don't put that Minor Healing away! Light Healing takes significantly longer to cast as well as using up quite a bit more mana than Minor Healing, so you'll want to keep both of them memorized and available.

Now that you have Ward Undead, try teaming up with a fighter and go undead hunting. Let the fighter take the damage while you crush the undead fiend with Ward. When you're comfortable, try fighting the weaker undead creatures alone. Look for parties to join and practice your group skills. Buy Lull and start taking on goblin, orc, or gnoll camps one monster at a time.

Terrible Teens
Life changes dramatically at level nine. At this level you gain access to your first real damage spell - Word of Pain. WoP deals damage to all the monsters in a small circle around you, and can clear a battlefield in a hurry. WoP will draw quite a bit of mana at level nine so be careful, but you'll find it just the thing you're looking for to deal damage while still being the party healer. Some other important spells level nine brings are Center, a powerful buff spell, Root, which will stop an enemy dead in its tracks, and Soothe, an improved version of Lull. Root is handy to stop a fleeing foe from rounding up his friends while you attack his back, as well as keeping a mummy in place while you pound it from a safe distance with Ward Undead. Soothe works the same as Lull, just better, and is well worth the upgrade. And Center is Courage part II - a much-improved version that no fighter in your party should be without. From nine to fourteen you'll be much more powerful, and well equipped to explore more challenging (and rewarding!) places. You and your party can try out Crushbone proper rather than skulking about the camps at the entrance, and Black Burrow trains won't mean an instant death anymore. Soon you'll be to level fourteen, which is the shining level for a Cleric. You can now cast Healing and watch that HP bar climb climb climb, while Smite is a sorely needed replacement for the now useless (except for luring!) Strike. You'll also have access to Expulse Undead - and if you thought Ward Undead was incredible, you haven't seen anything yet! Paired up with Expulse is Invisibility to Undead - the perfect complement to an adventure in the undead dungeons. Enjoy your new powers and abilities - you are well on your way to becoming a master Cleric!

Group Play
A Cleric can be one of the most rewarding classes to play in a group. When adventuring with your comrades, you are in charge of that most precious commodity - life. It is your sworn duty to keep your compatriots alive at any cost - lonely is the Cleric who can't master the ability of keeping a close eye on the health of his fellows while closely following the battle at hand. A well-played Cleric makes friends anywhere she pleases, has no trouble finding a group, and is never without a helping hand. The unsuccessful Cleric - well, he might as well hang up his hat and swear off the holy path.

One of the key skills you'll need to master as a Cleric is the best use of your abilities to help the group. For the Cleric there are three key roles for you to play:

Healing and Combat Support:
This is what being a Cleric is all about, and is the reason many choose not to follow the healer's path. If you don't see the excitement, value, and fun in being the person your comrades depend on to keep them fighting, a Cleric's life is not for you. In addition to the obvious healing, Cleric's have several powerful "buff" spells that increase hit points and armor class, provide resistance to certain types of attacks, and can cure ailments such as disease and blindness. Combat support also includes using spells such as Lull or Soothe to pinpoint the exact foes you will fight (or allow safe passage through a treacherous zone!), Root to stop a fleeing monster dead in its tracks, and Invigor to restore waning stamina when the battle drags on a bit too long. It's also the act of keeping on eye on your surroundings and watching for potential trouble during battle. Your name will go down in your partners little black books if you are the one to spot the King of the Minotaurs sneaking up on your fighters during battle!

Spell Damage:

Sometimes the combat is going particularly well, or your fighters are dodging and ducking blows like ballet dancers (maybe because of all those buff spells you've cast on them!). This is the time to survey the situation and see what you can do to help cause some damage. If you're fighting multiple enemies, a well placed Word of Pain might be the ticket to evening the odds, or perhaps you'd like to flex a little muscle and take down that Mummy in a minute with Ward Undead. Remember, though, it's always wise to practice restraint with combat spells, or all too soon you'll know what it feels like to have used all your mana to crush your foe, only to have nothing left to heal your bruised and weakened fighters when a wandering monster attacks.

Melee Damage:

At low levels, the damage you can inflict on monsters with regular hand-to-hand combat is on par with the other fighter levels, and therefore will make a difference in the battle. As you progress up the experience ladder and begin facing tougher and more advanced battles, this isn't the case, so only step into the fray when there's absolutely nothing better for you to do. There's nothing wrong with sitting down and meditating in the middle of battle - your friends will appreciate your forethought in recovering a few bars rather than lightly tapping a Crusader with your mace when his friends show up and start swinging!

Hints
Finally, here's a list of some helpful pointers, ideas, and concepts that will help you make your way in Norrath, and become a Cleric that Bards will sing tales of long after your passing!

If you find yourself alone in Norrath, the best things to fight are Undead. As a Cleric you have a natural ability to best these fiends from the netherworld, and there are several Cleric-only spells in your arsenal such as Ward, Expulse, Spook, and Sense Undead, that you'll be able to use with great effect. Solo'ng undead is also a good way to earn money and XP - you'll be able to go face-to-face with undead at levels a pure fighter type would shy away from.

Save your money! Your third rank spells will cost on average five platinum each - a small fortune in Norrath's depressed economy. Until you get to around twentieth level, being able to afford both the spells and the weapons and armor you want will be one of your greatest challenges. After twenty, you can look forward to a little leeway, as you'll be able to fight monsters that will give much more money and valuable loot, while the price of your spells will not keep up in relation to your money making ability.

As a Cleric you've got the easiest way to gain friends and allies in Norrath - use it! When you see a hapless soul stumbling away from a monster, help that person out! Heal him up and watch the tide of battle turn. Or offer your assistance to fellow travelers when the world is swarming with plague rats or other disease carrying vermin. When all is said and done you'll have made a valuable friend and ally; in Norrath, you can never have too many friends.

In battle, your eyes should be checking your comrades HP more than your own. If everything is going right, your fighters should be taking the damage while you're on the sidelines playing support. In a long battle, remember to Meditate between casting heals - at Level 11 you'll recover enough mana to cast a Light Heal every 2-3 rounds. And that makes a huge difference.

Just like any other spellcaster, you'll have a hard time working your craft if an Orc is belting you. Also, some monsters take offense at your healing and turn quickly to attack you. Make sure your party members realize they need to keep you out of direct fire if they want to benefit from your healing - if they know it's a matter of their life and death, they'll be sure to Taunt more effectively!

In a party, keep your fighters in tip-top shape between battles as well as during. When the fighting is over, make sure all your fighters are at full or near full health before recovering your own mana. Also be ready to apply Center, Holy Armor, Courage, or whatever other buff spells you have available as soon as your teammate signals readiness. It only takes one unexpected run in with a fierce monster to learn this lesson of readiness well.

Lull (or Soothe) and Strike is your friend! At a monster camp, control the pace and frenzy of the battle by selectively Soothing or Lulling certain linked monsters, then Striking from a distance the remaining monster(s) you want to tackle. Repeat as necessary until the camp is empty. This evens the odds and allows even a party of just two (you and your fighter!) to handle dangerous areas. Divine Aura grants you 30 seconds of invulnerability when cast. You can move while invulnerable, but you cannot cast spells or attack. 30 seconds is a LONG time when you're being chased down by a ravaging horde of ghouls or when you're providing a getaway for your compatriots. Learn it and use it!

Practice all of your spells you will be using in combat, and have a good understanding of their casting times, duration, and effect. When you're trying to get that decisive Expluse Undead in on the bashing, crushing, and slashing Greater Skeleton timing is everything! Be sure to practice both one-handed and two-handed blunt skills regularly as you progress through the levels. Because you must hit creatures that give you experience to raise a melee skill, it gets very difficult to raise skills once you've neglected them for a few levels (try hitting that Level 20 Gnoll with a two-handed blunt skill of 3!). When you do find that Magical Dwarven two-handed Warhammer, you'll want to be able to use it.
