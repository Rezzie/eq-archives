or "How I Learned To Stop Worrying And Love The Beauracracy."
This is my homepage, such as it is.  Many have tried and many have failed to make their webpages show the "true essence", "pure individuality" and the "incredible uniqueness of character" that makes them who they are.  I will not attempt such a thing.  Suffice it to say that this is some of me, and it is quite likely that too much of me would nauseate you...as it nauseates me.

Given the fact that tradition is often traditional for good reasons, I will include a short biograph, links to friends' webpages, and the obligatory pictures of family, and travels.  A short resume is also included, though I am not actively looking for employment . Any offers over $70k will be considered seriously based on the stability of the organization and position.  I have often said that I would never work for anyone else again, but nearly everything has its price.  ;)

Why the sub-title?  Well, everyone has little themes, subplots and motifs in their lives.  Independence and romantic intrigues dominate the psyche of the 16 year old, other themes are suppressed while this duet overwhelms the teenager's life.  The college student is beaten with academics and financial poverty, all else is simply background noise.  As sole proprietor of a three year old Computer Consulting and Repair business (On-Site Computers), governmental beauracracy has forced itself to the front of the stage and insists on making an attempt as soloist.  The great majority of this paperwork is related to taxes.  When a new business is started in Chicago... the city, state and federal governments all scramble over one another to take their cut of the small business owner's hard-earned money.  Most people don't notice or care, because they are snuggled into their cubes at offices working for someone else.  Being self-employed is treated like a crime in this country.  The punishment is in the form of seemingly endless annual fines (taxes)  for this, that and the other-thing.  These taxes are above and beyond the income taxes that the rest of Americans have to pay.  If you happen to be an employer, god-forbid, then the government gorges itself on you with a second wave of taxes.  If it weren't for my wife, Charmaine, I don't think that I would have ever "learned to stop worrying and love beauracracy".  (My ever-efficient wife, Charmaine,  takes care of all of the paperwork, so that all I have to do is make enough money to pay the glutinous governmental gangsters their protection money.)

Short Biograph

One year after the summer of love, I was born in the summer of 1968.  The year of the infamous Democratic National Convention in Chicago, where Mayor Daley Sr. had his goons (the Chicago police) beat up demonstrators.  Two days after I was born, the Soviet tanks rolled into Prague (the capital of what is now the Czech Republic) to put an end to the "Prague Spring " liberalization.   Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. was assassinated in Memphis and Robert Kennedy was assassinated in California.  This was also the year that marked Vietnam as the longest war in United States' history.

Born: 1968
Siblings: 1 Brother, 1 Sister, both older
High School: East Peoria Community High
Activities:  Debate, Band (Trumpet, Euphonium), Grace Presbyterian Church, AFS Student to Costa Rica
College:  University of Illinois,  Degree in Philosophy, but spent most of my time working on my computer.
Illinois Army National Guard:  Enlisted December, 1986 as a Private.  Transferred to Inactive Ready Reserve July, 1995 as a First Lieutenant.
Branch:  Infantry  Awards: Army Commendation Medal (twice), Overseas Service Ribbon, Humanitarian Service Ribbon

My parents worked hard and were frugal enough to send my siblings and I to a number of "summer camp" activities.  I met my wife-to-be at ISYM (Illinois Summer Youth Music) Camp  when I was 11 years old.  We were close friends through the years and finally got married March of 1997.

My trip to Costa Rica influenced my attitudes about the world greatly.  Having grown-up in the mid-west, I leaned strongly to the right concerning social issues.  After living in another country and culture for a few months, I became aware of the fact that many of our social norms are artificial, cruel and downright evil.  The racism, sexism and homophobia that surrounded me and seemed to blend into the background before I went to Costa Rica, now seemed to stick out like a neon sign.

Travel has always been the best way to learn a huge amount of information about the world in a very short period of time.  I learned more in three months in Costa Rica than I did in the rest of my 4 years of high school.  Other countries I have visited are: Honduras (with the Army),  and Scotland.  I have also been through parts of Canada, Guatemala, and El Salvador.

My interest in computers began with my neighbor's father purchasing a TRS-80 when I was between 8-10 years old.  Jim McCubbin and I would have to type-in the BASIC programs prior to playing the games.  Games like Hunt the Wumpus, Hammurabi, etc... were the coolest thing at the time.  My next brush with computers was when my brother (6 years older) was taking a course at a community college.  While he was in class, he would let me log into the main-frame using his account name and password.  I was around 10 and I spent the whole time playing ADVENT.  When there weren't any regular terminals available I would play it on the printer terminal (it printed out everything on the old green and white 14 inch wide computer paper).  What a waste of paper!  But this way I could take the printout home with me and study it more in-debth. After that I fell in love with AppleII's.  I would copy everything I could get my hands on using the standard tools like "Locksmith", "Nibbler", "Clone Kit", "Pirate's Friend".   The pinnacle of my software copy-protection cracking career was copying Wizardry.  (I had to remove the cover on the Disk Drive and turn a screw on a variable resistor in order to alter the spin-speed of the disk in order to copy it.)  The coolest games of that time were "Hard Hat Mac", "Aztec", "Wizardry", "Lode Runner", "Galactic Empire".  Occasionally I have to pull out the old AppleII and play them for nostalgia's sake.

I didn't get much time to work on C64's or any Texas Instruments computers, but most of the same titles were available on those machines as well.   In college I was introduced to the IBM XT.  I bought my first computer on a credit card.  For just under $3,000, I got a 286-12MHz computer with 2 Mb RAM,  a 20Mb hard-drive, and a VGA card and Monitor.  A real steal at the time.  Since then I have been trying to ride this monster-wave, we call "the computer revolution".
