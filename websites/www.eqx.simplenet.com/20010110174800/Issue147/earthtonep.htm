ISSUE # 147

 October 28th - November 3rd, 2000


EARTH TO NORRATH, EARTH TO NORRATH

Every once in awhile, or perhaps more often than that, I enter into one of those day-dreaming zones of unreality where it’s necessary for someone to repeat, “Earth to Nep, Earth to Nep! Are you there Nep?” Usually a few iterations is enough to bring my focus back into the same room as the person calling for my attention and asking me to respond to them. During those moments of spacing out, however, my daydreaming self essentially loses track of the presence and the human existence of the one nearby until, somehow, reconnection is made and I can see and attend to the very real person standing by me.
So, it’s easy, I think, as people become involved in the imaginary world of Norrath, for all of us to enter into a kind of disconnected state that can, if we’re not careful, separate us from the real people behind the characters of players and staff alike in Everquest with serious repercussions on our behavior toward each other. Every once in awhile, it might be a good idea to shout out, “Earth to Norrath, Earth to Norrath” simply to remind ourselves that connected to us by keyboard (unless there are now intergalactic aliens also playing Everquest), is a human individual not all that different from each of the other members of the EQ community.

Once the person we are playing with or the staff member we are dealing with is seen as a dehumanized persona or a symbolic figurehead, then, it seems our ethical or social obligations from real life often take a vacation from the interchanges that follow. Behavior and attitudes that few would espouse in daily life, such as lying or false manipulation or power mongering become more commonplace. Communication devolves into debates or battles or statistics argued between two sides, either of which may choose to regard the other as a hero, an object, an idiot, or, worse, a demon. Whether it be a person posting to a board who has entered into a fantasy universe where it is possible to forget that behind the figurehead of a Producer, or a Game Designer, or a GM is a real person or whether it’s a person sitting at a desk at Verant taking refuge in corporate rhetoric that boxes a real person into a labeled player object, there are frequent moments in the Everquest world where we all forget that the person we are talking to or about lives in a real world with problems and concerns and issues of which we have no idea.

It is, of course, impossible to know who that person really is in the give and take of the game and the internet boards. Nor is there a fast or accurate way to always determine if the circumstances they might claim or post about are true. There’s no way to tell. If someone posts that he has met the girl of his dreams (insert reverse genders if you choose), readers can find it hard to figure out if this is roleplay or a message from the real person. If someone likewise posts and speaks of a terminal illness or even says that life is too hard and suicide is the only option, it could be real or it could be someone posting in that manner for other reasons. If a representative of a game company connects with a player through the boards or in game, he or she is subject to the same atmosphere and can find it easy to objectify and dehumanize the other person.

Playing a character, whether in strict role-play or not, makes the situation even more complicated. When we play, we are all simultaneously ourselves in real life and our perceived characters in the game. At the same time our fingers are typing madly to keep our character living and breathing in the imaginary world, our real life selves are reacting emotionally and physically to the experiences of our characters. (Yes, the game still gives me that instant of queasiness when Nep falls down the face of a cliff.) Thus, at the same time a character might go running screaming from an ogre or playfully rebut an amorous ranger, there is also an interchange on two levels between the role-playing individual and the person playing the other character. If the circumstances are positive and if time permits, we may begin to get to know the character both as the character and the person behind it. With luck, we may even find a friend. The flip side of this duality is that if we run into negative circumstances such as the endless trains causing painful deaths that happened to me last night -- or kill-stealing or ninja looting or people promising friendship who really just want to use a particular attribute of another’s character -- then the intensity of dealing with the problem can make it even more difficult to see through the illusion of Norrath to the real persons involved.

I can think of many times when people have engaged my characters in ways I’m certain they never would have responded to me as a person had we been playing pen and pencil games over a table or discussing the same subject in real life. Certainly, it is easy to invent an illusion or untruth about another and to use the nebulous form of our player characters to persuade others that what is being said is, in fact, truth. It’s easy to say, “Oh I don’t need a bard <or insert class or race>” or “They’re a newbie, it’s ok for me to cheat them out of their kill.” or whatever. After all, as long as we are in the separate fantasy universe, truth can seem relative and humanity a forgotten illusion. In each of these instances, however, the lost balance between interacting as role-players and connecting as humans, resulted in problems, frustrations and sometimes even emotional pain.

In a way, the ages-old experience of militaries that dehumanize the other side gets played out again in imaginary worlds. As in a war, an otherwise ethical person, “forgets” that the one he or she faces online is equally real, breathing, thinking and feeling. We interact with the “Ogre-ness” of the person or the “GM-ness” without simultaneously remembering the man or woman or boy or girl at the other keyboard. When we are in this fantasy state and when we forget the reality of the people we interact with, then derogatory names and unethical behavior arise. A person, who would smile and shake hands with a developer or game god (in MUDs) if they were meeting in public, feels no barrier to making death threats or posting flaming rhetoric online. Someone who would cook you dinner and banter the evening away in a friendly living room, doesn’t think twice about stealing the spawn you’ve waited 6 hours for. It’s like becoming split, disjointed personalities when we don’t stop to hold the parts together with our ethical sensibilities and individual social consciences. Of course, I realize that it is a commonplace wisdom that all gamers are eccentrics and social misfits, but then there go the labels again, displacing the value of each individual in favor of broad stereotypes. The reality is that gamers are all people and in order for our online community to flourish, it’s important to come out of the fantasy world enough to remind ourselves to reconnect on a human and ethical level with each other even as our characters play out of our imaginations in Norrath.

I’m not sure how long it takes a playing community to evolve a system in game where real life social ethics reside side-by-side with the interactions of fantasy. Out of game, such as on the message boards or in interactions among players or between gamers and the company running the game, however, it seems reasonable that we work hard to always remember the person we are encountering is more than the persona we first meet. If we accept the premise that in or out of game we can set aside common decency and respect in favor of an illusion of disconnection on the human level, then, I believe, we will not achieve a cohesive community within the game or the Internet. Some may be comfortable with that. For me, community is what is important - and community is always made up of individuals, each of whom has the right to tap me on the shoulder and call, “Earth to Nep”, knowing that I can step back outside my fantasy experience and see and respond to them as a person.

    Essay by Nepenthia
