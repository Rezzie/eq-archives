ISSUE # 144

 October 7th - October 13th, 2000


Flowers of Evil

A sudden uneasy silence fell between the two Teir'dal, and even the rumblings of the molten pools nearby seemed to quiet for a moment. The scent of ash seemed fresh again as if they had just arrived at this moment in time from someplace far away, even though both had been here in the shadows of the lavastorm range for most of the evening. The youthful Teir'dal necromancer sat as she had for hours, staring in disbelief at what she had just heard her companion say. The battle-hardened Sister of the Indigo towered over the frail necromancer, with a gaze nearly as gloomy as the news she had brought.

Scattered in front of Furlespit were various cut-up patterns, discarded animal hide and bones, shavings of metal, thread and silk. The shameful necromancer and her equipment were generously covered in ash and soot from the volcanic surroundings, even as she sat comfortably in the black sand. Furlespit looked down from the warrior's gaze at her day’s work as if it seemed to have been for naught, and then slowly back up. As her tireless undead servant returned to her, he noticed Valarrah standing there and dropped his load of pelts and drew his sword. Valarrah quickly moved to parry the undead's sword as Furlespit motioned her pet to be still.

"Your Indigo issued garb makes Karn nervous," Furlespit warned her. Furlespit began packing up her supplies distractedly. "I must make Neriak by daybreak, I've a new instructor who would be displeased if I were late." She handed several loaded packs to her servant and looked towards the moon. "You will have to go without me, I've too much to do." Furlespit said, stepping away from Valarrah. Valarrah stepped in front of Furlespit with a scolding look.

"We do not have time for this, we must leave now." Valarrah commanded. Furlespit curled her arms tightly and stepped the other way around Valarrah and started down the path. "Furlespit, ignoring this isn't going to make it go away," Valarrah called after her. "The Dragoon Dispatches have been recalled, and Vizier Tor’Kyl himself issued the order. He’s been looking for a reason to start hunting you for a long time and now he has it.”

"The Vizier's word is not the word of the Father," Furlespit huffed, stomping down the path.

"They know that you are alive and are looking for you again," Valarrah persisted, catching up with the necromancer. "If you go back you will be killed."

Furlespit stopped and turned around, glaring at the taller warrior. "Did you come here to kill me? How can I trust you," Furlespit purposely looked up and down her Indigo-issued plate mail, "you have as much to gain for my death as they do."

Valarrah stomped and looked away in anger. "Furlespit the Dragoons have been withdrawn, and the Vizier announced to the assembly that he suspected that the Queen was dead. He was given some sort of vision from Innoruuk and wants to unite the Nation under the King. They will disband the Lodge if Xon doesn't conform to the new laws."

"I heard you the first time!" Furlespit growled. She turned away with disgust and continued down the path. "And now the proud are the scorned. And then the bureaucrats take housing. Then they grant positions of govern to Humans." Furlespit turned back to the warrior, "Next they cut to the chase and build a tunnel straight to Freeport, lined with shops, right?" A fire burned within the necromancer’s eyes, glowing green with fury. Valarrah stared back with a stone gaze. "Send the Dragoons afar, send the Acolytes with them. Start Necromantic research in the field. Cooperate with the Heretics and get everyone away from home. Then they court the have-nots and bring the surface dwellers to us." Furlespit continued stomping down the path.

Valarrah summoned patience and continued after her. "You aren't listening to me, and this childish tantrum has got to stop. I know where you will be safe, and have arranged passage." Valarrah ran to Furlespit's side. "I know as well as you do of the corruption in Neriak but you are jumping to conclusions, and every step towards Neriak is one of your last." Furlespit stopped and stared at Valarrah. "You have to trust me, I know your torment and I feel it too. Word on the wind is the family is rebuilding, and the Brotherhood is back on the prowl. They are looking for you, Furlespit. Do not underestimate them."

Furlespit gnashed her teeth. "Like the others did?" she spat. "You're right, I'm not listening to you. You speak with two tongues. How do I know the others fate was not met at your hands, while under the promise that you were misleading the guard you serve?" Valarrah scowled at the bitter necromancer. "You know nothing of torment. Your torment was slipping and lusting after one of your prey, and then losing him and having to face a life never knowing-" Furlespit paused as Valarrah's scowl turned hostile, and turned away. "Admit it Sister, after Beldro disappeared you gave up. You were taken by the fire within you, but without him left with sorrow. You went back to die. But when you got back instead it was to welcome arms." Valarrah's stance warned Furlespit to stop. "They must have congratulated you. 'Great work, you dismantled the F'leurs -" Without a word Valarrah lashed out at Furlespit and struck her over the head with the hilt of her sword, dropping her to the ground like a wet cloth. The undead Karn moved to fight back only for an instance before being sent to the ether by the furious Teir'dal warrior.

Valarrah calmed and then hefted the limp necromancer over her shoulder. "It’s for your own good," she whispered as she turned back the other way and moved quickly through the night.

Under a thick twinkling blanket of stars in a remote region of the Western Commonlands, Valarrah sat down to rest beneath a tree. Furlespit was showing signs of waking up soon as little sighs kept the weary warrior company. Leaning back and stretching she looked across the starlit rolling plains, envisioning the armies of the dead that would soon take the lands back, completing the destruction of Old Takish Hiz. For now however the plains were peaceful and serene, uninterrupted for miles and void of mortal construction. In the distance the woods of Kithicor clung to the base of the HighPass Mountains like a velvet cloak.

A free spirited breeze played through the trees and grass, creating a crescendo of sounds. Among them Valarrah could hear the call of sleep, but shook her head to stay awake. She glanced over to Furlespit, and suppressed a worried look. The strange Teir’dal had been at the center of a great deal of trouble, but she could not be allowed to fall into the hands of the Indigo Brotherhood. Furlespit had cheated death since birth, and carried with her a dreadful burden. The child’s curse was the cause for much ruin, but Valarrah remained all the more loyal to her, as did the other members of their band of rebels. The remaining members, that is.

Valarrah winced and clenched her fists, throttling the wave of emotions that plagued her as the vivid memories returned. Her knuckles cracked and her nails pierced her palms, as she watched the scene again. She had followed Beldro into the temples of Innoruuk deep within the last gate of Neriak, where he confessed to her the depths of his insanity and the true meaning of what it is to be ruled by chaos. She heard much that she was afraid to know, and suppressed the meaning behind his methods. With a flash of pain she tried to block out the memories, but the phantasm was still there. Beldro gifted to her a token of his power, a strangely crafted shield with archaic runes spilled over it. She knelt, clutching it and searching for her reflection in it, but instead saw the shadow of the Dragoons rushing into the chamber behind her. Her head spun around to look behind her and then back to Beldro, who looked towards her with a gaze beyond wisdom before turning his stare into the onrush of guards.
She openly betrayed them, crying out to stop even as the captain patted her shoulder with a smile. She snarled at him as she was promptly dragged kicking from the room, struggling to hold onto her line of sight of Beldro. With gusto he defied the might of the Indigo, sending waves of death’s chill throughout the chamber in an unholy display of power, while the Sabers played violent shadows through his show of evocation. An audible gasp escaped Valarrah as that sound echoed in her mind, filling the expanse of the Commonlands with its hollow cry. That sound that she heard as she was pulled out of sight of the melee and held by the guard. It was a sound she had heard no mortal make, and she had heard them all. The sound ripped through her until there was no more pain to feel.

Her defiance was her own disciplinary judgement, she was never questioned about her actions in front of the Captain. The wrath of Innoruuk himself played on her soul, knowing that her masters knew of her alliance to the rebel guild and taunted her with rewards for her good service to the Teir’dal nation. Instead they held her betrayal secret, and she swallowed the burden of living with that to protect those that remained of the F’leurs DuMal. She would protect them by not returning to them, and stayed in service in Neriak.

So many lost, she thought to herself as began to calm down. Furlespit had somehow been spared only because they believed the Koada'dalto have killed her nearly a year ago. The rest were hunted, and they were hunted until the remnants of their cause were forgotten. Soon after the last disappeared the hypocrisy of the Vizier was realized as the power of the Foreign Quarter rogue markets expanded to control more than ever. Valarrah gritted her teeth, staring at her toes. Now the Vizier had the mendacity to challenge the Lodge of the Dead with his laws, all amidst the stir over the Queen. Even the General seemed detached when he made the order to withdraw the search for the Queen. It was no secret that the Queen had opposed the word of the King, but not even he could oppose her will.

The Vizier spoke to the assembly of his vision as he was visited by the Father himself, and spoke of the Queen as a coal that had burned out too distant from the flame. He called for the unity of the Teir’dal nation under the King. With the voice of an avatar he trembled the very walls of the city. With the birth of gods came the passing of those who would hold themselves to be gods, he commanded. Yet to Valarrah the rumblings in Neriak sounded more like a nation ready to fall and Valarrah was not the only one listening.

“They will fall,” Valarrah said out loud. “He will fall.”

“Yes,” Furlespit said, eyes still closed. “Yes Sister, he will.” Furlespit sat up slowly, lightly touching the bump on her head. She looked around until she realized where they were, and stared up into the mountains. “We are meeting in the pass?” Furlespit looked to Valarrah. Valarrah nodded. “I take it the Kithicor Outpost dissented then? Is that your safe passage?” Furlespit wrinkled her brow.

“Yes they have, but not to our advantage,” Valarrah informed her. “Ambassador K’ryll was sent to bring them back into the fold. Unless we are lucky he will get there before we do, and they will probably suspect we would head through the pass.” Valarrah paused to gather her equipment. “No, we are not headed through Kithicor. One of the goblin mines in the thicket opens into the gorge.” The two exchanged knowing looks, and then picked up their equipment to leave.

Furlespit suddenly began searching through the grass around the tree. “Where did you put my other boot?” Furlespit whined. Just then the boot sailed towards them through the air, landing at Furlespit’s feet. Standing not far away were two figures, a Feir’dal tracker and a member of the Koada'dal Watch.

“A pleasure to meet you ladies under the stars this morn,” The Feir’dal mocked in his native tongue as they took battle stances and charged.
Valarrah deftly kicked a loose stone up and into the head of the wood elf, which caused him to stumble slightly, but before she could reach him with her swords the High Elf had met her and they exchanged parries. Furlespit quickly leaped away from the combat and sprinted away into the tall grass. “Get her,” the High Elf snapped has he pushed Valarrah closer to the tree. The ranger readied his bow but had lost sight of Furlespit, and began chasing her.

Valarrah and the high elf traded blows, neither gaining advantage. She skillfully drew his following shield blow away and brought a raised boot to his jaw, knocking him back. He quickly recovered and countered by sweeping her legs from under her. In a thunder of blade strikes she was back on him though, drawing blood, but not without him slipping his blade through and piercing her abdomen plate. She pushed him back and struck him soundly over the helm, then followed with a pommel punch to the jaw. He stumbled back and squared against her. In a whoosh of wind and gold sparkles he channeled the grace of Tunare into a wave of force, knocking the Teir’dal a dozen meters, slamming her into the ground.
She shook her head and leaped up, still writhing with pain from the blow. As he charged forward she hurled one of her swords at him, which he easily dodged. She feigned to throw the one from her off hand as he leapt forward with a threatening overhand swing, meeting her quick upward slash in flight. With the tuned fury of an artist she let into the paladin until he finally collapsed at the base of the tree. She disarmed him and pressed him up against the tree sitting.

Valarrah quickly looked around for Furlespit and the wood elf, but they were nowhere to be seen. Valarrah sighed in frustration, then heard a whispered prayer coming from the high elf. She quickly turned around and struck him soundly in the temple with the hilt of her sword, stood up and drove her blade deep into his chest. With a convulsion he cried out, “Tunare help me,” and then died. Valarrah picked up her other sword and what remained of Furlespit’s equipment and began running the way she had seen Furlespit last headed.

“Pixie feathers,” the wood elf cursed quietly, as he smelled the soil for signs of Furlespit’s tracks. He had accurately followed her well into the woods, and knew for certain where he last had seen her tracks but had lost the trail in a clearing of dead trees and moss. He quickly covered the area, searching for something he may have overlooked, but as more time passed he stopped, and looked back the way he had come. He had nearly given up when a pack of wolves came into the clearing, which also seemed to be tracking someone. “By the graces, children of Tunare have you smelled a Teir’dal in the area this morning?” he communed with them. It was then he saw the unholy red glow in their eyes, and realized he had crossed onto cursed soil.

Fear took hold and he leapt into a tall tree, climbing well into the canopy. He looked below at the pack of dreadwolves pacing at the base of the tree. Breathing hard, he rested his head against a branch for a moment, sighing in relief. He started to look around for an adjacent tree to cross to when he came nose to nose with his original prey, and felt her dirk pass between his ribs. Furlespit held on to him tightly as he flailed, trying to grab his own sword. She twisted the dirk, and ran her fingers through his wet hair. As the pain seared through him he heard her quiet chanting and felt his life force drain away. She kissed him softly down his cheek and onto his shoulder as he made his death throws. She held her hand over his chest and looked into his eyes until he was still and then kissed him again, letting him fall from the tree.

Valarrah searched well into daybreak and had still not found signs of Furlespit. In the distance in the woods she could hear the Dragoons meeting over breakfast. Indeed it sounded as if the Ambassador had reached them quickly. She hid Furlespit’s equipment in her own pack, standing behind a tree. She took a few deep breaths, hoping Furlespit was still alive. She marched into their camp, and they all stood and looked to her with interest.
“What do you have to report?” She demanded sternly.

The brightest of them came forward. “Scouts spotted one of the rebels headed into the goblin mines. We were just readying to head that way.” He said.

“Excellent,” she replied. “I will take it from here. You are all commanded by the general to report to the Neriak Commons at once.”
The Dragoon argued. “The Ambassador himself specified that if you reported here I was to accompany you to hunt the necromancer down. The others will report in my stead, and my lieutenant will have expected it.”

Valarrah suppressed her frustration, formulating a plan to lose or kill this Dragoon on their way to the mines. He was an adept swordsman, and would not be fooled easily. Wounded as she was she might not be able to defeat him in an open combat. The group finished their formalities and began their separate ways, all the while Valarrah planning his demise. “What is your name, soldier?”

“V’nain Ravensblood,” he replied as they walked along. “I’ve heard of your exploits in battle, Valarrah. It is an honor to hunt with you.” He said eagerly. She could occasionally feel his eyes over her form as they made their way quickly to the hills. In his every comment she sensed his interest, and wondered if he had sent the others back only to get her alone. This would work quite to her advantage if she played it right, she grinned to herself.

They traveled into midday and arrived at the mines, where they were met by a set of goblin miners and traders. Shaded under the towering Highpass mountains outside the mines the Teir’dal sat down for a drink and to eat with the goblins. They questioned the goblins as if they had seen Furlespit, but the goblins were in much more a mood to barter.

“Lady I think you would look mighty delicious in a necklace made with these rubies,” the leader of the group grinned wickedly. Valarrah frowned. The goblin scratched his head and licked his lips. “Pardon the saying,” he chuckled. “Perhaps these sapphires?” he continued, showing her a wealth of gems and stones.

“We have no time to barter anymore. Have you seen another Teir’dal this way early this morning? I was supposed to meet her.” Valarrah was losing her patience with the disgusting merchant.

“I just dunno,” the merchant conned. “I get my head in my work and I forget who I’ve seen.” He grinned.

“What lovely sapphires!” called a sudden voice from the way they had come, surprising the group. Valarrah turned into the shadow of a very powerful looking Teir’dal, dressed in the combat gear of a High Regent of Innoruuk. A troubled look passed over his face as he looked to the Dragoon. “What are you doing this far from your post, soldier?” he asked. The Dragoon held his head low. Valarrah felt a twinge of panic building in her.

“We are hot on the trail of a criminal to Neriak, Regent.” He said reverently.

The Regent smiled and patted the Dragoon on the shoulder. “Excellent work. Now head home before you are punished,” he commanded.
The Dragoon looked to argue, but the aura of hate was strong with the Regent. The Dragoon looked once for help from Valarrah and then hurried back down the path out of sight.

“About those sapphires,” the Regent cooed to the goblin. “I will buy them all.” He dropped an open purse of platinum in front of the goblin, who took the pouch with shaking hands. “Now,” he raised his voice slightly, “where is the girl?”

The goblin motioned them to follow, and Valarrah looked around for a way to escape. The Regent of Innoruuk was far more powerful than she, and seemed quite well informed as to where to find Furlespit. “She is this way,” the goblin said with glee, and Valarrah followed with her heart in her stomach.

“You mean she is here?” the bewildered warrior asked. The goblin nodded and led them down several levels into the mines, where they found Furlespit auctioning a set of her tailor-made whips to the head goblin taskmaster. Valarrah’s nerves frizzled as the Regent made his way promptly to the necromancer, who casually looked up to them. Furlespit and the Regent looked to each other for a long moment, no one making a sound. Valarrah stifled her panic as Furlespit quickly stood up.

“Your eminence!” Furlespit exclaimed, throwing her arms around the Regent, who returned the embrace warmly. Valarrah collapsed into a chair, wiping her hand over her brow. She felt a wave of tension pass her in relief.

“You two know each other?” Valarrah finally asked. The Regent turned a questioning look back to the warrior.
“It’s ok, she is with me,” Furlespit said. The Regent smiled.
“How did you know where to find me?” Furlespit wondered out loud.
“I knew the guard would lead me to you,” the Regent exchanged a knowing look to Valarrah.
“Your Sister Indigo is lucky that Innoruuk guides her, I expected to have to kill her.” He grinned wickedly.
“Come now, lets discuss this over a drink.”
