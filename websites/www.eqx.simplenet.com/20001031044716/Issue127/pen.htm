ISSUE # 127

 June 3rd - June 9th, 2000

The Pen is Mightier than the Cursor

I’ve been on a long hiatus from online RPGs. It has lasted coming close to six months now. Ill log on to EQ once every few weeks or so just to play a nasty Dark Elf cleric and keep looking for something I could have sworn was there. New developments in my personal life have a good deal to do with my absence but an underlying dissatisfaction is equally to blame. I really haven’t run into an online game lately that makes, what I consider to be role playing easy or even really gives it much support. Of course, lately, I’ve been playing those huge commercial MMORPGs such as EQ. Most are good games with good intentions and concepts but just end up falling short IMO. None of them have a gold piece on a good, privately run MUD, or even a copper piece on a good, old fashioned, pen and paper game.

After playing MMORPGs for a few years now, I’ve sort of come to a defeatist conclusion that “Massive” and “Commercial” are death knells for “Role Playing”. The logistics of keeping a dynamic vibrant world, the disparity of schedule for the thousands (or tens of thousands) of players. It all gives me a headache just trying to keep thinking of ways to bring a vibrant online world to life in such a way that “events” are the rule and not the exception and that intrigue is as key to the game as the ‘lewt’. I’ve got to give the industry a break though. They’re a new phenomenon and its creators are walking uncharted territory.

So while I’ve drifted from my CRPG addiction, I haven’t drifted from my long term obsession with pen and paper gaming. I used to ride my bike to a friends house when I was a kid and we’d run single player campaigns for each other, occasionally conning his older brother into DMing for us. When I “grew up”, gaming became something way to “geeky” to participate in. At one point, in high school, I swallowed my immature pride and tried to convince some guys I wanted in on their DnD game. They were too suspicious of my motives and all I received were accusations of foul intentions and looks of incredulity. I then tried to convince some of my friends (who used to play but also “grew out of it”) that we should start up a game again. I always just received a lukewarm “yeah, we ought to do that…someday” response. Occasionally it was accompanied by the looks one might give a mental patient that had just asked for a straight razor.

Of course, CRPGs hardly received the same scrutiny. I suppose using your imagination is a much greater sin than staring blankly at a screen for hours on end. So, mostly starting with Wizardry, I fed my desire to play a good RPG with these automated substitutes. When I first got online, using the dial up account of a friend who was in college, I discovered the world of MUDding. A whole new gateway was opened. Now I could revel in my inner “geekdom” with fellow anonymous players who were looking for the same experience.

I had pretty much given up on the idea of ever playing a pen and paper game again when during a new student orientation at the university I had transferred to, I ran across a campus organization for War and Role Playing games. Interested in the opportunity, I joined the club and later that week got back into playing pen and paper RPGs. I suppose it’s been about 6 years or so now that I have been playing pen and paper RPGs again on a regular basis. I really enjoy it; returning to that world of pure imagination was like rediscovering an old friend. And, speaking from years of CRPG experience (on and off line). No computer medium can really capture it for me. I’ve had a few privately run, close knit MUDs come close to offering the same experience as a good table top game; but there’s something about the immediacy of the Pen and Paper experience and the lack of that clunky digital communications anchor between you and the virtual world.

Currently I’ve given up my normal position as DM and am playing in a friend’s campaign. Elyas, my druid with a high, yet very selective, charisma is a real blast to play. While normally neutral to the conflicts of most ‘civilized’ societies, a certain army has decided to declare war on the elven kingdom. Elyas could normally care less, however, this army has decided that the best way to deal with the elves is to slowly clear cut the woods, creating a slash and burn swathe to the elven capital. Every person in that army is now Elyas’ sworn enemy. The rest of the party is of similar persuasion. The human knight is from a land which has been at war with the army in question for hundreds of years. The elven mage-thief in the party obviously has no love for them. The remaining thief has simply found our party is just really good at finding shiny things, which, much to her enjoyment, the druid really has no use for (which increases her shares.)

We have been playing for about a year and the campaign has seen the characters from first level all the way to upwards of tenth (my druid) level. There have been many glorious adventures and many clashes with this encroaching army. None of this would have been well served or even marginally reproduced by a computer medium. My druid has dotted miles of countryside with hallucinatory forests to slow the incoming army; the knight has proven on several occasions that he is worth more than the glowing sword at his side; the elven mage thief has angered a minor deity by pocketing some select gems from her sacrificial fountain; the thief, in search of revenge, has made it her life quest to “find that damn guy who sold her the fake potions”.

Earlier this month we decided that our characters had accumulated quite a bit of recognition and, more importantly, wealth. Many are well known enough that they may soon find themselves being sought out by more than just the bounty hunters and elite death squads of the army we have declared war on. They’ll soon be called on by fellow adventurers ready to swear oaths of loyalty. Further, our reinforced tank of a wagon is getting pretty full. Top this off with the two regular NPCs, along with my Druid’s pet tiger, the thief-mage’s owl familiar, the knight’s faithful warhorse and a team of very stout horses, and we either look like a circus sideshow or an entry in the Macy’s day parade. So, our group decided it was time to build a stronghold (much to my Druid’s disinterest.)

Without the need to wait for patches and updates or having to deal with broken economies and urban sprawl (a la UO), we were able to sketch out our own little keep and find just the right place to put it. No one is going to stand out front of our groups front door, drop 255 crates in just the right place and walk through the wall. We won’t need to reduce our index fingers to bloody stumps waiting for the DM to hand us quaint little messages like “you have become better at masonry”. My druid will be able to cordially talk a family of eagles into being lookouts without their deciding to peck my eyes out 2 hours later. Most importantly, my druid will most likely never been accosted by a stranger and asked to have an adult female swine hurled upon them (u cast sow?).

I suppose its obvious what I’m getting at here. I don’t want to just sound like I’m slamming the whole CRPG industry though. Its an amazing thing to be sure. The internet, online gaming and the brave new MMORPG genre are all simply phenomenal. Its an exciting thing to be a part of. Watching and participating in the growth of the online CRPG industry is a real treat. I’ve made some good friends doing it, played some great games, and have had some great experiences. I wouldn’t advocate giving up on them entirely. It will continue to grow and become more and more fascinating as time and technology move along.

However, they still shadow the simple pen and paper experience. It has continuously evaded pure expression in the digital world and will probably continue to do so for quite some time. So, if any of you fellow EQ players ever happen to get the chance to sit down around a table with some character sheets and some very funny looking dice; by all means do it. You definitely wont regret it.

    Article by Russell Linton
