ISSUE # 120

 April 15th - April 21st, 2000


Editorial - Oghma

The Karana rain came crashing down in a sudden rush. Trotting through the sheets of water, Oghma wished that Brell, in addition to all the holy spells He had bestowed upon him, had also given him a simple umbrella spell. By the time he reached Qeynos, he’d be soaked.

Pounding up behind him was a man clad in green armor. The man paused as he passed and cast a Spirit of Wolf on Oghma. “Come on Oghma, let’s race into Qeynos!”

The trip made much quicker by spell-sped feet, Oghma found himself racing through the Qeynos Gates and headed for the Lion’s Mane Inn with the green-clad man. A couple of steaming mugs of hot honey mead later, they tucked into a meal in a corner table.

“I am Aradune Mithara, Outrider of Karana,” the man said.

“Oghma, of Brell, at your service. I’ve heard of you wandering the Karana plains. You are a ranger, no doubt.”

“Aye, and I have a problem that I’m trying to solve. I need to make sure that my rangers and druids are notified of events that happen in the world when they come back in to the Glades or the cities.”

“What events?”

Aradune looked uncomfortable. “Well, events. Things that happen in the world.”

“The first part of getting the news out is that there must be news to get out - People won’t read a book unless it has a compelling story, or it is important to them to have read it. But, given that there are events, and events that matter, here are some ideas. Let’s call it the “Oghma project”. First I’ll talk about getting the news out, and tomorrow morning, we’ll talk about making news a bit.”

Aradune chuckled. “I had heard that you had grown up wanting to be a bard. This should be good.” He motioned for more mead. Oghma took a deep breath.

The Oghma project, part I

“Assuming there is news to be told, there are quite a few ways I can imagine to get out the news. What matters is who needs to know, and who is doing the telling. I’m sure that the Circle of Unseen Hands has things that they don’t want others to know, whereas the Guards of Qeynos may have things everyone should know. I’m also making an assumption that you, Aradune, will have the resources do to these things.” Oghma smiled.

Aradune chuckled. “Let’s just assume that I have complete power over Norrath, and go from there.”

Oghma grinned, then continued, “For broadcast news (things like the death of Lady Vox, or the Invasion of Qeynos) there are a few ways. You could have newspapers printed when large events happened, and distributed by newsboys. This paper should decompose after a while, so that your cities don’t get littered. Town criers could shout news also.”

Aradune looked thoughtful. “Many areas are remote, though. It would take a while for news to get out there.”

“I agree,” said Oghma, taking a sip from a bottle of mead. “But that is the price you pay for staying out of the cities. If people spent more time in cities they may feel a bigger part of events, because even if they choose not to participate, they now that things are happening. And events happen around us all the time without our participation, and it’s nice to find out about it, even if we took no part in it. People don’t need to take part in ANY world events necessarily, as long as the events move along and Norrath changes because of it.”

Oghma continued, “For legends and histories, there could be storytellers who, for a few silvers, could tell short legends. This could add background to the events of the day, and make people understand why Mayong Mistmoore opposes the Ring of Scale, for example.” Oghma paused. “I think that too few people understand why things are happening the way they are in Norrath. Most think that the recent happenings with the Ring of Scale are unconnected to the recent discovery of Kunark. The lure of adventure and loot clouds people to the true history, and the rich history, that Norrath has.”

Oghma frowned thoughtfully and took a sip from another bottle of mead. “A more subtle way would be to have conversations between people. There are already some things like this, but it would be nice to have it on a larger scale. If a player with a high faction walks near a person with a like faction, that person could divulge secrets with a whisper to one player, or start a conversation with another person where information is exchanged. Quest-like prompts (or not, even better) could allow people to jump in on conversations.”

“A cool way to get guild news out would be for each guild in each city to have one runner type person. For example, entering Qeynos as a rogue, you would see Fumblefeet, the rogue runner, come running up to you. ‘Hey Oghma, didja hear about how there is a merchant caravan full of jewels that left for Rivervale an hour ago?’ More details could follow, then when another player enters the zone ‘Oh, hey, Aradune’s in town, I’ll go tell him!’ And away Fumblefeet goes to share the news.”

“This is also a nice way to introduce random quests, not guild related, but that just ‘happen’ to a random player. These quests aren’t given to anyone who hails a person, but just that one lucky player with the right level and faction to get it who enters the zone every so often.”

Oghma motioned for more mead. “Of course, your entry into a city, with all these things may result in a constant babble of sound, people talking, shouting, players yelling… So you may want to make way to filter out news, in addition to the other filters that are already used when someone doesn’t want to be subjected to too much chatter.”

Oghma yawned. “I think, even for me, that’s enough talk for tonight. When I left Rivervale I had a heavy halfling lunch, and I’ve been on the road a long time today.”

Aradune nodded. “It’s time for me to rest, too. Perhaps in the morning we’ll take up where we left off.”

Oghma looked at Aradune, then at the bottles of mead. “You have complete power in Norrath? Could you make the tab for all this go away? Talking so much is thirsty work!”

Aradune chuckled. “Done, friend dwarf. See you on the morrow.”
