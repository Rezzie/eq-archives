ISSUE # 121

 April 22nd - April 28th, 2000


Dynamic Roleplaying

I couldn’t agree more with Absor’s editorial regarding the Verant sponsored events. “Finally!” I say, but also I’m inclined to say, “It’s a start, not the finish line.” Planned events are just that, planned events. I, like many others, would like to see an RPG based on a more dynamic model. Of course, be forewarned, I’m a roleplayer in a pretty big way so many of my suggestions don’t match up with the MMORPG status quo.

I wrote a letter to the editor a while back (Issue #23) in which I stated that the players were the key element in a successful online RPG. That is, a game where consistent and plot moving role-playing is taking place. I stated that it would essentially be the player's responsibility to fuel, and in a sense, monitor, the consistency of 'roles' that are being played.

There will never be a coded way to monitor this activity. Code may assist in determining improper 'roles' or behavior that is 100% inconsistent with the proposed virtual environment, but it will never be the final judge. Further, it will be quite some time before we see a gaming company muster together the willpower and sheer logistical genius to provide a large enough staff to monitor such activity as, say a pen and paper GM might.

I’ve also made the claim in the past that players must take a slightly greater interest in keeping the virtual world cohesive. They must add a little extra commitment for the sake of everyone's experience. It’s not enough to decide for you and you alone what roleplaying is. To truly play a meaningful role it must be a part of a greater community. People have a great way of getting behind a common goal and forging communities.

The same cohesive behavior takes place in online games. In EverQuest, the common goal seems to be the gaining of levels and equipment. An entire vocabulary has developed around this, not to mention social rules and faux paus. Spontaneously, people have gathered together around that one thing they have in common and made a unique community out of the thing.

However, the focus stifles what I would really call 'roleplaying'. Not many are really pursuing a role beyond being themselves and not many are adding to the virtual history of a fantasy world. There is a community, of course. A very complicated and rewarding one at that, to be sure. There is even a history for this community, though instead of being recorded in historical annals in a library in Norrath, its spread out through lists of patches, communications between the dev team and the players, and in real life discussion on message boards and news groups. Who is a 'KS' is much more pertinent to this community than 'who murdered Guard Nexxil'.

So how do you get players to recognize and focus on the fantasy world and its history instead of the ‘computer gaming community’ and its, for lack of a better term, OOC history?

This is where the company comes in.

If players are to truly play meaningful roles; if a true fantasy world is to blossom and remain cohesive - true to its own imagined history, true to its own society, the game itself has to showcase it. Rather than only giving the players a few numbers in common and leaving it at that, true in-game ties need to be forged and put into use. Its not enough to throw two thousand people into a room and say "Here’s a brief history, here’s a fantasy 'world', now play the game," and watch from afar as they mill about, only interjecting to roll the dice for them a few times.

Things need to happen in game that inspire and encourage roles that are consistent with the envisioned world. Some suggestions which I think would help the cause:

One character

This isn’t simply to stop 'twinking' or 'mule' characters. It’s to enforce accountability. Its to make players actually invest something in their characters and to avoid out of character temptations. Given only one persona from which to choose on an account, people would be much more prudent in their choice of role and their own actions.

Temptations include mule characters who collect or create items the main character cannot, which erodes the community. They also include twinking, which erodes community (relying on others) and even the overall balance of power in a game. Temptations also include 'gravy train' connections wherein those who consider themselves roleplaying make new characters and justify muling or twinking through some story of how their new character is 'related' to the old. In any case, the true answer is they are simply doing it to get ahead statistically and only in a secondary way (if at all) to support a chosen role.

Another possible bonus stemming from having a single character per account, the grief player who lives to simply make other players miserable would be easily singled out. Quite possibly, what happens in real life to such individuals would happen in game: shunned, they would only be able to carry the behavior so far until everyone was on to them. At some point everyone would be able to tie 'moron' with their persona and avoid them like the plague or justly pursue them to extinction. The only solution for the grief player would be to start a new persona and again take the time to develop a new character. In short, the grief players would only inhabit the lower rung of the power scale. True, some may persevere despite the negatives of being an easily recognizable sociopath, however, its my opinion that those that did would in a sense be taking on an interesting antagonistic role that might be drawn into the game's society.

In Game Guilds

Completely OOC entities shouldn’t be given presence in the world. Clan Zerg_Brood from Starcraft shouldn’t be encouraged to merely transport players into the environment and just add the game to a long list of other games they have a presence in. Guilds should be inextricably tied to the true history of the virtual world. The developers should create compelling in-character guilds all tied together by virtue of the game world's complex history which players can join and achieve rank in. I think a virtual world is severely compromised with the more things that are completely out side of its scope that it is forced to represent. Now this doesn’t mean that Clan Zerg_Brood couldn’t decide to all join some historically established guild and -in character- dominate that guild. In fact, if done properly, the organization and effort put forth could only add to the experience for everyone. Wouldn’t being a member of “The Outriders of Karana” (Aradune’s own organization) be infinitely more interesting and supportive of the in-character history than to be a member of Player Guild #20300? Not to say players themselves can’t make some excellent in-character guilds that are grounded in and add to the virtual history; but it’s incredibly rare.

Player Relations

No, not PR or tech support, but relations as in familial relations. Wouldn’t it be interesting to be another player's father, mother, daughter, or son? We've seen in some games weddings between characters which have a sort of true bond represented in game and in character. I think taking this a step further and assigning family trees on character creation would be quite interesting. Sure, there's probably a fifty-fifty chance that it evolves into chaos or that people take it seriously and develop true 'clan' like ties from it.

Less NPCs in positions of societal importance

In EverQuest my Dark Elven Cleric’s guildmaster lost my interest when I discovered his vocabulary was limited to three sentences. I knew he could never appreciate the fact that I groveled before him, or that I made sure to remove all weapons before entering the house of my god. But had there been flesh and blood behind that visage, wouldn’t it have been more interesting? Which leads to my next suggestion:

Character Careers

I want to see player's run the virtual world. I don’t want to see automated traders and money machines. I mean all that does is make sure the game retains that 'fast food' sort of imagery. No substance underlying players who are simply running to the ATM and back after 'camping a spawn'. I’ve played in enough games to know that there are players for every role. I guarantee there are a fair number of people who would enjoy spending the majority of their time running the Smith in Freeport or tending bar at the Blind Fish in Neriak. Some people like nothing more than to sit quietly and build trade skills and ply their wares. Perhaps players could state their character goals at the games beginning. Before choosing their profession (or class) they simply select the path which best suits their envisioned character. A tradesman, an adventurer, an entertainer, or maybe a bureaucrat (head of a guild or mayor of a town) A -role-; a true one which has meaning to the world beyond the all too trite 'my sword is bigger than yours'. Players should be able to change these roles in the course of play, of course. Though the one they choose should make up the bulk of the players true skills.

Not to say NPCs have no place. But they should be simply used as tools, period. The real action, the real dynamics are with the players. They are alive and breathing and the only way of producing anything which resembles such. NPCs could be guards controlled by the PC captain of the guard; they could be criers in employ of the PC Mayor or apprentices to run the PC's shop when he’s not online or out of the office. They shouldn’t be the sole maintainer’s of the game’s history or the sole source of that histories action and change.

No planned events

Logging on should be the event. The world should be interesting enough and vibrant enough that every session is an event. This is the benefit of having players as the centerpiece. No need for a GM to log on as 'random 10,000 hit point bad guy_0001' that can live long enough to spout out some slightly relevant story and then slay everything they walk into. The players themselves, if actually drawn into the virtual world and its history, would provide this. Further, players who just aren’t lucky enough to be on at the 'right time' won’t feel slighted, for really anytime should potentially be full of intrigue and an interesting event.

Another point, people are clamoring for online worlds to be dynamic then sitting on their hands! How can it be dynamic when the biggest portion of most online “RPG”s is devoted to rout, static behavior?! Of course, it only helps when the game producers recognize this as well and provide ways that players can be empowered to manipulate and involve themselves directly with the world.

Time

This is the biggest issue perhaps. Players would log on and find massive changes each time. Perhaps the Freeport smith logs on and finds he’s been out of goods for the past real time week. Maybe the Dark Elven commander of the Indigo Brotherhood logs on to find Neriak has been overrun by Halflings and his watch slaughtered. True, there would be large gaps as, for obvious reasons, players will always log on at different times. It may even be the case that players take advantage of this; the player members of the Halfling Leatherfoots organizing their raid during hours they know a large chunk of the Indigo Brotherhood guild can’t be online to help defend the city. Sure, it’s a potentially enormous stumbling block in such a dynamic, player-centric game, but well, it does definitely insure the game is indeed dynamic as per request. Major events should be logged each day and given to players to peruse as the game loads or in a way similar to the patch message’s of EverQuest so every player is kept up to speed on the evolving state of the game.

Well, that’s my thoughts on this whole phenomenon of MMORPG’s and a few ways (of the hundred or so I can think of!) that I think they could be improved. Well not only improved but actually worthy of that little ‘RPG’ tag.

    Article by Russel Linton
