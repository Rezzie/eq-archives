ISSUE # 132

 July 15th - July 21st, 2000


Editorial - Oghma

I was reading the boards this week and came across this comment, and it got me to thinking…

i know you can cite the number of accounts verant currently has. but what are you going to do when EQ finally has competition? you do realize how many customers you are going to lose dont you? is the company policy "rake it in while we can"?

How many are we going to lose? That's hard to say. We all know that EQ has a finite life. Newer games will come along that will grab peoples attention, just like we've grabbed the attention of the current players. This is how the gaming industry works. You grab their attention and keep it as long as you can.

I don't think that this qualifies as a "rake it in while we can" policy, at least not in a negative sense. We provide a service offering and people decide whether or not they want to be our subscribers. This service offering is flexible, but only to the point that it remains the same offering. Given infinite resources, sure, we have tons of things that we could do for EQ, but will only do those things that make the game "EQ". We won't risk completely changing gameplay to the point that we'd lose the magic that is responsible for EQ's success.

But as for us not having "competition", we currently enjoy about half of the marketshare among those playing the 3 MMORPGs out there. That's fairly indicative of some current competition. If you recall, EQ was supposed to "Kill" the top MMORPG of the time (UO), and was supposed to be "Killed" by the next (AC). Neither of those things happened, as we all continue to co-exist peacefully. We each provide a niche that a varying and increasing number of subscribers enjoy.

-Gordon


Having been around the EQ community for about three years, I’ve often wondered about the life-span of the game. When the Team PvP servers were opened, first as one then as multiple servers, I was curious as to how long the servers would have a population spike and then settle down even to the point of extinction. Now, having been on a server that split (Karana) due to overcrowding, and watching servers be split and added - well, I thought I’d do some looking ahead from behind. (Yes, I really just said that.) I’m wondering about the life of EQ - I’m wondering what comes next. What could Verant improve as the market’s attention span shifts elsewhere, to re-attract the player-base they are currently enjoying?

I’ve been involved in MUDs since about 1988. As MUD programming evolved, MUDs would either stay running the same code or try to move into better gameplay by upgrading or changing to another code. Many MUD creators heavily modified existing code to bring their mud more in line with their Vision™ of what they wanted in a role-playing experience. The MUDconnector currently lists 1670 MUDs, a faintly accurate peek at how many MORPG Vision™’s there are out there.

Games where many people share a reality are typically challenged by several issues. Player interaction, gameplay (including graphics), game balance, in-game rewards, and setting/storyline/immersion all interact to make a game enjoyable or not to each individual player. How a game addresses these issues helps determine the player base, the level of enjoyment, and the duration of the game on the market. Currently, EQ enjoys the largest market share in this field because, overall, it does the majority of these things better than the other current games on the market. In some areas EQ could be better, and it’s these areas I’m going to focus on. I’m sure my Vision™ may be different than many, but bear with me. I won’t cover everything in this editorial, but I’ll start with camping, one of the downsides of EQ play. Camping is an important issue because it causes some of the worst player behaviors in the game - kill-stealing, the “owning” of a spawn or even a whole zone, and numerous violations of the “Play nice” policy, which wouldn’t exist if camping wasn’t an issue.

I think EQ developers were surprised at how many players decided to play by camping. Many of the Game developers were MUDders, and many played PnP RPGs, so they came from an “adventuring” background. In the interaction between fun, experience awards, and item rewards, Verant unintentionally tipped the scales towards experience and item rewards, and made it easier by having static spawns. MUD’s had nothing but static spawns - if you knew a room that had good loot you’d always see players there, but when translated into a rich graphics environment I think it was hoped that players would move about more. With Kunark they moved the items around a lot more, making for less static camping, but it’s still an issue. If I asked: “You will have a great time, risk death frequently and get 3.9 bubbles of experience and a minor magic item, or you can sit in one spot and get 4 bubbles of experience and one or two minor magic items, take your pick”, I think that most EQ players would go for the 4 bubs experience.

A recent post on the Stratics Westwood Tavern illustrated this. The poster had looked back at some of the polls run on Allakhazam’s http://everquest.allakhazam.com/ site, and discovered this fact:

For the poll that asked the question "What will you do when Kunark comes out?", the most popular answer was:

Head to the current popular zones hoping the crowds are gone 2686 30%

So this month's question is "How much time do you spend in Kunark?" The top two answers are:

I rarely go to Kunark 1964 33%
I spend some time in Kunark, but mostly play elsewhere 999 17%

Interestingly enough, this contrasts with an earlier poll, which asks:
Typically speaking, what type of player are you?
(1) 33.8% Explorer;
(2) 22.7% Role Player;
(3) 22.6% Power Leveler;
(4) 14.1% Chat/Socializer;
(5) 4.7% Newbie;
(6) 1.8% Kill Stealer (be honest).
Total Votes: 12375

What it comes down to is this: never underestimate the desire for players to take the easy route to success. With a whole new area to explore, new items, decent experience, etc most folks want to go where they have already been, despite that most people consider themselves explorers. (Yes, I know these polls are not necessarily accurate, but it’s better than guessing.)

The idea that an item is “necessary” is another odd fixation with players. Pure casters require a Flowing Black Robe, or warriors must have a Polished Granite Tomahawk (with the corollary being “if you don’t have these items you might as well not play”). If an item is viewed as essential you can dilute the issue by having many similar items spread out more, which is what Verant did with Kunark. It doesn’t completely fix this issue, because, on occasion there are (and should be) very rare, highly useful items which add to a player’s status, allowing all to marvel at his/her prowess in acquiring such a cool item. Quest items are another issue, too, but I’ll deal with that in a quest editorial.

What if a desired item was actually carried by several spawns in an area? To use an example: The Goblin King’s crown could be on the Goblin King, the Goblin king's page, his wife, his Champion, or the goblin crown polisher. Each may have it at varying moments during the goblin day or night. Each area would have modifiable story line, and the item would never be static. A rogue could, conceivably, enter and steal the crown for the page at the right moment, winning the item camp lottery. A warrior may pop in and best the Goblin King’s Champion, winning the item in a glorious battle. The key is to make it part of a storyline, not too predictable, even allowing for hints to be given out by other NPC’s elsewhere.

Goblin King: Page, take my crown to the crown polisher.
Page: Yes, my Lord King.

Page: The King wants his crown polished!
Crown polisher: Aye, send for it in two hours.

Mistress: Oooh Mr. Crown polisher, how does it look on me? I’m going to go look in my mirror!
Crown polisher: (mutters) Ok, but bring it right back.

Crown polisher: (screams) where is it? I need it back!
Mistress: Ooopsie! (giggle) I must have left it on the queen’s bedspread

Goblin Queen: What the? What’s this doing here?
Goblin’s Champion: (rolling over) Yeah, ow! It stabbed me in the backside.
Goblin Queen: Put on your pants and give it to the page outside.

Champion: Uh, give this to the King
Page: Yes sir!

Page: here you are, Lord King!
Goblin King: (wonders to himself) How come I smell goblin backside? I took a bath last month! (sniffs)
Goblin King: Page, take my crown to the crown polisher.

Etc, etc. (Let me apologize for this brief look inside the court of the Goblin King. It’s not pretty.)

AI in EverQuest could even address monster behavior when groups were in the area - Would you sit quietly on your throne if a party of 50+ players were sitting around waiting for you to show up? I’ll bet the Goblin King would at least beef up his guards. I think a Goblin King raid would be much more exciting if you rushed into his lair, reached the king, and slew him as more guards appeared to force you to retreat away.

When EQ was first being planned, the average bandwidth was 14.4, the average texture memory was 2M, and the processor speed was around (slightly less than) 200Mhz. What they could do graphics and game/combat mechanics-wise was limited not necessarily by the Vision™ but also by the constraints put on them by hardware. It is part of the reason why there aren’t more character faces, and may well be why static spawns were necessary.

This brings me to my point: From the quote above, Abashi shows that Verant is aware of the birth and death cycle of MMORPGs. If you had the most popular MMORPG out there, and you knew it would die out eventually, what would you do? If I were Verant, I’d take all the lessons learned from EQ, and put them into making a new EQ, with the increased flexibility allowed by high bandwidths, faster graphics cards with more Texture memory, higher processor speed. I’d also make expansions (Ruins of Kunark, Scars of Velious) to push the envelope a little bit more, and to test some emerging technologies. This editorial is one of the possible solutions to the EverCamp phenomenon - it might not be possible to fix EQ this way, but EQ- the Next Generation?

    Editorial by Oghma
