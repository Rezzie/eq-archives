ISSUE # 128

 June 10th - June 16th, 2000

An Uneasy Symbiosis

Several years ago, when I first began to mud, only a few months went by before I realized that the online world where I played was continually supported and enhanced by volunteers. Even the owners and main creators of the mud who paid to run it online and make it open and accessible to its players, were volunteers, building the world and creating a platform, a place, where people who came to play could also contribute to the community. Some players became coders, or worldbuilders themselves. Others were invited to become god administrators, watching over the players, enforcing the rules, helping with problems. I remember Bimble whose amazing talent was quest creation -- from writing the plot to developing the quest and running it. Yet other players prepared contests, wrote stories and poems, or even acted as teachers (thank goodness for Noke and Tagad for their tintin++ lessons) and mentors. The community was richly grown through the efforts of its members and the time they volunteered to it.

In a way, I suppose, it was inevitable that the online roleplay gaming environments would initially feel comfortable with and draw heavily from rpg volunteers. The Internet itself blossomed with donated time and energy -- freeware, cheap shareware -- people teaching others how to use the tools, people sharing their knowledge with those they encountered, people encouraging and assisting others in developing yet more permutations of game environments. Real life pen and paper roleplaying games have also always relied on the efforts of volunteers for actual play. A book full of descriptions of classes and races and spells and weapons is merely paper with illustrations and lists. It is only when the GM develops the campaign and players flesh out their characters, all volunteering their time to each other, that the game lives and is played out. Thus, it seems an ethic of volunteer energy has naturally evolved from both the efforts of players in the online environment and the roleplaying communities in real life. From the first pen and paper games through the muds and mushes and on to the graphical online rpgs, the games themselves have grown beyond the boundaries of their initial play structures to become platforms or stages on which the players often revise the scenery and costumes, write the scripts and songs and enact the plays -- all as volunteers whether a game itself is free or for a fee.

Until the emergence of the pay-to-play games, however, most issues involving volunteer recognition and ownership of creative product were relatively uncomplicated. If a mud was built using open source code and well-known literary worlds, for instance the Diku mud code and a Forgotten Realms environment, the creators needed to be sure to give acknowledgement to the mud code developers and needed to follow approved guidelines from the owners of the Forgotten Realms copyright but few other issues of ownership arose with only the most egregious of copyright violations bringing desist letters. As their worlds grew and became perceptibly unique, some mud creators did go on to try to establish ownership of this combination of borrowed material and personal and volunteer additions and enhancements. Some encrypted their code, requiring non-disclosure agreements from their volunteer coders, and some claimed that anything published on the site of the mud (or mush for that matter) was covered by an umbrella copyright and owned by the mud creators. Other games, however, maintained open code and open use (while most often maintaining a copyright), allowing anyone to use what they had developed as long as credit was given. Money, however, rarely changed hands and the volunteers didn’t see the mud owners as financially benefiting from their labor in these cooperative efforts. So, despite the occasional disputes over ownership and copyright, the volunteer ethic was solid, trusted and respected and ownership for profit was rarely an issue.

The pay-to-play games that first came out seemed to simply layer this established volunteer ethic and energy on top of their gameplay structures. The Realms, for example, like many games, used volunteers to help run the game and assist players just as the free muds did. Probably none of the games came into existence without the efforts of volunteers, from testers to those who have offered all kinds of creative input on game development, storylines and entertainment additions such as contests, quests and fan sites. It was a natural assumption of game developers and the player community alike that the offerings of the testers and players were for both the benefit of the community and the profitability of the company. Thus the not-for-profit models of volunteer involvement continued to be used to build flourishing and creative games even when a company would financially benefit.

EverQuest certainly, and from what I have heard, Asheron’s Call and UO too, have all availed themselves of the energies released by this volunteer ethic. From the input of the earliest beta testers through the continuing suggestions of players of today, for instance, the producers of EQ have relied heavily on volunteers to help flesh out the world and the classes, test the spells and weapons and suggest and help develop the more social/less technical elements of the game. I remember even, way back in beta one, wandering the road with a game developer asking me, Nep, how I imagined bards, what I thought they should be able to do and how I could imagine playing one, all at a time when the guildmaster at the bard’s guild hadn’t even learned how to sing yet, much less strum a lute. My experience with this opportunity to provide ideas and suggestions certainly wasn’t unusual. Thousands of testers and players volunteered their time, the use of their own equipment, their ideas and their detailed examination of gameplay to help make EverQuest what it is today. Of course, all that effort wasn’t entirely altruistic -- I mean, the testers and players do get to play and be part of an eagerly sought growing community. Regardless of the motives for donating time and energy, however, and given the platform of the game provided by EverQuest which rests solidly on a foundational ethic of including this volunteer energy, the testers and players have been able to develop and present thousands of ideas (superb through quite dreadful) to enrich the world of Norrath and build a connective web of ongoing player/volunteer enhancements and improvements.

So far then, this symbiosis of the volunteer ethic and for-profit game development seems to mutual benefit. Thousands of players have a world to which they can contribute their talents and skills and the game companies are profitable enough to ensure that these imaginal world platforms will survive for the enjoyment and benefit of the players as well as the stockholders. However, along with such profitability often come those who neither develop a game directly nor enrich it for free. While some may describe the newcomers as dark shadows of suits lurking in the boardroom (although others may be more charitable), it seems to me that this business/financial/legal intrusion raises several questions about the longterm effect of financial issues on the thus far fruitful and creative volunteer presence. Will the mesh of volunteer ethic and for-profit focus continue to hold? Will the increasing profitability of on-lines games create battles over ownership of ideas and recognition or recompense for volunteer donations? Will legal disputes arise that will tear apart this increasingly uneasy partnership of capital and volunteers?

There is, already, a court case of volunteer online chat helpers suing AOL. It is practically inevitable that some day a story line written by a player and adapted and used by an online rpg will end up in litigation over copyright. And what about the use of volunteer beta testers, a vital resource needed especially by start-up companies? Will they be seen as volunteers or will they eventually be perceived as free and illegal workers? Will volunteer gods and guides blow down before the legal onslaught of the winds of financial responsibility and taxation? There is even the possibility of questions of ownership of a character. What if, for instance, a character became so well known in a virtual world that it became a living part of the environment that players talked about and wrote stories about? And then, what if a scriptwriter came along and said, “Wow, cool character and cool story - I’m going to write it up and sell it.” Would the ensuing disputes eventually destroy the very heart of the volunteer efforts?

Already, I’m sure, corporate meetings are fraught with warnings from the legal teams, and already volunteers have begun to question whether their offerings are recognized as being a true and unique portion of the development of the game worlds or whether their ideas are ploughed and exploited by game corporations for additional profit without recognition or reward for those volunteering. The Internet has fostered practices of free and open speech and expectations of involvement. Will there be a balance between what people want to give and what companies may safely take? Will new ways of recognition and appreciation arise that will satisfy both sides and maintain the partnership between the player communities and the game developers?

This is going to be one of those slightly messy essays that end in a slew of questions and a paucity of answers. It would be a great pity to close off the creative platforms of online games from the volunteers, leaving players few, if any, places to share their skills and talents and leaving them little way of benefiting themselves and their fellow players. It would also be a grave pity to place such heavy liability and tax burdens on the game companies that the volunteer ethic and presence would be severely suppressed leaving the game with only a small and probably way overworked contingent of helpers unable to replace the thousands of human hours that had so freely enhanced the game for all of its players. Is it possible to have a for-profit site that operates to both foster and use the fruitfulness of the creativity of its players in a cooperative way that is perceived as beneficial to all? I hope so.

    Essay by Nepenthia
