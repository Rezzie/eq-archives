Letters to the Editors

First, thank you for the great newsletter. I may not always agree with the views presented or the semi-pornographic art work, but I think it is a good effort and a help in community building.

I thought Snafu's article in issue 70 on stereotypes was interesting. However, one can only hope his liberal use of Real-Life stereotypes was part of the satire along with his over-the-top IC examples and that he was not serious in his use of what would be good role-playing.

Being near 30, I am finding it harder and harder as time goes on to not succumb to the stereotype of the obnoxious 11-year old or the 10-year old up past their bedtime (not to mention the other classic stereotypes of teens in online game play). However, while some of these stereotypes may be true, you are going to find out that a lot more of these people are in fact in their twenties and older. One of the best lessons of online gaming is that emotional maturity should not be confused with age in any way shape or form, especially when the anonymity of the internet is brought into play.

Snafu also tries to draw a distinction between the shadowknight who just-wants-to-be-friends and the troll-raised-by-humans, with the former being some sort of pathetic power-gamer's ploy and the latter being an excellent example of role-playing. Snafu needs to revisit gaming 101. The latter and all the minor variations are one of the broadest and most abused examples of stereotypes in gaming there is. It is one of the classic power-gamer's excuses for trying to avoid the built-in social balances of a powerful character/profession/whatever, in addition to be being one of the most tired and re-hashed concepts in gaming.

People look to fictional works where a character has such a background and works because of the context of the novel, then en masse' translate the concept to a single game. When you are in a large online RPG (or even a table-top group or gaming society) and this has been done already, by many, it becomes almost a worse offense than the shadowknight's behavior. At least I can reconcile the shadowknight in my own role-play by keeping in mind that he's really evil at the core and is just putting on a friendly facade to use people and to build his own dark powers that are antithetical to everything my character believes.

My personal reaction to stereotypes in EQ has been to use them more and more often when I see countless people trying to use these tired and self-serving excuses for role-playing in an orgy of power-play and avoidance of one of core penalties built into their race/profession choice. Thank goodness the NPCs are not as easily fooled or as willing to cooperate as most players.

The circumvention of the racial and professional biases built into this game world should not be so easy as claiming to be a nice guy raised by sainted humans, but something that occurs over protracted contact and successful role-playing.

Rather than minimize it, I say strengthen the use of bigotry in role-play in EQ in the face of players who seem in capable of balancing self-interest with role-playing. I just love the ones who on one hand condemn Tovax and on the other beg an unknown PC necromancer to find their corpse or the dark elf who for claims (in OOC) to be unlike her kin when she needs help but gleefully steals kills at every opportunity, for example.
