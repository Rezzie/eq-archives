ISSUE # 124

 May 13th - May 19th, 2000

The Train Stops Here

Well, I went and did it. Yup. Count me among the disenfranchised who yearn for a new game to play.

I cancelled my EverQuest Account on Thursday evening.

It’s not to say that it was a bad game. I think my reverence for this groundbreaking game comes through in the article below. But if it had been an exceptional game, I think I’d still be playing. I think my articles show that I haven’t been playing much, as they’ve begun to slant toward general role-playing topics rather than remaining in the realm of Norrath.

I notice that there are stages to virtual life. I will exclude the step wherein one writes for an EverQuest online newspaper for a year before the game even goes into beta.

1) Awe. First time you log in, there’s a real sense of wonder. It’s even a little overwhelming to think that you can pick a direction and go.

2) Frustration. As any newbie can attest, you have those moments where you wonder why you are bothering to play. Typically those moments are preceded by “A Kliknik prince attacks you for 5 pts of damage” and is immediately followed by “Returning to home”.

3) Satisfaction. When you finally achieve a few quests and a friend or two have shown you a few pointers, you have that soft warm glow of contentedness.

4) Exhaustion. Pretty soon you are logging on for many, many hours. I’m not sure I was ever as addicted to EverQuest as I was to the predecessor to this game, Tanarus. But it’s pretty close.

5) Fear. Eventually you start hitting areas where more often than not you wind up running for your virtual life. Even as you sit there in your chair in a well-lit room tapping the keys like a madman, bag of Salsa Verde Doritos and a Big Gulp of Dr. Pepper on the table beside you, you still feel the adrenaline rush of a man/woman in true despair as you do your best to amble your mortally wounded character into the nearest zone whilst the griffin tears away large hunks of your virtual backside.

6) Boredom. It happens with the best of games. You hit a plateau. For some it’s 5th or 6th level (as with a friend who left the game when beta ended) while for others it’s when they reach 50th level and there’s nothing left to challenge them. For me, it was level 16. I could have kept going if I cared any longer about the game at that point, but I had been playing since late in the first stage of beta… repeating my efforts as I tried out the various races and classes and rebuilt through numerous character wipes. When my 15th level character at the end of beta was wiped, I figured I’d never bother to play long enough to top that. Well, I played numerous characters, but I only bothered to push one to 16th. Admittedly, for a guy writing articles on gaming, 16th level is pretty low achievement, but achievement was never my goal so much as enjoyment. For me they are not irrevocably linked.

7) Longing. I so wanted to keep playing and to feel the fire again. Friends had told me that they stopped playing for a few weeks and when they came back their interest in the game had been redoubled. For me, it was the opposite. I found other things to do. Pretty soon, I knew the time had come.

8) Cancellation. I can’t explain why this was so hard to do. Maybe because of EQX and the need to keep attached to the game in some remote way so that I could continue to write articles. But it’s more likely that, as with Tanarus before it, EverQuest was still an ideal whose prime had passed for me. Logging in was no longer a joy. New patches only drew a sigh rather than the giddy anticipation of fixes to the game.

9) Is there a stage nine? Maybe there is. Could be that some day I so miss the game that I log back on, but it’s unlikely. I’ll just miss some of the players.

In this day and age of Norrath, everyone seems to be 40th level. The newbie-lands are bereft of wanderers. The once crowded low-level dungeons are now plentiful and ready to be plundered. But the buzz is not the same.

So what will I do with my time?

Lil’ Archy came to a very unceremonious halt. After more than a year of cartoon strips, I’m nearly bereft of ideas. I may just let Archy, Snaffu, Oghlin the dwarven bard, The generic PK d00d, Torchy the Fire Elemental, and the rest of the cast stay in limbo… much like the characters I left behind in the game when I terminated my account.

I find Ezmend, my highest level character, still pre-occupies my thoughts on rare occasion. Perhaps he’ll find his way into a paper & pen RPG, should we ever create an evil party.

I had sat down to write this article thinking that it would be about how things might have been better in the game, or what might have happened to keep me playing. But at this point I’ve spilled all my suggestions into previous articles, and all that comes out here are the fond feelings of remembrance of a bunch of lives I never really lived.

But let’s be less maudlin in ending this article. I think here I’ll include the things that I think would make a perfect role-playing game. Let’s assume for a minute that developers had all the time, wealth, and capability to implement my suggestions. I’ll make my suggestions based on this premise.

  • Individuality. What many games (especially online games) lack is the ability to set yourself apart from others. The developers of 3rd Edition D&D (Wizards of the Coast) appear to be making their game so that you have more choices for your character. Your new fighter might not wind up with exactly the same abilities as your last fighter or the one you made a decade ago. Being able to better select one’s abilities would be a great addition to online RPGs.
  • Appearance. Asheron’s Call has made an attempt to allow you to customize the appearance of your characters. In EQ, all body types were the same in each race. The faces were rarely seen up close and therefore everyone looked nearly the same. Clothes were the same. It’s hard to get into character when you are basically playing Generic character number 1791 on your server.
  • GM Intervention. Events in EQ were always so few and far between. And I was never online when they happened. The Neverwinter Nights model of having a GM for every group of players seems appealing. Unrealistic in a game like EverQuest, but then we are “shooting the moon” here.
  • Graphical improvements. This is the kind of thing that will happen. With time, every game will have a less “box-like” look to the figures. This is the one item that I’m relatively sure will happen in the very near future.
  • Less Abuse. I speak of players abusing players and of players abusing flaws in the game. Verant has always endeavored to make a level playing field. It’s the players who have always done their best to get ahead by whatever means possible. And for a “game”, that’s pretty sad.
  • No Character Selling. As nice as it is for someone who doesn’t have the time to build up a high level character to buy one and jump right in, it’s detrimental to the game in terms of all the useful items being camped.
  • Simple Interface. That’s not to say that EQ had a tough interface, because it didn’t. But the trend in computer games is to keep adding hotkeys. I don’t know about you, but I’m intimidated by any game where they have to include a picture of the keyboard with all of the actions mapped on it.
  • Scalable starting. I’ve been thinking about how people with little time to play never get to see much of the world. In a game like Neverwinter Nights, theoretically you’ll see anything the GM wants you to see. With EQ however, you are stuck at low level unless you *gasp* buy a character. One way around this would be to allow players to start at a higher level. They wouldn’t get all sorts of amazing equipment or anything… just the capability to go out and get it themselves, just like when you start off at first level. There may/may not be bonuses of some kind for starting out from the beginning. Maybe there’s a skill that you can gain at first and fifth and tenth levels (etc.) that you can’t get by starting out at tenth. In order for this to work there would have to be great restrictions on what equipment can be passed around and who can group up, otherwise you’d have somebody make a 15th level character so he can gather equipment and money for his first level friends. But if you were unable to pass anything except, say… food, and merchants would not buy food from player characters then you can get some mileage out of this idea.

There, now I feel like I’ve done something productive rather than just writing a tearful goodbye to the game I’ve played for… how long? I’ve sat in front of a computer for how many hours? Jeez. I really do need a life. Think I’ll see how much they go for on E-bay.
