ISSUE # 128

 June 10th - June 16th, 2000


Editorial - Oghma

I’ve been taking a break from editorializing, since I’ve been a bit busy with a new job. I’ve moved from working an hour away from home to working 5 minutes away, and the spare time I’ve gained I’ve used to work on some other projects.

I started a guild on Karana, made up of friends of mine from beta and good players I’ve met since then, and I’ve been really enjoying it. We have a weekly “Get-Togedder” and we are heavily on the role-playing side. We’ve taken breaks in fighting to play Leap-Froglok and believe me, you haven’t lived until you have seen 14 gnomes jumping each other’s bent-over bodies. Very fun! - someday I’ll have to post a screen cap of that.

What makes this guild leadership suddenly important is the announced Karana Server split. The Karana server has an interesting population. It is the “official” French server, so it is home to quite a few players that play in traditionally “off-peak” times. Thus, the Karana server, I believe, has the highest average population, rarely dropping below 800 players, and often reaching 2700 at peak times. My packet loss lately has been steadily in the 25-30% range, with frequent spikes into the 70’s. Onnie, a server friend, can’t even get into Karana with packet loss that bad on her phone lines.

Several servers have been split, and it seems to be a rather inexact science. One criteria used by Verant is the peak time population. They attempted to attract peak-time users by only allowing the move command during peak time. The hope is that those users who are placing the most population pressure (peak users) on the server would then be the ones to move. That way, peak usage will drop and put less pressure on the server. (perhaps dropping peak use from over 2500 to somewhere in the 1500 range.

The last big split (Veeshan and Bristlebane moved to Saryrn, Xegony and Bertox to The Seventh Hammer) resulted in s slight reduction in peak times on the old servers, but not much. Veeshan and Bristlebane still go over 2000 at peak times, and Bertox also. (I haven’t kept track of Xegony, but I am aware that it is lower than that.) Way back in beta during the discussion of server stress, Verant mentioned that 1500 was their goal for a server’s population. At the time they were unaware that the servers could actually handle much more stress, but generally by now over 2000 can be an accepted working ceiling for server population.

The difficulty lies, I believe, in the guild issue. As any guild member knows, there is no guarantee that every member of a guild can be moved over to a new server. Guilds, being active groups with active peak users then tend to NOT move, because of the risk of losing guild members. Ironically, the heavy peak time users then wind up staying on the old server, “nerfing” the server split. Abashi recently commented that in the last split only 40% of the people moved were peak users. Because of this, the Karana split is being done not to a fresh server, but to one of the new servers recently split into (Saryn or Seventh hammer being the most likely candidates) from high peak servers.

Our guild, as many others I’m sure, will not be moving to the new server, whichever it is. We don’t want to lose members, we don’t want to have to spend time leveling up a companion who wasn't logged in when a move command was possible. The players having the highest impact on peak population probably won’t move, leaving Karana server with a reduced peak population, but not really relieving much of the pressure. I believe that until a workable guild moving solution presents itself, server splits will continue having reduced impact.

What are some of the difficulties with this issue? Why is it so hard to move an entire guild?

Let’s say a guild leader calls for a move/stay vote within a guild and the whole guild unanimously votes to move. After being notified by the official guild leader, Verant could run a list of all the guild members’ accounts and move them all. They could continue this with each guild until the move population reaches the maximum decided upon by Verant.

Now, let’s go a little farther and be more realistic - let’s say that 80% of the guild votes to move, and some of the guild members haven’t played in that guild for several months having been more active in another guild or playing other characters. Suddenly it’s more complicated - a guild-wide move could result in quite a few players suddenly switching to the new server that were perhaps even unaware that their guild was moving! Troublesome, indeed.

Verant recommends that people don’t give anyone their station names, so a leader could hardly give a list of accounts to move so as to exclude the members who wished to stay. That would be open to abuse, anyway. If I knew someone else’s account, I could slide them into the new server without them knowing.

What if, like the guild creation process, Verant created a chat room mechanism where all those members who were in a guild and wish to move could all log in at once? Or, even better, have a number count that the guild leader gives when he sets up the channel and members who wish to move all issue a command in the chat room sometime before the move, and when the member count reaches the number count, that guild is moved. For example, say I have 40 members who want to move - I’ll create a chat room that is passworded and has a member ceiling of 40. If those 40 members all log in and issue a move command, poof, we move. If not, we don’t, having not been able to reach the number set. The accounts to move can be obtained from the accounts that logged into the chat room. I’m sure there are ways that I haven’t considered to address other difficulties. Perhaps accounts that are being moved could be asked to re-login at a prompt to ensure that they wanted to be moved?

I don’t have access to the figures and information that Verant does, but I hope that there is some way someday that guild moves could be accomplished - otherwise, the issue of non-peak user moves will remain un-addressed, and server splits will be less than satisfactory for all around.

Enjoy this issue of EQX!

    Editorial by Oghma
