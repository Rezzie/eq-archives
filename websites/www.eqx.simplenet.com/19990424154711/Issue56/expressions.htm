Even though I discussed this topic briefly in my From E3 to Today article, there are apparently more questions on the subject. This is likely because the implementation is quite complex, and takes some explaining if you haven't seen it yourself.

Let's consider this fictional story of Burtom, a Human Shadowknight born in Qeynos. Note the word fictional. As a shadowknight, Burtom is instantly hated by the Qeynos citizens. Upon walking into the streets on his way out of town, a commoner lashes out a feeble attack, calling for the guards to assist him. The guards come to the citizen's aid, leaving Burtom a bloodied corpse in the street. Burtom did nothing to provoke anybody. He was merely passing through. But, the innate hatred of his evil profession led to his timely death. With his death (or three or four), the Qeynos citizens feel more secure in their superiority over Burtom, and no longer view him as a threat.

Eventually Burtom makes his way into the world. He begins the slow and arduous process of cleansing Qeynos Hills of gnolls, skeletons, and other evil beings. All the while, he speaks to the townsfolk, learning their troubles and needs. He strives to gather information and deliver their desired items. Soon, the town appreciates his efforts, treats him as a friend, and the guards protect him as one of their own.

By the time Burtom requires training, he is regarded as a local hero. He descends into the darker regions, seeking out his guild master. As he reaches his destination, the shadowknight training officer shouts out, "Your actions have earned my contempt. Prepare to die!" Poor Burtom can scarcely react before he finds himself again floating above his bloodied remains. All of his efforts, while appreciated by some, have hurt the desires and goals of his guild, and they will make him pay the price until he can reverse the effects.

So, how does this really work? First of all, there is no such thing as "alignment" in EverQuest. This is not a bad thing, as most alignment systems we see on MUDs are unrealistic anyway. For example, if an evil character kills evil monsters, he tends to become good aligned. This isn't necessarily correct. If an evil character genocides a race of evil gnorcs, using their souls to feed his desire for death and destruction, should he really become good? I don't think so.

EverQuest handles this issue quite nicely with a faction system. Every NPC is a member of some faction. The human guards of Freeport are considered one faction. The paladin guild NPCs are considered a faction. The orcs of Crushbone are considered a faction. The skeletons that wander the countryside are considered a faction.

When you first create your character, you select a race, a class, and a deity. These things all factor into your starting relationships to each faction. For example, a necromancer is likely to begin as hated by the guards of Qeynos. When you kill a monster or complete a quest, it may change your standing with a particular faction. If you kill a gnoll, your standing with that faction will decrease. If you solve a quest for the rangers of Surefall Glade, your standing with their faction will increase.

Sound simple? Not quite. All of these factions are intertwined with relationships to each other. If they Qeynos guards are at war with the gnolls, then killing gnolls will also increase your standing with the guards. If the gnolls are allied with some underground mercenary unit, then killing gnolls will also decrease your standing with the mercenaries. It is very likely that killing a monster or solving a quest will affect your relationship with multiple factions. As such, all of your actions have a lasting permanent effect. This also means that your Dark Elf can spend enough time questing for the humans to eventually be accepted into their cities. Of course, you may not be allowed back in Neriak. Mindless killing of everything you run across could have far reaching effects that you will never know. As you enter a new zone, monsters you have never seen may be calling you an infamous villain and attacking you on sight.

To find out what your standing is with a particular NPC, you can /consider it. This not only reveals how your targets level compares to yours, it reveals how your target reacts to you. It may say that it regards you indifferently, smiles upon you warmly, or scowls at you, ready to attack.

The faction system is basically a web of relationships, tracking friendships, enemies, and alliances. It makes for a very realistic and exciting experience, fitting in wonderfully with the role playing aspect of the game.
