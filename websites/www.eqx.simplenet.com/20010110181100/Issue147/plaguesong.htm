ISSUE # 147

 October 28th - November 3rd, 2000


Plaguesong: The Beginning

Prologue

Oghma idly toyed with his empty mug. Age-old rings of purplish wine from the bottom of mugs made haphazard designs on the scarred oak tabletop. The tavern was quiet, and the muted sounds of those who had decided to stay in the common room and were settling down for a night’s rest made a murmuring backdrop to his thoughts. Oghma carefully rolled the bottom of his mug around the rings, making it travel forward and back, and side to side, all without lifting the mug or have the edge stray from a ring. With an odd, yet deep satisfaction at a job well done he put the mug down on a circle in front of him.

“Yet another tavern table circumscribed by drink,” he thought. Somewhere in that thought was an idea, or a song, or even an ironic metaphor, but he was too tired to push his creativity to find it. He had ridden a borrowed pony all the way from Two Ford to reach this small village. Five Oaks was deep in the Gnarley Forest, a village of about one hundred farmers, hunters, and the odd craftsman - not the large, urban centers he was used to frequenting. Sometime tonight someone was supposed to contact him, and tell him news of yet another village, Skorane, which, decimated by plague a decade ago, had suddenly become newsworthy again.

He hated riding ponies. It was not far below sea voyages on his list of bad ways to get from one place to another - his backside ached, his legs felt stretchy-soft and leaden, and he was unable to rid his nose of the smell of horse.

“Oghma, another round? Semaj and Pilfer have already hit the sack.”

Tiredly Oghma looked over at his friend Elec. The tall, slim elf was looking at him with a grin that contained about equal parts compassion and mischief. Elec had ridden along side him from Two Ford, but he was much more used to the saddle, and Oghma’s obvious discomfort was giving him no end of mirthful satisfaction. How like an elf! Oghma hoped that someday they’d find themselves in a nice dwarf city, with ceilings as tall as the crown of Elec’s head, and doorsills with endless forehead-smashing opportunities. Oghma smiled at the thought.

“I think this one is your turn,” Oghma said.

Elec nodded, and retrieved the pitcher of red wine he’d concealed behind him. “I thought you’d be ready,” he grinned.

“So, Semaj and Pilfer are horse-waddling too?” Oghma chuckled. If it wasn’t for his tough dwarven constitution, he may have been stretched out next to the two humans. It had been a long, hard ride, and Elec was the only one who’d ridden lately. As it was he was very sore, but not as tired as he might have been. He looked over at Semaj and Pilfer settling down for the night and raised his mug to their sleepy wave.

“Semaj just quietly lay down, but Pilfer was full of querulous complaints,” Elec smiled. ‘Why did we have to do this anyway?’ ‘Who is it we are supposed to meet?’ ‘Couldn’t we have taken a couple days to get here?’

Elec sat down and filled both their mugs, frowning. “Anyone look your way, give you a nod, anything?”

Oghma shook his head. “Not yet.” Oghma said, “I’m about ready to call it a night, though I think I’ll ask the innkeeper if he’s got a room with a bed. I’m not sure I can sleep on the common room floor tonight. Semaj and Pilfer are comfortable there, but I think I could use the extra padding.” Oghma winced.

Elec nodded. They sat in silence a while, two friends who, though they came from wildly differing backgrounds, were able to fill a silence together without discomfort. Semaj and Pilfer were friends of theirs also, though these two humans felt more comfortable in each other’s company, as Oghma and Elec did with each other. Oghma and Elec were the “strangers” in Five Oaks, as dwarves and, to a lesser extent elves were uncommon even in this part of the Gnarley Forest, which probably explained their initial friendship. Semaj and Pilfer rounded out their group, but they had been in Five Oaks already, preparing a house for the four adventurers.

Oghma turned to his right and removed the oilskin cover from his harp, which he had rested on the bench next to him. “Perhaps I still have a little left in me for a lullaby.”

Oghma struck a few chords to check the tuning on his harp. He winked at Elec as he turned a few pegs to get his harp in tune. “Let’s see if this innkeeper favors us entertainers.”

He began to softly play, his fingers a bit stiff and tentative at first, then warming up to his task. The soft notes of an old lullaby gradually increased in volume until it was a gentle undercurrent in the room. Common room sleepers quieted down to listen and the talking became a low murmur. Instead of singing the lyrics, Oghma hummed along, using his deep bass resonance to calm and soothe his listeners.

Elec relaxed back into the booth and poured another mug of the red wine. He had been raised in an elvish village, full of laughter, gaiety, and singing. Elvish music was light, soft, like rain falling on leaves, or birdsong. He had never even suspected there could be dwarven bards, thinking them too serious, too full of the craftsman’s ethic and the love of battle to be skilled in song. The few months he had known Oghma had shown him a new appreciation of dwarves, their dry humor, their deep love of stone and metal. Oghma’s music was, admittedly, sometimes ponderous - but there was a strength of purpose like the deep sighs of mountains which moved the listener like nothing he had ever heard before.

Oghma began a second lullaby, this one a tune Elec had heard as child. Elec looked over at Oghma in surprise and saw him grinning. The music cascaded from Oghma’s fingertips, the lighter elvish melody slowed and deepened, with dwarven harmonics thrown over the melody. The music seemed more soothing and at the same time sadder, as if it was taking the sprightly melody and turning it into a more meaningful sort of dance. It fit Elec’s mood almost exactly. Elec smiled in acknowledgement and shook his head as if to clear his thoughts when the song ended.

Oghma waved to the innkeeper as he walked by with some spare blankets. “Do you have a spare room upstairs for a tired bard?” Oghma smiled and struck a playful chord.

The innkeeper smiled and reached his hand from the pile of blankets. “Rhenn, at your service,” he said tiredly. “I do have a spare room. Would you honor me and stay a few nights? You’ll get the room free with meals for some entertainment for my patrons?” His sentence ended on a questioning note, and Oghma smiled and nodded in response.

“I’ll play a little longer, quietly, then head upstairs,” Oghma said.

“Third door on the right,” said Rhenn, and walked off with the blankets to distribute them to the common room patrons.

After a few more songs Oghma nodded at Elec. Elec sketched a wave with a yawn and went to sit down next to Semaj and Pilfer. Oghma headed up the stairs and was asleep as soon as he had taken off his boots.

Chapter 1

Oghma awoke suddenly. With a sudden sense of apprehension, he quietly rolled off the right side of the bed. Looking into the unlit room with his darkvision he could see that the window, closed when he entered the room, was now open. Creeping around the bed to get a clearer look at the room, he could see a huddled form below the windowsill.

“Who are you?” he whispered. “What do you want?” There was no reply. Oghma moved carefully toward the figure, which he could see was a human male of middle age. As he came closer he realized the man wasn’t reacting, wasn’t even… breathing. He rushed to the window to look outside, and saw a figure running towards the tree line, already about two hundred yards away and moving quickly.

“Damn!” Oghma cursed. If he were fleet of foot like Elec, or had Elec’s skill with a bow, perhaps he could chase down the figure. He looked down at the man and saw that he’d had a dagger shoved through his back and had been leaned up or had fallen against the wall below the window.

Oghma set himself to seeing if he could bind the man’s wounds, but it was quickly apparent that although strongly built and hardy, the man had suffered a mortal wound.

Oghma quickly went to the door and listened. Hearing no one, he crept quietly down the darkened hall of the slumbering inn. Arriving at the foot of the stairs, he looked across the common room. In the flickering light of a single oil lamp, he saw that his nearest companion, Pilfer, was about four sleeping bodies away, across the floor.

Carefully tiptoeing over the sleeping travelers, he shook Pilfer’s shoulder and, when he woke, made shushing gestures and pointed to the other two sleeping friends. “Bring them upstairs,” Oghma breathed quietly.

Pilfer nodded and gathered himself together. Oghma tiptoed back across the sleeping people and waited for Pilfer to wake Elec and Semaj and waved them upstairs.

When in the room, Oghma told the group what had happened and Elec, a cleric of Fharlanghn, god of wanderers, checked the man’s body.

“Looks like he didn’t even see it coming,” said Elec softly.

Pilfer’s eyes glanced from side to side nervously, and his feet began the quick tap-dance of imminent flight. “We didn’t do it! We need to get rid of the body fast so we don’t get caught!”

Semaj grunted. “Relax, Pilf - running away will make us look guilty. Besides, we don’t know who he is or why he’s here.”

Pilfer began to rummage through the dead man’s clothing, checking pockets and pouches. After finding assorted coins and a nice-looking dagger, he pulled out a crumpled note from an inside pocket.

“Leralth - the Staff of Incabulos - Skorane”, he read aloud.

“Incabulos? God of disease?” Elec shuddered. “I don’t know what the staff does, but it sounds pretty bad.”

Oghma nodded. “There is an old legend about the staff of Incabulos, but it doesn’t have a lot of detail. It was created in the Greyhawk Wars, at a point when his clergy was at it’s strongest. Supposedly it contains rot that actually dropped from the god’s body when the High Priest summoned him. The clergy was defeated later in the Battle of Garnor Ford and no one knows what happened to the staff after that.”

“So what does it do?” Semaj asked.

Elec shrugged worriedly. “No one really knows, but any item consecrated to Incabulos would deal with increasing his power on Oerth. Since Incabulos is god of disease, it could be anything from a simple staff that causes disease with a touch or that can control disease causing undead to something more insidious, like something that can increase incidence of disease over a large area, like a city.

“Even worse, it could do all of that. Cause disease, create and control diseased undead, and create a large area where disease overwhelms the population’s capacity to deal with it. Once that happens, disease would spread quickly throughout the city. Enough disease in an area like that could create a nexus point to bring Incabulos to Oerth.”

The thought of such a horrible artifact loose momentarily left the party without words.

Oghma was the first to break the silence. “Look, I’m going down to rouse the Rhenn the innkeeper, so this gets taken care of before the village awakens. Hopefully he’ll believe me when I say we had nothing to do with his death. Maybe he’ll be able to tell us who this dead man is, too.”
