ISSUE # 104

December 11th - December 17th


Favorite Stuff - Oghma


I really don't want to write this. I've been avoiding it all week, and it's now Friday, and I've edited everything I have to, including everyone else's essays, and dang it, I've got to produce. So, without further ado, or whining on my part, here is "Wut I have to say".

Ok, here's some ado anyway. The reason I don't want to write this is that I can't possibly encompass two years of my life around EQ in a single essay. I can't thank all the people that need thanking, I can't praise my co-workers enough, and - I'm lazy. To be done properly, this essay should be much longer than it is.

I've decided that it is too difficult to pick "one" favorite of each of our staff members, so I'm going to point out what I like best about their work.

Archanalia's touch with a simile and a good indicator of his occasionally sarcastic humor are his Mig Hammerer stories. I am always amazed at Arch's display of creativity across many fields, artistic and literary.
The Albino Rhino.
The Wounded Oak.
The Death of P. K. Costly.
The Grassy Gnoll.
I also love his wallpapers, and he did almost all the work for the Girls of Norrath Calendar.

Razor often wrote things that had nothing to do with EQ but were entertaining anyway. His look at computer newbiehood brought back fond and not-so fond memories of that first day I unwrapped the new PC. No Experience Required.

Indicative of Ian Ash's deft talent with fiction, this short story is like many of his - good characterizations, dialog, and an interesting plot. Ian has a wide range of depth and talent, and I wish I saw more from him every week. Kip Goodleaf.

I'd seen Onnie's thoughtful, funny, and the occasionally insane posts on the forum.everquest newsgroup, and thought she would be a great addition to the staff. I was surprised, though, at the quality and depth she showed in her first major contribution as a full-blown staff member: Nalixa's journal http://www.eqx.simplenet.com/Issue100/nalixa.htm . What really endeared her to me was the sentiment behind her earlier contribution Oh Where, Oh Where, has the role-playing gone?

I miss Wynn, and his broad knowledge of beta-testing and general game-playing. His Beta-testing 101 was a timely reminder that we as testers needed to go beyond playing and hammer at the game like we wanted it to fail.

As far as my contributions - I still think that my first submission isn't that bad! Roleplaying Outside the Lines. I hope that it feels that way for you, too, because this was a real act of creative courage for me. I'd often wanted to write, and had of course written school papers, but my EQX writings made me very apprehensive, because I wasn't too sure that what was in my head would come out right on paper. My all-time favorite, and the one I still get e-mail about, was originally published for the EQ Vault, and republished for Issue 75: The Romance of Role-Palying. As for my editorials, I can't churn them out like Absor, but I have a few favorites, Issue 100, Issue 67, Issue 44, Issue 88... Heck, just read all of em.

I have so much fun, and Absor mentioned it in his essay, just being part of our staff meetings. If you want a good, roll-on-the-floor-laughing-til-your-eyes-blur-and-can't-see-the-screen time, come to one of our staff meetings. Absor picked a good example to show what idiots we can be with just text and a chat room, so please take a look at his essay.

And, I must really praise Absor. He has done all the html work for this since day one, and has done it every week for two years. All hail Absor! Not only is he the grunt behind the newspaper look of EQX, but he also write editorials that are always thoughtful and while we disagree at times, I always respect where he is coming from with them.

In conclusion, I just want to say that you really should look through our back issues. An incredible amount of effort and creativity is buried in these pages, and I just got the chance to look through some things that have been done and I am astounded at the quality of the work done. We've fairly well chronicled the entire EQ experience, from pre-beta to the present, and I hope we stick together as the EQ community matures further.
