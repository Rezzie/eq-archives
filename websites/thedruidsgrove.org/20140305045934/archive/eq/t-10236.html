Yeah, the <a href="http://hermanmiller.com/CDA/SSA/Product/0,1592,a10-c440-p8,00.html">Aeron</a> chairs have gotten really popular in the last few years, you probably see them a lot in various offices. However, they're not cheap, they usually run around $500-700 easy. Used, they're still usually over $400. I <i>would not</i> suggest any of the knock-offs that are made of this chair. They usually skimp on the mesh bindings, so your chairs will start to fray like wicker.

However, if you're like me, and you're a cheap bastard, I went into places like Best Buy and CompUSA/OfficeMax/OfficeDepot/Staples for my $100 or less chair. Staples and the other various office stores usually have <a href="http://www.staples.com/Catalog/Browse/Sku.asp?PageType=1&Sku=451585">this chair</a> or some variant on sale for $40 or less. Don't expect Italian leather or anything like that, but it usually serves it's purpose.

When you go looking for a chair, I suggest making a list of features you want, like I did. For my chair, I had to have:
- Adjustable arm rests (move up and down)
- Adjustable seat height
- Adjustable seat position (ie: I wanted one that rocked back and forth, not just locked)

Cloth or leather didn't matter with me, as long as it wasn't, you know... hot pink or something. One thing to check out - where ever the wheels go - make sure that part is sturdy (metal if you can find it). The sockets that hold the casters in place are the part most likely to die first.

I found a good little gray fabric chair at Best Buy for $45 with everything I wanted. Lasted me a good five years before I accidentally ripped the seat open while opening a package :(

11-16-2004, 09:37 AM
Oh, and one more thing - when buying a cheap chair like I did, make sure that the seat is padded well. On one of the cheap chairs they had at OfficeMax, you could feel the screw that held the seat onto the lifter through the cushion. But heck, if it's cheap enough for you to suffer through that, you could always buy the chair and then get an additional seat cushion.

I lied, couple other things -
- If you have back problems, prepare to spend more. Chairs with real lumbar support are usually expensive (jacking up the price over $100 easy).
- Figure out how much space you have to use your chair. Don't get a gigantic full sized chair when you only have a 2x2 area.
- Full sized or half size? Do you need a chair that you rest your head on, or is one that only comes up to your mid-back fine (I like mid-backs, I hate full sized ones).

A little story.

I manage a power plant. This included setting up all aspects of a new organization, including buying office furniture. The furniture included the usual desks, tables, chairs, filing cabinets and shelves.

The power plant has a control room that is manned 24 hours per day.

By far the most expensive piece of furniture in the plant is the chair used by the control room operator. It cost $1,500. From a business perspective, comfort, functionality and back support for the employee doing the most critical job in the plant is absolutely essential.

If you use a computer chair a lot, you shouldn't scrimp on the cost. (No, you don't need to spend $1,500 --- Lol). With the possible exception of a bed mattress, a much-used ergonomic chair is the most important piece of furniture you will buy.
