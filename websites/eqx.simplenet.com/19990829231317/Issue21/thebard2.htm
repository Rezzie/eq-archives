The Bard

The god Lugh, in the early Mythological Cycle tales of the Celts, was not allowed to rule them, though he was clearly the best ruler, because his hand had been struck off. It was not until Dian Cecht, the healer of the Gods, grew him a new hand that he could take his throne.

During battles, Bards were charged with recording the deeds of heroes, and would watch the battle in order to later recount great feats of arms and might at the feasting halls. Bards could elevate the stature of an individual in society with their praise, another reason why Bards were held inviolate. 		Bards were cultural figures closer to priests than to entertainers, and were allowed to pass freely for kingdom to kingdom, even between kingdoms at war. A Bard’s position was politically neutral, and he could not take sides in a dispute. His task was to discover and expose truth, and enlighten others to it with his power over language. The Celts believed that there were three magical melodies: the song of healing, the song of joy, and the song of sorrow. The bards semi-magical reputation comes both from the three melodies and their mastery of the power of language. Who a Bard is in Celtic culture also depends on which area he was from. Irish and Welsh Bards have many similarities, but as history affected the different areas settled by the Celts, the traditions and position of the Bard changed. The Irish term "bard" actually refers to the lowest rank of poet, one who had not completed his studies. The "Filidh", or seer, was one rank higher, and was in turn out-ranked by the "Ollave" or master-poet.
