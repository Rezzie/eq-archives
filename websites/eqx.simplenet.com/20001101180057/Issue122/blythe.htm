ISSUE # 122

 April 29th - May 5th, 2000


Blythe

Blythe rubbed his hands together quickly, trying to stay warm. A small fire darted in front of him, the flames rising and falling with the beat of his heart. He looked into its depths. It reminded him of the fire inside of him, the fire that had caused him to run away to this forgotten land. He could still feel the hands of his father on his chest. Squeezing the life from him. Only a slight whisper from his mother had brought the man’s anger to a close. Blythe had never imagined that the news of his marriage to Hena would bring this. She was such a frail flower, dancing in the corners of his mind. His father had cried at first. He figured it was from utter shock that the strongest man of the elven lands would become like a child. Blythe had looked at him with the deepest concern.

“Don’t be sad to see me go pa... we won’t be leaving for long... our honey moon should only last a few days.”

Blythe saw the color of his father’s face fall from the purest white to the red of the inferno blazing before him.

Blythe fumbled with the choker around his neck. The only thing he had to remember Hena. He clutched its emeralds, feeling every contour of the gems. A tear fell down his face and the fire died. He sat on the barren earth in darkness, not caring what befell him. His life’s desire was over.

“How dare you disrupt our family, Blythe! Let your girl know that she is never welcome in your prescence!” said his father, as Blythe reeled back in a stupor. Blythe spun on his heels and fled from the room. He leapt off the top of the stairs to the forest floor. He remembered feeling no pain whatsoever. Blythe dashed across the forest to the other edge. The slums of the high people. He drew the cloak of fur around him as he ran, so no one could report the obvious to his father. As he finally reached the door to her dwelling just above the earth, he sat down to think about the best way to approach the situation. I will tell her the whole thing is off, he thought. He caught his breath and felt a strong determination and courage in his breast. He rapped upon the door five times.

Blythe awoke in the morning to the call of a wild bird. It sounded close. He felt for the bow and his quiver of arrows upon the ground in the hours just before the appearance of the sun. Grabbing hold of them, he climbed to the top of the nearest tree. He could see the bird, it was slightly bigger than a raven. He would take it down in one shot to the head. He felt the direction of the steady wind and aimed slightly to the left of the bird. He fired, with a silenced twang. The bird gave a shrill cry of terror as the arrow was sent flying into its beak. Blythe hurled his quiver and bow to the ground in anger. He was a failed hunter. Not to mention a failed husband.

Hena answered the door, without a second thought. Rapping upon the door five times was their secret custom for entry. He smiled as she appeared in the doorway in her nightgown. “You awoke me, Blythe.”

He smiled despite himself. “I came to keep you company in the darkness of this day.”

“This darkness would never touch me.” She poked him. “There’s no reason to fear for me.”

“Well invite me in at least. Maybe I need protection.”

Hena laughed. “Imagine a meathead like you needing protection.”

He looked her deeply in the eyes and suddenly his courage failed him. He turned his back to her so suddenly it put out the candle that she held. He was choking on tears.

She laughed again. “Very funny Blythe. Wheres the strong man I know so well?”

“His father has killed his hopes and desires, Hena.” He paused for several seconds. He could feel her hand shaking on his shoulder. He felt the need to reach out and hold that hand. He knew it would only delay the inevitable.

“He has said that you are not welcome in his prescence...” he said, turning back around to face her.

She looked confused. “But...”

He held a finger to her lips. “We will run away together. There is more to the world than this dreamless place that we live in.”

“Blythe... I will go anywhere with you.” She said, tears running down her face.

“We shall leave on the morrow then.” He said as he disappeared into the darkness.

She blew a kiss to the night which held the man she loved.

Blythe sat in the same tree for three days before his need for drink overwhelmed his depression. He shifted his weight slightly and came crashing to the ground. He felt sore everywhere, but he felt no cuts when he mustered the energy to stand up on weakened legs. He put his head to the ground and began to sniff for a supply of water. He found a faint trace of it westward and dragged his body toward it.

Blythe slept the rest of the night in his own bed when he got back from Hena’s house. He prayed that his father had not noticed him slip out. Sure enough, his father was fast asleep in the bed next to his own. He quickly fell into deep dreams where his love for Hena overwhelmed all of his problems, past and future.

When Blythe finally reached the water he scooped his hands down into it and splashed it upon his face. The cool refreshing sensation made him jump into the water, regardless of his safety. He drank the water that surrounded him and let the current carry him to wherever it felt like taking him.

After several hours he heard a churning of water and looked about. He climbed out of the water quickly and examined the strange contraption that had almost taken his head off.

“Who goes there?” came a fierce voice.

Blythe spun around. “A weary traveler that has come from far reaches of this world.” He found himself facing a man half his own height. A gnome.

The gnome looked at him inquiringly. “I haven’t seen an elf since my return from your fine city several years ago.”

“I never appreciated its beauty. Probably because I have never left its confines before. What is that machine there?”

“Its a hydropower generator I use to fuel my flying machine.” The gnome smiled.

Blythe gaped at him. “Wow.” Was all he could utter.

The gnome smiled. “Didn’t catch your name by the way. My name is Pinter the Second. After the great adventurer of old.”

“Blythe,” Blythe said as he attempted to take in his surroundings. He was in some kind of outdoor workshop. “You don’t practice magic from Pinter’s time do you?”

Pinter the Second laughed. “No. I’m a mere tinkerer looking to better our lives here with new machines. You are welcome to stay here as long as you like if you will help me with the daily chores.”

Blythe nodded and grinned. “I will stay here as long as you will let me. This place is amazing.”

Pinter lead him to the sleeping quarters which were dug into the earth. The beds were too small for Blythe and he said he would sleep outside. Blythe smiled to himself as he looked at the new window of opportunity he faced.

When Blythe awoke, he smiled as he recalled his dreams of Hena. He jumped up in bed, ready to start the day anew again.

A knock came upon his door. “Come in,” Blythe called.

His mother appeared. Her face looked grave. “Your father is at Hena’s. He saw you come in last night.” She looked at him sadly. “He told me not to tell you of his actions with threat of death. Your father has gone insane with any thought of saving Hena. I would leave now if I were you.”

The feeling of agony rushed up inside of his body and he barely managed to hold the anger toward his mother for not waking him sooner. He rushed past her and flew down the steps and was before Hena’s door in five minutes flat. He threw the door open. Hena lay upon the floor, blood still flowing from her wrists. He looked at her in silence and removed the choker from her bed, placing it around his own neck. He failed to see a note upon the table. The note read:

Treasure your time on this land, Blythe. I can’t leave. This is my home. Listen to your father and don’t be angry. There is nothing either of us could have done to stop this. I will love you forever.

Hena

Blythe’s hands shook. He raced out the door in a fierce rage. He could smell his father. He was at home.

Over the first few weeks at Pinter the Second’s workshop, Blythe proved himself willing to take on more than his share of chores, often helping Pinter with his own. He organized the wheels quickly and correctly. He was ten times the cook Pinter was. The list went on.

One day as he was watching Pinter fiddle with a hydro powered light, he asked, “Is the magic of Pinter the Adventurer truly dead? It is said that it lives on in the city of Yugan.”

Pinter shook his head. “That is the stuff of dreams, boy. It is merely a legend.”

“Can I try out some of these machines Pinter? I promise I won’t damage them.”

Pinter thought for a moment. “Everything but the light. It isn’t ready yet.”

Pinter watched for several hours as Blythe dug tunnels through the Earth, looking for metals to be used for the wheels and connectors. When Pinter suddenly seemed tired, Blythe recommended that he should get some sleep.

Pinter nodded. “Don’t take the flying machine out. I’ll see you in the morning.”

Blythe kept riding around in the mineral searcher til he was certain Pinter was asleep.

“I will find you, Hena.” He whispered to himself. A crazed glassy look had enveloped his eyes. He crept quietly to the flying machine. Pinter had posted a warning sign on the side of it. “This machine is totally unsafe and may crash at unknown intervals.”

Blythe sat down and began to pedal quickly. The flying machine quickly took off, and Blythe found himself rising quickly. He looked into the sun. “I am coming, Hena! He shouted toward it. He grasped the choker around his neck. Suddenly, the flying machine began to sputter and one of the blades of the propellor broke off and fell to the ground far below. “I am coming to join you Hena.” He whispered. He tossed the choker toward the sun and watched as it disappeared in the distance as he fell downward, back to the earth.
