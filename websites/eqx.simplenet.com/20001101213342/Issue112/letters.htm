I read Absor's editorial in issue 110, and as I read through the letter in the editorial, tears came to my eyes. I have a friend who had Hodgkins and my grandfather died of stomach cancer before I was born, so I can almost imagine I can relate to this man's story. I an relate to it slightly because of my own use of Everquest. I believe that people use games, books, and movies to escape from the reality of their own lives. They might do it to escape from either the bad reality of their lives, such as the person who wrote that letter, or simply from the dull monotony. I don't know about other people, but when I play EQ for a few moments I AM Udias the elven druid or Beldarus the barbarian warrior. Its an escapee from the aspects of life we don't like, and it's also a way to reach out and make friends. Every time someone does something nice for me in EQ, I somehow remember their names and I have a great longing to see them again. This isn't anything like the random chattering of chat rooms. This is a whole other existence in a way. Everquest is truly a great thing for people who have problems. It won't make cancer go away or make a cripple walk, but it can do directly what walking and health do indirectly: it can make someone happy. I think that for any entertainer this is the greatest goal, a kind of mission. Happiness. Thanks for all the fun.

-Matt Richardson
(aka Beldarus, Udias, Ranyc, and Euaemon)
