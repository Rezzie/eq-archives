Norrathian Etiquette Revisited

This sure feels like a cheap way to write an article. Ask people to give their opinions and then fill the article with 'em. Hardly any work for me. But I'll do my best to be witty and rational, and to try to fill my word quotient for the week.

In issue 87 I described and experience, a bad one, in which a player broke several 'rules of etiquette'. It occurred to me near the end of the article (no, I don't plan most of the things I write, I just type and see what comes out - that's why I'm not getting paid to do this) that I probably have a different set of rules than the rest of you do. And it's not fair to hold someone to rules unless they are either generally agreed upon or are just plain common sense. So I asked folks to send in any rules that they live buy while playing EverQuest.

I was going to categorize the responses and sort them all into neat little packages, but I'm pretty lazy. There are a lot of letter, so this article is pretty long. If you're in some sort of big rush, then you can skip to the bottom and see my summary, a cute little list of etiquette. Each of you seemed to have very thematic responses, indicating that you see a specific type of thing that bothers you a lot, or have a pet peeve (more on that later). For example, Galedor sent me the following (and I was kind enough to insert my comments):
  • If a group of people are shouting an argument across a zone, and you don't want to listen to it, simply turn off shouts/ooc or leave the soon. Shouting at people to keep it to tells (or even to keep it to ooc) simply does not work. Your best bet is to ignore it.
  • If there are only two people arguing about something via shouts/ooc, ask politely for them to use a more private means of communication. If this does not work, remember that you can easily ignore them.

To me it's all a matter of what they are talking about. First off, I believe anything goes in /ooc, as long as it doesn't break any of the common sense rules about speaking in public, such as vulgarity. I see no reason to tell people to stop using /ooc, it's sort of the catch-all channel. Though I do think it's far more polite to take a one-on-one out of character conversation to /tell. I really don't even see a problem with characters using /shout to hold conversations, after all they are yelling to each other, and taking a yelling match to /tell is out of character. Though it is also perfectly acceptable for someone to /shout at them to shut up. Again, as long as it's all in character.

Expecting anyone to turn off /shout or /ooc just to avoid a conversation isn't right. If the /shouters or /oocers are being vulgar or /shouting out of character, then sending them a /tell asking them to stop is reasonable, after all they are breaking rules of common sense.

By far your best suggestion is to ignore it. I have /ooc and /auction turned off, because I'm not interested in out of character conversation. I use /shout liberally because I am arrogant enough to think that I might entertain and bring some life to those around me (unless I'm playing my Tier'dal, then the reverse is true, bringing Hate and death to those around me). I've gotten pretty good at ignoring the occasional out of character /shout.

It's also a good idea to make your first assumption a good one. So if someone is calling you names, it's likely that they are talking to your character, not you. And even if they are, stay in character and respond. Keep it in character and you'll enjoy playing a lot more.
  • Do not shout for SoW or buffs. As a shaman, I refuse to cast SoW on anyone who shouts or simply asks around without seeing who is about. If you want an SoW, check the /who listing for either a shaman or a druid (or even a ranger if they're old enough), and ask them privately.

Or something like that. I agree, all this shouting about SoW bugs me. Two of my pet peeves in one annoying yell. First, I don't really like abbreviations when talking in a role-playing game. It's lazy and it's not likely that your character actually yells "S. o. W." The other peeve is, well, how many fifth level characters would already know that there is such a spell, much less that it's called Spirit of the Wolf?

Well, I agree and I disagree. I certainly agree that /ooc is not the place for auctions. However, I will only sell things with /shout. Obviously because I have /auction turned off. But to me /shout is a perfectly good thing to do if you want people to hear that you have something to sell.

How about Absor's Guide to Etiquette in Norrath.

First the four General Principles of EverQuest Etiquette:

  1. Respect your fellow player. Behind every player character in Norrath is a real person. And people deserve respect. Your Fier'Dal Paladin will respect that Teir'Dal Cleric, but you should respect the Cleric's player, even if that requires a quick /tell to them to explain "Hi, nice to meet you. I'm only role-playing, don't take this personally."
  2. Stay Calm. Don't accuse. Don't yell. Sure, it might make you feel better in the short term, but it doesn't help the situation. Those that are breaking etiquette on purpose (or without concern) won'' care how mad you get. Some thrive on making people angry. And someone that didn't know that he was being rude will respond far better to a kind explanation then to yelling. Remember, this game really is a community.
  3. Assume the best. People make mistakes. Some people see things differently. And sometimes it's possible that both of you are right. If you allow your first impression to be a good one you will enjoy the game a lot more. And because of you're having fun and being positive, so will others.
  4. Some things are never 'in-character'. It may seem in character for a Troll Shadowknight to stir up all the denizens of Unrest and drag them around, causing the deaths of dozens of lightbringers and happy Bards. It's not. Not because it's not something that the character would do, maybe he would. But because it angers the other players, not their characters. In a way it's an abuse of the switch. The good guys that are killed in this way have no way to prevent it, nor to deal with the cause. It's probably not in character for a Paladin to retaliate with the same maneuver. And he can't just kill the Shadowknight unless he will accept a duel. Some things transcend the game and affect the players. Don't do those things and then claim that you were being in character, because you were not trying to piss of the character, you were trying to piss of the player. Of course being a victim of rule 4 should never make you forget rules 1 - 3.

Specific Rules of Etiquette:

Group Etiquette:

When you join a group, you become part of a little society. You really do need to learn enough about them so that you don't break their social customs. When you first join a group it's a good idea to ask a few questions out of character to find out how they do looting and what their goals are. Here are some basics:

  • Loot in the order set up. If there is a looting order, follow it. Don't bother being greedy. People remember, and if they're your peers now, they will likely continue to be your peers. You lose more by angering them in the long run than you do by swiping a few gold from them in the short term.
  • Role-play with your group. If you find yourself in a group that role-plays, even if you generally do not, then do your best to join in. You may find that it's fun. But don't be excruciatingly out of character.
  • Don't role-play with your group. The converse is also true. If you find yourself in a group that is only interested in gaining experience and stuff, then do as they do. Try not to let your role-playing cause a situation that gets them killed. For example, as a bard it is very hard to chat and weave songs. My fingers are pretty busy just keeping the songs up. In my normal groups I'll lose a few spell beats to rip a few good in character jokes. But in a power-gaming group I keep all of my chatter to down times.
  • If unhappy, leave the group. If you are in a group that you don't like, leave. Be polite about it. Explain to them why you feel the need to leave. If you are in a tough spot stay until they can replace you or until you are in a safe spot. And if someone tells you that they are leaving the group and why, don't get mad at them. They are being honest. And you'd rather play with people that share your goals, right?
  • Groups are bigger than just the people you see on the little list on your screen. Apply all of these rules to the larger group, those in the same zone.

Chat Etiquette:

The first thing to do is list the uses of each channel.

/say is for talking to those near you. It is an in character channel.

/shout is for, well, shouting. It spans the entire zone, and is an in character channel.

/ooc spans an entire zone. It is an out of character channel

/auction spans the entire zone. It is only for selling items.

/tell is to a specific person. It's use as out of character or in character is entirely up to the two people involved. Reaches anyplace in Norrath.

/gsay conveys messages only to your group. It can be used as in character or out of character chat.

/guildsay conveys messages to your entire guild. It can be used as in character or out of character chat.

First let me comment about the channels that can be either in or out of character. To me, and to most of the people I know, /tell and /gsay are always out of character channels. It's just not to easy to explain how you chat silently with one person anywhere in the world, or how you chat to only these four people while standing among a few dozen. Though one person I know that will emote /em whispers to Absor and then use a /tell. Inventive and a nice way of putting /tell to use for us role-players.

  • No vulgarity. All of us can survive the occasional curse word. And if you're with a group that knows you, curse all you want over /gsay. But be brave and learn how to verbally abuse someone without resorting to vulgarity, it's an art form that is under appreciated in EverQuest.
  • Try to consider others when chatting. When using /shout remember that everyone in the zone hears you. While I don't think that all one on one conversations have to be done in /tell, if you really seem to be bothering people with your shouting, stop.
  • Don't chat out of character on in character channels.
  • Don't spam, even if it's in character.
  • Don't get upset. Remember your /ignore feature.

Combat Etiquette:

Try to keep in mind that combat can be very hectic. Stuff happens in a battle that is hard to sort out at the time, or even later. Keep calm…

  • Ask first. Ask if someone wants help before jumping in. And ask for help if you need it, don't expect it.
  • Observe spawn camping order. As much as I hate that it has come to such a thing (and I'll leave before getting involved in such a thing), respect the order. Get in line with the rest. Just like in group situations, being rude for a small gain now isn't worth the trouble and hard feelings it causes later.
  • Don't steal kills. Don't do it. But don't assume that everyone that attacks your monster is trying to steal it. With lag and poor framerates it is certainly possible that you both attacked it first.
  • Trains happen. Most of the time they are due to poor planning, unexpected events, and just plain bad luck. Don't get mad about them. But try your best not to cause them. And scream like a little girl when you find a few dozen enemies beating on you as you flee. Give warning and you might get the help you need to make it to the zone.

Social Etiquette:

I know, a bit repetitive, but what can I say. Being social should require that you understand and obey the rules of etiquette for the given situation. This is sort of my catch-all category.

  • Don't inspect people without asking. Even though I personally see nothing wrong with it, I also know that it upsets some people. So don't do it. This is one of those cases of upsetting the player and not the character.
  • Don't Spam. There are all sorts of spam in EverQuest. Repeated spell casting, repeating chat messages, repeated actions that give emotes (like drinking), and I'm sure others that I've forgotten. In some cases it's a good thing. Like when my Dorf pals and I get together, we make it a point to let people know that we spend all of our cash on alcohol. But it you are going to do something repeatedly (like casting for practice), move to an isolated location for the sake of those around you. And, no matter how important your point is to you, nobody wants to see it over and over again.
  • Don't stand so close to me. Oddly enough even in EverQuest we seem to have a concept of personal space. So if you can help it, don't stand on top of people. Give them an arm's length or more.

Enough of this prattle. I suspect this will be a case of preaching to the initiated. Those that are social will nod their heads and agree (with perhaps a few exceptions). Those that like to rattle the cage or that are more interested in expedience over etiquette just won't care. But I feel better for doing this, and hopefully I've helped out some.
