ISSUE # 129

 June 17th - June 23rd, 2000

RPing Evil in a MMORPG

I spent my time in EverQuest playing a Dark Elven Cleric of Innoruuk. Aside from the usual wrangling with stats, trying to train the right skills and looking for the next best hunting ground, I tried to play the role as well. I’ve always enjoyed Rping the bad guy, maybe that’s one reason I prefer to be the Game Master for pen and paper games as opposed to a player. Online, in various MUDs and a few other large scale commercial games, I’ve tried to take on the role of the antagonist. Let everyone else pursue the well lit paths of the hero, I’ll be that occasional cloud that darkens their travels. However, it rarely worked as well as I hoped it would.

Being a GM for a pen and paper game and playing the antagonist offers some similarities to Rping evil. I provide a challenge for my players, keep them on their toes and generally make their fictional lives more interesting. There is a certain understanding that exists between the players and the GM and expectations on both sides. The GM provides the setting, the obstacles and even assistance in times of need… anything that keeps the story interesting and furthers the players enjoyment. The GM tries his or her best to find a pace and level of challenge the players are comfortable with. The players do their best to overcome these obstacles and, further, do their best to just relax and have fun. If they are at each other’s throats it is hopefully in the spirit of roleplaying their characters and not any true anger toward each other.

A single GM for a small group of players provides direction. A GM in a pen and paper game sets the pace of the story and in a sense arbitrates the outcome of certain actions. Playing the obstacle, playing the antagonist, requires judicious thought in order to keep from upsetting your players. I’m reminded of Absor’s recent editorial about the Evil GM syndrome: “The one that delights in the suffering of his players. The one that will rarely kill a character, but will gladly maim them because maiming is far more brutal and doesn’t allow you to start a new character. He gives us stuff only because he plans to take it away the moment that we get attached to it.” Any GM that does this will find they will start rapidly losing players. Not many players want to play a game under an egomaniacal power-mad GM.

In a MMORPG there is no such restraint and guidance. This puts some serious responsibility on the shoulders of the players. When people are roleplaying they need to act in a responsible manner that is respectable to other players, just as a GM has to be respectable to those he is running a game for, in the interest of the overall group. Everyone is there to have fun and everyone’s role should add to the enjoyment and depth of the overall setting. If everyone is afflicted with something akin to the “Evil GM” syndrome, things generally degenerate to a simple first person shooter style frag-fest. Powerful players camp newbie grounds, personal harassment becomes rampant, and players begin to do anything to get ‘an edge’ over the competition. In such a point and click world of death, complex, interesting roles are quickly forgotten and players take to simply being competitive game players. The game becomes only a shadow of an RPG.

A point to note: Being nice to a player and nice to a character are two completely different things. The plight of those roleplaying evil is plagued by those claiming to be roleplaying evil who are truly just grief players in disguise. These players constantly appear in online games and ruin the role of antagonist in an attempt to find an excuse for their abhorrent and completely non-roleplayed behavior. Roleplaying evil means you are acting as an evil character in that particular setting would act. Doing things such as exploiting bugs to your benefit, killing characters indiscriminately without any attempt to add something to the community story, or doing things which interfere with normal game play has nothing to do with roleplaying evil. That’s simply being evil or amoral as a player and a person.

Of course, in-character conflict isn’t a bad thing. It’s necessary if anyone is to claim to be roleplaying an evil character. But I think players themselves (and thus their characters) must be given the tools to deal with such a menace. Given the ability to deal with the threats each other presents…something in addition to hunting down transgressors in packs. The biggest problem, in my opinion, with players RPing the full spectrum of “evil” and “good” has been the failure of any large scale online world to allow itself to be regulated by those very players. Developers prefer, it seems, creating special “code” to deal with anarchy in their utopias or relying on mostly unorganized (through no fault of their own) and overworked volunteers. These offer up new problems. Many “solutions” are indiscriminate in who they effect. Those who choose to roleplay evil are hit just as hard as the usually intended target: grief players. Further, these solutions exist in a state which is far removed from the setting and any role a player may have selected. Accessing these means a break of fiction. It’s either some omnipotent “customer service rep” to the rescue or the stalwart (and obviously artificial) AI.

The creators of EverQuest took note of the profusion of player slaughter and anarchy in other online systems, I believe. This was the main drive behind one of their solutions, the PK switch. They saw no more efficient way than to simply disallow it through hard code. It works quite well in some cases. Sure, many players will just find other ways to annoy and harass if they choose. However, the sheer body count and the worst loss a player can suffer, that of a well planned character to an inconsiderate player, is definitely kept to a minimum.

But does their world benefit from it? Yes, definitely. But only because of one thing. The focus is shifted on the standard servers from player conflict to player versus the environment. Players are meant to operate as teams in a constant struggle versus the environment. Cooperation in this endeavor is really the only means to play the game. Conflict between characters is simply viewed as anti-social and not benefiting of the way the game is “suppose” to be played. Roleplaying an antagonist is, for all intents, dead and at best anti-social. Even on the Team and unrestricted PK servers of EverQuest, much of the game is one of controlling spawn points and besting the environment, before your enemies can.

This must be very confusing for a first time roleplayer. Take, for instance, someone who wishes to become a Dark Elf. They read the history of the race; a birth from heinous deeds and their race’s founder, the god of hatred upon Norrath. They see signs of aggression with the “good” races in the background story and quests provided around their slightly xenophobic home city of Neriak. But the game itself portrays a different view. All players are united against a common enemy, the environment. Dark Elves and High Elves mingle; Shadowknights, presumed paragons of hatred and evil, risk life and limb to help clerics of good. Anything goes so that the environment, the true antagonist, is overcome. The story however, is now lost and roles simply fall to the wayside.

Roleplaying evil, as I found, in these conditions is a fruitless endeavor and mostly misunderstood. Taunt your Dark Elf brother who just helped a human kill one of Neriak’s dragoons and you are anti-social, lecture a fellow cleric of Innoruuk on sharing her god given (from the god of hate mind you) powers with light races and you are given a lecture in return on not being a racist. There is simply no place in that game for a player trying to roleplay an evil character. In fact, it could be argued that no one is interested in the roles at all, and to the extreme the story as a whole. Players are simply players; their characters simply digital extensions of themselves out to beat the AI into submission in a decidedly OOC way.

This is much the same as I have experienced with many other commercial MMORPGs. Playing a role other than ‘team player versus the digital world’ often gets you branded anti-social in a real world sense. Now, I have had some great success playing evil characters in smaller MUDs. In a smaller community with a more selective player base, it’s much easier to nurture a truly cohesive environment where every player has a part in the overall story. In the big commercial games, story is lost and replaced with automated efficiency that can never truly draw the players in for any length of time. Roleplaying happens in these MMORPGs but only in tiny pockets when the mood suits the players; something which could, sadly, be accomplished in a simple multi-player (non-massive) CRPG behind passworded sessions.

I would love to someday truly take on the role of an evil character in an MMORPG. Now that would be a great game. I’m ready to provide a living, breathing obstacle to the heroes of whatever world it may be. To try to add to a large-scale and intriguing plot which encompasses that entire world and all who set foot on its digital ground. Heroes of that brave world beware, I’ll be there in the shadows of that well lit path.

    Article by Russell Linton
