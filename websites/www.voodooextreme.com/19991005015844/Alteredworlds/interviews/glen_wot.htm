An interview Sacrates conducted with Glen Dahlgren, producer/designer of anxiously awaited title: The Wheel of Time.  Sit back and enjoy this short interview...

Sacrates: This may sound cliche, (Since all other interviews do this it seems) but could you state your name, what you do, how you smell after programming for hours on end, and what you think the advantages of anti-matter power are over Nuclear? (Basically all you have to do is answer your name and what you do...i'll let you off on the others :))

Glen: I'm Glen Dahlgren, the Designer/Producer of The Wheel of Time, a new 3D strategy/action fantasy game using the Unreal engine.

I smell like early morning dew, when it's hit by the first rays of sunlight, as it drips down the back of a sweaty gorilla.

I believe that anti-matter power is very under-rated.  I mean, you don't see anti-matter plants all over the country do you?  And let's face it, if there's an accident, who wants to put up with a generation of three-eyed fish and living out bad nuclear holocaust movies?  I'd much rather the entire planet disappeared in a cool Next-Generation digital effect.


Sacrates: What kind of storytelling method will you be using? In game cinematics
using the game engine or prerendered cutscenes?

Glen: It's not nailed down yet, but I'm planning on showing cutscenes between levels using the game engine--plus a few "cutscene-like" events that will occur in the game itself.


Sacrates:  As we all know, you are using the Unreal engine. So what do you think so far? Is the engine as robust as we hear it is? In regards to this question, (And since WOT will do a lot of outdoor environments) how does it handle with these outdoor environmetal scenes? We haven't really seen much of what it can really do with outdoor stuff yet.

Glen: Although I haven't played with some of the up-and-coming technologies, I firmly believe that Unreal is one of the only engines that could handle what the Wheel of Time requires.  Our art direction and game design demand the versatility that Unreal provides.  Take a look at our latest screen shots to see half of this equation, and keep an eye out for more information about the other.

Actually, Unreal can handle huge outdoor scenes, as long as you're smart about occlusion and creative level design.  I've now seen some outdoor Unreal levels that look fantastic.  The fractal effects applied to water, the lush textures, and the organic feel to the terrain really accomplishes a lot.  Look for some great outdoor scenes in WoT.


Sacrates: Since WOT will feature multiplayer, have you figured out what you will be using for players to connect? Will it be via Gamespy etc? Also in regards to that and the interesting way in which you are making single player, how will we be playing? (Deathmatch, Co-op, etc.)

Glen: This hasn't been nailed down yet.  Part of the answer depends on the final network implementation that Unreal uses, which we're waiting for (although, not for long).  As to how you'll be playing, I have to be vague, but I can say that you'll have the opportunity to engage in elements of both Deathmatch and Co-op.

Sacrates:  And probably your favorite question.....what are the minimum requirements looking like? Are you guys planning on utilizing the Voodoo 2 card etc.

Glen: It certainly wouldn't be a bad idea to snag at least one Voodoo 2 card for this game.  I know *I'm* going to.  However, it's too early to nail down the exact requirements yet.  I think it's a safe bet that e will require at least some form of hardward acceleration.
