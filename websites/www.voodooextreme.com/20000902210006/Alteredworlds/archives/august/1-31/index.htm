Onto the combat portion of things. Each character in the party can have a "script" that dictates how they behave in battle. There are choices for a few of the classes, but I couldn’t spot the "hang back and shoot spells" choice for the wizard in the party. So you set up how the people will behave AHEAD of time because when combat starts you typically don’t know where or why or with who. If you party is moving across the map, suddenly one will yell a battle cry and take off. Sometimes the other people in the party will help and sometimes not. Sometimes they just stand behind other people because they can’t figure out how to get around to attack. This is not Diablo, folks. Clicking like mad does nothing. You pick a target and hope for the best. The reason that they have the complex scripts is because combat is quite confusing. With no "real" way to control the people all you can do is sit back and watch. What doesn’t help is that the trees often will block most of what’s going on if you’re outside. This makes wolf attacks all the more interesting.

BG Part III
Gamesmania has part III of their Baldur's Gate guided tour up. This time they take a look at the combat system. - Bowmeister

When your party enters combat, suddenly things get all Diablo, or WarCraft. Creatures advance on you, and they don't waste any time, so you'd better be ready to respond. Hostile creatures appear with a red circle underneath them, and if you hold your cursor on them, a sword icon will appear. Click the creature, and all selected party members will attack it. You need only do it once...party members will hack on the creature until it's dead or they're dead. They will also pursue it if it flees, or shifts its position.

You can't just click and forget though. If one monster appears, you order your party to attack it, they surround it, and usually the results are a forgone conclusion. But what if there's more than one monster? What if they attack from different directions? You then must quickly select the right characters to attack the threat. You can click portaits or assign them individually, or you drag a box on the screen (like an RTS) to select more than one unit, and order them to attack. It is therefore very important that you have your party in correct formation, and that you know where what characters are, with what weapons and spells. In other words, be ready before combat even starts.

BG Part II
Gamesmania have part II of their Baldur's Gate preview up. This time, they take a look at the story/characters, and the layout of your party of intrepid adventurers. - Bowmeister

Your party can move in formation--and it should too, particularly out in the wilds. One stray arrow striking your character and it's adventure over. Fortunately, you can change the party order. All you have to do is drag and drop the portraits on the left. You will also notice the quick formation bar at the bottom of the screen. The party leader is indicated by a silver dot (the others are gold). You should make a formation where your main character is surrounded by the others--put the party bruisers up front and let them do the arrow catching. You can move in a straight line, single-file, T-formation, circle, circle with party leader in the centre, and so on. It all depends on party size. A smart player will change the formation and party order based on who's hurt, who's got the weakest armor, who's got the best weapons, who's the best shot and who's got the lowest hit points. Novice thief and magic-user characters are particularly vulnerable during the early stages.

BG Multiplayer
Baldur's Gate Chronicles has a hugin' blurb on multiplayer in BG from a news group post by Ray Mazyka. Here's the goods (It's big, but it's all juicy info.) - Bowmeister

I wish we could make them that fast (and have them still be good :) - more likely, mission packs will take about 4-6 months each, so the first one will probably come out about 4 months after the main game, since we started on it last month.

However - with regards to multiplayer - six players per game, max, but you can have any number up to six in any given game (even as few as one, but I explain why this is useful below). Each player controls one or more characters assigned to him or her by the leader of the game.

Here's how it works: the "leader" spawns a new multiplayer game. Anyone with the single player game can create a new multiplayer session (and since the art of the game is stored on five CD's, each player requires a full copy of the game to play). After the multiplayer session is created, players can join in either by logging in a new multiplayer session with the correct IP address (TCP/IP over the Internet) or over a LAN - or else they can connect over the Internet game connect service (free) that we plan to set up. Alternatively, they can connect via a modem or serial cable (we have not implemented these last two yet but plan to over the next month).

The leader controls a lot of things, and we like to make the analogy that the leader is like a DM - someone who ensures that the game runs smoothly, that no one is cheating (unless he or she wants to allow deathmatches or cheating - you never can account for individual preferences!). The sorts of things that the leader/DM controls include: a) what can be brought into the game (in terms of importing characters from the single player game) - either only stats and a level one character (created at the time of the multiplayer session or stripped down from a character in a single player session of a player), or stats and EP/levels, or stats, EP/levels, and items; b) whether players (and specifically which players) can pause the game, initiate dialogue, initiate spending group gold, look at other player's inventories, leave areas, edit characters, etc. The whole point is that no programming can ever control the truly creative minds that game players have - cheating will happen, and only a human DM can moderate it effectively. Our model is the old style pen and paper RPG of AD&D, and we want to recreate the experience of playing AD&D around the kitchen table with a group of friends.

The story in the multiplayer session of Baldur's Gate is not random; rather, it is the same as in the single player game. We chose this for several reasons. First, we wanted the story to be coherent and well-designed, much like a pen and paper module. Second, since there are no tiles in the game, we cannot generate random terrain at all in this game. The DM/leader in BG multiplayer is limited to coordinating the activities of the group - he is the leader of the party, not a DM in the classic definition. Third, the multiplayer game that we wanted to develop is a long term one - we wanted a group of players to keep playing together through a long session over a period of weeks rather than joining up with a new group of strangers ever day - we felt that the group would get to know one another and even begin to roleplay, which was always one of the best parts of playing the pen and paper RPG's of old (at least for the dev team :).

We also decided to implement the ability to play multiplayer with one player only. The way this would work is that one player spawns a new game, but does not listen to "join" requests from other players. In this manner, you can get around the single player restriction of only being able to create one character. If you choose to play this way, you can create some or even all of the party, or even import several favourite characters from single player to begin a new game or a mission pack. We recommend you try some of the NPC's who can join you though - they have great sounds and personalities and will make the game much more entertaining.

If a leader wants to drop out, he can email the save game file to one of the players, who can spawn a multiplayer session and load in that save game file, then wait for everyone to join. If an individual player wants to drop out of the game permanently, he can save his character locally (for use in other multiplayer games), and the DM/leader then can reassign the control of that player's character to another player (including the DM). That player can be deleted or edited by the new controlling character, and a new character could be created or imported (according to the import restrictions imposed by the DM/leader).

There is extendibility in the multiplayer model that we have created - you can plug a mission pack into the main game and either play it single or multiplayer.
