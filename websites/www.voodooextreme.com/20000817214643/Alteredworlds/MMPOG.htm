MMORPGs to go the way of the RTS?
by: Certis

One minute you're looking at three games: Everquest, Asheron's Call and Ultima Online. The next minute it's just like the RTS deluge of a few years back all over again. But this time it's much more dangerous for the unwary online game developer. Warcraft II and Command & Conquer were released and soon followed by legions of piss-poor clones trying to ride the coattails of their success. How many of the games actually succeeded financially? Age of Empires and Total Annihilation come to mind and of course Starcraft and the C&C sequels did well. That leaves dozens of games that bombed horribly. 

Taking what history has taught us, look at what's happening to massively multiplayer online role-playing games (MMORPGs). Ultima Online was sold at retail after a long series of beta testing by the public. At last count, UO had about 150,000 paying subscribers.  Following Ultima Online we have Everquest, a 3D-accelerated monster backed by Sony that currently has over 300,000 players subscribed. Asheron's Call is the newest entry in the race for your online gaming dollar. Backed by Microsoft, AC still has a way to go before catching up to Everquest. All three games charge 10 dollars a month to play in addition to their retail box costs. 

As consumers, we are slowly getting used to paying a monthly fee for a game. In fact, its easy to justify when you consider the time invested and the cost for that time.  

My experience is limited to Everquest so I will use it as an example. I started playing EQ in beta 3. As I became used to the environment and all the commands, the time I invested slowly began rising to the point that an all night session was not unheard of. Three months later my total time spent with my wizard was about 22 days. That's 528 hours. Keep in mind this is only one character. I also acted as a guide for a few months and played other characters as well. 

Knowing that people have spent hundreds of hours with a character in a now familiar environment, do developers really think they can pull gamers from one world to a new one? Granted, as popularity grows, more gamers will likely take the plunge. But there will certainly not be enough to support the number of games in development.  

First, we have Anarchy Online, a MMORPG set 30,000 years into the future. As you can well imagine, there will not be any Orcs or Elves in this one. 

Next we have Horizons: Empires Of Istaria. This will be a fantasy role-playing game in the tradition of Everquest with many different races to choose from. 

Shadowbane has recently secured GoD (Gathering of Developers) as a publisher. Here we have yet another fantasy MMORPG to add to the mix. 

Unknown: Quest for the Golden Dragon is a single and multiplayer game. It should be interesting to see how this works out since the multiplayer game can support approximately 1000 people. At this point in time, they say there will not be a monthly fee to play online. 

Hero's Journey has yet to score a publisher. From what I have read it holds promise but for now we shall have to wait and see.

Middle Earth Online is a wild card since the original development team was axed last fall. Sierra claims the game is still in development. Beyond that we have no info. Lets hope they continue development! 

Star Wars Online is being developed by Verant Interactive, makers of Everquest. Of all the upcoming games I consider this one has the most potential. Verant has learned a lot from Everquest and I expect the marketability of Star Wars will help push it into the mainstream.

At this point, everyone knows my opinion of Origin. Regardless, Ultima Online 2 is currently in development. It will be much more graphically intense than it's predecessor and from early information, leans a bit more towards the gameplay of Everquest and Asheron's Call. 

Last we have Everquest 2. Although it has not been officially announced, I think it is safe to assume Verant is going ahead with development. 

So, that's six upcoming projects with countless others in development that are unannounced. Roughly 600,000 people are currently playing MMORPGs and there could be maybe double that in the next year or so. Considering how much it costs to build and maintain a persistent online world, I doubt there is enough pie at the table for everyone. 

Best of luck to everyone currently working on a MMORPG. With all the competition, you're going to need it.
