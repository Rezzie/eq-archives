Mirage?
Gamer's Alliance pointed me to an up and coming 3D Adventure from Bacchus, Mirage. The game is still in it's VERY early stages, with some screenshots and a technology demo that only runs on the Rendition v2x00 up on their page. They are working on a glide demo though :).  Here's the premise so far. - Bowmeister

In the beginning, there were the One and the Void. Now the Void was formless and empty. And the One said, "Let there be a Gate," and there was the First Gate.

The One looked through the First Gate and saw within a reflection of the Void. The One circled the gate but still saw nothing but empty Void. Curious, the One entered the gate, and the First Gate swallowed and consumed the One.

Thus did the One create the heavens and the earths in his own image. For, when the Gate swallowed the One, the Void within the gate assumed his likeness, becoming the First World and its firmament.

And upon the First World were born the Children of the One, whole and adult, as if they had always been. The Children gazed with wonder at the heavens and saw within the stars the form of the One.

In time, one of the Children, the Son of the Dawn, sought and found the First Gate and saw within the primordial Void from which the One had come. Frightened, he did not enter. However, the Son of the Dawn took a Sherd of the First Gate and brought it back to show the other Children.

Much they marveled at the fragment and the Exotic matter of which it was made. Wonder became jealousy, and the Children fought over the Sherd. Finally, the Son of the Dawn returned to the First Gate and brought back more sherds.

Still, this did not appease the Children, for they had found that from the sherds could be made new, albeit lesser, gates. The Children gazed curiously at these new gates as had the One before, and they poked and prodded at the reflections of the Void within. And the gates swallowed and consumed the prods, as the First Gate had consumed the One. And the Voids assumed the likeness of the prods, forming from their essence new heavens and new earths.

And the Children entered their new worlds, to fill them and subdue them, to rule over the denizens of the seas and the birds in the heavens and over every living creature that moves on the lands.

FF VII Review
Avault has thrown up a review of Eidos' conversion of the playstation classic. I haven't checked it out (well I did ooh and ah over the pretty colors in the fighting demo) but it seems to be getting some rather rave reviews. I dunno, I always found console RPGs very lacking. - Bowmeister

There are two groups of gamers reading this review -- those who have experienced the PlayStation version and those who have not. To be honest, there is no compelling reason for console aficionados to purchase the PC version. Other than enhanced graphics, nothing is different. For PC devotees, this is an important release, despite innumerable technical problems. Nothing like it exists for the computer, and although its approach is dissimilar to traditional PC offerings, it is a genuine work of art. Hard-core purists might confer an upturned nose, but I would argue that gamers who recoil from more cumbersome RPGs will take to this game. The powerful emotional punch of the far-reaching narrative, the engaging character interaction, the fierce battles, and the promise of a fresh, magical experiece might prove alluring, and bring new gamers into the RPG fold. However, there will be those who, for good reason, turn from the game due to graphic anomalies, inferior sounds, and technical dilemmas. The overall score is based on the current state of the game, and for that reason, it falls short of greatness. The game has its brilliance, so the final verdict is up to Eidos and Square Soft.

Mordor II surprise
Well now...looks like we finally get to find out who the distributor of everyones favorite openGL RPG is going to be. None other than... Interplay! Yep, Black Isle Studios, Interplay's RPG division, officially announced the game in a press release today. Check the whole thing here, or dig the condensed version below. - Bowmeister

IRVINE, Calif. -- From Black Isle Studios, the role-playing game (RPG) division of Interplay Productions (NASDAQ:IPLY) comes Mordor II TM, which is in joint development with the Bellevue, Washington-based software company, VB DesignsTM and will be available for Windows® 95 CD-ROM for release in Winter 1998. This epic fantasy role-playing game provides for both single-player or an Internet-based multi-player experience.

The Mordor II gamer can run a Mordor server and be his/her own online or off-line "dungeon master." The more robust the host server, the more gamers can join in. With this feature, RPG'ers can have access to the Mordor universe without the added expense of monthly online fees they have experienced with such games as Ultima Online. Additionally, Mordor II gives RPG'ers their first chance to have the use of the design and editing features action gamers have had access to for years with games like DoomTM and Quake. Interplay and VB Designs also plan to release various game tools and editors that will allow consumers to create and customize their own role-playing game adventure. These will include a map generation tool, a quest scripting language, a monster editor and will even allow the player to change the art in the game. Mordor II will only be limited by the imagination of the gamer.

BG Random Bits
Baldur's Gate Chronicles popped up these tasty "Random Bits" of information.  There's also a blurb that STRONGLY hints at an Online version of BG coming down the pipe. - Bowmeister

The 8x8 area changes were tested off of the CD yesterday. The load time was 25 seconds for the larger areas. (This was on a 32x CDROM though) so the times are quite reasonable. That means that the average user (8x) could expect a maximum wait of 2 minutes between levels.

When you travel from area to area, there is a travel screen with a loadbar. The travel sketches merely enhance the notion of travel between areas while the data for the next area loads..

You only see with infravision when the member of your party with infravision is selected. With infravision, any monsters that you normally wouldn't see because of the fog of war are visible, but tinted red. Once they enter the vicinity of your character, they become visible as usual.

There will be a whisper function so that individual party members (in multi-player) can talk only to eachother without the other party members knowing.

Avaris
Lorien over at Gamer's Alliance posted this bit on an upcoming Anime adventure from Bifrost Creations.   Check the full post here or dig the cut and paste below. - Bowmeister

Avaris is a fantasy RPG being designed and produced by Bifrost Creations and set in the mysterious and volatile kingdom of Valikorae.

Avaris will be presented in a 1st person perspective 3D engine which will occasionally switch to a 3rd person perspective, dynamically moving camera to capture each scene in its most atmospheric light.

Bifrost is attempting to model behavior in a new and innovative way which should facilitate unfolding the story in a non-linear, non-scripted fashion through the computer controlled players. There are currently several people on the team and they are presently in the middle design stage.

Avaris will feature a full 3D engine. The engine will dynamically scale the complexity of models to maximize the graphics potential of your system. The game uses two display engines, one for open outdoor areas and a separate engine for towns and dungeons. Outdoor areas use full 3D heightmapping for a realistic looking landscape. Indoor areas are able to use complex objects for walls, floors and ceilings. The entire world is presented in full 3D meaning buildings with many floors and dungeon areas that can overlap one another.

Another M&M VI review
Adding to the endless parade of reviews on New World Computings latest installment in the Might and Magic Saga, is this excellent review over on Gamepen. This is the best review I've seen on the game so far (not of course including my own which should be up soon), and actually points out all the flaws and inconsistencies in the game.  Nice work.  Here's a sample. - Bowmeister

Upon first boot, the most notable and obvious improvement this game has to offer is its graphics. For a non-3D accelerated CRPG, the graphics in MMVI are simply stunning. The rolling sky is gorgeously depicted and the game’s entire myriad of environments ground you well within the world of Enroth. Granted if you run off and compare them to say, Unreal, they won’t hold up as well, but as far as CRPGs go they look great. The only problem may be for users of slower systems. The 3D graphics of MMVI have three speed settings corresponding to the turn rate of your party. Rather than being able to gradually turn from side-to-side smoother than the ‘Fonz, owners of dated systems will most definitely find themselves having to skip a lot of frames with every turn/step, leading to a somewhat choppy game world. Still, on my Pentium 166 (admittedly a rapidly aging beast), the skipping of frames was fairly negligible and, after a couple of hours of play, completely forgotten.

While not implemented as well as the graphics, the sound is also very solid and a definite improvement over the Xeen games preceding MMVI. The music does an effective job of setting mood and tone in most cases, and the clang of steel on steel and the hissing of a viscious cobra are at least reasonably convincing. The problem is the sound effects themselves get very repetitive. I mean, after hearing an Evil Spirit howl at me two hundred times, I get the gist. The following three billion times you get subjected to the exact same sound can get a little old. It’s a minor complaint to be sure, but worthy of note to those of you who can’t sit through TV commercials, even the good ones, more than two or three times (Doo-be-Doo-be-Doooooo).
