21? Questions w/ Ben Cooley

by Kairn (April 2, 1998)

Before you is an extensive interview with Ben Cooley of Cinematix Studios Inc. The company that is bringing you the amazingly attractive RPG Revenant. Kairn had a chance to sit down and chit chat with Ben about various things..... and on with the show!

Kairn: Let's do what all interviews do... who are you and what is your role on the Revenant project?

Ben: My name is Ben Cooley, and I'm co-designer and core system programmer for Revenant. I also oversee the artwork, and have taken on a lot of the production responsiblities.

Kairn:  Currently what is the status of Revenant at this time? 25%, 50%, 75%?

Ben: Between 50% and 75%. About 50% of the artwork has been completed. The system is about 80% done, and the gameplay and performance seems to be working fairly well, though we are still making changes to control and animations. The level design, dialog, traps, scripting and spell effects are about 25% done right now.

Kairn:  Early on in production was Revenant originally going to be called Forsaken: Thrall of Chaos? If so, what caused the change in name? If not, forgive me for this stupid question!

Ben: Akklaim. Forsaken was trademarked by them during the period when we were developing the game. For a short time while we were trying to come up with another title, EIDOS used the interim name "Thrall of Chaos", which incidently fits with the storyline quite well. Shortly after that we came up with "Revenant", which fit our original story perfectly, and was a killer name to boot.

Kairn: What was the inspiration for Revenant?

Ben: Moorcock's Elric series. I don't really think there's been a succesful fantasy RPG with that dark fantasy mood to it. It's not really demonic, like Diablo, nor is it the knight in shining armor type of fantasy like Tolkien or Ultima.

As for the game design, we owe a little bit to Diablo in the sense that they established (or more correctly ressurected) the idea of an easy to
get into RPG. Compare the amount of time needed to get into playing
Diablo to any of the Ultima series and you'll understand what I mean.
Certainly fighting games such as Bushido Blade, Tekken, and Toshinden for the realtime elements, and of course Ultima, every one of which I've played (well, okay, except for five).

Kairn: How big is the world in Revenant? Will it be small similar to Diablo or huge like Ultima Online?

Ben: Pretty much a cross between. Revenant is based on a single island, with a mountain range dividing the two sides. The island itself takes about 5-10 minutes to traverse from top to bottom, yet it is not continental sized like Ultima Online. Of course, there are five main play areas in Revenant, including the town, western island, mountain caverns, eastern island and temple, the catacombs, and a wierd labyrinth type thing at the end. Each area has approximately five or so levels or main areas, with specific monsters, puzzles, and gamplay. There is no area of the game where all you do is simply kill monsters and collect items.

Kairn: With Eidos asking to add internet support have you had to change the story or background any to support this multiplayer aspect?

Ben: No. We decided at the beginning to lock the player character to a specific character with a specific name. This player character has an
attitude and a personality that comes out in the dialog and events in the game.

When we agreed to do multiplayer, we decided to retain this concept, but add additional characters with equally strong personalities, but with differing strengths and abilities. The original character system never had 'classes', but used differing character stats for specialization
(similar to Ultima Online). It was simply just a matter of rebalancing the stats for the multiplayer characters to give them dramatic advantages in different areas (i.e. spellcasting, combat, or stealth) to provide a completely different play for these new characters. The main character, Locke, is evenly balanced in all areas to allow the game to be played single player.

We have decided to allow the multiplayer game to be played in two modes. The first is group play mode, where the players can play through the main game together as a team. The second is dugeon hack mode, where the players can directly enter dungeon levels and play as a team or do deathmatch. We haven't decided yet whether to create special levels specifically for the dungeon hack mode, though we are leaning towards this.

Kairn: How will the internet play work, will you or Eidos setup a dedicated server similar to Battle.net, so that players may join in on any game at any time.

Ben: At this point, we are looking into using Mpath. We will see.

Kairn: Do you expect to address the problem of PK'rs with on-line games? If so, what type of plus' or minus' will you incorporate to help soothe those big, ass sores... err, I mean players?

Ben: Multiplayer games will be togglable between 'deathmatch' mode and 'group play' mode, where player killing is not allowed.

Kairn: Who are the characters? And how many are there?

Ben: There are quite a few characters in the game. The main character is Locke D'Averam, he is a Revenant, a ressurected warrior from an earlier age who initially remembers little of his past. The muliplayer characters include Bayne Ironhand, an older veteran and an exile from
his homeland, Navarro, a shaman from the East who is generally regarded as a charlatan, and a woman who calls herself Morganna, rumored to be an assasin and a poisoner.

Kairn: As far as weapons go, what types of weapons will we see? How many are in the game?

Ben: There's about 25. They fall into five main catagories, which determine the 3D animation the character uses when wielding them. They are swords, bludgeoning/mace, axe, bow/crossbow, and knife/dagger.

Kairn: Can we expect to see NPC's walking and talking in Revenant? What will their roles be in the game?

Ben: Ohhh yes. Because of the realtime 3D characters, and our motion capture system, we have exploited this to a degree people have never seen before. Expect to see monster guards sleeping on the job, townspeople playing cards at the tavern, monsters arguing in the dungeon, and a little Drugh (orcish dude) who'll give you a 'whippin' for 50 gold.

Kairn: Now, what type of enemies will we most likely run into? Could you give a list and maybe a short description for each? If they aren't classified of course.

Ben: There are about 25 different monsters I think, and they include Dragons, Orc like creatures, Stone Giants, Skeletons, Undead, Wraiths, and.. oh.. I can't quite remember all of them. Many of the creatures are unique to Revenant, and defy easy description (like the Gob).

Kairn: How will combat be handled? Will the player have any control over how the battle is fought, weapon wise? Like for instance jabbing, slicing, or cutting with a sword. Or will it be point and click, ala Diablo?

Ben: Combat will be dramatically different from any RPG anyone has ever seen. When we decided to do Revenant, we wanted to exploit the power of realtime characters. This meant we wanted to do really cool combat, similar to the type of animation and effects you see in fighting games.   The ground rules we decided on were:

  1. The combat has to remain stat based, and NOT twitch based, though good tactics should make a big difference

  2. The combat had to be EASY to do, and not require any complex combos, moves, etc.

  3. It should be fast paced, and give you the feeling that you're really kicking some ass, instead of just 'hack-hack-hack'ing.

What we've come up with is a combat system where you can choose from 4 initial moves, which grows to 12 moves by the end of the game. Blocks can be manually activated, but usually are automatically activated based on the characters or monsters experience. Each attack move has its own advantages and disadvantages, and its effect is modified by the particular monster you are attacking, and the weapon you are using.  Each attack has a vulnerability to a monster counter attack, has a possibility to hit, a damage value applied when a hit occurs, and a stun value which affects the monsters recovery from that attack. For example, overhand or leaping attacks can do a great deal of damage, but are more vulnerable to monster blocks or countermoves.

All monsters have very specific weakness and attack patterns, (which can sometimes be quite complex). Once the player learns these weaknesses, he can exploit them to greatly increase his success when attacking these monsters (similar to the way Wing Commander I had combat tricks for the various Kilrathi fighters). In all cases, if there is a dramatic difference in strength between the character and the montster, the combat will be fairly simple, with one or the other being taken out almost immediately.

Kairn: Now with the magic system, how does one acquire magic? Will it use Tomes, scrolls etc...?

Ben: The magic system is based on small jeweled talismans, that the character finds as he plays the game. These talismans, when arranged in the proper order, form the spells. The player can also use special talismans called power or effect talismans which will change the distribution, power, and visuals of a spell. In this way, some spells the character uses at the beginning of the game can get quite impressive and cool by the end, and the character never has to use just 'the latest and most powerful' spell.

Kairn: Ok now let's get to the engine, Revenant is using a true 3D engine, correct? Is everything in the game 3D based, like say pillars that are sitting in the middle of a room, are those sprites or polygons?

Ben: Revenant uses a combination of 2D tile backgrounds and realtime 3D characters, puzzles, traps, doors, and other moving elements. The tile background uses zbuffering to allow the tiles to be placed anywhere on the map in 3D space, and to intersect with eachother. In addition to the 2D z-buffered tiles, the system supports both static and moving colored lights which are pixel accurate on the backgrounds (not tile accurate like Diablo).

Kairn: With characters being polygon based, what is the max polygon count on the characters? Will the characters have true physics applied? Meaning say, you're fighting another knight and he knocks you, say, on your chest plate with his sword, have you implemented true physics where you can be knocked on your tookus?(sp?)

Ben: All characters are realtime, though its nice that at the size they are on the screen they look just like sprites. The physics engine supports gravity and bouncing, though the character has to be specifically animated to look like he's falling down. There is also multiple levels to any given game map, with stairs, etc. though they are actually 2D and therefore won't support bridges or overhangs.

Kairn: Are you going to have support for the OpenGL API? And are you going to have support for 3D Card Accelerators?

Ben: 3D accelerators.. yes absolutely. Though the game will run without acceleration, DirectX 5.0 does not currently support alpha textures in software, which will mean that most of our spell effects will not look very good in software. This is being changed in DirectX 6.0 however, and so software should be an option at that point. Most 3D accelerators right now are somewhat faster than software, though with the polygon sizes we use, we do not get a great deal of acceleration because of the higher polygon fill rate of many cards. I don't see us doing an OpenGL version right now, as DirectX seems to have more hardware support, though I wouldn't count it out.

Kairn: How is the Revenant engine handling lighting? Will it be pre-rendered or will it be real-time lighting, with shadows etc..? Also, it looks as though you have added the ability to have colored lighting in the game, is this true? If so, is it going to be 3D card based or software based?

Ben: Yes, lighting is handled both in the realtime characters and 3D objects, and also on the 2D background tiles. The lighting is pixel accurate, and uses a high speed algorithm we developed to allow for real time colored lights for both moving and stationary lights. Moving lights are a special case, and limited to one or two on the screen at the same time.

Kairn: What do you think some of the outstanding qualities in Revenant are? What do you think will make it standout from some of the other RPG's coming out the near future, like Baldur's Gate, Diablo II, Ultima IX?

Ben: We are not doing a big drawn out complex story, a dungeon-hack equipment-fighting game, and we will not have long dialog trees, scavenger hunt style gameplay, and a huge game world. What we are doing is fast paced non-twitch realtime play, massive quantities of motion captured animation that allow you to really 'control' your character instead of just pacing him around a map, spectacularly atmospheric art and lighting with lots of different tile sets, lifelike characters and monsters and cool NPC interaction, diabolical traps and puzzles, and some really pretty music. One of the things that makes the game reallyunique is the terrific concept art we've used to set the tone of the game, which we owe to the work of Den Beauvais and Jae Kim, as well as the talented people on our own staff.

Kairn: Ok.. and now last but not least, what do you see in the future for Revenant? Do you plan on a Revenant II?

Ben: Well, we'll have to see how everything comes out. Of course I'd like to do another Revenant, and expand upon the world we designed for the first game with more cities, a complex storyline, and expanded technology and gameplay. Of course that's pretty far into the future right now, and we're just busy trying to put everything together for this first version.

Kairn: I want to thank you very much Ben, and we can't wait to see the final product!

Ben: Hey, it was fun. Hope you've got everything you need.
