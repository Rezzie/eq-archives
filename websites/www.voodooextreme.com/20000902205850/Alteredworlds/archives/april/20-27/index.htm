WOTTY updates
Now here is a game that gets my blood boiling. I am reading the books like a happy lapdancer right now!   Anyway, the Monday night update arrives in all it's glory! Thanks to WOD for that goodness! It's a big un...so poor yourself a cold one, sit back and enjoy! - Sacrates

Everyone's working hard, which is important mostly because of the two imminent time sinks known as the CGDC (Computer Game Developers Conference) and E3 (you know). I've been going to the CGDC for years. Every trip has been worthwhile, but my last visit was eaten up by all of the publisher meetings as I tried to schlep WoT--what a pain. Now, I'm looking forward to actually attending a few seminars, schmoozing at the parties, and catching up with a few old friends.

Soap box time: I've always enjoyed the CGDC because it was focused on giving hard-core game developers a place to gather and exchange information (with the occasional bozo in the mix--which was fun, but for entirely different reasons). It used to be that, if you tried really hard, you might be able to spot something other than a tee shirt and jeans. Nowadays, the suits are starting to view the conference as just another place to do business. Regardless of the fact that I did exactly this last year selling WoT, I don't like the trend. There's a time to focus on marketing and PR, a place where every conversation should be filled with buzzwords, and it ain't CGDC. Speaking of which...

I've got nothing against a world of hype as long as it's not masquerading as anything else, and our friend E3 is no hypocrite. This will be my first time there and I'm really looking forward to it. I'm anxious partially because, as a designer, I have a lot to say about this game that has been kept quiet--I've got some really impressive stuff to reveal and I can't wait to do so--but I also want to see E3's traditional millions in marketing money spent everywhere I look. As long as the publisher (which will obviously be announced sometime between now and then) is footing the bill, I'm planning on surrounding myself with official WoT chain-mail bikini babes (that's a joke, Sabrina)! According to lots of sources, E3 is like nothing else, and it's a show not to be missed. Let's hope they don't keep me chained up in that little room forever!

On to the actual work: I've divided up the story into its component missions, objectives, cut scenes, and assets, then started to assign them to the level designers, artists, and animators. It's much easier to run towards the finish line when the race course is clearly defined. But all of this only confirmed that The Wheel of Time is going to be an epic game. In the last week, fantastic new architectural sketches, magic effects, and textures have flowed in--but the size of the game had me worrying a little about our ability to create everything with the same attention to detail displayed in the microcosm. This is why I'm ecstatic to have introduced another artist to the team. Like many of the other team members, I'd worked with this talented fine-artst extensively on previous games, so she's already proven her abilities. I'm expecting nothing but the best. When you add her to the art team already in place, the epic scope isn't nearly as scary. Of course, this means I no longer have the money to hire an assistant producer... :-(

I'm actually particularly busy right now with all of this, so you won't find a letters section this week. I do appreciate everyone's response to last week's column; many of you thought it was the best yet. Of course I intend to keep on doing it, but making this game has to take precedence over meaningless drivel. Thanks for tuning in. I promise to be much more meaningless next week.

Until then!

Even more BG
Sweet Mary, mother of God.....is this a Saturday or a Monday? I haven't been getting this much news since I began this experiment. My head is starting to spin with all this big game info. And just to add some cream to the topping I am gonna add this little post from the BG message board. Who says Sacrates isn't the nicest guy on Earth? (GPRPG) - Sacrates

Joe: Some people have indicated they might not like the one PC with up to five NPCs for a party. In multiplayer, it seems that six PCs can be created and used as a party. Would it be possible to start a multiplayer game with six PCs but the host plays all of them? This pseudo-networking would give a single player the option of playing between 1-6 PCs with the balance as NPCs if desired.

BioWare's Ray Muzyka : Yes. Though the only practical issue this would help is to allow you to create all of your initial party. So, if you really, really want to create all of the characters in your party, we have decided to allow you to do this by playing the game in multiplayer mode and creating all of the characters. It's actually a side benefit of multiplayer mode, but we debated against locking this out but thought we'd allow it in case you want to do this.

This (creating your entire party as first level characters off the start) may sound appealing, but I must really recommend you play with as many NPC's turned PC's as possible (at least in single player mode), and here's why (or at least why I would):

1) You have a lot of NPC's to choose from - and whether they join you (and become PC's) is entirely up to you. You choose if an NPC leaves, too, unless you p*ss (sorry, annoy - the browser filter got me too here!) one off (the NPC PC's have feelings too)

2) The NPC's interact with each other in interesting and amusing ways - ala Jagged Alliance (great game)

3) We spent a lot of time writing dialogue and a lot of money recording NPC sounds - and created PC's won't have any unless you record some for them (yes, you can do this, but the created PC's have fewer sound slots than do the PC's that once were NPC's).

When I refer to NPC's I mean any character that is not part of your party. PC's on the other hand are either characters you create OR NPC's that you have asked to join your party.

Lord RumblePants of FallenPyre: I must inquire if the multiplayer setup will be hosted by something similar to that of battle.net where one can meet other gamers and share a jug of ale and a tale or two.

Interplay's Chris Parker: This is actually something that we've been trying to figure out for awhile now. The current plan is just for TCP/IP,LAN,modem. However, since some service providers assign users IP addresses every time they log on, some users may have difficulty finding their host.

So, we are thinking of putting up at least a dedicated chat board that people can alt tab to and find other players for their games (BG will tell the user their IP address from the multiplayer screen).

We'll see. Interplay is supposed to have a Battle.net server system up sometime soon, but that would take more programming time....so don't count on it. The chat situation is more likely.

Gothica goodness
Jason Struck was over at the Realm of Gothica message board yesterday and answered a few questions regarding the World editor for Gothica and How big the Realm is.

How big is the relm of Gothica? bigger than Ultima 8? Bigger than diablo 2? or about what size?

Gothica is done in a very different, and unique way
than both those titles. The game that ships is going to
be quite big. Probably around the size of Diablo 2,
or a bit bigger. However, one important goal is the fact
that we want Gothica to be highly interactive and fun. No
long boring areas of walking. Every area will have
ton's of cool stuff going on. We'd rather do it
this way than have a mega large world that is just
boring forest between towns :).

This is a hard question to talk about because I
can't totally divulge the setup of the world as
of yet. We need publisher support for the idea
we are working towards. I can say something
really cool that should get people interested:

The team has always worked towards the goal of
modularity and expandability in Gothica. This
allows the ability to plug new quests, world
areas, items, and magic into the game. To this
end, we are making everything modifiable. If
you go to our web site there is a screen shot
of a 3D Cad editor we created for Gothica. We
are currently debating releasing this when the
game ships for people to play with creating there
own areas. We'd like to see different servers
running different, unique worlds. This is still
not set in stone right now, but this is the way
we want to go.

-Jason

The Editor looked complex. If we did use the editor and sent you in areas, would
: you use it?

The editor is actually very simple to use. All
construction is drag and draw. You just think
what you want in your head the the interface is
really intuitive. We've done it this way specifically
for the public. For NPC's, each has a special
dialog to make modification really simple. There
is a special interface for making complex shapes
liked curved staircases. You could learn to edit
in 5 minutes. It's *much* easier than a Quake
level editor and *much* more powerful.

On release, we'll work to build an area where people
can put worlds they have created for others to

lot's of simple, concrete information regarding
construction in Gothica.

FF VII demo

I managed to play the demo of FF7 today. Here's the details... all this can be found on the news page of PC Fan.

Well, I played the Final Fantasy VII demo today, and let me go on the record by saying: I am impressed! Here's the skinny...

The demo starts right before the Makoro reactor and your party must make its way to the Golden Saucer. Once you reach the Golden Saucer, the demo ends.

FMV is seamless just like the PlayStation version, but the quality is no where near - in other words, you can see the transitions because they stick out like sore thumbs.

Despite the game running at 30+ FPS, the character animation is still rather choppy. In fact, other than the animation running a tad faster, owners of the PSX version will notice no difference.

This game is incredibly sharp. Playing it in a crisp 640x480 environment makes it all worth while for me.

Playable characters in the demo include: Cloud, Aerith, Barret, Red 6, and Tifa.

A variety of spells are in the demo such as bolt, cure, fire and the first two levels of every charaters' Limit Breaks.

Each character also possesses one Summoning Materia. For instance, Cloud has Bahamut, Barret has Ifrit, and Aerith has Chocobo attack.

Music is identical to the PSX version (barf).

Backgrounds are pixelated and are not halfway as good looking as they are on the PSX version.

Loading time: there is none!

Barret's lame Ebonics are still present. The Final Fantasy VII demo should be available within the next two to three weeks. Keep your eyes peeled. It does take up a minimum of 75MB of harddrive space, so make sure you have the room.

Comment: All in all, I was impressed and I look forward to seeing the final product. Way to go Eidos.

Inside the sequel...
Gamespot UK has posted a nice tyddie interview with Dan Freed of Raven Software, you know the smoove brothers that are bringing us Heretic II!

GSN: You're building Heretic II on the Quake II engine. What features have you added to the engine?

DF: First of all we're running the game with a 16-bit palette instead of the standard 256 colours that the Quake II engine runs. We also created a new bone system for the game that will allow the characters in the game to do torso twists (MechWarrior-like), and we're working on enhancing the AI. There will also be cutscenes in the game to help build a richer plot.

One new thing that we're adding is a new frame compression system. Since there are more actions in the game with jumping and climbing, there needed to be more animation, so we've figured out a way to do 1,000 frames of character animation to bring more movement to the game.

Gothica Goodness..
I spy'd over at Psychonews that Jason Struck of Tinman Studios, you know the Gothica folks... has revealed some sweet details about the game on the message board...

How is your hero controlled? by mouse or keyboard? also, is the terrain a grid or can u take a step, or a 1/2 step, or whatever. is it possible to have weapon controls? like ctrl-left is slash left, and so on?

" In Gothica, you can control the character using the keyboard, joystick, or mouse. There are no predefined step amounts and no grids. The world is created using our world cad editor (check out the shot on our web site) in true 3D. Walls and surfaces don't have any restrictions. We are working on a system of multiple attack types in Gothica. We are also thinking about using special shield blocks as well. "

How are the townspeople in Gothica?

" Right now we have a town that we are working on with towns people. Each town person is controlled by a script that allows him/her to carry out a unique daily schedule. You can converse which each person and they will all have different conversations. We don't have simple shops such as an armory that sells everything. Instead, shops are very specialized. This allows for unique shops that sell unique items in special areas. "

How does archery work in Gothica?

" Since Gothica is true 3D, there are a lot of cool things that work perfectly (and physically) in full 3D. Archery is one of them. There is an archery skill in Gothica. This affects many things. Some include: your ability to use certain bows, draw strength, firing rate, distance, and aiming skill. You have to physically aim at the creature. Then, depending on your skill, the arrow may home in on a more vital area. "

When your hero gets damaged, will he react?

" Force is a factor in combat. Hitting a creature, or being hit by a creature, all cause some sort of force to come into play. The large bone worm creature seen on our page can pack an incredible forceful blow. One that can actually throw your character back pretty hard. Body part damage does not affect the character. It does affect monsters though. You can cut off or smash limbs, heads, etc. A kill swing might behead a creature and send it's head rolling across the ground with a sick bounce. "

In the main screen of the page, it says you do stuff for good or evil. How will that effect how things happen in the story? Can you specifically choose evil or good? What would happen along the lines of enemies?

" If you are in a city and you choose to kill a townsperson, the guards will come. They don't magically appear, but will come running from where they were. It's possible to get away, but just like real life, being a murderer is difficult. If you escape the town, the next time you come back, depending on the elapsed time and your appearance, guards will have a chance (based on distance) of recognizing you. If this happens, you are in a battle/run for your life again. "

Is there anything planned for guilds both ingame/outgame in Gothica?

" We want people to create Gothica guilds so chances are we will have usermade skins, logos and team flags. There will also be NPC guilds in the Gothica. "

oooo doggies... this is going to be one hell of a game... - Kairn
