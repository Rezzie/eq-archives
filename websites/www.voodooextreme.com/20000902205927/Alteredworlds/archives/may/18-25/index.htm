Even more Valhalla goodness
I have been extremely interested in what the actual game designers thought of Unreal and how their games will compare. Needless to say I was chatting with Ryan Dueckman of Valhalla about just this subject and he gave me some food for thought. BTW if you enjoy this subject check out my recent Interview with Ryan on Valhalla! - Sacrates

  Yeah, I've been playing Unreal, it certainly lives up to it's name ..   It's going to be hard getting too much work done THIS week... :) As far as graphical effects in Valhalla go, I don't think you'll be at ALL dissappointed, even a year from now when it's released.
Valhalla is designed from the ground up to be extremely extensible and expandable, with a minimal amount of work.  Instead of applying simple "Textures" to objects and faces like in a modern graphics engines, we apply "materials".  A standard material for Valhalla can
include animated textures, reflections(Real and simulated), bump maps, detail maps, even weight and other properties.  However, if a level designer wants a certain effect for an area(Say, grass rippling
in the breeze), he simply creates a new "Special effect" material, with all the properties for a standard texture.  The programmers are then able to think about the effect and what the best way to simulate
it would be, and they can program it in when they have the time, without anyone having to wait for it to be implemented.  Likewise, if a programmer comes up with a new effect(Like wood burning in realtime), all that's needed of the level editors is going through
the main material library, and setting one or two new properties for all the object groups.  No need to redesign any of the level.  This gives us freedom to add any special effects we can think of throughout the design process, without a major time hit.   (That's my
guess on why Unreal took so long to get finished.)

BG Interview
Today seems to be interview day no doubt. PCFan has an interview up with Dr Ray Mazyka of Bioware and Baldur's Gate fame. Check it out. - Sacrates

Joe: Important question: How does one engage battle in Baldur's Gate, similar to Diablo's point and click reference or is it more calculating like (Microprose's) Darklands? And how does one control battle events with multiplayers in Baldur's Gate?

Dr. Ray: More calculating, but the interface allows for some default actions so if you L click on your enemies your party members will attack them.  For more detailed actions, you use the hotkeys or the quick buttons at the bottom of the screen and then select a target.  Overall I think it is a nice blend of the two systems - allowing for a high degree of control, but easy to use.  We've spent over a year refining the controls and they have gone through at least four major revisions to this point.
 
Joe: The screenshots look incredible by the way. Now, since you're not using tilesets and replacing it with prerendered backgrounds, how does gameplay and control work? Similar to Final Fantasy VII and Resident Evil?

Dr. Ray: No, the game is real time, and you guide your party over the landscape much as in Diablo, except that you have much more control over what they do.  We also include a pause game command that allows you to stop the game and assign party members actions.

EQ Interview
Gamespen RPG has got some good stuff on EQ including some new Screenshots and a big fat interview with Brad. Damn am I envious or what?! Anyway he posted a few small tidbits from the Interview to tide us over until he gets the rest ready. - Sacrates

Q. Player killing or "pkilling" has always been part of multiplayer role-playing games, and EverQuest will allow players to attack and kill each other if that’s the style of gaming they want to enjoy. But EverQuest, quite uniquely, allows players to opt out of pkilling, and allow them to role-play in an environment where they are safe from attacks by other players. Please explain how the "no-pkilling" switch will work in EverQuest, and what limits it puts on players who choose to use it.

A. Player killing is a subject that is debated constantly on the net, and both sides to this eternal argument have great points. We want to accommodate both gameplay styles, but we don’t want to separate the players in terms of which servers they can play on. So when a player creates a character, he or she will choose whether they want to participate in player killing or not. If they choose not to, then they will be immune to other player’s assaults, and can only die in battle against NPCs. But those who like the added danger of player vs. player competition can simply create their character with player killing switched to on and then do battle with those who have chosen similarly. There has been some concern that this feature could limit freedom and role playing, but if you really think about it, the bottom line is that the only thing you can’t do in EverQuest because of the PK switch is kill another player who doesn’t want to be killed. And while this will purposely curtail the few who can only have fun at the expense of others’ enjoyment, the rest of us can go ahead and have a great time playing the game, regardless of whether our personal definition of role-playing includes player vs. player or not.

Q: You have indicated that there will be twelve races that players can choose from in EverQuest. Tell us a bit about the different races in EverQuest. Will the races have a history of interacting with each other? Will certainly races have "evil" or "good" alignments?

A. Evil and good are a bit more relative in EverQuest than in other games… for instance, I doubt the Dark Elves really feel they are evil, however the Wood Elves certainly view them that way. And there is indeed a history of races interacting with each other… wars, racial hatreds, and the like.

The Update!
Yup....Monday's are a time of goodness for WoT fans like myself and many of you. Monday's are WoT Development update days and it would be a sad day when we didn't get one. Read on and enjoy: - Sacrates

I know that when the earliest I can start writing the update is 7:00pm, there won't be any Mail fun. I'll say my piece about it, then no more complaining. E3 preparations are taking all of my time. I'm hoping that, after it's all done, things will settle back down and I can indulge in some Viewer Mail and Counter Contest goodness. I haven't given up on them (and it's actually quite hard not to write them based on some of the material that I've received lately). I think that, after you've read about what we're showing at E3--either here or in the press following the event--you might forgive me.

Naturally, we'll be showing the Fortress of the Light with all new skies (based on the latest Unreal engine build); this was the level used to produce all of the current screen shots. I'll be using that to demonstrate The Wheel of Time's artistic direction and many of the game's basic features.

I'll also be introducing a new environment, based on the Fortress of the Forsaken architecture, which is absolutely stunning. I was going to whip up a simple level to showcase some the game's trickier AI and combat situations, but since the Forsaken's primitives were coming along fairly quickly--and were so attractive--I asked one of our level designers to incorporate them into my test design. Pieces of this map are coming together right now and the results are breath-taking. The architecture and texture maps were intentionally designed in a completely different style than our previous level, but they are no less impressive or effective than those in the Fortress of the Light. Our artists and level designers are clearly maturing in this process. I plan to release some new screen shots at E3 from this environment to illustrate what I'm talking about.

We're also toiling hard to polish up citadel customization and magic effects. Soon, I'll be spilling the beans about both of these design elements and explaining why they're so different than anything that's come before. I think we're going to see a whole new breed of people who excel at this game: people with a real understanding of strategy, those who can think on their feet as well as pull a trigger.

I participated in a round table at CGDC that discussed the licensing of technology to build games. One person there voiced the opinion that the only advancements possible were in the realm of technology; different games that used the same engine were essentially the same game. Well, he was wrong. No one who sees our demo will think that they're looking at Unreal, or any other game using our beloved engine. No one who plays our game will mistake it for Quake, or any other FPS.

It's difficult to be proud and excited and not sound immodest; I hope you'll give me the benefit of the doubt. If next week doesn't swallow me up completely, I'll be back to tell you all about our progressing demo. If not, I'll see you when the smoke clears and you can decide for yourself if it's me blowing the rings. After preaching my cause for so long, I'd much prefer my work to speak for me.

Until then!

Q. Can you please go into HOW you plan to Curb PK'rs.
A. This seems to be the heart of many people's questions. Most of you feel the game will become a blood bath of evil pkr's outside the game.. Well listed below are a few of the many solutions we have come up. All of them are either online or are being placed online..

a) We have plots, spells, skills that only good or evil can have... Some have both.. This will allow Evil characters to hunt around for things they want and good can hunt for theirs. (SO evil will not be bored)

b) Monsters will hunt down evil characters not just good. And we have certain monsters that will only hunt down evil such of that that only certain monsters will only hunt down good..

c) You can go to prison if caught. And it sucks. Not like UO you get out in 15 minutes it could be Days. Hey this is real life..

d) We are working on a trial system. (We hope to have it ready in Phase 1 of the Beta test)

e) NPC's will have guilds on both sides..

f) The combine level of a party can not attack or kill a party up to 4 levels below them. So if a party has 4 people in a area and wishes to pick on that little 2nd level mage think again.. The game will not permit it.. UNLESS the mage wishes to attack those 4 people then it becomes fair game.. (Note Certain area's will not have this system) (Example: City of Hellish)
