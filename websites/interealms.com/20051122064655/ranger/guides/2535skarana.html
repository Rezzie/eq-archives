The Strike Force's Guide to Levels 25-35 at the South Karana Spires

The Strike Force's Guide to Levels 25-35 at the South Karana Spires

When the Strike Force is choosing among hunting spots, it looks for a good mixture of four things: xp, coin (or coin-equivalent), rare MoBs/drops, and a relative lack of danger. For characters in the 25-35 level range - especially Rangers and Druids - the "Spires" area of South Karana offers all four things in abundance.

This Guide is divided into two main parts: (I) a discussion of how to hunt at the Spires; and (II) tips on why, how, and where to "Track-n'-Whack" rare MoBs in SK. By combining a steady diet of gnolls from the Spires with the occasional rare MoB, you can steadily level and earn cash while exploiting every opportunity to nab a rare and valuable item.

For comparison's sake, the Strike Force is composed of a Mage, a Druid, and a Ranger of approximately equal level. We started at the Spires when we were all around 25th level (and the resident Tesch Mas Gnolls were even-to-red to us), and did not leave the area until we hit 35th level and some of the gnolls stopped giving xps. In other words, everything discussed in this Guide can be done with a group of at least three characters - this is not a solo guide.

I. Hunting at the Spires

The Spires are found in South Karana surrounding the entrance to the Splitpaw zone, roughly at loc neg3000, pos800. If you run straight south from the bridge that connects North Karana to South Karana, you will eventually come upon the Spires. The Spires are impossible to miss for anyone traveling in the southern part of SK - look for the three tall talon-shaped towers jutting skyward around a small hilly area. Indeed, the Spire area is supposed to represent an upside-down gnoll paw pointing skyward, with the Spires serving as the gnoll's claws and the hilly area between the Spires representing the pads of the gnoll's paw.

Each hunting site at the Spires offers similar benefits: steady (and mostly-static) spawns of melee-only gnolls that provide excellent xp and loot; just enough downtime between spawns to stay healthy and mana-happy; minimal danger of wandering MoBs and trains; and close proximity to the Splitpaw zone entrance for those rare instances when a hasty escape is needed.

    1. Spawn Points and Camp Spots at the Spires
      2.

      There are four hunting spots at the Spires: the three Spires themselves, and the Strike Force's preferred spot on "The Ledge."

      The three spires are known as the "South Spire," "Middle Spire," and "North Spire." Quick use of your Sense Heading skill will show you which is which or, if you are standing at the Splitpaw entrance (it is a hole carved into the side of a small hill in the middle of the Spires) facing away from the entrance, the South Spire is to your right, the Middle Spire is directly ahead of you, and the North Spire is to your left.

      The Spires increase in difficulty as one goes from South to Middle to North. Only one gnoll spawns at the South Spire (every 6-10 minutes or so). The Middle Spire has a slightly faster spawn rate and a small chance that two gnolls will spawn. The North Spire spawns alternate between the standard static gnoll and a pair of gnolls that start to run as soon as they spawn. The runners briefly split apart (heading in opposite ways), then come back together in front of the North Spire before heading, in tandem, counter-clockwise away from that Spire.

      Camping the three Spires is relatively easy - stand with your back to a Spire, walk about fifty paces, and turn around. Once the gnoll(s) spawn, pull and kill as you prefer, and as best suits your group's makeup. The three-member Strike Force generally used the "root-&-shoot" method: the ranger would Root the gnoll, the ranger and druid would slap DoTs on the gnoll, and then everyone would throw in a DD or bow-shot. By the time the Root broke, the gnoll would be around half-strength and would not take long to eliminate. As long as one party member can keep an eye on the spawn, the other members can meditate and heal up for the next spawn.

      The "Ledge" (the Strike Force's home-away-from-home from levels 30 to 35) is the fourth, and hardest, camping spot at the Spires. To find the Ledge, stand directly in front of the Splitpaw cave entrance, facing the entrance, and look toward "one o'clock" (you will be facing Northwest). You will see a flat plateau, with a hill rising to its left and a downward slope falling away to its right. If no one is camping the Ledge, you will also see a lone gnoll staring right/North.

      The Ledge generally has two types of spawns: a single static gnoll, and a gnoll that starts running North (downhill) as soon as it spawns. Every so often, the Ledge will spawn two static gnolls right on top of each other. The Ledge also gives you an excellent vantage point from which to pull the pair of runners that spawn at the North Spire - those runners move clockwise around the Spires until they get behind the Middle Spire, and then they climb the hill to the left of the Ledge. Thus, from the Ledge, you can pull the runners either when they are coming around the corner from the North Spire (if you are looking North, you will see them) or when they have come to rest atop the hill. Moreover, if you wait the runners out after they climb the hill, they eventually will separate from each other and you can pick them off individually.

      The Ledge can be camped from two places. Most groups stay on the flat ground about fifty paces to the North of the Ledge, and pull the gnolls down to them. The Strike Force, however, opts to camp on the Ledge itself. We take positions on the southern edge of the Ledge (the side closest to the Splitpaw zone entrance), facing North-Northwest, and deal with the gnolls from there. This high ground gives us an excellent vantage point over the entire Spire area - we can see when a Spire is abandoned (and then pull spawns from there to the Ledge), we can spot the North Spire runners as they sprint around the Spires, and we are close enough to the Ledge spawns that "unintentional" KSing from us (along the "Oh, didn't know you were camping that spawn" type) is impossible. Most importantly, the Ledge is extremely close to the Splitpaw zone; because the Ledge is the one place at the Spires where you might encounter a "mini-train" of sorts, we recommend that you stay as close as possible to the zone entrance.

      We advise progressing through the Spires (from South to North) and then to the Ledge as you level from 25 to 35. Do not progress through these steps too quickly, as your inability to hold down a camp (particularly the North Spire runners) can wreak havoc for every other Spire group. At 25, the three-member Strike Force had all it could handle with the single static South Spire gnoll spawn. By 30, we could camp any of the three Spires without danger, but the Ledge still presented a real challenge (especially when a North Spire group could not handle the pair of runners). By the time we left the Ledge at 35, we could effectively handle both the Ledge and any "extra" spawn that we could pull from any of the three Spires.

      One final note on the four Spire camps: they seem to attract an inordinate number of soloing Druids (usually in Wolf Form) who kill by kiting. As such, just because a Spire (or the Ledge) appears to be uncamped does not mean that it truly is vacant. We generally wait a few minutes (or until the next spawn) to ensure that the camp is "ours" before settling in.

    2. The Tesch Mas Gnolls
      3.

      Because the Tesch Mas gnolls are the only MoB-type that spawns at the Spires, it pays to know your enemy. The gnolls range from 25th to 29th level and are melee-only. Like gnolls that you might previously have fought in BlackBurrow or HighHold Pass, the Tesch Mas gnolls will aggro on you if you come within range, especially if you are engaged in a melee with another gnoll. Like those other gnolls, the Tesch Mas variety also will turn tail and try to run once you have them below approximately 20% of their hit points.

      The Tesch Mas gnolls have a wide variety of drops. Each one will have some cash, ranging from the 1 cp booby-prize to a maximum of about 4 plat. About one in three gnolls will wield Fine Steel weapons - although these gnolls hit harder than their unarmed counterparts, each FS weapon brings about 6 plat from a merchant (the centaur merchants in SK gladly will purchase these from you).

      The gnolls also drop four categories of magic/quest items: Splitpaw armor, magic weapons, minor Splitpaw magic items, and Tesch Mas scrolls. The Splitpaw armor pieces - Belt, Mask, Tooth Necklace, Tunic, and Gloves - are suitable for characters in the 10-15 level range. The downside to Splitpaw armor is that it does not bring great value from merchants and, thus, you have to lug it out of the zone and go through the time-consuming auction value to maximize its cash value. We sold these items at first, but later began to use them mainly as newbie give-aways. Stats on the Splitpaw pieces are as follows:

      Belt: 5AC, +5 vs. Poison

      Gloves: 4AC, +3 Dex, +2 Wis

      Mask: 3AC, +5 vs. Poison and Magic

      Necklace: 2AC, +5 Cha, +5 vs. Poison

      Tunic: 9AC, +10 vs. Poison

      In addition to the FS weapons, some Tesch Mas gnolls drop two magic weapons: the Dagger of Dropping and the Gnoll Hide Lariat. The Dagger is a 4/22 Piercing weapon that procs a Weaken effect at 6th level, and the Lariat is a 3/20 1HS weapon that procs a Stun effect at 25th level. Both weapons proc for the gnolls as well, so try to keep Lariat-wielding gnolls away from your casters. The Dagger is an solid weapon for low-level casters, but we found that it did not auction easily. The Lariat is a decent off-hand weapon for low-level Rangers, Rogues, and Bards, and, because of its Stun effect, a great weapon for higher-level casters' pets. Every so often, a high-level necro will wander through SK auctioning to buy Lariats; try to hold your looted Lariats until you have this sort of excellent sale opportunity!

      There also are two types of "minor magic" items dropped at the Spires: the Split Paw, and the Preserved Splitpaw Eye. The Split Paw gives you several charges of "Dance of the Fireflies," an outdoor- and night-only lightsource spell. The Eye serves as a minor lightsource (about the same as a fire beetle eye), gives you +2 on you Poison saves, as well as a right-clickable "Glimpse" (farsight) effect. At night, you can always tell which gnolls will drop the Eye, because they will be surrounded by an aura of light.

      Finally, some Tesch Mas gnolls drop Tanned Splitpaw Hides. These items are Lore, and look like rolled-up scrolls (as opposed to other, worthless, dropped hides that look more like an unscribed spell or newbie note). If you right-click on the Tanned Splitpaw Hide after you loot it, it will "open" and you will see a Volume number from 1 through 4. When you collect a full set of four, you can turn them in for one of two quests: Karana's Tear, or the far more valuable (and time-consuming) Gem Inlaid Gauntlets. We will not reproduce the quest steps in this article, but will say that the Karana's Tear quest simply requires you to give all four scrolls to a Qeynos NPC, who hands you xp and the Karana's Tear earring (+5 Sta). The Gem Inlaid Gauntlets quest requires several cross-continental journeys and interactions with NPCs, but results in an excellent piece of armor usable by all races and classes: AC8, Dex +3, Sta +3. Players often place the Gem Inlaid Gauntlets in the same value category as Treant Fists and Lupine Claw Gauntlets. We have been able to sell Gem Inlaid Gauntlets for between 350 and 500 plat, adding a whole new profit tier to the Spires. The gloves are Lore, though, so you can only quest for (and auction) one at a time.

    3. Wandering Dangers at the Spires
      4.

One of the best features of the Spires is that it attracts very few wandering MoBs. Lions, Mist Wolves, and Shadow Wolves occasionally will wander about, and might even aggro on you, but do not pose much of a threat to a group powerful enough to camp the Spires. Similarly, you will see the odd Splitpaw Gnoll and Escaped Splitpaw Gnoll; these gnolls are much less fearsome than their Tesch Mas counterparts (apparently, the Tesch Mas and their cohorts have evicted the Splitpaw gnolls from their former lair) and will not put a strain on your group.

Various centaurs (and, less frequently, aviaks) also roam around the Spires area, but these MoBs do not aggro unless provoked and, given the plentiful hunting at the Spires, the Strike Force never saw a need to provoke them. Aviak Avocets will give you xp all the way to 35, but they are tough to kill and their drops are not worth the hassle.

There is one wandering MoB that paths through the Spires and will both aggro on you and pose a problem for you: the Undead Cyclops (and, very rarely, a still-living Cyclops). The Undead Cyclops has a lot of HPs, hits hard, and does not carry valuable drops. Fortunately, you almost always can tell when the "UC" is coming: not only is it large enough for you to spot from far away, but players in SK tend to shout out its location as it roams around the zone. If you are healthy and mana-happy, con it and take a crack at it; if not, just avoid it and let it walk past you.

Remember, the Splitpaw zone is always just a few seconds away, especially if you are SoWed. If the Undead Cyclops proves to be too much to handle, or in the rare event that you encounter a swarm of Tesch Mas gnolls, zone into Splitpaw, heal up, and zone back. More often than not, your camp site still will be free.

  1. "Track-n'-Whack" at the Spires

Once you get comfortable at the Spires, you will find yourself rolling in xp, coins, FS weapons, and other loot. The Spires, however, have one more tremendous advantage as a hunting area, especially for classes that can track: SK's most sought-after rare MoBs (and their rare drops) tend to spawn within tracking range of the Spires.

Accordingly, the Strike Force has developed a very profitable hunting strategy that we call "Track-n'-Whack." We now explain this tactic, and discuss our actual experience with it in SK. After reading this section, we are confident that you will be looking for trackers to join your group any time that you are camping the Spires.

    1. How to "Track-n'-Whack"
      2.

The mechanics of "Track-n'-Whack" are not complex; perhaps the most demanding part of the tactic is the level of focus and patience that it requires. We have found that committing to this strategy is well worth the trouble: one rare drop is the difference between a good day at the Spires and a GREAT one.

To effectively "Track-n'-Whack," you really only need two skills in your party (beyond the ability to kill whatever MoB you engage): tracking (to find the MoBs) and the Spirit of the Wolf spell (to catch them, especially the key named MoBs which, as noted below, often travel at SoW speed). Druids have both of these key skills but, because a Ranger's tracking skill is far better than any Druid's, we strongly advise having a Ranger to serve as the group's tracker. This is not to say, however, that a Druid's tracking skills are redundant: in certain cases, a rare MoB will show up on the Druid's short-range track before it shows up on the Ranger's screen. Thus, even if you have a Ranger tracking, it can pay off to have your group's Druid keep an eye on track as well.

The preparatory steps for "Track-n'-Whack" are rather straightforward:

    1. always have all buffs (especially SoW) up on all characters;
    2. agree on a pre-set "marching order," with the group's tracker in the lead;
    3. each other character hotkeys: /follow [character in front of you];
    4. the tracker hotkeys an ALL-CAPS /groupsay message such as: /g RARE MoB ON TRACK; TELL ME WHEN YOU'RE READY TO ROLL!!!; and
    5. each other character hotkeys a /groupsay message like: /g Good to go!!

Once these steps have been taken, all that remains is for the tracker to constantly hit Track and keep an eye out for named MoBs (see below for a handy guide to SK's named denizens). Indeed, the Strike Force's Ranger even tracks during gnoll battles - the named MoBs are so rare and valuable (and the Tesch Mas gnolls so easy to kill, root, etc.) that our Ranger is under strict orders to activate his "Rare MoB on track" hotkey even if a gnoll was just pulled.

Once a named MoB spawns, the steps to take are obvious: the tracker hits his "Rare on track" hotkey; the other group members hit their /follow and "Good to go" hotkeys in quick succession; and, when all members have sounded in, the tracker takes off after the named MoB, towing his group members behind him (and, if possible, letting the rest of the group in on the identity of the target).

The tracker will almost always be the first character to get a visual fix on the target. When he does, his job is to check (and inform the group of) the target's /consider and engagement status. For the /consider status, a simple "Blue" or "Yellow" will do. There is no need to inform the group that a MoB has not yet been engaged; if, however, the MoB currently is being fought, the tracker should inform the group (to prevent inadvertent kill-stealing) and bring the chase to a halt. In this situation, we advise remaining very close to the fight - if the group fighting the MoB flees or dies, you will have first dibs on an already-damaged MoB.

Assuming that the MoB is not previously engaged, the tracker should pull the MoB to the group as quickly as possible. We have found that a bow-shot from our Ranger is the fastest way to engage a named MoB: our Ranger can hit from far away and, most importantly, without stopping his sprint toward the target. If you are competing for the MoB with another group, a split-second could make all the difference between nabbing a rare drop and watching another group enrich themselves at your expense.

Once the MoB is beaten and looted, the group members should reform their marching order (again, hotkeys make this easy) and instruct the tracker to head back to their vacated Spires camp site. Even if the named Mob ran you all over SK, there is an easy way to get back to the Spires: the tracker will almost always be able to track to a Tesch Mas gnoll and, thus, the Spires, from the spot of your battle. With a little luck, your spot at the Spires will still be open and you'll be able to resume your gnoll slaughter, hopefully with a new rare drop in hand.

    1. Who to "Track-n'-Whack"
      2.

During our extended stay at the Spires, we have managed to track, and whack, nearly every rare MoB in the zone. We also have been lucky enough to obtain nearly every rare SK drop. What follows is an anecdotal guide to the identity and rarity of the various named SK MoBs and their drops, as well as a few words on the MoBs that we either have not seen or never bothered chasing. The spawn rate figures are based upon approximately 75 hours spent in SK.

    1. The Big Four
      2.

      Based upon their potential drops, we believe that the "Big Four" named SK MoBs are: Quillmane, Coloth Meadowgreen, Groi Gutblade, and Grizzleknot.

      Quillmane, a named Pegasus, is the most prized MoB in the zone. Quill, as he is known, is about 29th level, and has two drops: the common Feathered Leggings (AC4, +7 Sta) and the grand prize rare drop for the entire zone, the Pegasus Feather Cloak (AC6, +7 Dex, Effect: Levitate). Quillmane seems to spawn in the area between the Aviak city and the Spires, and tends to wander toward the Spires. We have tracked and killed Quillmane four times, and gotten the Cloak once

      The Pegasus Cloak is valuable for nearly any character as an equipped item, as it provides excellent AC and Dex stats as well as unlimited mana-free Levitation, but what puts it "over the top" in terms of EQ value is the fact that the Cloak is a key component in the Mage epic weapon quest. Because it is No Drop, you cannot sell the Cloak itself, but see below for a brief discussion regarding the value and mechanics of auctioning looting rights to Quillmane.

      Coloth Meadowgreen, a named 35th level centaur, gets our vote for toughest SK MoB to kill. He is SoW-fast, hits hard, and has a ton of hit points. Coloth commonly drops a regular Longbow, but occasionally drops the Runed Oak Bow (21 Dmg, 43 Delay, 125 Range). Coloth is not as rare as the other three MoBs discussed in this section, but you should not try to engage him until you are level 30+ (we waited until we were all 32+). Since we started hunting him, we have tracked and killed him five times (three times in one two-hour span!!), and he has dropped the Runed Oak Bow once. The Runed Oak Bow sells for 750 to 1,000 plat because it is one of the best bows that a non-Ranger can equip (it also is an upgrade from the Trueshot).

      Groi Gutblade, a named 25th level troll shadowknight, is an extremely rare MoB - in all of our time at the Spires, we only have tracked him twice. Groi commonly drops a Ring of Shadows (+2 Dex, 3 Charges of Gather Shadows), and rarely drops a Lionhide Backpack (8 slots, 35% weight reduction). We got to him once (beaten to the punch once when SoW unfortunately dropped during our run at him.) and were fortunate to obtain the Lionhide Backpack. Groi opened with Harm Touch and then entered melee; once he was in melee, he was not a tough kill for three 30+ characters.

      Grizzleknot, a named 28th level treant, is the only major SK MoB that we have not been able to slay. Indeed, we only tracked him once, and were unable to even get started on our run before some lucky group killed him. We believe, however, that he spawns in the northeastern corner of the zone (near the treants), and that his distance from the Spires may well explain why we only have seen him one time on track. Grizzleknot apparently drops the Treant Staff (8 Dmg, 35 Delay, 2HB, procs Root), and Grizzleknot Bark, which is a component in the Shaman Totemic Breastplate quest. We have heard that a Totemic BP can sell for 5,000 plat so, if you are lucky enough to nab the Grizzleknot Bark, you may well want to complete the BP quest for sale value even if you do not have a Shaman in your group.

    2. The Second Tier of Named SK MoBs
      3.

      The following three named MoBs are worth hunting because, even though their drops are not quite first-tier in power, you still can make a pretty penny selling them: Gnashmaw, Narra Tanith, and Synger Foxfyre.

      Gnashmaw, a named 18th level undead gnoll, drops a Serrated Blade (6 Dmg, 28 Delay, 1HS) and a Vial of Rabid Froth (1 charge of System Shock). We have tracked and killed him once, and he dropped the Serrated Blade. He was melee-only, very green, and easy to kill. The Serrated Blade is a nice "first magic weapon" for a lowbie.

      Narra Tanith, a 28th level Druid, also has only come up on track once in our Spire days. She commonly drops a Silverish Scimitar (6 Dmg, 22 Delay, 1HS), and rarely drops a Crown of Leaves (AC1, +5 Wis), which we were lucky enough to get. Like any Druid, Narra Tanith uses Root and DoT spells (as well as SoW), so it would not hurt to have Cancel Magic ready to cast when engaging her. Be warned: killing Narra Tanith will drop your faction with several good-aligned groups based in Qeynos. The faction hits are worth it for a shot at the Crown, though, as wisdom casters eagerly will equip that item well into their 30s, and as it can bring close to 1,000 plat at auction.

      Synger Foxfyre, a 27th level Druid, commonly drops a Double-Bladed Bone Axe (18 Dmg, 48 Delay, 2HS), and less frequently drops a Bark Shield (AC15, +5 Agi). Synger also uses Druid spells in combat. We have only tracked and killed her once; she dropped the Bark Shield for us. She was SoWed when we engaged her.

    3. The Third Tier of Named SK MoBs
      4.

The MoBs in this category have drops that were relatively useless to the Strike Force. Depending upon the makeup of your group, however, one or more of these MoBs may well be a prime target for you.

Cracktusk, a 25th level named Elephant, seems to fight above his stated level. We only tracked and killed him once. He dropped two Mammoth Tusks (used for a quest) and a Giant Cracked Tusk - a two-slot container that no website we have seen knew what to do with.

Ghanex Drah, a 15th level gnoll necromancer (and his pet), is almost always "up" on track. He drops several pieces of minor magic (i.e., a +3 Int earring, a +10 mana earring and a Crysknife (4 Dmg, 21 Delay, Piercing)).

Gnawfang is a 15th level named Lion. He drops Gnawfang's Pelt and Gnawfang's Tooth, two quest items We tracked and killed him once, but got no drop.

Knari Morawk, a 20th level named Aviak, drops an Aviak Feather (Mage research component). We tracked and killed this bird once.

Lord Grimrot: There are two versions of this spawn, an undead one and a more-powerful (and rare) "live" one. Grimrot's drops are for a Cleric/Paladin shield quest; we simply have chosen to ignore him to date. We regularly see him on track, but do not know how frequently each of his incarnations spawn.

Mroon, a 24th level named cyclops, has never shown up on our track. He apparently drops Mroon's Toy (20 Dmg, 70 Delay, 2HB) and a Topknot Headband (AC3, +3 Str, +3 Sta, +1 Dex, -10 Cha).

Various Gnoll Shamen: Shaman Lenrel, Shaman Ren'Rex, High Shaman Grisok, and High Shaman Phido all occasionally spawn within tracking range of the Spires. They range from 14th level (Lenrel and Ren'Rex) to 20-25th level (Phido). Their drops are unexceptional; they drop a few quest items (Darreb's Skull and a Red Headband) and occasionally a Runed Totem Staff (9 Dmg, 37 Delay, +5 Mana, +5 HP, 2HB). We track these frequently but have stopped hunting them.

A Werewolf: like the ones found in WK, the SK werewolf is about 25th level. The SK one drops a Locket, which is for a Paladin quest.

    1. Tracking for Pay: Money for Nothing

We close this Guide with a few words on one final way to make some cash while camping the Spires: hiring your tracking skills out. Although, in theory, a character might be willing to pay for the location of any named SK MoB, only two such MoBs in our experience have ever catalyzed characters to actually send zone-wide shouts offering plat in exchange for the location of (and/or looting rights to) the spawn: Brother Qwinn and Quillmane . If you do see a message like this come across your screen, be sure to write down the auctioning character's name so that, in the inevitable rush that starts when your track shows the right rare, you will remember who wants to pay you.

Brother Qwinn, a 60+ NPC, is part of a Monk quest and has a very uneven spawn schedule. Sometimes he is up for a whole day, but sometimes you can go several days straight without ever tracking him. We have seen desperate Monks offer as much as 500 plat for Qwinn's location. We recommend that, if you track him, you send the tracker in pursuit to get the /loc, and use another group member to ask the zone if anyone is searching for Qwinn (or to send a /tell to any character that previously had offered plat for the /loc). We advise not divulging Qwinn's /loc, or even the name of your group's tracker, until the buyer pays in full.

Quillmane is a more complex matter, because high-level Mages looking to complete their epic weapon quest could care less about most Quillmane spawns - as noted above, the Pegasus Feather Cloak drops no more than 25% of the time from Quillmane. We have, however, seen Mages offer up to 5,000 plat for looting rights to the Cloak, and have heard of offers up to 10,000 plat in value. That is a lot of cash for the mere use of your tracking skill!

Of course, there is no way to tell what Quillmane will drop until he is slain, and the No Drop nature of the Cloak makes it impossible to just loot and sell it. Accordingly, if you are looking to sell looting rights, we advise that you NEVER let on that you are tracking Quillmane until it lies dead at your feet. Then, carefully right-click on Quill's corpse - if you see the Cloak icon, be extra-careful not to put your cursor anywhere near it. Have one group member keep a right-click on the corpse (to prevent ninja looting) and have another member do the auctioning. We advise proceeding in three steps: (1) first, send /tells to any Mages who previously stated their interest in the Cloak; (2) then, do a /who Magician to identify the Mages in the zone, and then send them /tells asking if they are interested; and (3) finally, do a zone-wide /shout asking if anyone wants to purchase looting rights to the Pegasus Cloak.

Whatever you do, do not spend more than five (or so) minutes auctioning the Cloak - like any corpse, Quillmane's corpse eventually will disappear, and you certainly do not want to be left empty-handed. We suggest that you accept no less than 5,000 plat for looting rights - your potential customers will be high-level Mages who quite literally have nothing better to spend their money on.

 

The Strike Force plays on Luclin and would rather remain anonymous.
