Topic :  Class Abilities

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

Now its official. Mages and Clerics are the only people without mana regeneration spells.

I've only one comment for this thread so I'll keep it brief.

It is not our goal to make every class the same. One class getting an ability or spell doesn't entitle everyone else to that ability as well. Even when we give that ability to a bunch of other classes and not do one or two, it does not entitle everyone else to the ability.

Wizards and enchanters remain the only casting classes unable to heal themselves. Clerics are the only pure casters that can't cast invisibility or levitation. Mages are the only pure caster who cannot natively cast any movement-speed debuffs. This does not mean that there is something wrong with those classes, just that they have to depend on others to gain that capability.

- Gordon

Topic :  Clarity II

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

Is Clarity II giving more mana than Clarity I ? It appears to make little difference to the regeneration rate when sitting.

I have just tested both spells and Clarity II is faster for regeneration. I have a timer system here which lets me test it exactly.

The reason it is hard to tell is that the meditation rate is so high when this spell is received that the difference between the two spells is harder to notice when sitting.

On the other hand, it is much easier to notice when you are standing because you don't have the large meditation bonus being added each tick.

The other reason that it is harder to tell is that mana is bar based for reflecting the increase, and it can be hard to tell exactly how fast it is recovering, and when it is totally empty and full.

- Geoffrey Zatkin

Topic :  Moving Server

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

Over time, servers develop their own atmosphere or community based upon the personalities of those playing on them. With a relatively small population of players on a server, people build (or hurt) their reputations, and live and work in concert with others that they have in essence "grown up" with.

We feel that this community is one of the things that is responsible for the success of EQ. People are not faceless characters cloaked in anonymity, but instead must reap the rewards or consequences of their actions. This, over all, tends to encourage people to behave more honorably in their adventures.

Based upon the same logic that we use in regards to not changing names unless they violate the naming policy, character moves are likewise prohibited as it allows someone to escape their reputation, and also breaks down the positive aspects of a community.

We have in the past allowed characters to be moved under certain limited circumstances but that started causing more problems than it solved, and we stopped doing them. The only reason that we allow them now is for the purpose of a spreading out population, based upon the logic that enough people are moving from the server that reputations tend to follow the move. We are also planning on allowing some PvP players to move to another rule set in the near future, but that is the extent of the program.

For heaven's sake, proper policy is a general yardstick, not a prison. Want to make a lot of customer service points ? Start considering each situation on its own merits, rather than set a bureaucratic policy that cannot cover all circumstances and then refuse to budge from it.

Every time we make an exception to policy in the name of servicing special needs in one specific case, a certain course of events follows:

1. People find out that it happened.
2. People come here to the boards complaining of favoritism, wondering why they were turned down for their specific request.
3. The GMs are inundated with requests for the same service based upon similar circumstances.

As an example, we've always had a policy disallowing character moves. We used to make exceptions to this policy in the event that players felt that they were being harassed by the people on their server (This was usually a big guild vs. little guild issue). Though we never announced this exception to the policy, word about it, and the correct way to phrase a request, got out. It was not long before we were getting TONS of move requests along these lines.

This sequence of events follows nearly every time we make an exception to policy. Consistency is the only way to avoid the problems associated with making exceptions to policy, and leads to better customer service overall.

- Gordon

Topic :  More on 3rd Party Programs

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

Also, such macro programs assist disabled people who can't easily work around the limited interface of many such programs. I know you don't intend to limit these people, but that is what you would be doing. What if someone came up with an interface that responded to voice commands to play EQ because they were limited on what they can accomplish with a mouse ? You want to ban such people from your game.

It is absolutely not our intent to limit the access of disabled people to the game. The suggestion and subsequent elevating of this concern is misdirection of the issue at hand. There is no hidden oppressive meaning in the original or subsequent statements.

After thinking about the wording for a while, here's some wording that might be less open to misinterpretation:

In reference to "macroing", we're opposed to any software, device, or contraption, used by a player to perform tasks in-game while not at the computer. This is considered cheating.

I think that this wording should substantially eliminate the concerns of the disabled or those with impaired dexterity. It looks like it should also address the specific device (dunking bird, solenoid actived keyboard depressor, etc.) questions that people have mentioned.

- Gordon
