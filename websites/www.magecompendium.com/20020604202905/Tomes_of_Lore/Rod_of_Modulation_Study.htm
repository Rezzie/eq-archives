Rod of Modulation Study

 

 

Introduction

 

I have seen and been a part of the debate on the usefulness of our new level 44 summon, Modulating Rod.  Debates have raged in many forums in many threads and yet most of what is posted is opinion.  Here is my attempt to quantify the benefits of the Rod.  Take issue or agree as you see fit.  This was as much for my own information as for yours.  My hope is that in sharing it with you, we will have a basis for further debate that isn’t tied up in opinion.

 

The Spell

 

Magicians can now summon a Modulation Rod, which has a single “charge” of Modulation.  (Technical note: this item is consumable.  That means when the effect is used, the item vanishes.  Therefore one could argue it isn’t a charge).  The effect converts 225 health points to 150 mana points instantly.  The spell is a level 44 summon, meaning only magicians level 44 or higher can cast this spell.  The Rod is LORE (can only have 1 at a time) and NORENT (it will vanish on logout of link death).

 

The Weapon

 

The Rod is actually a 1HB weapon with 8/26 stats.  It’s a decent weapon.  However, with the exception of Monks it isn’t likely to be useful to those levels 44+.  Those who have melee skills either have better weapons (warriors, rangers, paladins, shadow knights, rogues) or are casting rather than swinging a weapon (clerics, shaman, druids).  Pure casters don’t melee at this level and therefore won’t use this as a weapon.  All in all, it’s a good weapon.  However, its use solely as a weapon is very limited.

 

The Effect

 

The Rod’s real power comes from its ability to modulate.  That is, exchange health for mana.  The exchange is very good at 66.6% (2 mana for each 3 health).  However, it is limited to one-time use.  Once used, it vanishes.  The negative drain on health will be stopped by Rune.  As a Magician has no class-based magical skin, I was only able to test with Rune.  (I had an Enchanter friend cast it on me to test).  Of course, the Rune is negated by the effect.  Net, net is if you have Rune up when using this effect, you will take no damage, lose the Rune and gain 150 mana.

 

The Executive Summary

 

The Rod can be summarized as having an effect that will do an approximate 300 points of damage to a mob in exchange for 225 points of damage to its user.  The potential for the Rod to do more than 300 damage is small, but not to be ignored.  In dire emergencies, the Rod may supply the mana needed to evacuate, but the conditions for this to happen are rare.  The Rod is not a replacement for the manastone—it is not viable for buffing.  It has limited use in mass resurrections.  It is an ideal weapon for monks who’d like to play around with 1HB.

 

The Detail

 

Hypothetical Party: Cleric, Enchanter, Magician, Rogue, Warrior, and Wizard

Now, don’t take issue with the merits of this party.  I think it’s typical and viable.  In any event, it’s for example’s sake.

 

Scenario 1 - Buffing

 

The Enchanter and Cleric of this party each have pre-fight buffing duties.  Buffing can take a lot of mana.  Those lucky enough to possess manastones likely make heavy use of them while buffing.  Manastones convert 60 health points to 20 mana points (33% efficiency).  Can the Rod assist the party in this scenario?

 

Each summon will require 200 mana from the Magician and 225 health from its user to return 150 mana to its user.  In this scenario, the cost to produce and the cost to consume the Modulation effect make the return negative to the party.

 

Conclusion : The Rod isn’t a tool for buffing like the manastone.

 

Scenario 2 - Mass Resurrection

 

I’ve seen it argued that the Rod will be of great benefit in the planes for mass resurrections.  That is, each person has a Rod prior to going to the plane and if killed, gives their Rod to the Cleric upon resurrect.  How useful is the Rod in this case?

 

If the Cleric is casting the level 49 resurrection spell, it costs 700 mana.  A Cleric likely complete heals those just resurrected, but to give favor to the Rod, lets assume those resurrected are healed by druids or shaman rather than the Cleric with resurrection duty.

 

Cleric resurrects 5 people: 5 x 700 = 3500 mana needed; 6 rods (Cleric’s own and 5 resurrected player’s rods) provide 800 mana at a cost of 1350 health.  Basically, for each 6 rods, the Cleric gets a “free” resurrect as long as someone is healing him/her.

 

If the Cleric has to heal him/herself, complete heal will require half the mana gained (400 of 800).  Of course, should the Cleric be casting lower level revive or resuscitate, the Rod will provide a more assistance, although healing the damage the Rod does to the Cleric will require the same mana to undo.

 

Conclusion : The Rod will help in mass resurrections, but doesn’t offer significant improvement in the overall efficiency.  Note that once this is competed, the rods would likely be replaced.  That will shift the mana drain from the Cleric to the Magician.  (Replacing 6 rods will require 1200 mana.  The rods provided the Cleric with 800 mana.  Further, mana was spent healing the Cleric).

 

Scenario 3 – Overwhelming Odds

 

There are many, many scenarios.  Far too many for me to cover all of them.  I’ll have to be general in my analysis of this scenario.  When a party is faced with overwhelming odds, those odds come from: a single mob that is too powerful for the group’s current condition and/or level or too many mobs for the group’s current condition and/or level.  In either case, the group can choose to try to fight or to flee.

 

Should the group decide to stand and fight, how much extra damage can the group do by using the rods?

 

“Damage Boost” is the percent of mana the Rod supplied to the percent of overall damage delivered for the spell.  This can be calculated in one of three ways :

 

Average Case : Go strictly by the numbers.  The Rod supplies a percent of the mana for a spell and therefore boosts the damage potential by that percentage.  Take the highest DD the caster can toss and multiply its damage by the percent of mana the Rod can supply.

 

Typical Case : Most casters have more than one offensive spell memorized.  Typically, these have different mana requirements.  For example, a Magician may have both Lava Bolt and Shock of Swords memorized.  In this case, the last offensive cast of the Magician may be “boosted” from Shock of Swords to Lava Bolt with the extra 150 mana from the Rod.  Whatever additional damage done by the player with the Rod over the player without the Rod is the increase in damage potential the Rod has given.

 

Maximum Case : The caster did not have enough mana to even cast their “second” offensive spell until they used the Rod.  In this case, without the Rod, the player would not have cast anything, but would have just stood there like a bump on a log.  The entire damage done by the player’s “second tier spell” is the damage potential of the Rod.  This won’t likely happen often as high-level DD spells require a lot more than 150 mana.  Further, the range of mana for this case to come into play is small.  Taking the Magician example, for this case to apply, the Magician must have 100 mana, which when combined with the Rod’s 150 mana will be enough to cast Shock of Swords.  But if the Magician had 250 mana, the Magician could cast Shock of Swords without the Rod.  The chance that this situation will occur for all party members simultaneously is rare (speculation).

Notice an interesting statistic.  The average damage and typical damage are generally centered around 333.  Given this, we can summarize the Rod’s effect as doing 225 damage to the user in exchange for an average of 333 damage to the target.  Total party damage (using ADB) is 1544 against a living target.  Not enough to kill many level 44+ mobs.

 

Caveats :

 

I chose spells from each class to illustrate what I felt were fair calculations.  I only play the Magician class.  I may not have chosen what someone who plays a specific class above would have chosen.

 

The above calculations are to show damage in an emergency situation.  Therefore, only direct damage spells were used.  DOTs were ignored as being too slow to save the party from being overwhelmed.

 

Maximum damage were used.  Lower level spells may be less likely to stick, but are also less likely to get interrupted.  Higher-level spells are also more likely to fizzle.  I don’t have the numbers for all of that.  Therefore, your mileage may vary.

 

There are many ways to combine the above and create different scenarios that don’t involve straight damage.

 

The Cleric may use the Rod’s effect to cast a heal.

The Enchanter may use the Rod’s effect to summon an animation, charm or stun.

The Magician may use the Rod’s effect to summon a pet.

The Wizard may use the Rod’s effect to evacuate the party.

 

Conclusion : The damage potential of a caster is raised approximately 333 points at a sacrifice of 225 health points on the part of its user.  To get this benefit, a Magician must invest 200 mana points in the Rod prior to the battle.  The use of the Rod is therefore limited in that typically, it will do only 48% more damage to a mob than to the player that uses it.

 

Should the group decide to flee, how much use is a Rod?

 

This one is easy.  All evacuate spells are 300 mana.  The Rod might be able to cover a fizzle, but the Wizard or druid will need at least 150 mana before the Rod is of any use.  Note that using multiple rods in this scenario is possible, it isn’t real viable as players would need to pass them to the caster while taking hits.

 

Caveats :

 

Typically in the planes and on raids, very large groups go into the zone.  You simply don’t evacuate when things get bad on a raid.  If members are dying, they are healed or they die to be resurrected when possible.  Therefore, the Rod will not be of use for evacuate unless the entire raiding party (all groups) calls for an evacuate and the casters have at least 150 mana, but less than 300.

 

In my own experience, evacuate was called early against mobs we could not hope to beat. There’s little point in standing and fighting when things go so horribly wrong, death is certain (not talking about planes or dragon raids).  Generally this means the Wizard or druid is protected as much as possible while they try to evacuate.  In this case the Rod will be of little use since the caster will still have plenty of mana.

 

Conclusion : The Rod has a very small chance of being needed to evacuate the party.  If that chance comes up and the spell is successfully cast, the benefits are huge.
