    Yep, I was right. I didn't like it.
    *sigh*

    It's always frustrating to me to try and discuss this topic because it's just so obvious to me how much more fun the game would be if there were less (or no) class restrictions based on race. This isn't to say that I'm right and you're wrong and neener neener neener if you don't like it. Not at all.     But...

    On some level, it's just so blindingly obvious to me -- so fundamentally self evident -- that the game could and would be so much more fun with fewer racial class restrictions (or no racial class restrictions at all), that I have a hard time explaining it. It's like if someone said "Why shouldn't I burn off this dog's tail with a blowtorch?" I wouldn't be able to explain logically and rationally why they shouldn't do it. WHY CAN'T I MAKE YOU UNDERSTAND HOW MUCH MORE *FUN* THE GAME WOULD BE IF I COULD BE AN OGRE BARD OR AN ERUDITE WARRIOR?!?"

    (Actually, since I'm a gnome, I'd probably grab them and just shake myself back and forth briskly while they remained motionless. Ah, the perils of being the proper size while surrounded by all you giants... [biggrin])

    Having said all of that, I'll try to explain why I feel the way I feel...

    Like many (perhaps most) of the people who play EQ, I have an extensive background in reading Science Fiction and Fantasy books. I grew up reading authors like Asimov and Heinlein. As I got older, I started reading more fantasy and less SF. These days, I'm much more likely to read some cheesy Forgotten Realms book than I am to read Science Fiction. What can I say? I just like the swords and sorcery Fantasy setting.

    But the characters that have always held the most interest for me were those characters that were "different." Characters that didn't adhere to standard Sword-n-Sorcery conventions. I think the first character like this I ever ran across was Elric, from the Moorcock books.

    Elric was the antithesis of the typical sword-n-sorcery hero, particularly of heroes like Conan and Fahfrd. The typical Sword-n-Sorcery hero was big, strong, and physically powerful. They were often uncivilized (both Conan and Fahfrd were barbarians). They had no little (if any) tolerance for the arcane arts. And they were very clearly Good Guys(tm).

    Elric was physically frail, to the point where, before he got Stormbringer, he required drugs to survive. He embraced civilization and longed for its trappings when he was away. He used Sorcery to further his own goals. And he was not always a Good Guy(tm).

    And not only was Elric a departure from scores of fantasy heroes which preceded him, but he was also a rebel among his own kind. He disliked the decadence into which his own race had fallen, and was responsible for leading the sacking of his own hometown. This (among other things) is how he earned the nickname "Kinslayer."

    But Elric was just the first. He's been followed by a string of other characters, heroes and anti-heroes (and occasionally villains) who were interesting because they didn't fit into pre-existing notions of how they should behave. Probably the most well-known of these characters are Drizzt Do'Urden, the Dark Elf Ranger from the R.A. Salvatore books and Dragonbait, the Lizardman Paladin from the "Azure Bonds" books by Jeff Grubb. And there are dozens of others.

    And I'm pretty sure that I'm not the only one who finds these characters much more interesting than their mundane counterparts. Any Human can be a Ranger. But a *Dark Elf*? Why would a Dark Elf be a Ranger? Why isn't he Evil like the rest of his kind? What drove him away from the underground and towards the light? Where did he learn his Ranger skills? The story behind these questions is much more interesting (to me, and to lots of other people) than the story behind why J. Random Human became a Ranger.

    This interest in non-standard, convention-breaking characters has followed me all through my RPG career (spanning over 20 years now). I have always played a non-standard character in all of my RPGs. I was the player who was always pestering the GM to let me bend (or break) the racial class restrictions a tad so I could play something fun and unique. My favorite AD&D character was an Orc Warrior that my GM allowed me to run.

    Even within EverQuest, I have always played non-standard characters. The reason I picked a Gnome Warrior is because they're different. I also have an Ogre Warrior who was raised by Erudites. He wears glasses, and speaks with a cultured, sophisticated accent -- right up until he goes Berzerk, at which point he degenerates into traditional Ogrespeak and starts yelling "THROG SMASH! THROG KILL! GRAAAR!" (He keeps talking like this until the Berzerk wears off, at which point he apologizes to his group and becomes painfully embarassed about his behavior.) I once started a Human Wizard and dumped 15 points into STR and 15 points into STA and claimed that his mother was a Barbarian, and he'd inherited his physical (and mental) traits from his mother's side. But his father had been a Wizard. And his grandfather had been a Wizard. And his great-grandfather had been a Wizard. So he was determined to be a Wizard as well, even though he was much more suited to the life of a Melee class.

    And so on, and so forth, etc. I have always had an interest in playing non-standard characters, because standard characters are...well...standard. And to me, "standard" equals "boring."

    To this, you say:

    "Roleplaying requires a setting. You can't roleplay
    characters without a universe for them to exist in.
    Characters, therefore, should be a logical outcome of
    living in that world (and of course that world needs
    to make sense). Iksar paladins don't fit into the world
    of Norrath. Iksar have been isolated for a very long
    time. They have only one god, and he certainly doesn't
    support the idea of paladins. That is a function of the
    nature of iksar, as they exist on Norrath. It's part of
    the world. Creating a character that is explicitly
    contrary to that world isn't roleplaying. "

    Allow me to point out two things.

    1) Verant defines the fiction. If Verant had created the Iksar race and said "There is a heretical sect of good-aligned Iksars hidden within a secret camp in the Field of Bone. Iksars who follow this sect can be Paladins." then *voila*, that's the fiction. That's what they did with Erudites. Erudites are noble and good -- except for those Heretics in Paineel. So you can't really justify your argument with "Well, that's not fiction." You work for the company that creates the fiction, and they can define any fiction they choose if it suits their needs.

    2) Having said that, yes, I agree that setting is important. And, for better or for worse, Verant has set the initial fiction in stone. Iksars can't start off as Paladins. Ogres can't start off as Bards. Erudites can't start off as Warriors. And I agree that it would be kinda silly to have completely unrestricted race/class combinations at character creation. I mean, I just can't see an Ogre Paladin guild in Oggok.

    But.

    People (and Iksars, and Ogres, and Erudites) can change, if they want. Who's to say that an Ogre Warrior, travelling outside of Oggok for the first time, might not run across a bard in the Feerrott, and be entranced by the songs of the bard, to the point where he becomes determined to be a bard himself? Who's to say that an Erudite, after visiting Qeynos for the first time, becomes filled with visions of martial glory after visiting the Grounds of Fate, and becomes determined to be a Warrior?

    So for me, I'm not irked by the fact that my beginning class selection is racially restricted. But I do think there should be a way to change to a different class via a quest.

    Before you start shaking your head and saying "No no no, out of the question," hear me out. I have what I think is a perfectly workable (in the sense of "won't unbalance the game and won't break fiction") idea to allow in-game class changes.

    1) For every class in the game, create at least one avenue where anyone of any race/class/deity combo raise faction with a guild of that class. It doesn't necessarily have to be an exciting or easy way to raise faction. Maybe it's tedious and boring. And make it so where it doesn't raise faction with anybody else but the guild in question.

    2) Create an NPC that gives out a "change of class" quest for that guild. The quest should be challenging on the order of the Crafted Armor quests.

    2a) Make it so where you can't switch classes if your race could have been that class from the beginning. So if you're a human, you can't switch to any class but Shaman (since humans can be every other class).

    3) Require the player character to be at least 20th level and have "Ally" level faction before the NPC gives out the "change of class" quest.

    4) Once the character completes the quest, the character undergoes a ceremony which transforms him/her into a 1st level character of the desired class -- with the same stats as a 1st level character of the desired class. All those weapon skills you've been raising for 20 levels? Reset to zero. All those other skills you've been learning for 20 levels, like Bind Wound and Baking? Reset to zero. All those hit points you had as a 20th level character? Gone. You're a 1st Level character with the same HP as any 1st level character would have based on your class and STA. You may have been a 20th level Ogre SK with 1000 hp before, but now you're a 1st level Ogre Enchanter with 30 HP. All the equipment you had on you? Gone. You're now the proud owner of 5 biscuits, 5 flasks of milk, and the standard Newbie weapon for your class. (Sure, this could be bypassed with the help of a friend. But then it's just like twinking, and that's pretty common in the game. So you're not really making the situation worse.)

    4a) The character's deity is also changed if the new class is incompatible with the character's original deity. The new class/deity combination will not improve faction with anyone. Everybody who disliked you before still dislikes you. But faction can get worse, depending on the nature of the deity change.

    5) This is a one-time only quest. Once you've changed your class, you can't change it back, or change to another class. (Word gets around that you've changed your class once, and other guild leaders will refuse to accept you on the assumption that you're a dilettante.)

    So, to illustrate an example:

    Throckmorton the Troll wants to be a Ranger. To become a Ranger, Throckmorton has to first find a way to increase faction with one of the Ranger guilds in the game (Kithicor Rangers, Protectors of the Pine, or Champions of Faydark) until he is considered an "Ally" of this guild. Note that being an Ally with one of these guilds doesn't necessarily convey the automatic ability to wander nonchalantly up to the guild trainers. For example, if Throckmorton raises his Protectors of the Pine faction without raising his JaggedPine Treefolk faction, then his trip to see Hager Sureshot in Surefall Glade will end the minute he runs across one of the druids in Surefall.

    Once he has found a way to increase faction to Ally, and found a way to approach the "change-of-class" quest NPC without getting killed, he then has to wait until he's 20th level before he can even do the quest. Once he hits 20th level, he's given a quest along the lines of "Go get a common drop from a rare MOB and bring it back." Once he completes this quest, he is transformed into a 1st level character of the new class. He has the original STR, STA, DEX, etc stats that he started with initially, but all his combat skills are reset to zero and his HP is calculated based on his new class and current Level and STA. So before the change, he was a 20th level Warrior with several hundred HP. Now he's a 1st level Ranger with 30 HP.

    Since he can't be a Ranger and worship Cazic-Thule, he also had to switch deities. So he picked Karana. Alas, the only people who've heard about his conversion are the people in Neriak, Grobb, and Oggok. So anybody he was KOS to before, he's still KOS to now. Plus he's now KOS in places he used to not be KOS in.

    The reason I think this will work is because:

    1) It won't be a cakewalk. Being allied with the Bard Guild in Qeynos doesn't mean a thing if you're KOS to the Qeynos Guards and the Qeynos Merchants. So if you're an Ogre who wants to be a Bard, you'll either have to find some way to sneak in to the Bard Guild without being seen by any of the merchants or guards, or you'll have to raise faction with the Qeynos Guards and Merchants as well.

    2) It's not easy to abuse. Requiring that someone be Allied faction *and* 20th level before even being given the *quest* to change class will keep a lot of folks from doing it, because they won't want to go through all the tedium of raising faction for 20 levels, only to get to start all over at level 1 and go back through the first 20 levels again.

    3) It's not unbalancing. Your faction won't be any better after the class change than it was before. However, it might very well be worse. And being an weak Erudite Warrior or a dumb Troll Enchanter will confer its own challenges.

    4) It's consistent with existing fiction. Yes, in the world of Norrath, most Ogres are mindless brutes who eat dwarves and beat each other with swords just for funsies. So no Ogre can *start* as a Bard. That's just not part of the Ogre culture on Norrath. But if an Ogre goes adventuring, meets a Bard, and decides that he RILLY RILLY RILLY wants to *be* a Bard, he can, if he's willing to pay his dues.

    5) It encourages creativity and roleplaying. People will be encouraged to come up with reasons for why their Gnome is a Shadow Knight, or that their Ogre is an Enchanter, and this is a good thing. And, for the most part, I think that the majority of the people who would even bother doing this will be those people who are inclined towards roleplaying anyways.

    Now, obviously, this may not necessarily be an easy coding job. But from a gameplay, game balance, and game fiction standpoint, I think it makes a whole lot of sense. I can't think of any negatives that would occur from a system like this.

I cannot express how much I would [b]***LOVE*** to play on a server that had a mechanism like I describe for changing classes. I play on Brell. I've been playing on Brell since August 1999. I love my guild, and I love my guildmates. And I would leave them all in a heartbeat to play on a server that would allow me to be an Ogre Bard or an Erudite Warrior. (Actually, I wouldn't really be leaving them. I know 'em well enough that they'd be racing me to the new server so they could play a neat new character.) So I'm asking you to please please PLEASE look this over and don't reject it out of hand just because you don't personally agree with it. Even if this isn't something you would want to implement on all the servers, I think it's something you could easily implement one one server. So those of us who wanted to be unique characters could do so, while those of you who think it "breaks fiction" wouldn't have to play on that server and wouldn't have to be confronted with High Elf Monks and Dwarf Magicians.

    Thanks a lot for your time!

    My response to you is the same that it was to the last person. In a world with no restrictions, you can't be an iconoclast.

    I love to play the outcast. I love having some sort of stigma attached to my characters. In a recent D&D campaign we were to play members of a once-great society that had fallen to a plague. They now find themselves surrounded by wild lands. Into those wild lands they toss their criminals, and those criminals are often bitter.

    When handed the character creation information, I read that we could play outcasts if we chose to. The GM stressed this several times in the written material and in person. I know my fellow players, we've been playing together for years. I knew that at most three of us would choose to be outcasts. It turns out only two of us did (the other went to the other extreme, hating outcasts to with a passion).

    I wouldn't have chosen to be an outcast if it wouldn't have been a hard character to play, and I certainly wouldn't have done it if I thought that the majority of the group would have done it.

    But that's a small group, and a group that knows that when our GM says "playing an outcast is going to be HARD", he means it.

    When you're talking about thousands of players, a large number of which are fans of the same fantasy authors that we're all fans of, you'll find that a large number of players will want to play the outcast. They either won't believe that it will be hard to do, or they'll expect things to be made easier if they complain enough.

    That's why I love the new rules for Sullon Zek. You can play the iconoclast, the outsider, and there are real in-game consequences for that choice.

    Without those consequences (making it hard to accomplish won't be a consequence, once you've done that, then things will be smooth sailing) you're unique Drizzt will be one of many Drizzts, and he won't be nearly as much fun. And he certainly won't be the unique character that he is in the world of his creator. In a novel the character can remain unique. Make him an option in a massively multiplayer game and he will become too common.

    That's my opinion, anyhow.

    quote:

    Hey guys, as I get ready to head out for the Fan Faire I wanted to sit down and write up this post I've been putting off all week. A couple quick notes.

If the Rain killed the NPC and the server was unsure where the damage came from the corpse would poof, so these spells were coded in this manner to prevent that from happening. I'm working with the coders to come up with a better solution. We're all pretty busy right now so unfortunately I can't give you a time frame on when a better solution will be implemented.

Brad on Exploiting Bugs
- Aurum @ 7:34 PM PDT
Brad spoke on exploiting bugs in response to more feedback and letters received about the banning of the guild Conquest. Here's the update from the Dev Corner:

    Hello all,

    Recently I've not only received emails specifically regarding the Conquest guild issue, but also about exploiting in general.

    First, some people are confused as to what an exploit is and how they're to know what an exploit is. Some are also afraid they might accidentally exploit and then be disciplined.

    An exploit, quite simply, is using a bug in the game to your advantage. Most of them are obvious: attacking NPCs that are stuck, or the ability to duplicate items or money. But we do realize it's not always easy to tell.

    To this end it has been and will continue to be our policy to warn people when they are caught exploiting. It basically works like this:

    1. You are caught exploiting, you receive a warning and the exploit is explained to you.
    2. You are caught exploiting again (this includes either literally being caught or caught and we have evidence you were aware it was an exploit -- in other words, premeditated exploiting) and you are suspended for a period of time.
    3. You are caught exploiting yet again -- you are banned.

    A second issue that's arisen is the question: why do we care if people are exploiting? Are we just upset they're not playing the game the way we wanted them to?

    The answer is honestly "no, that has nothing to do with it." The following is an email and my reply to it which I think more clearly explains why we cannot allow bug exploitation in EverQuest:

    -----Original Message-----
    From: Robert Mayer
    Sent: Tuesday, June 12, 2001 7:30 AM
    To: bmcquaid@verant.com
    Subject: Question

    You guys seem to be holding players responsible for doing things the game allows but you don't intend to happen. You are labeling as "exploits" anything that is possible, but doesn't fit into the game as you designed it.

    Then I guess I failed to communicate the issues very effectively, because that is absolutely NOT what we're doing. We are labeling exploits as we always have, that being using bugs to your advantage in-game. Conquest used spell stacking bugs to their advantage and they used a safe zone (an area where a pathing bug made it such that an NPC wouldn't pursue those it was mad at) to their advantage.


    Now correct me if I'm wrong, but it seems that in effect you're saying "look, we aren't perfect, we can't code a game that is 100% free of loopholes because nobody can, but you should as players be able to infer what we intend, and if you do things that are clearly going against our intent, that's an exploit and you'll be disciplined."

    Yes and no. We're saying that taking advantage of bugs is NOT ok. And we're saying that 1. knowing you're exploiting a bug and 2. where and to what end your exploiting, are both factors in determining how we react to a player or players who are exploiting.


    Whew. I've never heard a game company essentially say that the players are responsible for the developer's programming, design, and implementation errors. No offense, because I realize it's a bitch to run a game like EQ, but it seems absurd to me to expect players to do anything but try their utmost to use every single tool you give them, deliberately or accidentally, to succeed. After all, the only point of EQ, beyond chatting, is to level up, and the only rewards beyond the social come from leveling up and killing things. You put umpteen thousand people into a world and tell them "go forth and kill MOBs," and by gum they're gonna do it. You can't reasonably then expect them to adhere to something like "oh, and only kill MOBs the way we intend for you to kill them!"

    No, they're responsible for not taking advantage of bugs in-between the time they're discovered and when we have a chance to fix them. And we've always had this policy. UO certainly has this policy, and I think AC does to an extent now. And I'd submit the majority of persistent, massively multiplayer community based games in the future will have these policies. Why? Well, because what you do in a community based game like this affects other players. If you take advantage of a dupe bug, you can hurt the economy, and ruin the play experience of hundreds if not thousands of other players. If you take advantage of a pathing bug and kill a monster to obtain loot that you really shouldn't be able to, then the guy who did work harder to obtain that same loot without exploiting is not going to feel very entertained. Rather, he's going to feel frustrated and perhaps even regret that he did it the 'right way'. It's the same as in real life -- society is generally NOT ok with taking short cuts (especially at the expense of others). Insider Trading is not ok. Cutting through a parking lot to avoid traffic is not ok. Embezzling and stealing and cheating to advance oneself at the expense of others is NOT ok. And so why the heck should it be ok in the communities and societies that are being developed in online computer games? Because it's a game? Does my sense of accomplishment and are my feelings about right and wrong somehow less valid inside a virtual reality than they are in the real world?

    No man is an island, and that includes a person in Norrath.


    From reading all of the public info on the Conquest incident, it seems to me that these guys simply took advantage of holes your programmers left in the game. If an NPC can't summon or move for X seconds or due to Y geometry, um, tough titty to you, Kitty--fix it. If you leave it in, you can't ethically blame someone for taking advantage of it. It's your responsibility, and you'rs (Verant's) alone, to make the game unexploitable.

    Agreed to a point, and expanding on that, it's Verant's responsibility to make sure we are offering an entertaining and fair play environment for our customers. And so we fix bugs, and we also crack down on players who would use bugs to cheapen the playing experience of others.

    And I disagree it's unethical to blame a player for taking advantage of a bug. If a person leaves the door open at their place of business, thereby creating a hole in the company's security, yes, it's the person's fault for doing this. But that doesn't mean it's ok for someone to come along and enter the building, taking advantage of the open door. He gets blamed too.


    Now, if players change the code, hack it, or abuse other players, disciplinary action seems warranted. But to even bother suspending players whose only real "crime" seems to be to take advantage of coding problems on your end--well, you're asking way too much of your customers.

    But taking advantage of coding or data problems can in some cases ABUSE OTHER PLAYERS, which is the point I apparently failed miserably to communicate, because it keeps coming up, not just in your email but in others.


    Once you create the world, it isn't yours any more, just like when a novelist writes a book. If someone wants to interpret The Stand as an allegory about French cooking, there's nothing Stephen King can do about it. Yeah, an online game isn't as passive as a book, but it's the same concept. You guys seem to be holding on way too tight. Who cares if they "exploit" the flaws in the programming? Turn off the zone, fix the flaws, and go on. Every single exploit you guys identify should be seen as an opportunity to improve the game or refine it--that's good, and it's your right. But to discipline players for finding your mistakes… man, that's pretty low.

    Again, reading a book is not a shared, community based experience. Misinterpreting a book, and for a better analogy, cheating in a single player game does NOT hurt the play experience for others. But, and this is the key point here, cheating in a shared community based online experience DOES.
