    quote:

Absor Infobits
- Aurum @ 7:52 PM PDT
Things picked up for a change on the Developer's Corner today. Here's the latest from Alan VanCouvering:

    Faction Cap Messages
    At this time faction cap messages are broken. Players that are at their cap (either low or high) are not getting a message telling them that their faction cannot be raised/lowered. The caps are still in place and everything is working as normal. No caps were changed. It's just the messages that are broken.

    We should have this fixed with the next patch.

    Problems Logging In
    If you're having a problem logging in and the system is telling you that your Game Card does not exist, there's a way around the problem.

    Find the testeverquest.exe file in your EverQuest directory and run that. After the Test patcher downloads files to you, click cancel (you don't need to actually log onto the Test server, just download the files). Then log into the game as you normally would (you'll probably end up downloading a few more files).

    This should let you in the game again.

    We're sorry for the inconvenience.

    Lending a Helping Hand
    [Quote: Hey all,

    I want to share with you an EQ experience I had last night.

    I was in Rivervale making hundreds of heady kiolas, in prepration for some tailoring work, when a "can you help me please?" appears in my chat box. My first thought, of course, was that the person wanted a port (im a druid) - I am normally more than happy to port people, but I was well into making heady kiola's and really didn't want to break my rythym (I needed 320 of the damn things :)) - if you have ever done much trade skill stuff, you will know what I mean. My usual good nature won out though (heh :)), so I turned around, ready to do a port.

    Standing in front of me was a naked level 1 halfling warrior. My next thought, of course, was "damn beggars". I didn't say that though, and asked this person what I could do for him.

    "Where do I go to kill things?", he asked. I was a bit shocked, I'll admit - it's not the sort of question I get asked alot these days.

    "Misty Thicket is where you should be hunting, as a level 1 halfling", I replied.

    "How do I get there? And are there dragons there?"

    After explaining to him that no, there were in fact no dragons in Misty Thicket ("says dragons on the box") and SoWed him and said "Follow me, friend". When we got to the Misty Thicket zone, he mentions "I just bought the game." Instantly, I go into massive help mode - I have a huge soft spot for true "newbies" to Everquest (not your beggar types, but your real, "hey-what's-this-game" type newbies). True to form for most computer game players (myself included :)), he hadn't read the manual, so over the next 2 hours or so, I explained to him the very basics of the game (how to attack, how to set up hotkeys, /loc, how to get his newbie tunic, how to train skills, etc, etc) and then took him out to Misty Thicket, threw a few buffs on him (no damage shield though :)), and helped him get to level 2. He may have only been killing what most of us regard as newbie "yard-trash", but he was *loving* it.

    "I'm the man! I'm the bat, wolf, rat and bixie killing man!" he declared at one point :).

    As I helped him, and answered his questions, it occurred to me, for the first time in a very long time, that EverQuest is a bloody hard game to get started in.

    I have been playing for almost two years now, and have spent 18 months playing Sorran and over that time have learnt so much about the game, that I almost take that knowledge for granted. Answering this newbie's questions really slammed home how *vast* this world of Norrath is. I took pause and sent a silent /salute to Verant for this extraordinary creation. Many of us may question many of their decisions, but the fact remains, regardless of your specific complaints or concerns, there exists a truly magical world at the heart of it all. It is breath-taking in its scope and granduer - once in a while, take the time out from your xp camps or raids to "smell the roses". I got a chance to look at Norrath through the eyes of a newbie last night, and it blew me away all over again.

    Most of us can never again experience that true moment of newbie-wonder, not even in another game. We know too much about MMORPGs work now - no matter the game, we expect there to be a "newbie" period, we know there will be "spolier" sites to get info from, we know there will be certain classes/skills/items that will be better than others, we expect hackers/cheaters/scammers, and so on. The creatures, the game mechanics, the environment may all be totally different from game to game but, at their core, they are all the same - they are all MMORPGs. For those of us already playing them, we can never again be a true newbie.

    For me, Everquest is my first MMORPG - I clearly remember my wonder as I ventured beyond North Qeynos for the first time and into the Hills of Qeynos - I was awestruck at this *huge* zone (hah! :)), amazed at the sunsets behind the hills, terrified of following the road deeper into the Hills. I can still appreciate the visual splendour of the world of Norrath, but I am far more jaded player than I was 2 years ago.

    I am a level 60 druid, wearing nice gear, proudly wielding my epic weapon, and lucky enough to be in a great guild that is currently exploring the "end-game" of Everquest as it stands at the moment (North wing of the Temple of veeshan, Sleeper's Tomb). I have a lot of fun, and get to fight some pretty nasty creatures. But you know what? For two hours last night, I envied that level 1 halfing warrior, taking his very first steps into Norrath. I mourned for the loss of my MMORPG "innocence".

    So again, /salute to Verant for letting us all into Norrath (and /bonk for the silly decisions of course :)).

    And /salute to that halfing warrior who let me share in his first steps into Norrath - you may have thanked me for my help, but I am far more indebted to you than you can imagine.

    Cheers,

    Sorran
    Heirophant of the Vagrants
    Tribunal Server
    World of Norrath.

    P.S. Maybe other's could send in their "newbie-wonder" moments here? :)]

    Nicely done.

    I remember a few moments like that. There's a certain awe in being there when someone gets to experience things for the first time. It's always a blast to help out a truly new player. I encourage you all to give it a try. :)

    Rather than starting up a thread here about "newbie-wonder" moments, how about doing in on the Newbie Zone? I think this would be a good topic for that board.

    I posted it here mostly because you reminded me that some of the best times I've had in game were showing people how to play. And it's nice to post a bit of a sappy story some times. :)

    Thanks.

New Edition of Ask Brad: 08-14-01
- Aurum @ 5:33 PM PDT
A new installment of Ask Brad was published on the Developer's Corner:

    Ask Brad -- 8/14/01

    ----

    Why are quests in EQ taking more and more people to complete? It seems to me that this game has gone overboard with making content for the 50+ people in very large guilds.

    It now takes more then one full group to complete a guest for any item with ok stats, and in many cases it takes 3 to 4 full groups to get items for quests. Why? Only 10 to 15% of the people who play EverQuest will be able to get any of the better quest items and I am not talking about the UBER items.

    I hope to see when SoL comes out that quest can be done with just one person, or at the max of one full group.

    -Gindain

    P.S. I love the new screen shots of SoL. More, More! :)

    Luclin is indeed being designed moreso for one or two groups of players and focused overall less on raids. It will also provide content for all levels, 1-60.

    I do think, however, that Velious’ attention to raids and other epic battles was and is important. While it is true that not everyone will reach 60 or have the time to involve themselves in large guilds, the playerbase as a whole still enjoys that content vicariously. MMORPGs need rumors and legends of awesome, virtually unbeatable foes against which mortals send armies. Just looking around at various message boards you can find all sorts of players debating the high-end of the game. In fact, when we sometimes change aspects of the high level game we get all sorts of feedback, both negative and positive, from players who aren’t even close to experiencing the content we changed. Why? Because it’s a goal for them – they plan on encountering that content one day, no matter how far in the future.

    I remember playing a certain MUD several years ago and being low level and in a newbie dungeon. I overheard some mid level players talking about another group who’d allegedly fought a dragon. Immediately I approached them and asked them if there were really dragons in the game, and if so why haven’t I seen any? Where were they, I inquired, and what were they like?

    Well, not only did they have no clue how to get to where dragons were, they also let me know that it would take months and months of hard work before I’d even have a chance to encounter a dragon. That was an important day for me. It was at that moment I knew the MUD was for me – a game that had legends and rumors and where experiencing the ‘end-game’ was something to really strive for.

    So while I know we’ve been criticized for paying too much attention to ‘uber guilds’ and their needs and wants, I do think there needs to be a balance. There should be content at the high end for single groups, massive raids, and everything in between. And to that end, Shadows of Luclin should complement the Scars of Velious quite well.

    Ps. More SoL screenshots are on there way – hang in there :)

You might get more people to continue to play, and pay, for both if you give a monthly fee discount if a player has both accounts active. Maybe make the EQ fee $5 for players also paying for SW:G. Any chance of this happening?

    Tennaj
    Cleric of Farstorm

    While it’s certainly far too early to announcing pricing plans, we are definitely thinking about how it might work with the suite of titles we’re planning on having one day. I think there probably will be many players who might be interested in multiple premium titles, and I think we should have pricing that is attractive to them. I think its both in our interest and the players out there that they are each able to experience EverQuest, SW:G, Planetside, and Sovereign and decide which, if not all, they are interested in playing.

(After the upcoming Vah Shir and Beastlord additions) I was asking simply because I would LOVE to play a dragon-derived race...like a half human/half dragon. I guess similar to Iksar, but based on Dragon rather than Lizard descent. Because they were humanoid rather than full dragon, there wouldn't be any issues with unfair advantages over other races. This race would be good as a Shaman, Enchanter (dragons and gems/gold *grin*), probably Warrior...although they would probably lean more toward magic type classes. Perhaps even a class similar to a Mage with dragon-specific pets? I'm still playing around with the possibilities, but I'm sure you all would think of something :)
    Thanks for your time.

    -Worddeity

    That’s a neat idea and I certainly wouldn’t rule out the possibility of future new races and classes. But, honestly, I doubt one would be too lizard-like (or dragon-like) in that we already have the Iksar. We’d probably be more inclined to introduce a race significantly different from any other existing race in order to make the game more interesting.

    Yes.

    I think it’s related to risk vs. reward. Mid level dungeons, especially on older servers where higher-end items have trickled down, just don’t have treasure that’s appealing enough to lure players into a more risky area. One of our hopes with the Trivial Loot Code is to be able to put better items in mid-level dungeons in order to entice mid-level players back into them.

I guess what I'm asking is why do you continue to develop new mid level zones when a large majority of the current mid level zones never get used, while at the same time high levels are forced to fight in zones with 80 plus people.

    Nylak

    In addition to making sure there are several high-level dungeons on Luclin, we also plan on converting some of the existing and underused older dungeons to high-level zones. We realize that it’s not just treasure, but also distance that can frustrate low and mid-level players and that some zones are just too far away. Around the time of Luclin’s release or soon after, several of these zones will be changed such that higher-level players are their target audience.

    Wow, if I recall correctly, I think it was mostly Steve Clover’s idea. It was also, by the way, Steve Clover who originally named the game ‘EverQuest’.     In other words, there will be three total SKUs:

    1. EverQuest Trilogy (EQ+Kunark+Velious)
    2. The Shadows of Luclin (Luclin only)
    3. EverQuest mini-box (EQ only)

GM Event on The Tribunal
- Aurum @ 5:14 PM PDT
I received a new report from Alvaron Silverleaf:

Today the oddest thing happened. A Chef Gokke, an ogre hailing from Neriak apparantly, sought out people in the Western Commonlands to retrieve the heads of his lost dinner, three little rats. Being a ranger myself, I sought out these rats not necessarily to help the ogre, but to protect the people of Freeport from getting nipped by the beasts and catching some disease. I ended up finding them racing around the entrance of Freeport, along with 15 other people trying to kill them. Apparantly, Gokke might have slipped something in their water since they were racing around and jumping all over the place, evading the blades of any who tried to hit them. Spells didn't even seem to work upon them! Me being of only my 10th rank, realize I am not the best at either magic or swordmanship. So, being the resourceful ranger that I am, I take out my bow, say a quick prayer to Tunare, pull out an arrow, aim and shoot. A hit! Making more hits than misses, I finally fell the beast. About to shout my victory, a scoundrel of a wizard runs up and takes the head of the rodent before I can! Needless to say I was furious, but realizing there where still two more rats, I began the chase once again. This time, others had been able to hit the rat so it was already a little weak, but weaker still after I unleashed many more arrows into it's hide. Victory again, though this time I grab the head before anyone else and safely stash it in my pouch.

Then I raced to Neriak as swiftly as I can, avoiding the grasp of the dreaded dragoons patrolling the forest. Upon my entering, I find the Chef with a few other people, one of whom had another of the three heads. I walked slowly up to the brute and handed him the rat head. He smiled a big toothy grin and handed me a nice reward of 100 gold. As I was about to walk away, he stops me and says he has one more thing. So I stop and he goes a little table and takes out an obviously moldy loaf of bread, cuts two slices and slaps the rat head between them. I thought it was for him. I was wrong. He comes back to me, and hands me the sandwich with the whiskers peeking out the side. Now I have seen many a battle and in war, one sees many an unpleasant thing, but never have I ever smelt or seen anything as nausiating as that sandwich and I know only Tunare kept me from becoming violently ill at that moment. So taking the sandwich as carefully as I can, along with holding my nose, I put it into my pouch. Perhaps some dogs will find it appeatising. I go over to a nearby corner and rest a moment after my long run from Freeport to here. As I waited, Chef Gokke started getting impatient about the last head and said he was really starting to get hungry for his dinner. Of course, some Teir'dal began showing up at the time also and heard Gokke's laments. One casually told him to eat me, or at least take my head and eat that. Needless to say, with a now drooling ogre and a circle of cackling and grinning dark elves beginning to form around me, I grabbed my gear and raced out of their as quick as I could, practically running right into the dragoons. Thankfully, they were too startled to see a Feir'dal running from their own city to react quick enough and before they knew it, I was gone.

Well, I can assure you, that will be the last time I aid an ogre, Tunare forgive me. Hopefully next time, my adventuring will keep me well away from any half-starved, half-ton, foul smelling brutes seeking aid.

"From the journals of Alvaron Silverleaf,
Waywatcher of the Fay and loyal ranger of Tunare ..."

/ooc

I think the best part was watching all these high level people trying to run down these little rats and not really being able to harm them. Even more funny was that people thought I was part of the event since I was really the only one hitting them ;) All and all, after I got my 100gp and rat sandwich, I stuck around alittle bit and me and the GM Chef Gokke played around, resulting in me getting another 50pp for my roleplaying with him. Perhaps I didn't explain that well enough.

It's not always the ease with which a bug is found that determines when it gets fixed. Yes, a 'smaller' bug will often wait to be fixed behind a larger one, regardless of the effect of the bug. A bug that is causing barbarian kilts to turn pink will probably wait behind a bug that causes barbarians to take 50% more (or less) damage in combat.

But it also has to do with how easy it is to fix something. This aggro bug has, obviously, been pretty darn hard to pin down. Its importance has been high the whole time, but as you can tell by the fact that we already thought we had it fixed, fixing it hasn't been an easy thing.

The perception that we prioritize bugs based on some desire to be mean to our customers is a wrong. I can only guess as to its cause, but it appears to be that people take far more notice of the bug fixes that we make that they don't like than the ones that they do.

I'm certainly not scooting around. We fix bugs as fast as we can, prioritized by their affect on the game. There may be cases, and the aggro code is one, where the bug was introduced by a recent code change and fixing that might take priority over some older bug of similar import. But that happens as a matter of practicality. Someone was just looking at that code and it's ultimately easier to fix something that was just recently broken than something that will require research to reproduce and examine.

To be brief, I think the heart of the frustration you're seeing is from the fact that the pet aggro was a bug before this "abuse" was discovered. And what the general feeling is, is that there was no rush to fix this bug before, when it negativley impacted players, but now that there's a way that it can positivly impact them, it becomes a higher priority.. even though it's the same bug. Now I think that leads to the feeling some people get that "They don't care if their changes hurt me, but they'll 'nerf' anything that helps".

I understand that this is the issue.

The problem is timing. We have been working to fix the aggro bug since the last patch. We would have patched a fix for it next week in any case. The things going on in Kael haven't changed that at all. But because we also had to place a stopgap measure to prevent people from using this bug during the next week before we can get it patched, people have assumed that we are only fixing it because people were gaining easy experience and loot from it.

I can't help the timing. It just happens that way.
