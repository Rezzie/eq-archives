Gordon Infobits
[2 Comments] - Aurum @ 1:24 PM PDT
Gordon kept busy this afternoon with several new posts to the Gameplay Board:

  • FD & /Quiting Out?
    [Quote from original post: "FD will work 25% of the time on wanderers, 90% of the time when the creatures return to their post."]

    All creatures forget about a successfully feigned character once the creature returns to it's spawn point. That of course doesn't apply to creatures that don't have a static spawn.

    [Quote from original post: Monks hate having to do it, and would prefer that FD worked the old way so that we could use our skill without endangering other adventurers in dungeons. This "cheat", as you call it, is your friendly neighborhood monk's attempt at being as little an inconvenience to you as possible. Don't give them a bad time about it.]

    It has been suggested that make anyone feigned automatically "stand up" upon a /q, however we don't have any plans to implement that in the foreseeable future.


  • EverQuest Isn't for Everyone
    [Quote from original post: As a casual gamer I don't have hours and hours to sit and play. Unfortunately to get anywhere in this game that's what is REQUIRED.]

    It's only "required" because you perceive the need to attain the same levels and equipment as the guy who plays 12 hours per day, even though you only have that many hours per week to play yourself. There are many players of this same type, one which I refer to as the "time-starved powergamer", rather than the "casual gamer".

    The time-starved powergamer holds the same desires and drives as the powergamer, but only has limited time to play due to real-life concerns. The player is driven by advancement and acquisition, whereas the casual gamer is more interested in the gaming experience.

    Would it make sense for us to create a game based around the 10 hours per week figure when the average player plays around 23? The game is set up around that average figure, and someone playing that average amount of time can expect to progress at an average rate. The consequence of this is that people who play double or triple that progress much faster, and people who play half that often want to progress as fast, on a calander basis, as those who play double or triple the average.

    Regardless the advancement or acquisition rate in game, there will always be people who play less than the average. It wouldn't make sense for us to gear the game so that people who play less than average progress at fastest rate.

    [Quote from original post: As for racing to 60, well you assigned us levels and when you set a cap you set a goal. Everyone wants to achieve that goal although the vast majority never will. 50 was attainable but 60? It's a pipe dream for most and probably the number one reason people will quit EQ because they simply accept the fact it is beyond reach.]

    We never expected every play to reach level 50, and certainly never expect everyone to reach level 60 either. The "average" player will reach "average" levels and will never "max out". The important thing is that those levels are there for the people who decide to advance to that point and have the time to do so. I see from your post that your character is level 39 now. That's a very respectable level for someone who can only play a few hours per week.

    [Quote from original post: Make it so we each have an equal chance to achieve all that is achievable in the game with the exclusion of Veeshan's Peak. Make that dungeon so tough that 200 of your best powergamers can never make it 100 yards into it. That should keep them occupied for a while.]

    Games of this nature have to service a broad range of different player types. Sometimes servicing one type of player molds the game in a direction that isn't liked by another type. This will always be the case. Your post seems to suggest that you think that every gamer, regardless of how much time they put in, should be able to go everywhere and do everything. Would it make sense for us to make a game geared with 10 hours per week as an average? Or what of a game geared to 5 hours per week on average? To do what you suggest, that being allowing the 10 hours per week player to advance and acquire at the same rate as is enjoyed by the current 40 hour per week player, we'd have to set the average to about 2 hours per week. What fun would the game be for the average player then?

    [Quote from original post: EQ should allow everyone the same opportunity to achieve the same items and experience ]

    Opportunity is a constant. Everyone in game has the same opportunity. They then decide how much of that opportunity they wish to pursue, guided by personal desires and real-life factors. The variable here is the time that you have to play, and it is that you do not have.

    I do not think that it is fair to say that EQ does not support the casual gamer. It would be fair to say that someone who has more time to play EQ can advance and acquire at a faster rate. However, is there anything really wrong with that?


  • Discussion of the Week: Continued
    I'm impressed with the number of well thought-out discussions taking place in regards to this topic. As many of you have noticed, the size of the original thread has reached the point where it needs to be closed. I still have to go through it and replies to this one as well to compose some comments, which I'll do later today. Until then, here's the continuation of the thread.


  • Weapon Nerfs
    Sevrus_MT,

    My initial post on the subject announcing the plans in regards to these weapons said:

    "While we have no problem with weapons of this delay, they should not have been placed as loot on anything other than deity or dragon level encounters."

    The weapon quoted is indeed on such an encounter.


  • 20 Questions
    [Quote from original post: 1. Camping. Why couldn't the "long portion" of the camp be put at the OTHER end of the camp? Perhaps the NPC needs time to "craft" the quest item and changes in some way to show that they are ready to give the reward. Let the other items spawn almost continuously, and people would NEED to camp anything but the reward PC. ]

    It's a doable idea, but wouldn't this be just a camp by another name? Whether I'm camping a creature that I want to kill, or one I want to talk to, it's still a camp. In this case, I can see 20 people standing around an NPC waiting for him to "be ready" and fighting over who can hand him the items fastest.

    [Quote from original post: 2. Are there currently any suits of armor that give some "hidden" effect or bonus by virtue of wearing the entire suit? ]

    No, there are not. It's a good idea though that might have some application for later implementation or future products.

    [Quote from original post: 3. Are there currently any weapons in the game that have any "hidden" effect? ]

    Not really. What an item can do is expressly stated upon it. We do have some plans for more cursed weaponry in the future, of course we just might have to be creative to "hide" the effect of the curse.

    [Quote from original post: 4. If the answer to 2 and/or 3 is "no", are there any plans to add this feature to the game? ]

    See above.

    [Quote from original post: 5. You once said that GZ was going to conduct some tests with the wizard's spells vs. the other caster classes. Have these been completed? ]

    Actually I believe that Brad said that, and it is indeed our opinion that offensive power is appropriate for the class.

    [Quote from original post: 6. Why, exactly, was the definition of the wizard class changed on the everquest.com page under the classes section? ]

    What change and when was it made? To my knowledge, it has read what it reads now since release. I believe that there was a change at one time in the beta period where it was changed from "damage" to "magical damage", but that's all I believe happened. Was there something else?

    [Quote from original post: 7. Are there any plans to introduce items that directly influence/enhance trade skills? (Gnomish Jeweler's Loupe, Dwarven Smith's Hammer, Elven Sewing Needle, etc.)]

    Do you mean items that allow you to make better items with trades? It's a good idea, but we no firm plans in that direction.

    [Quote from original post: 8. Why, since dwarves in fantasy literature (and even in EQ) are the finest smiths in all the realms, is there no ore in Kaladim? It even has a mine. ]

    Good question. There's no ore in Kaladim? Hmmm, I'll check into that to see if it was a design or an oversight.

    [Quote from original post: 9. Why can't enchanters be given a high-level illusion that allows them to change into the nearest ANIMATE object? ]

    Races are a component of faction, and you adopt whatever race-faction you emulate. There's a lot of special case-coding to handle the animate races that players currently emulate. Opening that up to nearly everything else would be extremely problematic, if not impossible.

    [Quote from original post: 10. Are there any plans to introduce a spell or item that absorbs magic directed at the wielder, perhaps converting it directly into mana/hit points? ]

    There is already a bard song that has a damage shield that absorbs magical damage. There's also a cleric "reverse damage shield" that heals anyone that hits the target. That's the extent of what we have, and nothing new along these lines are planned.

    [Quote from original post: 11. Are there any plans to introduce a spell or item that REFLECTS spells back at the caster? ]

    No.

    [Quote from original post: 12. Why can't the monk-only weapons that look like gloves be worn on the hands, and add directly to the ATTACK stat instead of requiring them to be 1h blunt? ]

    Items cannot be set to use hand-to-hand skill, and combat can't go to an "equipment" slot to get combat statistics.

    [Quote from original post: 13. If Verant doesn't like "spoiler sites", then why are there no spell descriptions anywhere in the game? ]

    I think that you've answered your own question. We don't like spoilers. We'd prefer people figure these things out on their own and maintain their own knowledge-base.

    [Quote from original post: 14. Will players ever be able to construct golems? (A.k.a. summon a temporary "golem-type" pet through some complicated quest) ]

    We could certainly, by use of trade skill or quest, award an item that summoned a pet of any particular type, however we have no plans to do that at present.

    [Quote from original post: 15. Necros can charm the undead; enchanters can charm...well...everything. Will Magicians ever have the ability/spell to charm elementals or other summoned creatures? ]

    No, we have no plans along these lines at present.

    [Quote from original post: 16. Are there any plans for an expansion geared toward the 10 - 30 range of player rather than the higher levels? ]

    Velious is geared towards a higher level-range, however Kunark has support for all level ranges.

    With Velious, the thinking is that there is already plenty of content to support the continually migrating portion of the customer-base that is in the 10-30 range.

    I'm unable to talk about any post-Velious plans at this time.

    [Quote from original post: 17. The Trackers' Water Extractor - This is currently broken, is it not? The purification tablets do not seem to exist, and the NPC that mentions them won't respond. ]

    I inquired about this time time back, when Kunark first launched, and it was verified at the time that the tablets do exist. If I need to check into it further, please let me know.

    [Quote from original post: 18. Bandages vs. Aloe Swatches vs. Maneater Plant Salve vs. Tailored Silk Bandages: Is there any difference other than price i.e. More healing, faster healing, etc. ]

    No difference at this time.

    [Quote from original post: 19. Are there any plans to give clerics any type of special ability that is tied to whatever deity they worship? ]

    No, there are currently no plans along these lines. However, there are plans and implemented pieces of deity-specific quests that return deity-specific rewards.

    [Quote from original post: 20. Are there any races and/or classes that you, personally, would like to see added to the game? ]

    If we added a warrior/wizard hybrid, could we call him a "fire fighter"?

Gordon Infobits
[4 Comments] - Aurum @ 6:10 PM PDT
Gordon made a few more posts within the last couple of hours on the Gameplay Board. Also of note is the start of this week's discussion:

  • Discussion of the Week: Fun
    Let’s talk about “fun”.

    Last week I queried everyone’s thoughts regarding a “Discussion of the Week”. Though the number of different responses was almost as great as the number of posts, many of them dealt with fun, or with concepts related to fun.

    I think that it can generally be agreed that EQ’s customer base is very diverse, and with that diversity comes many different types of players. EQ strives to provide as many different avenues for “fun” as possible, leaving it up to the player to make decisions regarding what avenues they wish to pursue. Some find the social aspect of the game to be the most fun, while others have a good time by advancing in levels. Some measure it in items, while others prefer character development along the lines of mastering trade skills or languages. Still others want to personally witness as much as possible within game.

    Our goal with EverQuest is to entertain you for the greatest amount of time possible, and to do this we must provide activities that the players consider “fun” in nature. I’ve spoken many times about the concepts of risk and reward, but those concepts can be broken down to the perception of fun. Setting pure socialization aside, as it is one way of having “fun” that does not require a challenge, the goal is that “rewards” provide excitement, entertainment, etc. (cf. Fun), to the players that overcome challenges.

    Last week we discussed camping, how it impacts the game, and what the driving forces behind it are. I think that from the responses to that thread, we can agree that camping relates to the concept of “fun” in one way or another. Players either enjoy camping itself, or enjoy something that camping will provide, if not the process itself. The social gamer might enjoy camping because of the opportunities for socialization provided in the downtime between kills. The leveling gamer might enjoy the prospect of raising in level. The item gamer looks forward to the items. More indirectly, perhaps the exploring gamer doesn’t enjoy camping for experience or loot, but gets fun out of exploring new situations, for which they need the levels and items to do, without explicitly caring for those levels or items.

    The questions I’d like to pose are:

    What is it about EverQuest that entertains you? If you had to label yourself as a particular type of gamer, what would that type be? Though I’m sure that everyone has particular issues that they feel could use improvement, everyone either has fun in game, or foresees the prospect of having fun sometime in the future. For instance, there are players that feel that once they reach their particular goal (whether it is one of items or levels), they *then* can go have fun.

    If you are of the type that does not feel they are having fun now, and foresees fun once reaching a specific goal, what is that goal? What is it about that goal that makes you feel you can only have fun once you reach it? Internally, we refer to it as a “comfort-level”. What defines that comfort-level to you? What do you foresee will be “fun” once you’ve reached that comfort-level?

    When Kunark came out, we raised the level cap. If you define yourself as one who must achieve a comfort level prior to having “fun”, did the raising of the level limit also “raise the bar” of your comfort level, effectively putting you far below it? Or did you enjoy the level-cap increase? I ask because before the level cap was raised there were thousands of people who were out having “fun” in game. Once the cap was raised, the focus for these people quickly shifted back to the “must-level-fast” philosophy.

    Assuming that you have “fun” outside the tangible effects of levels and items, do you feel that “fun” is a just and suitable reward for a risk? Do you have fun outside of the scope of items and levels? Will you give some examples of where you’ve had fun without a tangible reward? While I understand that players who value only items and levels typically do not have fun outside of the acquisition of those rewards, I would imagine that most fall somewhere in between one player-type an another rather than directly in the middle of one of the ones I’ve defined.

    If you could make any change to the game, feasible or otherwise, to increase the amount of entertainment you get or fun you have in the game given your particular gaming style, what would that change be? Other thoughts? I’m looking forward to seeing discussions between the players on their various perceptions of fun.

    This should make for a pretty good discussion. Often here on the boards, we fail to get a good impression of the different types of gamers out there. It’s not unusual to see two posts on the opposite side of an issue related to “fun” claim to both represent the majority. I really doubt there is a “majority” for most issues in EverQuest, because as in life, very few things are black and white.

    This is an opportunity to jump in and be heard.


  • Station Security
    The connection in which your station name and password is transmitted is a "secure" connection. The boards themselves are not, since there is no reason for them to be. The only personal data that is transmitted after the initial entering of the station name and password is the chat handle, which is not tied to any information that is important for security.

That leaves two possibilities, either there's something wrong with the field that's reporting the last date that it was changed, or there was some kind of export problem. More info Monday.

    Concerning items in combinable containers, I think that I'm going to chat with the programmers Monday and see if they can make the containers eat any items in them upon logoff/Linkdeath. I think that there'd be no question of the intent of those bags then.

    Also, I notice a couple of people have brought up a thread where I indicated that accidently pressing "Combine" was a risk of using them as a container for item storage. That's true. That is "a" risk, and that was brought up when people were asking for a confirmation window (yes/no requester) when combining. Though that is "a" risk, I don't think that I ever indicated that it was an all-inclusive and balancing risk.

[1 Comments] - Aurum @ 2:47 PM PDT
Gordon posted a few message this afternoon on the Gameplay Board. Here they are:

  • Pets Drowning
    This is covered in the FAQ. Basically NPCs can do things that PCs can't to make up for the fact that NPCs can't be as intelligent or adaptive as players.


  • Partisan Pack Follow-up
    There was another thread that someone started in regards to an item known as the Partisan Pack, a combinable quest bag given out by an NPC. Since that thread has gone a little off-topic, I thought the follow-up which I promised would do better in a new one.
    Apparently in the last patch, the item changed from a 10-slot container to a 6-slot container, causing some to lose item stored in slots 7-10.
    Since people have grown accustomed to storing items within quest-bags, a change of this type should have been pre-announced, in order to avoid item loss. We do indeed apologize for the change being made without that advance notice. We have logged data for any items that were lost by the change, so you may contact a GM for assistance.


    The original thread on the Partisan pack shifted later to a topic about combinable containers in general. I wanted to separate out the two issues and discuss them individually. I've started a separate thread on the issue concerning the unannounced change of the partisan pack, which is a bag given by an NPC for the purpose of completing a quest.

  • More on Containers
    In this thread I'd like to discuss my comments where I said:

    "Concerning items in combinable containers, I think that I'm going to chat with the programmers Monday and see if they can make the containers eat any items in them upon logoff/Linkdeath. I think that there'd be no question of the intent of those bags then."

    After reviewing this post that I made earlier, I do agree that it appears to be a threat when viewed out-of-context, and understand the general reaction that followed. The point of the post was to respond to multiple posts from people earlier in the thread who had no idea that they shouldn't use combinable containers, particularly free bags given by NPCs to complete quests, for general item storage.

    I'd thought that the number of people using the bags for this purpose were the exception rather than the rule, an observation that was apparently mistaken given public response to my post. Given that public response, we won't be taking any steps to "destroy" items in combinable containers upon logout or linkdeath.

    I do apologize for the way my response was phrased and hope everyone understands that neither I nor other members of the tuning team actively seek out ways to make the lives of the players more difficult.


  • Cut EXP Rate
    [Quote from original post: Lets halve XP and Double Heal/Mana Regen It would allow you to take more mobs faster... that means more moving around in dungeons... less down time. more mobs dead. More play time... less down time. You would have to use your time a bit more wisely to make up for the exp cut. You would need to kill things a lot faster to keep the same rate of gain.]

    Unfortunately, though the idea looks simple at first glance, it's not that simple. Doubling mana regen rates would at least double the potential killing rate. That means that we'd have to increase spawn rates. At the same time, we'd have to halve loot drop rates for both coin, mundane, and special items. The doubled spawn rates and doubled killing rates would use more processing power and bandwidth on the servers, which isn't feasible from a cost-perspective.

    Finally, the suggested change would only bolster the claim that we "force" people to kill non-stop. I don't believe that is true currently, however might be were we to reduce the time available for social interaction within the game.


  • Cannibalize
    [Quote from original post: Here i am helping my girlfriend level in FM...she(lvl 28) is fighting things and i(lvl 53) am just casting my slow on them and healing when she needs it. I can be sitting down while she beats the thing to half-way of it's health and it doesn't even notice me, but the second i stand up and cannibalize the things turn on me no matter how bad she taunted it and beat it when i have not casted anything on it but slow at the beginning of the fight]

    Since you cast a combat speed debuff on the creature, you were put on the creatures hate list. When you then cast cannabalize on yourself, you are casting a beneficial spell on someone that the creature hates, which causes the amount that it hates you to increase.
    --
    The combat speed debuff has duration, so it adds hate each tick that it is on. Beneficial spells add a significant amount of hate (ask any cleric or bard). Both of those issues contribute to the problem that you cite.


  • Warrior Tournament Cancelled?
    I'll check with Daegarmo on this tomorrow, see if I can find out what is going on.


  • Gambling in EQ
    Though these are good ideas and we have given tremendous thought to them, we elected a while back not to pursue gambling within the game. Frankly, the biggest hurdle is a legal one. Since people are selling in-game items and platinum for real-world cash, it would allow someone to convert their gambling winnings to cash, and cash into a gambling medium. That would leave it open to being argued that gambling in EverQuest is gambling in the real world.

    Though we are leaving the option open for future products, and looking into the legal issues involved, it's not a possibility for EverQuest going forward.

    As far as camping goes, about all we determined is that people differ widely in opinion on the issue. One of the most common suggestions is that we work actively to promote more rewards for playing the game in the preferred manner, and leave rewards as they currently exist for camping.

    Unfortunately, doing that would require making playing the game in the preferred manner easier, safer, faster, or more profitable, which would in our opinion decrease the fun to be had from it. In our opinion, there is more fun to be had from playing the game in the preferred manner than by camping, and that fun should be the sufficient additional reward to get people to do it.

    However it's obviously not, so we're left with reducing the reward for camping as an option. Of course, many made it clear in the threads they they prefer camping over doing anything else in the game. Hence our quandry.

    I think that at this point we can continue trying new ideas going forward, but try to avoid making sweeping changes to the way existing things work. We'll of course have individual exceptions to the philosophy in both directions, but on an as-needed basis. That said, there were some very good ideas included and good insight offered within the thread.   • An Explanation
    [Quote from original post: So agian I was just wondering what is the different between the song and this.]

    The difference is that the NPC is moving. It is carrying out its commands and making progress towards that goal. Movement introduces a variable that injects some risk into the situation. Now where these particular people were fear-kiting, perhaps there is a pathing issue that reduces the risk, but that's something associated with a specific point in the world, not an ability that can be taken anywhere.

    The "Fear" spell tells the NPC to flee. It's the same "flee" that they do when they run low on health and try to get away. They can't really *get* anywhere, so the only reason they flee is to inject additional risk.

    An NPC that is fleeing, and is then rooted, will turn around and fight. An NPC that is fleeing, and then snared, will not turn around and fight, even if their movement rate is reduced to zero (an effective root). "Effective Roots" don't have the drawbacks of a root (e.g. fighting NPC, unpredictable breaking of the spell).

    Our choices in this case were: Make it so all NPCs will fight back if their movement speed is reduced to a certain point, or, limit the ability of snare spells to reduce their movement rate to zero. We did the latter. I'm sure that some could make a case for the former based upon situation.
