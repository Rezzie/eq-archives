    So, after 750 miles and $1000 later we discovered what was once kosher had suddenly turned into the proverbial Sausage McMuffin with Egg in the face of the Torah. Absor Infobits
- Aurum @ 6:53 PM PDT
Hitting the Developer's Corner after a big weekend, here's all of what Alan had to say today:

    The Cat Who Walked Through Walls
    [Quote: Forgive the Heinlein quote, I couldn't resist :)

    I am a new player, so this question may be long since answered but I could not find it anywhere so thought I would ask it anyhow. Creatures can see and walk through walls. This appears to be common knowledge, something I found out the hard way.

    I was prepared to simply shrug and live with it until a madman came in out of the desert and killed me through a wall while I was shopping. Is there a plan in place to change/repair this 'feature'?

    Again apologies if this is a repetition. Oh, and thanks for creating a new addiction for me! :)

    Alerius the (all singing all dancing) Bard ]

    Quoting Heinlein titles is fine with me. A good bard needs to be aware of the great bards, after all.

    Yes, that question has been asked several times. But I'm more than willing to answer it again.

    Creatures walk through certain walls on purpose. The huts and inns you see out in the plains of Norrath, such as those out in the Commons near Freeport, don't prevent the passage of NPCs because if they did they would be easily abused. For example, Hill Giants would not be able to attack a player character through the roof of the inn, but you might be able to stab at his feet. And creatures used to get stuck against the geometry of those buildings all the time. So, while we certainly understand that it seems strange to have a bear wander through the walls of a hut, it's something that needed to be done.

    Bullshmidt wrote:

    [Quote: Absor-

    So if you are running ME and are seeing issues, make sure that you've checked the other potential problems (usually sound or video drivers) before thinking it's the operating system. It might be something you can fix more easily than changing OS's…

    Roleplaying or Just Playing?
    StormFront wrote:

    [Quote: I happened to be looking through various posts and discovered the Role-Playing or Just Playing comments. I thought I would just give my thoughts.

    First I have a High Elf Paladin, and have wondered about the way others would precieve my character when he comes across gruff with the evil races. Not wanting to party with them and such so I created a Dark Elf ShadowKnight just to see what the attitude of the land was towards those characters. To my surprise most people seemed to enjoy playing the DE and even though they were despied it never seemed to affect them. On a couple of occassions I have witnessed true DE tactics at work. Once I saw a DE ShadowKnight ask to join a group which sent these RPs in total hysteria, just to have the DE walk off gleeful he had stirred them up. Take it for what it's worth.

    I also have a Wood Elf Druid, I understand the statement that we are to be the protectors of nature. However there is a fine line and others have asked how a Druid could attack a wildlife entity. My answer has been that of what most Native American believe which is how I feel the Druids must have been like(Though I admit I could be totally wrong). Druids wish to protect there environment but understand that they must live upon it and have no problem with the hunting of an animal as long as the entire animal is used and not wasted on momentary gain or sport. The senseless killing of many animals would get to them, but using them for food or clothing is acceptable as long as done with respect to them and not meaning a massacare of everything. I work with tailoring but kill only to equipment myself and for food when I am low and a town or merchant is not available.

    However I can see instances where role-playing becomes difficult especially when stakes are high. When your after that important item or you have lost your corpse and can not locate it and it has the offering you have to make to your god for a specific quest (just an example). Would you be willing to bend your character to accomplish these? In real life you sometimes have to swallow hard and deal with a situation. Perhaps allowing a character to be designed with regards to who they can group with and who they can not, dependent on their deity. If you were to lock out charcters from grouping solely because of race or class then you lose a bit of the reality. Maybe instead they suffer a faction hit and will need a way to correct for it, just a thought. A kind of atonement for their transgression. Having a instance where a character can deviate from normal can add something to the fabric that we call Norrath, making the area even more unknown. I have enjoyed RPing sometimes it is just interesting to determine why someone might act the way they do and come up with the reason even if they are wrong or not. I myself would like the ability to select the races or classes that I might fear or shun, granted there should be automatics, since I could have a quirky personality and noone is quite sure why.

    Sorry to be so long but that is my thoughts about it, I think it would be fun to play on an RP server! ]

    I think it's great that you've taken the extra step to play on both sides of the fence. A lot of people will play a single class or race for a long time and perhaps fail to understand some of the issues involved in playing one of the classes that they don't normally get along with. I'm not talking about 'my class is better than your class' stuff, but about how the communities work. For example, I've gotten the impression over the last two years that folks playing Dwarves tend to be more likely to roleplay than others. I don't know why, or if it's even true, but I've played at least a little as every race in the game, and I think it's true. Or I've noticed that it is a lot easier to be in-character when playing a good cleric than an evil cleric. As a 'good' cleric, it's easy enough to heal people when they ask. But for an 'evil' cleric this just might not be in-character (though it can be).

    It is always easiest to abandon roleplaying when it comes to the 'high stakes' stuff. The temptation is great. And most of the time it's in-character to do such things as dealing with a necro to help get your corpse, even if your character might normally never do such a thing. High stakes for the player is usually high stakes for the character as well (your character doesn't want to lose his stuff either). But there are cases where you should sacrifice what you want for what your character wants. When the two desires are not the same, roleplaying means putting the character's desires first.

    And I do agree that it seems that the best way to 'enforce' any sort of roleplaying (though I don't really like the idea of 'forced' roleplaying) would be along the lines of deity restrictions. How easy that would be to do in EverQuest I don't know…

    [Quote: I specialize in Halflings, and role-play everyone to the tee. It is my favorite part of EQ. I am the leader of a Small Races Only Guild and we exclusively roleplay, especially the worshipers of Bristlebane.

    I think the idea of a Roleplaying Only server is great...only, could it be a new server where everyone has to start over at level one, no switch-over characters and high level twinks from other servers. That way everyone can start at the same level, and grow as a server together. Just a thought.

    This board format is great!!

    Apathos
    From a lot of Servers and a lot of Personas, all Halflings ]

    I know of a few Trolls that specialize in Halflings too…

    IF there were to ever be such a thing as a server with rules built with roleplaying in mind, then it's my opinion that it would have to be a non-split server. You're right, a server like that would need to grow up under the new rules.

    Dulc wrote:

    [Quote: Up until March, my husband and I had played on only non-pvp servers. We love to roleplay, but eventually gave it up for the most part, because too many players reacted in a negative way. We had a paladin and cleric couple, for which the paladin was my Champion. He played it very chivalrous and honorable, no thees or thous, but maybe the use of "m'lady, m'lord, sir, mistress" etc. made people feel uncomfortable. We had reactions ranging from disgust to apologetic. There were a few that jumped right in and roleplayed with us, but for the most part it was a struggle.

    Anyway, a friend offered to help us discover Tallon Zek through a roleplay story and we accepted. What a difference! Suddenly we were fearing for our lives, not just from npc's but from characters for which we had no idea what they might do. We are playing "shorties" and the added threat of the "dark alliance" has just enhanced the roleplay. This make me think that a roleplay server should be pvp, but with a safety net around the cities. On non-pvp servers, people can say whatever they wish with no reprecussions for the most part. But if a "light" race challenges a "dark" race in pvp, you may have the whole zone on your back. So yes, I personally am enjoying roleplaying a dislike of the evil races on Tallon Zek. But I wouldn't do it on a non-pvp server. Hope that makes sense. :)]

    Well, it can be argued that the use of thee and thou wouldn't be appropriate for roleplaying in Norrath, as none of the cultures really use it, as far as I know. I've seen the same thing, though. People getting uncomfortable with use of more, um, flowery language. But then, I get uncomfortable around the use of words like 'plz' and 'r'. :)

    Fear is a useful roleplaying tool. I don't think that it can be the basis of roleplaying in EverQuest on its own, though. I understand your point, though. The idea that you can attack another character based on whatever rules are set up tends to improve the interaction between those characters. On a standard server a High Elf is limited in the ways that he can express his dislike for Dark Elves in exactly the same way that he can't express his dislike of a rude Wood Elf. On Tallon Zek the High Elf has the ability to be more aggressive to a Dark Elf than to a rude Wood Elf, and that tends to make the willingness to be aggressive towards Dark Elves higher.

    Kingfish01 wrote:

    [Quote: No, no, no. You are absolutely wrong, and that's what frustrates me about EQ.

    Your idea that "pacifists make poor adventurers" is patently false, but indicates why EQ is a challenge for roleplayers.

    Now, I've enjoyed reading these comments and your responses abou this, but I must take issue with you.

    Because of its design, EQ has no room for explorers, historians, cartographers, merchants, sages, hermits and evangelists (is not faith an important issue for EQ characters?). Why can't these characters exist? Because EQ only tolerates combat oriented roleplay.

    Why can't players be multi classed, changing careers later in life? Why can't they experience conversion and change faith? I enjoy EQ, but your gameworld exists in a narrow box.

    Adventure involves more than killing things, but EQ is almost exclusively about killing things. EQ is fundamentally a "kill something so you can kill something bigger" game. It's quests almost always involve the death of an NPC. But it doesn't have to be this way!!!

    EQ SHOULD have ways for player to gain experience in other ways. The easiest would be to award experience for successful skill development. It should be possible for a player to be a 30th level blacksmith, tailor, or whatever, and still not be able to fight their way out of a paper bag. In theory, this is possible, but is unreasonable. Tradeskills depend on high level spells and materials, making it necessary for Master craftsmen to be also master casters for the most part.

    Personally I think it is a programming cop-out to make almost all experience gain related to killing things. A little creative thinking could expand the roleplaying aspects of the game immensely, without sacrificing anything that currently exists.

    Sorry for the rant, but it frustrates me that EQ could be so much better, but I don't see that happening. ]

    Pacifists DO make poor adventurers. There's nothing false about that statement.

    What I think you disagree with is that adventuring is a poor focus for roleplaying. I disagree.

    While the focus of EverQuest is adventuring (much like most pen and paper games), it certainly doesn't exclude the roles that you've mentioned. Explorers certainly have a place in Norrath. Explorers in a dangerous world are basically adventurers anyway. Historians, cartographers, merchants, sages… all of these are not roles on their own. If you are playing a cartographer that likes to sit around the house and read maps, or a historian that likes to read history but doesn't like to talk to others about it, then you don't really need a multiplayer game. Hermit is so much not a roleplaying idea (sure, the GM could use it, but as a player character a hermit would never, ever, be in a game… Evangelists would be something that would be hard to do in EverQuest.

    Yes, you can play a cartographer in EverQuest, and play one well. But I won't argue that such a character is somewhat limited by the mechanics and content of the game. Most certainly you can't make and sell (or give away) maps in game. That is partially a design limitation, but primarily a technological one. EverQuest does not have any sort of automated map making system by design. But then if it did, who would need a cartographer. As for drawing your own maps in game, that is more of a technology issue than a design one (or, if you want, it was a design decision not to develop the technology to allow such a thing).

    But none of the careers you mentioned are directly prohibited by the game itself. In most cases where they are limited the limitation comes from technology or time restrictions, not a desire to hinder roleplaying. The best example would be your evangelist. Despite the fact that you can't actually change the little word(s) on the character description that the player sees, you most certainly could 'convert' people. You could convince them to work for the betterment of your chosen deity. You could convince them to do things that would bring their faction in line with the factions best suited to your deity. You could convert someone to your religion if the player of that character was convinced that his character would be converted.

    No, the game doesn't really support such activity with gameplay features. We can't support all such features. I wish it were technologically and monetarily feasible.

    Oh, and it is possible to be a 30th level tailor and still not be able to fight your way out of a paper bag. It would be really hard, but it could be done.

    There's no cop-out here. To program a game (and get it done before the programmers die of old age) a feature set must be chosen. Some things won't make it, everything can't make it.

    We just work day to day to get closer to the perfect game.

    Sorry, that was a bit long-winded. :)

    [Quote: Part of the problem with roleplaying is the designers don't always consider those that want to roleplay when they do the quest.

    Example: Iksar monks have to worship Cazic Thule, yet to do their Epic Quest, they have to gain the respect of the human monk guilds, one of which is dedicated to Quellious. An iksar "properly" roleplayed shouldn't have to do human quest.

    Azzad ]

    Well you can look at this two ways. First, if your Iksar wants to trick the stupid Humans into liking him so that he can gain something he wants, there's no need to sacrifice any roleplaying.

    But if your Iksar is not the type of lizard that would do such a thing (an honorable lizard-monk), then don't do it. I see people using the word 'forced' to get an item all the time. Your Iksar does not 'have' to do a human quest. That's one of those things that I talked about before. It's one of those decisions where you either choose to roleplay and not get something that the player wants so that the character can remain true. If your Iksar doesn't want to do the quest because it's not in his character, having him do it is a concession to the player.

    I know that the argument here is that the Iksar should be able to get the item by some other route. That an Iksar monk should be equally able to get that item if his player is roleplaying or if he's not. I don't agree. I, personally, like the idea that some paladins (as this is the most common example) will be able to roleplay in a world where they get to make a hard decision. Fair isn't a part of roleplaying. So while it might be fair to argue that from a game design standpoint such a quest is unfair, it's wrong to do so from a roleplaying standpoint.

    Anyway, keep in mind that a lot of my reply in this post is my personal opinion.

    As a representative of the company, I will send your concern about the quest to the team. I know a guy that's interested in looking into that sort of thing. :)

    A Change To Trade Containers
    I'm sure we've all done it once or twice (or more). But instead of closing your tailoring kit, you combine it. Suddenly you've lost some stuff that you really wanted to keep.

    Well, since EverQuest opened we've been warning you that this was one of the risks that you took when using lightweight trade containers for their carrying capacity rather than just using them for making things.

    We've decided to make a rather dramatic change for the better when it comes to trade containers.

    With this upcoming patch, trade containers will no longer combine items that are not a valid and complete recipe.

    This means that if you accidentally hit combine, the container will not destroy your items. This also means that if you make an error and put the wrong item in the container while trying to cook something up, it won't destroy those items either. This will be a huge boon to craftspeople all across Norrath.

    But this removes the only drawback to using trade containers as backpacks. Their reduced weight and often larger carrying capacity made them more desirable as bags, with the risk that you might just lose something if you made a mistake.

    So, with the logical change in the way combinable containers work (I mean, really, how often does a sewing kit eat a halberd?), we are also going to make them less desirable for just carrying stuff around in. We will be increasing their weight rather significantly. Where many of these containers weighed under 1 pound, they will now weigh as much as 4 pounds for the largest sewing kit.

    Along with this we are making a change to tailor made backpacks. In case you didn't know (and I sure didn't), most tailor made packs have a built in weight reduction of 5%. We are raising that to 10%. We will also be displaying the weight reduction of all containers with the information shown on a right click.
