interesting read. Really though, I can see the wisdom of their policy even in your case (although certainly, it flies in the face of most of our conception of what "Play nice" should mean). The point that someone is there first, is completely unverifiable -- save for the GM or guide actually seeing you there camping it. So enforcing a First come-first served precedent only serves to promote bald faced lying in this regard. I'm sure that selvon (?) would readily admit this to be true. I'm sure that most GM's would sympathize greatly with your position. Unfortunately, were they to award the next spawn to you, just how long would it take the "Bad Guys" from figuring this ploy out, and begin to lie about being there first, as a rule.

Enforcing the PNP rules as written, make it so there is no benefit to lying about "who's there first" And of course there is the real ability to abuse this rule -- as your experience attests. But then the GM's response to this abuse can be quick and effective. With no ambiguity.

Were the FCFS rule be applied, then you have a much more sinister abuse of not just your time, but that of the GMs. Finding out who is lying. And involving witnesses et al. Undoubtedly someone like that druid would just flat out lie (more than your accounting shows) about being there first, and many others could be roped into a circular argument/discussion about who's there first.

In the end, a GM's time has been monopolized for a great deal of time, on a conflict that has NO HOPE of being resolved truthfully. Additionally, there are now several players (you and all the witnesses), who are unsatisfied at the result.

The fact that this druid would have been naive or stupid enough to admit that you were there first is irrelevant. Because he/she would learn from such a ruling. As would every other "Bad Guy". They would lie in order to force a roll off.

And presto: You are now back where you started from.

My advice: Play PvP servers. Exact a little revenge. One instance of vigilante justice will certainly make you forget SOME of the pain.

#3   Name: Severian ()   Date: 5/6 @ 7:59 AM PST
Well, unlike the author I did understand the basic thrust of the play nice policy, and saw the problem with his reaction to his situation early on. It is not about fairness, it is simple "no-one can make any claim on a spawn", the only exception being that if one of the individuals or groups could gain experience and the other was too high for it, preference would go to the "level-appropriate" group. So his feelings that he should get the spawn because he was there first, while perfectly reasonable and even accepted by a huge portion of EQ players, is actually undercut by the play nice policy (and by his resorting to it and to a GM to enforce it). His conclusions about the situation, and concerns about the impact on gameplay and player relations, however, need to be taken very seriously. I credit him with a well written and thoughtful look at the implications, although he really did let his emotions get the better of him in the last paragraph, distracting from the central message. IMHO Play Nice is a terrible solution to a significant problem in EQ, and I think it moves the ingame player community in the wrong direction. I don't have a better top-down solution myself, but I do prefer the player-created solutions (first come first served, turn taking, time invested considered, lists, etc.), that have been created by the community, even if they have their own flaws and do not solve the guild monopoly problem itself. At least they sprung from the players, and are widely perceived to be an attempt at fairness, where the ham-hamded play nice policies essentially require players to abandon fairness in the name of a policy whose essential virtue is that it is enforceable by a given GM who appears on the scene with zero knowledge of the situation. That is, if players take up "play nice" in preference to previous methods at all, which I haven't seen myself (although I don't camp 8 hour spawns or hunt in Level 50 areas). In fact, the players who try to be fair and who feel previous methods fairer will continue to use them, and the bad guys who just want people to get out of the way of them getting their phat lewt asap are the ones who will arrive and rely on "play nice" both to justify and carry out their self-serving actions. But at least it will be easy for GMs and guides to handle, even if the selfish players are the ones rewarded by it more often than not.

Let's say baseball tickets are going on sale for the next season and people wait in line overnight, some perhaps for a couple days, to get them. The ones who have been there for a substantial time, or who have been through this in previous years, will recognize those around them as fellow diehard fans, and see the ones in the very front as having paid their dues to get first crack at it. Then an announcement is made by the office they never intended to have lines like that (police don't like people in the streets overnight) and the ticket sellers won't enforce the line order. Most of the people in the front will respect the line and the people around them and stay put. The ones who don't, the ones who think fairness is for suckers, start to jump the line, elbow their way up front, and are directly rewarded for their behavior by the ticket sellers, who sell to them first.
