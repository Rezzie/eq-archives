Snaggle's Review of Anarchy Online

Quick Summary
Anarchy Online is a game with great potential that suffers from awful playability, poor planning, and lack of foresight on the part of the game creators. The game has nice graphics and a decent user-interface. It is easy to start a game session and stop a game sessions.

Playability
In its current state the game is almost entirely unplayable. Killing one monster will sometimes take as much as 3 hours of constantly relogging into a crashing game. "Zoning" or moving from one area to another works about 20% of the time. 40% of the time the game will crash when zoning. 40% of the time you will receive a message that says the server was unable to load your zone.

This game suffers from time-warps, albeit tiny ones, every time it crashes (which is about every 15 minutes). Visibility is poor, and newbies have no way to easily afford "night vision" goggles, which means expect 50% your first 12 game hours to be stumbling around in the dark (that is, when you can log in).

Skinny players are at a disadvantage as they can get stuck behind machinery and trapped in walls. Make sure to choose a class that can easily kill itself to escape these pitfalls.

One playability advantage to AO is, unlike EverQuest, you can load a game, run a mission, get phat lewt, exp and retire to your apartment all within a 45 minute span. You do not have to spend 3 hours wandering from zone to zone searching for players to make a group. The game seems designed to be very playable solo. Grouping will advance you faster, but solo play is not near-impossible, like in EQ.

Also, you can sit down and basically "save" your game in the middle of a mission.l When you log back in, you can pick up where you left off. Unlike EQ, you don't have to worry about having to log back in to an area where you will die and have to spend hours on CR. Which brings up another plus . . .

Corpse recovery is also done well. Basically you bind at "Insurance terminals" When you die, you are "reconstructed" at an insurance terminal. You collect your "corpse" from a nearby reclamation booth. This ends hours of aimlessly wandering a zone shouting "Has anyone seen my corpse?"

A final playability plus is the character development system. The character development is far superior to EQ or Ultima Online. Allowing chracaters of same race/class/level to have widely different skills. Each time you reach a new level, you spend development points on certain skills. The skill costs are weighted by race and class. Character advancement is very similar to many popular table-top RPG systems.

Planning
Last night someone said "The person who thought that one server could handle all of this load should be shot." Another replied, "No…. slowly tortured."

The game creators should have seen these problems ahead of time. The game was supposed to be stress tested. I logged in from 4am to 8am EST to test how well this game works during the least populated hours. I still crashed every 25 minutes, couldn't zone, and suffered from intolerable lag.

I do not see how "FunCom" will be able to recover from this oversight. It will take too long to set up hardware to accommodate demand. It will take too long to reprogram how the server software handles network traffic. There is simply to much work to be done to repair this game, and it is unlikely that Funcom will have the resources to do so.

Apparently they have 35,000 players in AO. Their login server can handle 700 logins per hour (max). You do the math.

In the meantime, Funcom has hundreds of thousands of screaming customers who paid $60.00 for this game and another $20.00 just to log in. These customers will not want to wait the months it will take to repair the flaws that make the game unplayable. Especially as there are alternative graphic-based MMORPGs out there to choose from.

Graphics
The graphics for the game are very nice -on par with EverQuest or Ultima Online. The user interface is also well-designed and easy to use. All of this helps little when your character can't sit or stand for 10 minute stretches because of network lag.

The actually variety in graphics is only average. Many of the textures on character armor look similar. Characters do not look as different from each other as they do in EverQuest.

Game Mechanics
The game mechanics in AO are very poorly documented. More than half of the objects you interact with in the world will have no supporting documentation. Well over half the actions you take, like making groups, setting up chat channels, creating items, using items, will be undocumented.

One of the worst features of this game is the inability for two people who start the game at the same time to actually play with each other. I tried contacting my old EQ buddy Sanctume for some "barshing" in the Omni-1 zone newbie area. The problem is that there is not just 1 newbie area, but many possible spawned newbie areas. When you enter one area the Server will randomly place you in an existing "spawned" area or create a new one. The odds of you ending up in the same area as your friend are slim. After hours of trying, Sanctume and I were unable to meet with and group with each other. How sad.

Later, as you progress in levels, it is easier to "hook up" with your friends. However, there is still always the possibility of the two of you entering the same zone only to have your buddy shunted off to an alternate spawn of that area.

Finally, the outdoor NPC vendors are only able to talk with one person at a time. This causes ludicrously long lines or even mobs of players who surround the NPC, constantly clicking on him to try to get his attention. Other "machine" vendors, and spawned stores do not suffer from this problem as much.

Conclusion
While the concepts behind the game, the graphics, and the Sci-fi aspect of the game are appealing, the awful playability destroys any hope for the game. I give AO a rating of "Almost Useless".
