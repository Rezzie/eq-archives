There is this thing in the world called "reality".

No, I am not going to get metaphysical ... I am uninterested in whether we are self-fulfilling entities or brains in a vat in a New Jersey basement ... no, no, no, I want to talk about everyday reality.

All of us, everyone, feel that, for the most part, our impressions of the world are reasonable. We look back at the days of pre-enlightenment mumbo jumbo with scorn and raise our malt liquor in a toast to our well formed society.

But the truth of the matter is, all of us, everyone, take a lot of stuff on faith alone. All of us have certain impressions that we feel to be basically true, yet we have absolutely no idea where these impressions came from. Sure, we try to tell ourselves that we have created these impressions ourselves, but the fact of the matter is that society has created a lot of these impressions for us. Here is a short list:

1. Women can't have sex without love.

2. If you go out in the cold rain bare foot, you will catch a cold.

3. Sitting too close to the TV damages your eyes.

Trust me, there are billions of impressions like these swirling in our heads this very minute.

But where do these impressions come from? Well, they come from all over the place, but in our age of enlightenment, a lot of them come from these pernicious things called "studies."

Let's take the television example listed above. There is not an American (or perhaps a Canadian) alive who has not heard this. I have absolutely no idea if tv damages the eyes or if this is merely a myth, but I do want to use the example to make a point about "studies."

Let's go back to the 1950's for a moment. Television was a new media and, with every new technology, it no doubt created a lot of suspicion and eagerness in the eyes of young professors seeking tenure. Let me make up an article to make my point:

"In a recent study, scientists have discovered that tv damages your eyes!"

Wham! A myth is born. The populace gets excited and there is a mass movement of sofas away from the satanic glare of the tv screen.

Of course, it may just be that when you look at the study closer (which no one actually does), you will find some interesting caveats:

"In a recent study (by the Institute of Institutional Institutes in a Hut in Belize), scientists (men with PhDs in theological nutrition and sports atrocities) have discovered (by taping mice face forward into television sets) that tv damages your eyes (if you put your eyeball directly on tv)!"

Whoa! This changes everything completely! This study is a total fraud!

And yet, the damage is already done. A myth is born!

[note: remember, I am just making this stuff up to make a point. TV could very well damage your eyes, I have no clue.]

[note: I am well aware of the irony of me making up stuff to criticize people making stuff up.]

So, as you can see, our "reality" may not be as grounded in fact as we all wish it could be.

Now it is time to trash the Stanford study. Let's work through the article linked above.

"PALO ALTO, Calif., Feb 16 (Reuters) - As Americans spend more time on the Internet they are not only seeing less of their friends and families, but also spending less time stuck in traffic, shopping in malls and watching television. "

Ok, here is problem number one. The media. I would have felt a lot better if the opening paragraph had started out with "According to a Stanford study ..." Instead, the first paragraph is presented as a fact. This may seem like a small point, but remember that many people do not bother to get into the heady details of the actual study. They read this paragraph and already start assimilating it into the fabric of myth without giving it much thought. TV outlets like CNN, NBC, etc. no doubt pick up on this too and send Tom Brokaw out to smirk to the world that the internet is, factually, making people little isolated wonk monkeys.

"A new study on the Internet's impact on society released Wednesday reached the conclusion that the more time people spent online, the less time they spent with real people. But it also revealed some possible benefits, including a reduction in the time spent on some of life's other drudgeries and mindless activities."

Real people? In scientific studies, there is this thing called an operational definition. If you are going to study the effects of chocolate on sexual desire, for example, you need to operationally define desire so that it can be measured quantitatively. The same goes here. What, exactly, is a "real person"? What does this mean? If I am talking to someone on ICQ, is this not an interaction with a real person? Admittedly, I am parsing words that Reuters, and not necessarily the study, presented, but I have read several articles about this study (I cannot find the study myself) and all of them seem to make this distinction of "real people" versus ... gosh, "fake people" I guess.

"Despite these trends, the study, by the Stanford Institute for the Quantitative Study of Society at Stanford University, found that the majority of people who use the Internet still have not seen a significant change in the way they spend their time. About two-thirds of the Internet users surveyed said they spent less than five hours a week online."

Ok, here is the KEY paragraph that most readers, journalists, mutants, dogs, cats, etc. will skip over and not remember. This is very very key: the study found that the majority of people have not seen a significant change. However, as you will soon see, this key fact will be totally ignored so that journalists and scientists can start leaping to some mighty large (and unfounded) conclusions.

"But as Stanford Professor Norman Nie noted, the percentage of heavy Internet users is growing, and will likely increase as a portion of the overall population as the Internet continues to reach new households."

Oops, here we go. The professor has moved from scientific data to the realm of opinion. Lock up the kids, a Stanford professor has said heavy internet usage may go up! We are all heading for moral decline!

``The Internet could be the ultimate isolating technology that further reduces our participation in communities even more than television did before it,'' said Nie, who was the chief investigator in the study.

This is just totally and completely irresponsible. This statement right here absolutely pisses me off. He takes his data (which we will look at in a minute), completely misinterprets it (to fit his somewhat Luddite tendencies) and then starts leaping to these totally irresponsible opinions, comparing the internet to tv (like comparing apples to oranges). Any beginning statistics student would get an F for saying unfounded shit like this.

Another key finding of the study was that the more years people have been using the Internet, the more hours they tend to spend online. In addition, sixty percent of regular Internet users say the Internet has reduced their TV viewing, and one-third spend less time reading newspapers.

Ok, here is some data. The study found that 60 percent OF HEAVY INTERNET USERS WHICH A MAJORITY OF PEOPLE ARE NOT!!! PLEASE SEE THE QUICKLY FORGOTTEN PARAGRAPH ABOVE watch less tv. Is this a bad thing? What, is television now seen as some sort of wholesome community activity? The study also found that one third OF HEAVY INTERNET USERS WHICH A MAJORITY OF THE PEOPLE ARE NOT spend less time reading newspapers. The implication here, of course, is that the intelligent act of reading a paper is being supplanted by the evil internet. I suppose the study did not consider that people actually read fucking news on the internet (I know I do) therefore eliminating the need to buy a paper.

One activity that tends to increase with increased Internet use is work. While a small fraction -- about four percent -- of those surveyed -- said they cut back their hours at the office after gaining Internet access, 16 percent said they were working more hours at home without cutting back at the office. Another eight percent reported increases in time spent working at home and at the office.

Sixteen percent of people now work more at home!!! What?!?! Lock up the children! No one points out, however, that a lot of things correlate with the internet. Are they working more at home because of the internet, or is it that because of the internet driven economy, more companies are pushing their employees to work harder to bring their companies into the internet revolution? I dont know, man. This is pretty fishy to me.

Several Internet companies were quick to issue their interpretation of the study, and point out that by shopping and planning trips online, people should actually have more time to spend with other people.

Let me rewrite this:

Several evil Internet companies with their own self-interest in mind were quick to scurry around to cover their butts.

The online shopping site Brodia.com even offered some tips on making Internet surfing a less isolated activity. ``It may boil down to where your computer lives in your home,'' Brodia said in a statement, advising people to place their computers ``in a communal room or high traffic area.'' Doing so, it said, would help ``create an environment where family may flourish in all the same ways it did when everyone ate dinner or watched TV together.''

Hey, I have no problem with that, but do you see what has happened? When TV started hurting people's eyes, I posited above, sofas were moved all around the country. Now, based on a bunch of very very iffy data used to jump to large conclusions, a company is suggesting people rearrange their house, totally ignoring that A MAJORITY OF THE PEOPLE ARE NOT EVEN COVERED BY THIS STUDY!!!!

Bah.

This study is totally irresponsible and Stanford should be ashamed. Even if all Americans were spending less time watching TV, reading newspapers, etc. (and this is NOT what the study suggests), it is still grossly irresponsible to ignore the fact that the internet, by its very post-defense-network existence, is EXACTLY a community. That is the whole freaking point. I probably communicate with 20 times the number of people I communicated with before the internet revolution. Bah! This is crazy stuff!

But the studies will keep on rolling. Why? Because newspapers need copy and professors need tenure.

But before you start rearranging your furniture, please remember my favorite phrase when dealing with perceptions of reality:

caveat emptor
