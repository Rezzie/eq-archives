Part 1. Romeo and Juliet is a TRAGEDY.

Whenever we think of romance in literature, what is one of the first literary works to pop into our minds? Romeo and Juliet. It's not difficult to see why. These two star crossed lovers spend most of the play speaking unbelievable words to each other and they end up, tragically, dying for their love.

I have two points to make about this:

a. "you kiss by the book" - Juliet
b. Romeo and Juliet ended up DEAD.

Shakespeare's plays are many things, but I believe they are primarily morality tales. They are not morality plays in the sense of earlier literature in the English lit canon, but they always seek to teach a lesson. Almost all of Shakespeare's comedies followed the traditional hero pattern of "hero encounters adversity - hero hangs out with societal misfits for a while - hero overcomes adversity and brings the boon back to his society." Most of Shakespeare's tragedies follow the pattern of "flawed hero breaks himself/herself on a rigid code".

If you think about it, Romeo and Juliet seems pretty far removed from the tragedies of Hamlet or Othello or MacBeth. What could be more different than two teens in love and three grizzled psychotics going slowly ... psychotic?

Does this mean that Romeo and Juliet somehow breaks from Shakespeare's tragic mold? Not at all; and believing that Romeo and Juliet is somehow different from these other tragedies is completely misunderstanding Romeo and Juliet.

In all of these cases, the true tragedy does not lie in the feuds between courts or siblings or whatever but in the heroes' inability to free themselves from some sort of rigid code. Hamlet is driven insane by his rigid sworn allegiance to his father. Macbeth is ripped apart by his fiercely dogmatic duty as king, as is Othello.

What does this have to do with Romeo and Juliet? Everything. No other line more elegantly states this than Juliet's comment to Romeo after a kiss:

Juliet: You kiss by the book.

Ah, how romantic. Um ... not really. Here's a modern translation:

Juliet: You kiss as if you picked up a Kissing for Idiots book and studied it ... you really kinda suck.

Whereas Hamlet, MacBeth and Othello were broken on the rock of monarchical duty, Romeo and Juliet were broken on the rock that is the good ole fashioned Plutarchian notions of love.

Gah, how do I sum up Plutarch. Quickly, Plutarch's tales were widely published in Shakespeare's time as Plutarch's Lives and Shakespeare borrowed HEAVILY from him. In many ways, Plutarch was in Shakespeare's time what Shakespeare is in our time. When people thought about love back then, they thought about Plutarch. It is difficult to overstate how incredibly important Plutarch was in the "love tradition" of English literature.

And it was this kind of mindless following of Plutarch that Shakespeare was criticizing in Romeo and Juliet. Romeo and Juliet were very much in love, but they were in love according to the book (I cannot emphasize this enough ... their speeches are classic Plutarch both in form and function). Have you ever wondered why whenever they meet, they tend to talk to each other in 5000 word paragraphs? This is no accident. Here, Shakespeare is showing that they are not communicating as much as they are shouting love poems at each other.

Romeo and Juliet die, not simply because of their warring families (the obvious reason), but because they are so seduced by the language of love. Do I need to tell you that many of Plutarch's heroes died because of love? Shakespeare is simply too smart for this to be a mere coincidence. In fact, the absolute genius of Romeo and Juliet is that it is now considered to be one of the greatest love stories of all time, recited by countless lovers, when it is this exact kind of blind recitation that led to the tragedy to begin with.

<dramatic, Plutarchian pause>

Because let's not forget, friends, that the death of Romeo and Juliet may be aesthetically pleasing from a far, but from their point of view ... THEY ARE DEAD. This is not a good thing to be. It has always astonished me (even at a young age) that one of our chief western documents on love ended in a double suicide. To summarize what Shakespeare really means (I think): if this is love, I will stick with porn, thank you very much.

Pshew.

 

Part 2. Um, What Does This Have to Do with Anything?

Read back over the real world examples above. These examples have everything to do with the points I just made on Romeo and Juliet.

In our "modern" society, we no longer run around reading Plutarch. Neither do many of us run around reading Shakespeare. Since we don't get our lessons in love from the classics, where do we get them from? Well, we certainly get many lessons from legitimate sources: our parents, the church, etc.

But we dont really listen to our parents or our church, do we? Hell, no. Here is where we really get our lessons in love:

1. Billions of self-help books.

2. Therapists

3. Oprah

4. Self-administered magazine surveys (you do it too, men, dont deny it!)

5. Bubble gum pop movies.

6. The Cackling Klaknak (poor souls)
