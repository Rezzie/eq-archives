Are Computer Games Art?

After playing System Shock 2 for a few hours, this question immediately leapt to mind. I offer a few thoughts on the subject

Before I can answer the question, "Are computer games art?" I should first define what art is.

I have no idea.

This is such an impossibly difficult term to define that I would hazard to say it defies definition. However, I kinda like what my dictionary has to say and I will stick with that for now:

Art: "The conscious use of skill and creative imagination."

I think the best way to talk about gaming as an art is to examine how film shook up the critical community when it came about. Film was a difficult media to define also because it contained many elements familiar to the painting community (mise en scene, etc.) but it also contained strong narrative influences more familiar to the literary community.

Some critics went as far as to suggest an auteur theory of film, which was a fairly interesting, somewhat successful attempt to compare the director of a film with the author of a book while at the same time, many of the more experimental films were being shown in art galleries as a new kind of visual arts exhibit.

Eventually, of course, film developed its own critical language that combined features from both camps. Looking at the history of film criticism is not unlike watching the development of a child into adulthood. Early on, film criticism was dominated heavily by the criticism of its parents (visual art and literature) until, after a great deal of turmoil (the auteur movement), film finally came into its own, sloughing off some lessons from its parents while keeping others.

If you are wondering why I am giving you a brief summary of film criticism, let me tell you that there is method to my madness. In several recent gaming articles both online and in print, editors have drawn a link between film and games and I think they are right. In many ways, games achieve the holy grail of films, total immersion, by actually planting the user in the game ... something a film could never do.

But aside from that key difference, many games share a common bond with film. Games often have at least a semblance of a narrative and there is also a very conscious emphasis on the quality of the game's visual art.

But the question you have to ask, especially when playing games like Carmaggedon or Tomb Raider, is whether the game itself is a work of art or whether it simply contains works of art. I don't think there is a simple answer to this question. I would say, however, that just as the film community is dominated by films that are not art, so it is with games. To deny the art of some games doesn't gainsay the whole. I honestly do think some games, in their entirety, should be considered works of art.

What games should be considered art? That's hard to say. I will make up a list tho:

1. Myst - I absolutely hated this game, but I have to give props to its art. There were times that I was in absolute awe of the quiet, yet beautiful craftsmanship of the game.

2. the Final Fantasy games - I have many problems with the linear nature of these games and I hate the big headed characters, but this was one of the few games where I actually became a little upset when one of the main characters died. Does that make it art? I dunno, but I think it does for me.

3. System Shock 1 and 2 - Rock on, man. What an extraordinarily beautiful but visceral game. If psychologically violent films like Coppola's The Conversation are art, it is hard to dismiss the System Shock series as mere games.

There is oh so much more I would like to say, but this will serve as a good introduction I think. I am interested in hearing what you guys have to say.
