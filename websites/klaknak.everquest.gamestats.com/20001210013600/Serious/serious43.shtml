Save Gems in Daikatana: Puss Filled Sore or Disease Ridden Puss Filled Rat Meat?

by The Cackling Klaknak


I went to Babbage's the other day.

As soon as I walked passed those large pillars that I had previously thought to be security devices, I felt a peculiar "twing" in my brain. A few minutes later, solely against my own volition, I walked out with a purchased copy of Daikatana.

This is not going to be a review of Daikatana (ok, here it is: it is not as bad as everyone desperately wants it to be). Instead, I want to talk about this little game feature (seen in Daikatana) called a "Save Gem."

Back in the era when side scrolling games were king, the save point was the extremely annoying court jester, seemingly there for only the king's amusement. Whenever you took your little character through a level (let's call him "Sonic the Hedgehog"), if you died before you reached a save point, you had to do the whole blasted level over again. This led to what Stanford physicists have called The Mario Rule which states:

"The probability of your dying is inversely proportional to the distance between you and your save point."

I have never been sure why console games remained obsessed with save points, but I have always secretly believed it had something to do with either the gaming code or a marked causal relationship between game development and sadism.

When what are now "traditional" computer games began to take their rightful place alongside the console side scrollers, the save point philosophy began to decline. Whenever a game attempted to use a save point system instead of a "save anywhere" system, the save point way almost always became a Con in the reviewer's pro/con summary. When Galapagos came out several years ago, the save point lasted maybe two weeks. The first patch issued by the company immediately implemented a "save anywhere" window with their apologies. Damn straight.

So imagine my surprise when I opened Daikatana and discovered that it used a "save gem" feature. I couldn't have been more surprised if Glenn Miller had popped out of the box and started playing a brass instrumental version of Murder Was the Case by Snoopy Doggy Dogg.

As I sat their seething and cursing the secret "buy me now!" brain manipulator that Romero had somehow placed in my brain, I developed a theory as to why this cursed feature was placed in the game.

Anyone who is familiar with the BBS of an FPS has probably seen a post like this:

"You l00zers! I completed Half-life in 2 hours, without saving, with both arms tied behind my back while freebasing Clorox."

Certain gamers take a great pride in going through an entire level without saving and the debate between savers and nonsavers is kind of like a mini-FPS version of the eternal PK vs. nonPK RPG debates.

But I think the argument against save gems or save points or save whatevers is clear and I will back it up with a story. When I worked at another company, I worked with a woman whom I will call Nelda. Nelda was a very small, very quiet woman who could stand under the business end of a rocket booster during launch and declare quietly that there is a "chill in the air" while pulling her sweater closer around her.

As such, there was always a huge debate during the summer months. Most of us wanted to have the AC cranked up really high, while Nelda and a few of her minions wanted it off because it made the office too cold.

My argument always was this: If you are too cold, you can put on a sweater. If you are too hot, you can't just start taking off clothes while working in an office.

Anyway.

What does this have to do with the save game? Lots. If you revel in running through each level of the game without saving, you can do that with the "save anywhere" feature. Just don't save. However, if you like to save your game before, say, trying to jump on that tiny radioactive ledge with a 5000 pound Gargantua on it, you should be able to. Using a "save anywhere" feature encompasses all players, whereas the nasty little "save gem" feature caters to only a small subset of players.

So, to answer the age old question, are save gems puss filled sores or disease ridden puss filled packets of rat meat, I would have to answer a most definitive yes.
