Bullshit Incorporated - Company Seminars

by the Cackling Klaknak

I have a dear friend (I will call her "Jen") whom I email everyday. Three of our core topics is the ludicrosity (tm) of self-help books, company management and BDSM. Thankfully for you, but regrettably for me, I had to attend one of our company's annual "seminars" dealing with workplace issues this morning and it pretty much covered all these bases. As I sat squirming in my chair, I began taking notes. Not to learn, not to listen to ... but to ridicule. So here we go.

The paradigm (tm) is simple. Let me draw it out for you.

1. Employees' morale is low because of shitty pay and unrealistic work schedules.

2. The employer senses this and forms a committee.

3. The committee decides to hire a motivational speaker.

In a way, I felt sorry for the guy they called in today. In case you don't know, I work for a company that is about 70% psychologists and there is probably nothing scarier for a motivational speaker than for him to offer up his half-assed, acronym filled ideas to a bunch of people who do real psychology for a living. But offer he did, and boy was it pathetic.

First off, the name of the seminar was "The Art of Practicing Safe Stress" and the speaker was "psychohumorist" (tm) (he trademarked this, people, I am not making this up) Mark Gorkin.

After dispensing with the usual pleasantries, he began on the same path that every wrongheaded motivational speaker uses to make money. Here is the how-to guide for corporate motivational speakers.

1. First, talk about the problem. This gets people to agree with you. If they agree with you on this, it makes it easier for you to cram your bullshit acronyms down their throat later. Example from today: This guy basically gets up there and says: "Boy, I sure hate stress." and then begins spewing Reader's Digest humorous anecdotes. Everyone snickers and pounds coffee.

2. Second, talk about the problem "clinically". All you have to do is grab the ole DSM IV and list the signs of the problem. This gives your speech an air of authority.

3. Make up some sort of group project where the people at the tables can get up and start bitching about things. This keeps people from falling asleep and makes them feel like they are contributing. Example from today: We had to make a list of things that stress us and represent it pictorially.

4. After the group effort, club the people senseless with your stupid acronyms. You use acronyms to cover up the fact that every goddamn motivational speaker in the world tells you the same goddamn thing. By using an acronym that is particularly clever like P.A.R.A.D.I.G.M or G.O.! you will be called to give seminars around the world.

Example from today: Here is how you deal with stress: A.A.A. I will let you figure out what it means.

So here is my rant. It may take me a while to get back to the psychotic humorist (tm) but stay with me.

What is the main goal of a company? To make money.

I have no problem with this, but here is where many managers go wrong:

Because the goal of a company is to make money, it does not make sense to give employees adequate raises, benefits, etc. etc. Paying employees costs money. We are here to make money, not spend it. Ergo, the employees are wrongheaded to ask for more money. We know what's good for them. They are being unrealistic.

This may seem overly dramatic, but I don't think it is. I have worked for a very large publishing company, for a small business and for a health organization and this attitude pervades managerial decisions. Management seems to be missing a core logic when they throw money all over the place for marketing and building projects while constantly snipping away at the workforce. Let me state what should be the true managerial logic.

The goal of a company is to make money.

The best long term way to make money is to nurture a happy workforce.

Flash in the pan marketing schemes, cost cutting, new fucking "paradigms" etc. are all well in good, but if your workforce is fundamentally unhappy, you are throwing good money after bad.

Of course, this is not to say that a typist deserves $100,000 a year, child care, 6 months of vacation, etc. etc. but if you are paying her minimum wage and pay as you go health care, don't come bitching to me if production and quality in your typing pool is stagnant.

Let's just take that example for a moment. Here is the traditional way managers handle stagnant production:

1. Form a committee to look at stagnation.

2. Invite one token person from the typing pool to sit in on it. Give him or her no power. When he leaves, talk about how you are tired of hearing employees bitch.

3. Hire a consultant to look at the organization of the typing pool.

4. Reorganize according to the consultant.

5. Send the typing pool to a motivational seminar.

6. Hold a picnic. Give each employee a pen.

7. When revenues are down, tell typing pool that since their production sucked, you don't have enough money to give them much of a raise.

8. Watch as some of your best typists leave for better jobs. Only the shitty ones stay because the shitty ones realize they probably couldn't get a better job.

9. Hire a bunch of scrubs. Pay to train them.

10. Watch as production falls even more.

11. Go to #1.

Do you see where I am going here? I wish to hell I was making this stuff up but I can assure you that I am not.

How do you fix this stuff? Here is the Klaknaks' guide (tm) to creating a healthy worksource (tm). I realize that revenue (tm) is the singlemost important thing and that some of these things are sometimes unrealistic, but I bet if managers spent as much energy on employees as they do coming up with the latest marketing gimmick, they could find some creative ways to make this stuff happen:

1. Stop hiring motivational speakers for anything. There is not an employee alive who sees this stuff as anything other than pure bullshit. I am serious, people! It is downright offensive, I think, when employee complaints are answered by management via a horseshit peddler.

2. Move HR to the second spot on your company's revenue disbursement list. Here is the order: 1. Customer Service 2. HR. Again, this I know from personal experience. Whenever our company made lots of money, instead of spending it on employees (who made all that money), they spent it on marketing gimmicks. Of course and certainly a company needs to expand, but if you expand your company (ergo workload) at the expense of employees, you are asking for trouble. Besides, I am not saying to totally ignore marketing, infrastructure, etc. I am just saying you should move HR ahead of marketing and infrastructure.

3. Encourage employees to offer suggestions and then comment on those suggestions and act on the ones that are good. How many of you out there have been on a committee where employees make suggestions (some of them good) and then every single one of them are ignored without one peep from management? Just as with the motivational speakers, employees are incredible bullshit detectors, so if you are going to run the risk of giving them more bullshit to be angry about, make sure you actually listen to what they have to say and act on what they have to say.

4. Treat your employees like you treat your stockholders. This seems fair enough. Companies bend over backwards to assuage the people that pump money into the company, but they do nothing to assuage the people who use that money to make the company. If you have happy stockholders but unhappy employees, eventually the stockholders will become unhappy too. I guarantee it.

5. Encourage your employees to go out and get drunk together. Ahem. Hey, I am being serious!

Anyway, this is my take on things. Today was just a downright offensive experience for me and more than once I started doodling on my pad and coming up with ideas for my own business. Thanks for listening to me vent.
