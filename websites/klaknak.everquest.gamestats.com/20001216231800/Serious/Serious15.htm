I don't have a lot of time to get into a discussion, but I just want to present another confusing 'is it art' distinction that has bothered me for a long time. You see, I am a software designer by profession. And I have long contended to friends of mine (none of which are either programmers, or people that agree with me) that my finished product, the CODE itself, is a work of art. Although I don't think anybody in their right mind is going to hang a software version control system on the wall of their studio, there is a measure of aesthetics in software.

It is very easy to tell an 'elegant' solution from a mess, or a 'kludge'. The definition you have printed, "The conscious use of skill and creative imagination.", only supports my point. The more imaginative a software solution is, the more likely it is to make me a lot of money. However, I must concede that this definition can be applied to make too many things art, and I don't think that is what you are shooting for. For example, a 'con artist' fits this defination nicely. So, I believe we must further qualify art in order to make it a useful thing. If picking women up at a bar is the same type of art as composing a symphony, I haven't gone where I'd like to go. And although I have never been able to solidly define it, here are a few steps I believe that have led me closer to a strong definition of what is and isn't art:

Classic Art Forms - Since we have some relatively indisputable forms of art, we can fairly start with an assumption that these art forms share some characteristics that define art. At least, if we get to a definition of art, we can assume these art forms will fit those definitions. Examples of classic art forms: painting, sculpture, symphonic music, literature. Intention - Does someone need to start with the intention 'I am creating art', in order to do so? Or conversely, if someone does start with the intention 'I am creating art now', is the final product always art? Architecture comes to mind here. Some buildings (the Parthenon, some of the Newport mansions, etc.) were designed to be aesthetically pleasing. Others are designed completely for utility (tenement houses, strip malls). Far more common are the buildings that need to serve a function, but still should look nice. Are all architects artists, is every building a work of art? There are two other art forms, photography and film, which are even more glaring examples of this disparity. If I pick up the camcorder, am I an artist? Hardly. If Ansel Adams grabs a poloroid and takes a picture of me, is that art? Can a filmmaker accidently not make art (several Van Damme flicks come to mind)? If I take enough pictures of a tree with my 35mm camera, do I have a chance of any of them being art? Clearly this is open to debate, but I think it is safe to say that intention is not the essence of art.

Utility - Can art be useful? A painting certainly can't be, in a conventional sense. But what about a piece of furntinture? I've seen chairs in art museums. On the other hand, my office guest chair over there is certainly not art in my opinion. Again, a dead end. Opinion - this is the only place I see left for debate. What if we define the essence of art as 'something abstract, symbolic, or representative that creates an emotional reaction'. Problem is, things can be art to some people and not to others. Is this a problem? I think not. 'Beauty is in the eye of the beholder'. This defintions works for me, as I often feel the same way after being punched in the face as I do after looking at a Klee painting. When Old Yeller dies, its as if my girlfriend has left me. My adreneline starts pumping when I read a Salvatore novel, or when I listen to Slayer, just like when I am standing at the top of a black diamond mogul run. And yet, when I look at my office chair... nothing. Based on that, computer games, being a virtual experience, is just as much art as the cover of Dragon magazine, as long as you react to it. And my code... well, whatever floats my boat. Bah, I rambled. Comments? -Kellan.

Are computer games art? Yes and No. Are computer game players artists too? Yes and No. Let me explain. As your definition (which I really like BTW) says, art requires both skill and creative imagination. I think that most computer games have both. Lets pick a popular game like... I don't know... Everquest. Does EQ count as art? I think so. I think it is quite apparent that EQ involves alot of skill. The designers made a beautiful world, that required no small amount of programming skill. The GM's keep 99% of the players happy which takes ALOT of people skills. Imagination? I have extreme difficulty coming up with names of random mooks in the Pen and Paper RP game I run. Yet they came up with names for probably 10,000 people, zones, treasure, and even monsters. Also think of the quests in the game. The Freeport Militia and the Paladin's Guild? The Heretics and the Other Spellcasters in Erudin? The list goes on and on. And if you wanted to, you could just immerse yourself in the number of plots running around Norrath.

But do all games have both Skill and Imagination? I'm sure everyone can think of a Doom/Quake clone that had no creative thought behind it. Games with the artistic merit of either Televangelists or Friends. Those are not art, but rather a distraction from what's really out there...
