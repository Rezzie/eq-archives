To answer the question in short: there is no way that the farming/item devaluation can ever be solved by verant by rewriting the loot rules of the game, a.k.a. TLC. Another approach must be used.

Here's why:

In the real world, the economy works in accordance with the law of supply and demand. The price of any given product usually arrives at an equilibrium point, a naturally selected price for it, which is the highest price that the buyers are willing to pay and the lowest that the sellers will agree to sell at.

Now, keep in mind that in a real economy, the lowest price the sellers are willing to accept is determined mostly by the variable costs they incur - a combination of the price of raw materials used and/or labor used. The seller has a cutoff price-point below which he will not supply any more items to buyers because his costs exceed his profit and he will be netting a financial loss. In essence, Nike will not sell shoes for $5.00 a pair if it costs them $5.25 to make that pair of shoes; same applies to its competitors who will not undercut Nike prices too much for fear of losing money themselves. Such natural supply limits are what keeps the prices of products stable in any free-market economy.

Imagine the same economy, but with one variable set to zero - the variable, or product, cost. The seller can obtain the goods he sells at zero raw material cost to himself and no labor costs, as he doesn't have employees to pay wages to. With no cost to himself, no price he sells the product will be too low, because anything he charges is pure profit. In such a situation, the seller will supply as many items as he can sell, with no regard for the price dropping because he will never be at a loss. To return to the shoe analogy, Nike will be more than willing to sell the pair of shoes at $5.00 a pair if Adidas is selling at the same rice, especially since it cost both Adidas and Nike $0.00 to make the shoes in the first place.

The first example is the real-world economy; the second, the EQ player-run economy. The lack of variable cost (and fixed cost, though I've omitted it in my explanation for simplicity's sake) means that there is no check on the amount of goods supplied to the economy, and is the reason for the rampant inflation of item prices. After all, a high-level player farming items for sale invests nothing, no raw materials and no labor costs as he does the camping himself.

Is there a way to fix it? Yes, there is one way that I can see to fix the item inflation/farming problem, and that is to introduce real cost to obtaining each item. A way needs to be found to attach real financial cost to obtaining any item in the game, otherwise it will quickly become very cheap.

One example of this system in place are the crafted armor quests - the gems for the armor cost a certain amount, and the armor prices tend to remain on a certain level matching those gems, so that those who have done the quest do not incur a financial loss. The fact that many of the crafted pieces can be bought cheaper than the gems required for the quest discourages people from doing the quests - naturally limiting the supply of those items into the economy. Eventually, with fewer and fewer people doing the quests the amount of crafted armor pieces in the game will decrease to the point where ther price rises and the quests become worth doing again, again restoring the influx of them into the economy and keeping the price at an equilibrium.

Such a mechanism needs to be implemented into the item drops in the game. The change would be simple - make each item dropped a quest, with two components - a dropped item and a cash amount in either common gems (like rubies, sapphires) or simply pp (as in the Journeyman's Boots quest). The dropped component would need to be NO DROP, like the crafted armor components, so it won't ge sold on its own at no cost to whoever is selling it.

I suspect that Verant has not implemented such a system as we have in place because they wanted the players to "work" for their items as opposed to buying them in a store; they made them monster-dropped. This would keep that idea intact as the quest piece will still need to be looted from the mob (and thus worked for). It would also stabilize the economy by keeping the price of items high since nobody, not even high level players, would farm the quest pieces and turn them in with rubies/pp for items to be sold for less than what it cost to obtain them.

How is this good?
- Twinking for the high level characters, and therefore their "reward" for being high level, remains intact as they would be able to camp the quest piece/pay the pp OR buy the item on an open market for a comparable sum.
- The rampant camping would be eliminated. This may sound miraculous, but once all those who can afford the item at its market cost have the item, the usual EQ mechanism - a continuous supply of items forcing a drop in their price - would not work now, because if the price drops below the cost of the quest it wouldn't make economic sense for people to farm items at a loss.
- People of the appropriate level could still do their fighting and attain items; they wouldn't be camped 24/7, since after a while increasing supply with a demand at a certain price saturated would yield a lower price and make farming un-economical. New items would, of course, be camped for a few weeks after their release until their price drops to the eqilibrium level.

As I see it, introducing real variable costs to obtaining items would satisfy both sides here: mainly, it would eliminate item farming as a way to get rich, since after a while there wouldn't be people to sell the items to, and lowering the price to broaden the market wouldn't be possible as it would result in farming netting a financial loss. Case in the point: crafted armor quests. Still sold, price staying solid, not many farmers on the pieces since the market for them is saturated and lowering the price on items would mean losing money.

Furthermore, it would still allow high level players to twink their alts, albeit not as cheaply, and it would eliminate the rapid devaluation of EQ items.

What is required is for Verant to implement those quests and set prices for the items in the economy. The player economy would still remain intact: not everyone wants to spend the time camping the quest pieces, even though it would be possible, the way not everyone in a real economy would want to produce their toothbrush when it's easier to buy it. The quest items, as mentioned before, would have to be NO DROP as to necessitate spending the money for the quest before obtaining items - otherwise the quest pieces would simply replace items themselves in trade.

That's about it for my suggestion on how to cure the EQ player-run economy. Look at the real world, look at EQ, see what's missing in EQ from the real world and implement it, then let the law of supply and demand take its course.

Any thoughts?

Your real world economics aren't as fully considered as they might be.... You need to consider the need in capitalist societies for every company (at least those owned by public shareholders) to make the most profit possible. This provides pressure to raise prices.

Pressures to lower prices include competition, government regulation, customer abilities to purchase. Under competition, we should include alternatives available that aren't exactly the same, but serve many of the same functions. There's also a pressure to reduce prices to avoid maintaining inventory (which may be taxed, and in any case, ties up capital invested, and costs to protect, etc.) It can be better for a company to take a one time loss (especially if there's a tax write off) than to incur continuing losses and keep capital tied up.

Nike, your example, makes a MASSIVE profit off its shoes, such a large profit as to make labor costs, for example, nearly negligible. It can do so because the pressures from competition and the public willingness/ability to buy it's products are in line.

Now, eq economy: pressures to raise prices include player perceptions of time investment, desire to get plat, etc.

pressures to lower prices: increasing availability of alternative items, player perceptions/dislike about spending time in marketing zones, dislike of having bank slots taken up by stuff one doesn't use... AND player level and ability to obtain equal/other items themselves (or have items passed along by generous guildmates).

Result: prices of older items have dropped on mature servers, UNLESS the item is desired by higher level players who prefer to pay rather than spend time getting something. (I'm betting leather padding goes for more now than a year ago on most mature servers because higher level players are skilling up for cultural smithing.) On my server, many lower level chars are twinks, outfitted in stuff from friends who've moved on from, say, quest armor to velious or kunark armors.

To be honest, the economy isn't broken in any way, but rather working in very predictable ways. Your issue is that things you want to make a large profit from (or want to resell for what you paid ages ago) won't make the profit you want. That's not a broken economy, but broken expectations about the economy.

Expect deflation in an eq economy except for items higher level chars want and don't want to get themselves, and you'll probably be right.

The problem with the EQ economy is not that there is no cost to obtain items. As someone pointed out, there is a labor cost.

The problem is that in a real world ecomony, it functions on the basis of supply and demand of SCARCE resources. In EQ items that enterthe economy stay in the economy forever. Eventually, every item in th game becomes common, thus it's price drops. Items that drop more rarely still lose value because of this, they just lose it more slowly, because they become common more slowly.

Item value decays for a particular person because as theyincrease in level, they need better and better equipement. However, the items that they used before can still be passed on to lower level characers for whom the item is still acceptable, or even highly desired. Unless real item decay is introduced, the player run economy in EQ will continue to deteriorate. Already on older servers, many items that once command high prices are sold to merchants. Most trade skill items that could once turn a profit for the maker are useless junk. Try to sell a banded tunic when people are selling better dropped items for less. It's impossible.

These days, level 30 characters, NON-twinked, can easily purchase armor and weapons better than those used by many level 50 characters before Kunark came out.

Because there is a greater supply then demand, there is a scramble by sellers to unload items, and a lower interest in the buying population so the price must decrease.

Thankfully, through expansions, new zones, new quests, etc. new items are constantly introduced into the game.
