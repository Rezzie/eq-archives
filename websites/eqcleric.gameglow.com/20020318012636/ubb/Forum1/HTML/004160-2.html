What you pay your $9.89 a month for is access to the EQ play servers and space on each server to store your characters.

This is a service.

The TOS does not guarantee you the right to have "fun". It does not mean you will be treated well or with respect. It does not mean Verant has to care about you one way or the other.

The only reason I could see Verant getting sued would be if they terminated an account without probable cause as listed in the TOS. For instance, someone says something on the official EQ boards Verant doesn't like and bans them. I'm not sure if the TOS covers that so you could, theoretically, sue them for damages.

The key here is what the TOS does and doesn't say. I'm not a lawyer and don't speak legalese so I can't be sure. All I can tell you is how it was explained to me.

As far as I know Verant can't be sued for, say, a disgruntled ranger quitting because Verant refuses to fix his class. Fun is a very subjective thing and Verant never promised anyone fun.

Just my 2cp...

I personally have been saying this is the core of the problem for quite some time. Verant was a smallish company before EQ came out, and most likely only had a Devo Engineering team, and not a Sustaining Engineering team. Coding new stuff makes money. Fixing code in old stuff does not. I have been in Abashi's place, actually, for a MUCH less popular product. We suffered the same problems. There were major, crippling bugs that did not get fixed for some 14 months. The reason for this was that we did not have a separate sustaining engineering team, and the programmers(and their managers) had to choose between coding something new, exciting, ground breaking, and money making, or 2 year old code that in many cases, they did not write. I see a LOT of the same behavior in Verant, so I assume that their sustaining team is either A)lacking in talent, B)lacking in staff, or both. Abashi could be going to the programmers/higher ups with the problems that he sees as legitimate, they might just not be getting done.

That being said, I don't think that Abashi is doing that good of a job being the spokesperson for the players. One of the BIG tips to being a successful CR person is to sympathize with the customer, and make it known to them that you understand their plight, and that you will do whatever you can to address their issue. Another big tip is to honestly answer the questions, even if it is not what the people want to hear or it makes you look bad. I would much rather hear "What you reported is a bug, but we don't know how to fix it" than "It is working as intended", when everyone seems to agree that it is indeed NOT working as it should, or has in the past.

Abashi really should start trying to get the customers on his side, by any means possible. This will cut down on the threats, cut down on the sniping from the players, and I think will gain a better sense of communication between Verant and the customers, which I think was reason he was brought on board.

I do feel bad sometimes for the guy, I wouldn't like thousands of people to curse my name.

quote:
One of the BIG tips to being a successful CR person is to sympathize with the customer, and make it known to them that you understand their plight, and that you will do whatever you can to address their issue. Another big tip is to honestly answer the questions, even if it is not what the people want to hear or it makes you look bad.

Isn't there some tension between these principles? If you believe that class X is balanced, and one of your customers says that class X is weak and needs to be improved, how do you respond? If you tell them, we think class X is fine, the customer will definitely not think that you're being "sympathetic," or that you're going to "do whatever you can to address [his] issue." If you tell him "yes, you're right, I understand," you're not being honest, because in fact you disagree with him and do not plan to change the class.

quote:
I would much rather hear "What you reported is a bug, but we don't know how to fix it" than "It is working as intended", when everyone seems to agree that it is indeed NOT working as it should, or has in the past.

You seem to assume that Abashi is being dishonest if he says that "it's working as intended," if "everyone seems to agree" that it's not. Isn't it also possible that Verant and some people on the message board simply have different ideas about how the game should work? With anything as complex as EQ, there are going to be many areas where reasonable people can differ on game design; whatever Verant chooses to do, some of its customers will be upset.

You often see posts on the message boards to the effect of "everyone agrees that X needs to be done, therefore Verant should do it." Usually, the "everyone" in question refers to the posters on a given thread on the message boards. From Verant's perspective, this often doesn't mean much. Posters on the message boards are a minority (probably a small minority) of EQ players, and they aren't a representative sample. You really can't conclude from posts on the official message boards that "everyone" agrees on anything.

No, I don't think Abashi should be fired. The problem isn't Abashi, it is VI and their views on CS as a whole and also testing as a whole. I'll speak from three perspectives here: programmer (my job), Test server resident (play), and as a former guide. Abashi is only the tip of the iceberg of VI problems.

The guide program functions EXACTLY the same way as the "whineplay" boards. Guides that do not toe the party line and post negative comments on guide boards are severly censored or even fired. I left the program after feeling the frustration of this as one member after another on my guide server quit or were fired. A server that had no Senior guide for over 3 months. Abashi was a part of that culture and helped create it. Guides brought up many legitimate concerns last summer. After being told to be quiet or things were working as intended, the program was eventually revamped.

Fast forward to the future and the entry of Abashi to the "whineplay" boards. The same mentality is there. We are told it works as intented, there isn't a problem, etc. Then months later, changes are made.

All these things are simply the cultural view of life from the VI mountaintop. Phrases like "The Vision" and "Your in our world now" says a LOT about how VI views itself and its customers. It automatically creates and "Us" versus "Them" mentality for both parties. This permeates all the way down to the coding and testing level, I would argue. On a place like Test server, very few people are actually "testing" as you would normally test things wrt software. Maybe a dozen or two tops. The rest of the population is largely ignored or penalized by VI's record keeping and shoddy testing methods and shoddy backup methods (hence the i-wipe at the end of the Kunark beta and probably another i-wipe at the end of Velious testing). I can tell you this, every single bug or anomoly that I have encountered and reported over a year of playing there has went live. Every last single one. Not a single exception.

A lot of this has to do with growth, I really believe. A lot of companies that are not mature go through this. VI is a GREAT company for putting out a game and one of the worst for supporting it. You have VI, 989 studios, Sony, the semi-independent guide program all located in different locations, multiple chains of command and multiple visions about what is important for successful gaming. All of this leads to the problems that we now have.

The bottom line, for me, isn't Abashi, it is VI in general and their attitudes. Yes, Abashi could take a lesson in customer service and how to interact with people better. But replacing will not change one single thing about how VI itself operates. Therein is the problem.
