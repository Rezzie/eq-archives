Given that a character is just a set of tables in a database on one server, it should be fairly simple to move characters from one server to another, IN PRINCIPAL. However, this is probably not something that Verant would want to automate, and here's why.

Imagine 6000 people on YOUR server, all at once.

As a lark, people would participate in mass-migrations from one server to another, flooding and probably crashing the server. Or perhaps people will get wind of a special event happening on one server, and several thousand people will flood that server for the event. Still no good. Yet another thing: distribution of items. What happens when those ultra-rare (or unique, one-per-server) items all get bunched up on one server?

Additionally, Verant will not be inclined (I would suspect) to implement a policy of manually moving characters from one server to another (except possibly in the most DIRE of situations, although I couldn't fathom what that situation would entail). Even if there were such a policy in place to move on request, there is too much customer support interfacing that needs to go on. You think there aren't enough GM's now? It was bad enough before we had the /surname command, when people were waiting DAYS for an available GM to com give them their name at level 20. Perhaps a one-time per character (or per account) move policy might satisfy a large number of people.. For instance I started my character on Tarew Marr, and I knew only one other player in the game, and she was on TM, and that's why I created it there. Of course, she was level 20 or so when I started, and she's never going to be able to group with me (unless we both hit 50) because of that split in levels. Then I talk with some friends out of town and find out they started playing last month, on a different server. If I start a new character, I have the same problem, a split in levels, and vice-versa. But my level 16 cleric would be able to jump shards, and start grouping immediately with their level 14-15 characters.

I think it would be great to not only handle those cases, but also for "de-crowding" some of the older servers.

How about this? What if Verant offered to take ~100 volunteers off of 10 different servers and put them on a brand new server? In actuality, they would probably take about 3000 people, I suppose that there are roughly that many total per server, with only half of those active at any given time (roughly). They could do it during a scheduled downtime, and next time you log in, your character is on the new server. I think this would be a GREAT way to spread some of the traffic from the more populated servers to the newer ones. I also think they should close quite a few of the servers, so that people can't create new characters any more... to stop already crowded, but popular servers from getting worse.

Perhaps I have a slightly naive view.... Certainly there are some valid comments, though.

On the state of the economy:

There are NO economics built into the game's code, other than 2 simple mechanisms:

1) a supply of "free" money and items gotten as a result of killing mobs (to a lesser degree this includes completing quests).

2) a "sink" whereby the money and items from #1 are destroyed or removed from the game, usually at a STEEP loss.

The "sink" is basically just the merchants (or any NPC to whom you would give items or money for any reason). You give the merchant some coins, you get an item. You give the merchant an item, you get a few coins, but SUBSTANTIALLY LESS than you would have paid for the same item. This is Verant's way of removing a glut of items and currency from the world. It is not, however, an "economy."

An economy depends on supply and demand (yes, we all remember this from our 4th grade social studies class, so I won't get into the specifics of it). If you sell 1000 bat wings to a merchant, all at one time, the first one is worth 8cp, and the last one is worth 8cp. In anything REASONABLY approximating an economy, the average price in that transaction would be considerably lower (as we all know, buying in bulk gives you leverage in setting the price -- part of the wonderful system of supply and demand). Until the game is altered so that some merchants will have so many bat wings on their hands they just won't take them any more, and still others need them so badly they'll sell their firstborn for them, there's no economy to speak of.

Of course, there are very good reasons for doing this. It stops the newbie zone merchants from cutting off the income of the new characters... who would want to sell in a newbie zone where 60-odd players are ALL selling rat ears for next to nothing? Another reason is that, unlike the real world, the computer can be manipulated. What would stop hundreds of people consorting outside the game to flood one merchant in order to drive down prices, or drive up demand with other merchants to maximize player profits? You can't do this in real life because people will adjust to the situation, people won't have a nearly limitless supply of items to sell and merchants won't have a limitless supply of cash to buy them.

What's the point of this? The point is that the only TRUE economy in the game is player driven one, and it's already faulty before a single player is on the server. It's predicated on the fact that the first person to obtain an item of inherent value sets the price. This is because the individual or group that first controls the spawn the item comes from, they can maintain that control for a considerable time, possibly indefinitely. There are rampant tales of guilds and some unsavory people farming (raping is a better word) rare items for profit. Let's face it, some of the items on some of the older servers are practically COMMON now, they've been collected so much, but the prices are artificially inflated. This is because the source is controlled by a small minority of players. The ones that have acquired the items (by questing or buying) are not to blame, they have theirs, and that's the only one they care about. But there are those that have gotten dozens or hundreds of a particular item, and THEY control the price because they are the only sellers in the market.

A new server is a race to control these spawns and items. The first to reach high enough levels and start obtaining these items are not the people that are interested in obtaining one and moving on, they are the ones that have deliberately worked to get there, so they can STAY there, and control a niche in the market. The person or group that could get to the Jboots first, for instance, could stay there for quite some time; no yield rule to drive them off, and until someone bigger comes along (and no one is bigger, or else they would have gotten there first), they are kings of all they survey... and you'll pay a ridiculous price if you want what they have badly enough. Until Verant does the only other thing possible... change the mob's drop table, and shuffle that item off somewhere else, more difficult to manage.

The point of my argument is that arguing damage to the economy is an impotent claim. Enough of a mix of people would be willing to switch servers that the higher level players would more than likely already have their items, and lower level players would not be threatened. Of course, the low-class element would be present always; there will always be those that have to monopolize something of value and charge a high price for a piece of the action, so what matter if it comes in the form of someone that power-levels to do it, or simply moves their level 50 wizzie over from Cazic-Thule server to do it?

The only real argument to be made is capping a server. From what I've seen, daytime usage is in the 400-700 range across all servers, and peak evening times are 1300-1900 across the boards. Verant stated from the outset that optimum is 1000 per server. My question: what went wrong? I recently read on the alt.games.everquest group a post from Brad McQuaid that each server has roughly 5000 player accounts (not characters), but due to peoples' schedules and such, only 1/3 of them are ever expected to be on at one time, which is why you never see 5000 at a time. Of course, the few occasions (holidays, etc.) when it's possible for more than the usual number to be online is what has me worried. Nothing could be a worse PR nightmare than soothing 3000 EQ weenies home for the holidays, pissed because too many people online brought down their server. You can't tell people that they can't play right now, try again later...

If you took the maximum number of people that could *reasonably* be on a server at one time, added an additional 250-500 to that, and capped the server, I think it would be ideal. It would probably call for twice as many servers as there are now, which is why it won't happen. I look at it this way: they are trying to minimize their maintenance cost, and I understand that, but the computers are not costing THAT much. So what if some servers are almost empty on off-peak hours, and barely at half capacity at peak hours? Happier customers, fewer crashes and less emergency maintenance are the benefits. They don't need to worry about staffing to handle networking crises due to overutilization. They won't be able to blame CERFNET for problems any more... with proper planning of the network segments they can avoid over-taxing their pipeline. Sure, they have twice as many servers to maintain software on, and twice as many hardware components to fail, but even those can be managed, and with hot backups and RAID for disks, and backups for other fault-prone hardware in stock, a hardware failure shouldn't be a major catasrophe.

Even still, THAT won't help the situation, because people won't willingly abandon their hard-fought-for characters on the older servers, and new players aren't automatically going to jump on the server with the lowest population. Moving characters from one server to another is the only solution to evening out the population, and even THEN, new people will still be joining, and adding to the population in an uneven manner.

It's depressing to belabor this debate to this point, but I'm bored... =) Everyone has great arguments, and all of those arguments are destroyed by the simple fact that Verant has no vested interest in giving us anything beyond the minimum possible expenditure that will maintain their income. If they can squeeze and extra 3000 players into the existing servers, that's money they DON'T have to spend on another server. When things get really bad, they'll do it, but not a moment sooner.

After all, we are talking about the people that nerf character abilities and tell us they've "finally fixed them."
