Ok, as a cleric, your Highest responsibility is keeping your group alive.

You will need to med through some battles, especially after level 15 or so.

In many cases, preventing damage is just as good or better than Healing it up. Buffs keep the party alive, and reduce the number of heals required. Chain Stunning at higher levels can keep a single creature's damage output almost to nothing.

I would strongly advise playing at least 1 full level un powerlevelled in each spell set so that you get used to what spells are needed when, group dynamics, etc. If you are powerleveled straight through, it's gonna be tough to understand what needs to be done in what situations.

The best friends you can have are an enchanter and a Tank who is good at keeping things off of you and the enchanter. If you have a chance to make any sort of regular group, I would strongly advise an enchanter and warrior/pal/sk as teammates.

Welcome Vasos!!

Here are a few tips from my experience:

1. Know your spells. Sites like castersrealm.com are a fantastic resource for getting the scoop on what each spells offers. I made a chart that I add to that lists spell description, mana cost, casting time, and class of spell. Call me anal, but it really is useful.

2. Getting powerleveled is a great time to get your one handed blunt and dodge scores up. Although I don't do it very often, it is nice to be able to hit every once in a while! http://eqcleric.gameglow.com/ubb/smile.gif

3. If you want to be a battle cleric, please let your group know before you join. Ack..no flames guys!! In the past few weeks I have heard more complaining from friends in the new lands about clerics that are too busy hitting and not healing party members.

4. Divine Aura and Divine Barrier are your friends...I suggest you keep them up at all times. I can't tell you how many times I've been saved by casting them when the call to zone has been issued.

5. Don't wait too long to heal your tanks. Not only is this important so that you keep them alive, but strange as this might sound, it can build a bond between the party. When I'm playing the role of healer, my tanks know that I'm going to do everything in my power to keep them as close to full health as possible. More times than not, we (as a party) end up having a lot more fun because the fighters aren't wondering if I'm going to remember to heal them, and I'm not wondering if they are going to protect me if I get attacked.
Serious here...take care of your tanks and you increase your odds of the tanks taking care of you.

6. Buff em all. The added AC and HPs your buffs add really do make a difference. Now, some people don't agree with the following statement, but I also buff pets. Your choice on the pets.
And if some tough-guy tank tells you "I don't need any buffs' don't pay attention.
Something else you might want to consider: let's say you're in DC zone waiting on some friends so you can go into Unrest. Go ahead and toss out a few heals and buffs on others in the zone. This does two things. It educates other players about the abilities of the Cleric class (not joking) and it makes friends.

Most of all have fun!!

Two words Vasos: Mana Efficiency!

As a Cleric you live and your Group lives on your efficient use of your available mana pool. Healing takes lots of mana, stunning doesn't! If you can keep the MOPs stunned they don't deliver damage to your Group and you don't have to heal, if your Group is buffed you have put "mana in the bank" pre- battle and therefore have to use less while engaged.

Healing efficiently is an art form, you have to develop a sense of when to heal and what level heal to use, you've got it right when every heal goes up to all but that last pixel of red on your targets health bar. NO mana wasted.

Being a Cleric is stressful, give your self a break, you WILL lose people. If you did the best you could don't feel too bad about it. When you are the healer in a Group it is a BIG responsibility you can't go AFK when you want, only when your Group is safe. If a warrior fails to get a critical hit, NOBODY notices. If a wizard fails to throw a nuke on time, NOBODY notices. If you goof on healing and somebody dies, EVERYBODY, and I mean EVERBODY, notices! Don't let it burn you out!

A few last very important words: Don't forget to have fun!

Welcome to our ranks and may your mana bar never go empty!

I think one of the best things a cleric (or any class, really, but especially a cleric) can do when in a group is communicate!

If you're going to use a big heal on a tank and they're the nervous type, a quick "Will complete heal at two bubbles, Tankguy" goes a long way toward making them feel more comfortable.

"Resolution coming around" lets people know you haven't forgotten to buff them but that you're trying to preserve a bit of mana for fights. Giving a quick mana bulletin helps tremendously as well.

Communication is key not just during battles, but also when forming up. Are you one of two or more clerics? Decide how/whether you will divide up duties ahead of time, who will be primary healer or who will be responsible for healing which party members -- stuff like that. Establish guidelines other group members can use -- for example, if you and another class share a spell, decide who will assume duty for that spell (e.g. fear, extinguish fatigue, nullify magic, etc.) so the other can clear a spot in their spell lineup.

Communication is key, not to mention one of the most fun parts of the game =).

I guess I should clarify a little here.

Pulling is an artform. One of the most elegant and respected art forms of our time. Seems a bit cheesy but when you have that kind of mindset you tend to try and outdo yourself time after time and you look to see how every pull you make could be made better.

I wil try and relate a most of what I have learned and give a general strategy when pulling.


What is the purpose of the pull? It is to bring a MOB or MOBS back to your group so that they can be killed, without bringing
anything more than you planned for.

What are the dangers associated with Pulling? If you are pulling it usually means you are going solo at it. Your group waits while you explore and so the chances that you will die as opposed to the rest of your group are much greater. Pulling too much. Getting caught in train. Basically the dangers are that you have an increased chance of death over any other member of your party.

Ideal pull? An ideal pull is one where you bring enough mobs back to your party that the fight lasts about one minute to kill all the MOBS and to give enough challenge to the group that they are not sitting around waiting for you. Whether the fight be with a single mob or a group it makes no differnece. The group should be fighting as fast as it can handle to make the best exp. However, there is a fine line between a perfect pull and a train.

How do you go about making that perfect pull? This requires a detailed answer. There is never any one answer though. Just some general guidelines to follow. The rest of it is determined by your personal judgement and abilities.

Be aware of exactly how much your group can handle and how much is too much. Any mistake here and you can cost everyone their lives. Make sure you know this is well as you can.

Make sure you know your prey inside and out. The more you know about what you are fighting the easier things will become. Must learn things like pathing routes when pulled, how large aggro radiuses are, which mobs are casters and which are melee, approx level and toughness of said MOB(s), how fast they run/pursue you, what resistances they have if any, etc.

Make sure you know the lay of the land so to speak. Knowledge of your surroundings is of the greatest importance. This includes things like Roaming patterns to various Mobs, locations of the nearest Zone point/safe spot(for when you make a bad pull you or your group needs to know where to go), various features of the zone geography which might cause problems when running a mob back to your group, various geography features that may be used to your advantage when pulling back to your group, and so on.

Primary objectives: Single out an appropriate number of MOBS to bring back to your group. Then make sure you don't get anything else on the way back again. Don't die in the process.

Secondary objectives: Do as much damage as you can to a MOB or MOBS before returning to your group, set up for later pulls by using strategy on which mobs to pull at which time.


Do whatever you can to accomplish as many objectives as you can as long as you put the primary objectives as highest priority. Don't
even worry about doing any damage to a pulled mob on the way back if it will compromise your pull in any way.

Some things that will work are using snare to pull if you don't have Sow on. If you have Sow don't snare and proceed as follows. This will allow to you to cast/bowkite a little back to your group. But do not do this through high traffic areas. You wil most likely get a few more MOBS in the process and start a train.

Casting a DOT in a pull is ideal if you are high enough level to cast it. This is essentialy free damage to the mob on the way back. After the initial cast it requires no more effort to do damage.

If in an outdoor zone USE HARMONY. As much as possible. Remember it is better to go overboard in casting Harmony then to not cast it enough times. Use it to break otherwise impossible pulls into a managable and profitable series of skirmishes. Be careful though as there are limitations to harmony that you must learn. Harmony reduces aggro ranges only. If you Harmony a mob and pull another mob who runs close enough or directly over the first mob it will still aggro. Learn the pathing of each mob in a pull and be guided accordingly. Some spawns are just not possible to seperate becuase of pathing and proximity. Learn which ones these are and then make sure you save them for last or prepare for them seperately from all other pulls.

And if you are the puller, INSIST that you choose where the group fights at. I.e the spot you want to pull to. If you are pulling you need to be most familair with the area and also you need a spot where you will feel most comfortable. Also make sure that whatever spot you pick is as safe as possible.

Have fun and good luck to you.
