My guild has had some discussion about points in the past and decided not to use it.

For us, (and this may not be an issue for other guilds) we want to see great items in the hands of people who will *use* them to benefit the guild to the maximum.

In a point system, there is nothing you can do from stopping someone with alot of points from taking the most mega-mega item for thier lvl 2 twink, if they wish. From what I saw, most of my guild seemed to find that idea terrible.

I had a conversation once with a cleric in Afterlife (who also reads these boards, maybe he will feel free to comment? /hint /hint) and he said that the biggest problem is hoarding points.

Example: Let's say your guild is on Dragon faction and you are killing stuff in the Halls of Testing. You are a chain wearing class and you really really want your Skyshrine BP. To win the next chain BP that drops *outright* with no random, you must have more points than anyone else.

This means that for some random time (could be a long time, depending on random drops) you must not spend *any* points on anything that drops, even if no one else wants it and it's a dream item for you. Why? You have to maintain a point advantage over someone else in case the coveted Chain BP drops so you can win it outright.

For myself, a point system would almost be a tremendous advantage since I go to alot of events and the list of items I want is very tiny.

I guess it depends on your point of view.

There are flaws in all the systems out there, except maybe Dikmer's (though I find it hard to believe you guys have never had a fight over loot).

Arbitrary loot has it's flaws. It's run by other people and as such, everything they award will be subject to inspection, speculation and rejection. They either penalize their friends for just being their friends ("I can't award you item X because people will think it's favoritism") or they take people quietly saying "Gee, he gave his friends all that good stuff and we got nothing" which is seldom true. If people really were being shorted on loot they'd have left a long time ago.

Point system isn't arbitrary, it's gambling with the odds stacked in favor of those with the most time put in. It's far more reckless, IMO, because you take any real control over loot out of your hands and put it in the hands of a crapshoot.

Example... let's say Dikmer has 98 points and Joenoob has 2 raid points and an plate BP drops in ToV. Dikmer gets 0-98 and Joenoob gets 99 and 100. Raid leader randoms and rolls a 99. Joenoob on his second raid has just gotten an item that Dikmer has put in hundreds of hours trying to get. Now, you explain to Dikmer why he didn't get the BP. Not too eager to talk to him, are you? Not to mention that Joenoob who's been in the guild for 2 weeks is now in a prime position to leave and go somewhere else, having gotten what he wanted.

You can't stop people from leaving the guild. Everyone in this game wears a facade. Some people wear them exceptionally well, others don't wear them at all. While few people will guild an obvious loot whore how do you tell the well hidden ones from the good players? You can't unless you guild only people you know RL.

Arbitrary loot is far better. It doesn't ignore factors like non-raid guild work (spell and gem management, raid organization, recruitment work, etc), time put in, overall use (can you really tell me a cleric needs a ToV BP over a warrior?), and need (let's say Joenoob had a Trak BP and Dikmer didn't).

Are there going to be arguments? Of course. Arbitrary loot is easiest at the ends of the loot cycle and is very hard in the middle. Let me give you an imaginary example...

Guild is farming ToV. They kill dozens of mobs for a handful of drops. Plate has been unbelieveably rare. A plate BP drops. First one. Goes to a warrior, no questions asked. Next few go to well deserving warriors who have put in tons of time without any BP upgrades (warrior BPs are really rare for some reason). The awarding of these BPs also enriches the guild as these warriors can now tank better. Then one day another plate BP drops. All the warriors have BPs now. Nobody else has a real need for a BP. Here's where the award system has trouble... the BP must be awarded on merit alone. There are 2 obvious choices here: a pally and a SK. Both have done great work for the guild.

BP is awarded to the SK, a friend of the loot caller. Pally and his friends get upset and leave, splitting the guild.

This is a bit extreme but I have seen things like this before. As I said, there's no way to know who will snap over a loot call and leave. But it does well as long as you recognize that loot awarded belongs to the recliever, not the guild. It's also important people look at the big picture and realize that giving equipment to those who have a real need or use can make you, as a player, better.

Why do all the systems break down? EQ is about levels and loot. That's it. Once you hit 60 you're out of levels and all that is left is loot. When you go for a long time without any new toys it hurts. The game is diminished to some extent. I know many casters who have complained about the lack of caster loot, for instance.

The key to keeping your system working is to set goals and remind people they WILL eventually get what it is they seek. It's always just a matter of time...

DKP stands for Dragon Kill Points.

Long ago on Mith Marr, a group of 3-4 guilds would regularly kill Vox/Nagafen. We had huge fights over loot and tried many different ways to distribute loot.

Our biggest problems where:
-First raid ever attendie winning loot.
-Veterans with 12+ dragons under their belt and no loot to show for it.
-Comunication problems, people we didn't like showing up, long setup times, etc
-Lucky People wining 2-3 items before long time Veterans got 1.
-Loot voting system giving to favored people, bribing for votes.
-Casters only showing up at Vox
-Melee only showing up at Nagafen
-Clerics all in hate
-Monks all in fear

Some of the principle leaders of these raids got togeather and planned to create a new guild by merging the raiders from the 3-4 guilds to facilitate the communicaton problem. We adopted a points based system to assure people who attended enough raids would eventually get something, and to give classes who's loot doesn't drop a reward for attending the raid.

The guild was Afterlife, the points system we called DKP or dragon kill points. Over the last few years we have had to refine the system to solve a few problems. The biggest change we added was IDKP, or Incentive DKP, to reward people for attending our failed attempts. Such as our 2 nights of hell figuring out how to kill Vyemm or the 4th warder. We also have a mains before twinks rule. A main with -20 dkp can win an item over a twink with 90 dkp.

Basically in the DKP system you:
Earn points for attending raids.
Spend points buying items.
When an item drops, the person in charge of lottos asks who wants the items, then looks at the DKP list. The item goes to the person with the highest dkp unless someone else in the lotto is within 5 points. So if I go in for a 20 point item and I have 100 points, and someone else has 95 points, we lotto it. 1-100 goes to me, 101-195 goes to him. We roll /random 1 195.

DKP has its ups and downs, but overall I'm very happy with it, and would not want any other system. That said its not for everyone.

Pros:
-Very impartial, no favoritism or bias.
-Low class bias, even bards can get top notch equipment. However, in some cases we do add a bias, Guantlets of Dragon Slaying for instance casters have a -20 dkp penalty. First bracer gives you a 20 dkp bonus, etc.

Cons:
-Lots of work recording who, what, when
-Casual players tend to feel left out
-Lesser items or items with too high point costs can rot.

"A main with -20 dkp can win an item over a twink with 90 dkp."

Mains before twinks, no matter what the points are.

"-Low class bias, even bards can get top notch equipment. However, in some cases we do add a bias, Guantlets of Dragon Slaying for instance casters have a -20 dkp penalty. First bracer gives you a 20 dkp bonus, etc."

The point cost will be the same, however a caster would need 20 points more than a melee to get Gautlets. This way a caster that goes on every raid will have a good chance at winning Gauntlets before a melee that attends less than half the raids.

"Do you have some kind of loot table set up ahead of time with all this info in there?"

The guild leader has a list of set values. We used to post it, but so many guilds directly copied it (often outdated version of it) then complained the points weren't right, we no longer publically post it.

"As far as the items with a "too high point cost" rotting, ouch! Would it work to give them to the highest point bidder?"

You could probably do this, but then people might purposely pretend they didn't want the item so they could bid fewer points than it was worth. Most the stuff that rots are class specific items after all of that class have. Point costs should reflect the quality of the item, how big of an upgrade it is, and the demand for it. Gauntlets of Dragon Slaying are some of the best gloves in the game, usuable by all, and therefore cost 20 dkp. Shield of the Dragon Kinda is marginal upgrade over Orb of the infinate void, and Yatikima's Shield is better, so it is only 4 dkp.

One question to also ask when looting at a loot system is Time zones of your players. This was a big problem with a point system that I saw in action and basically made it Impossible for those on other time zones to get any benifit from anything they may put in. Most weekday raids are held in the evening Eastern time. If a Raid starts at 7 or 8 PM Eastern this equates to 1 or 2 AM in europe. Now tack on 6 Hrs raid time and You get the picture. If you are able to hold raids on their times then this will not be an issue but the leadership that was in place did not want to be "burdened" with keeping track of the extra points. Anyways point systems can be effective but there are extenuating circumstances that have to be factored in also when deciding a loot policy.

Make one of the more active europeans an officer then. Or if they just don't raid at ALL, then you aren't exactly a raiding guild and this isn't an issue.

As for the stuff rotting. Yeah that happens with dkp, but you can usually adjust the point system easily if you have a) studied economics b) got brains. It really is amazingly complex, but it gets bit too complex if you allow bidding. Prices aren't really decided by the quality of the product, but just like in RL: supply and demand. If item X is a small improvement, but for every damn class there is (and there's no obvious better alternative) it's price will be pretty high compared to cost, whereas a better item with lots of 'competition' will have a lower price. As an example here mithril boots are more expensive dkp wise than boots of the storm for us. Why? Because the chain wearers are interested as well.

The guild I am in also use's a point system for very much the same reason that many have posted above, old world randoms where not very fair. One thing we do differently than many of the other point systems is that we do no set values for items. We allow all mains who can use an item and are present for the kill to auction for an item witht he points they have accomulated. This will set the base value and each auction there after for the same item will start at half the cost of what the item went for previously. This allows the players to determine the value of items. We have found it to work pretty well. The only issue's we have are setting the values of mobs, which is no real big deal.
