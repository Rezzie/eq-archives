I have never been so thoroughly addicted to anything in my life!

Magic Update
- Aurum @ 7:21 PM PDT
It was a quiet day on the Developer's Corner. The big update from Mr. Poe by way of Alan:

    Lawrence sent me this update to pass on to all of you.

    ----------------------------------------
    quote:

    Hey guys, as I get ready to head out for the Fan Faire I wanted to sit down and write up this post I've been putting off all week. A couple quick notes.

    The "problem" with rain spell pulses always being resisted if they will be the killing blow isn't actually a problem....It's an intentional design feature. The problem was much like the old DOT corpse poof buf. If the Rain killed the NPC and the server was unsure where the damage came from the corpse would poof, so these spells were coded in this manner to prevent that from happening. I'm working with the coders to come up with a better solution. We're all pretty busy right now so unfortunately I can't give you a time frame on when a better solution will be implemented.

    Kunark Evac spells: these will now work anywhere in the world. You won't have to be in a Kunark dungeon to use them. This change will apply to both Wizards and Druids evac spells.

    Druid Spell Egress: this spell will now work indoors. It didn't make sense for the group version to work indoors (a mistake on our part) and not have the self version work indoors.

    Shroud of Death: I added Illusion Skeleton to shroud of death on test server..... I removed it the next day I just didn't get to update spells on test. I forgot that in skeleton form trolls and ogres would lose slam, sorry for the scare. I'll see if I can think up some other way to give SKs skeleton form.

    Stacking issues: I continue to address these as time permits. We're working on some changes that will allow me to fix them much easier. More stacking issues should be resolved in the coming months. High level players should be pleased to know that I'm 99.99999% certain that Call of Earth and Speed of the Shissar will no longer conflict after the next patch.

    New Translocates: as many of you know there are new translocate spells up on test. I'm not going to comment on these other than to say you can expect that cool cat Al'Kabor to be out and about researching again.

    Now on to the "Burning issue". I know the Wizard community has been *patiently* awaiting the enhancement we have been working on The coding for the new spell effect has been finished and it is going through extensive internal testing. It has also been on Test Server for a week, but I doubt you would have seen it in a parser – cause I'm sneaky like that. Before I go into details on the new spells there's a few things I'd like to say. Honestly I feel that wizards are balanced fine from a power standpoint, to that end you will not be receiving an upgrade to your damage output. Where I do feel wizards need help is group utility.

    As I'm sure you can understand coming up with a highly desirable utility type spell for an EverQuest wizard is not an easy task. I've spent countless hours pondering a solution, I've read countless message board forums and emails looking at suggestions from you guys. Finally a solution was found! I'm not naive enough to believe that everyone will be happy about the new enhancement but this is an idea I've seen tossed around on a number of forums and I think not only does it fit in well with wizards being the masters of arcane transportation, I think it will make them very desirable in the 50 to 60 levels where they seem to be struggling for usefulness.

    The new spells will give wizards the ability to prevent NPCs from gating. There will be 3 versions of the spells starting at level 51. They will each have a different resistance type. One of the problems with duration based effects is stacking and negating the need for multiple characters of the same class. I am designing these spells with that in mind. If you want to 100% ensure an NPC does not gate you will need more than one wizard. I understand this may upset some people but the need for multiple wizards to "Anchor" an NPC won't really happen until you fight the more epic mobs with higher hit point numbers. For single group encounters one wizard with these spells should, in most situations, be enough to get the job done. My plan is to get these spells implemented and ready to go before the next patch.

    Lastly I would like to send a huge thank you to all the players who have reported stacking exploits to me through Email, IRC, and ICQ over the past couple of months. The feedback is greatly appreciated.

    Take care all!
    /Lakers secret handshake

    - Lawrence Poe
    ----------------------------------------
    :)

    Alan

EverQuest Character Status v1.0
- Aurum @ 7:09 PM PDT
From Golggen:

EQGuardians.com is pleased to announce the 1.0.0 Full version release of EverQuest Character Status. EverQuest Character Status (EQCS) is a first of its kind in EverQuest development. EQCS is an EverQuest utility which will allow players to list their online presence on any listing server (for now EQGuardians.com has the only authorized listing server at http://www.eqguardians.com/charstat/main.pl). Players can use this to list the following items on the listing server
  • Online/Offline Status
  • Current Zone
  • Level, Class, Race
  • Location
  • Closest Landmark**
  • User Biography*
  • Favorite Zone*
  • Other Characters*
  • Pet Name
  • ICQ, AIM, MSN, Yahoo! and/or Email address*
    * - You enter this information in the program
    ** - Only Gfay landmarks for now... all other zones comming in later versions.

All this information is pulled from the text in your log file, except choices marked with a single asterisk. Users can pick and choose which items they wish to display and which items they do not wish to display, so you still may post your status and remain anonymous if you wish.

Some may think this will cause lag since it is pasring the log file intermitantly, however, the way I designed it, it makes no lag at all (I even tested it on a machine running a Pentium II 300Mhz with 96Mb ram on a 56K and it ran fine while in EQ). When parsing, it will parse ONLY new lines in your log file, so it doesnt create any lag.

Okay, so you have to have the log option on for this to pull the information, what if you dont want to have a log file building up? There is an option included to erase already parsed information.

Also, be sure to keep an eye out for the release of the listing server. In later versions I will allow other websites to run a listing server of their own. Perfect for guilds!


Download available at http://www.eqguardians.com/download

Happy Questing!

    Roleplaying or Just Playing?
    [Quote: As an alternative to removing Common or making Dark Speech act as "Dark Common," why not simply make Common a language that players have to learn? It makes sense that someone raised in Kaladim might have heard some Common, but it's unlikely that they actually learned the language until they had a reason to go out into the world.

    This makes sense in the context of a trade tongue. Even though you might deal with dwarven merchants on a regular basis, if you're dwarven the odds are slim that you bothered to learn a new language when your native one works just fine.

    Alternatively, you could default common to start at only 1-2 points, perhaps representing the few bits of the language picked up by observing the local dwarven merchant purchasing leather gloves from the human ranger . . .

    --Ixsamael ]

    That's another possibility. We haven't really talked about how hard we want to make it to communicate on a roleplaying server. Making it hard to speak with folks outside your race serves to insulate the racial community, making them tighter knit and making it less likely (because it's harder) that someone will break down the racial stereotypes.

    But it also makes the game harder to play, especially if taken too far. It could be argued that removing /tell is the right thing to do on a roleplaying server, simply because it isn't something that your character should be able to do. But it is also an extremely useful tool for the player.

    And that gave me an idea… what about only allowing /tell to work for a character over X level? We might be willing to accept that all Norrathians have some ability to communicate mentally as they grow more powerful (guards can tell your religion, after all…). And this would insulate the lower level communities, making it more likely that young trolls will hang out with other young trolls, and young halflings will hang out with other young halflings, if used in combination with the removal of a common language.

    But is that too restrictive to fun gameplay? I don't know. We'll hash this out internally some time in the future. But we're not really looking at implementing a roleplaying server right away. We want to keep focused on the new PvP thing for a while, until we're satisfied with it. So keep hold of your ideas. I'll be asking for them later. :)

    [Quote: Well,for me (and I know I am in the minority here) I don't enjoy roleplaying in any game where the game mechanics themselves don't provide incentive for roleplaying(such as the "advantages" and "Disadvantages" of such "generic" systems as HERO and GURPS).I cannot help this.I have been a "systems freak" since I discovered "RuneQuest" and other great p&p RPG's and stopped playing simpler games like "(A)D&D".I would have no problem with roleplaying in EQ if I could choose a "disadvantage" like "Hates gnomes"(any gnomes conning me would get the "Scowls at you..." message ...that would be cool) or "weakness for beautiful women"(you can imagine a troll with this disad' having a small chance of becoming "charmed" or "mezzed" when within agro radius of a non-hostile high charisma elf maiden...that would be hilarious!).]

    I'm the other way around. Systems like Gurps and Hero System that encourage players to make up really bizarre character traits just to get points tend to bother me (love those systems, just not keen on that one part). And I think that RuneQuest rocks because it is a simpler system than D&D (though 3rd edition goes a long way toward removing the needless complications of that system). The RuneQuest world is exceedingly complex and detailed, but the game system itself is one of the simplest I've ever seen.

    The reason that I'm not a big fan of the idea of rewarding personality quirks with points is that, well, you should be adding those quirks to your character anyway. If your character hates Gnomes, then he hates Gnomes. Getting points for it doesn't make that quirk more valid. It's more the opposite, really. You tend to see far more completely ridiculous quirks in a system that rewards the player with points for them.

    My old gaming group (/em Absor lapses into his old EQX editorial style) is restarting our 'planar' campaign. The idea was to allow us to make the wildest characters possible. We would make up the world they came from, with as much detail as we wanted. We'd also be building characters with the express intent of making them as powerful as we can, because the game campaign is going to be really hard. Then the characters would be yanked to Sigil (the D&D 'center of the universe' setting).

    Well, in mild mockery of this idea, I created a superhero character. Once able to fly, call the lightning and rip cars in half, he had been reduced to mortal status by the trickery of an old flame. He was glad to escape his old world for the new one, where he studies magic.

    But I gave him a few quirks, as much to make fun of the whole idea as to get him points. His spells don't affect things that are the color yellow. This makes a lot of things resistant to his spells, and someone could easily kill him with a #2 pencil if they dressed themselves in a banana suit…

    But I digress. I've seen Hero system characters with some of the stupidest flaws attached to them, just because they needed a few more points.

    You can have quirks in EverQuest. And they are all the more meaningful because everyone knows that they are part of your character's personality, not some desire to have a few more points added to the power of your energy blast.

    In fact my character on Sullon, at least until he's wiped, is learning to really, really hate necromancers…

Brad on Exploiting Bugs
- Aurum @ 7:34 PM PDT
Brad spoke on exploiting bugs in response to more feedback and letters received about the banning of the guild Conquest. Here's the update from the Dev Corner:

    Hello all,

    Recently I've not only received emails specifically regarding the Conquest guild issue, but also about exploiting in general.

    First, some people are confused as to what an exploit is and how they're to know what an exploit is. Some are also afraid they might accidentally exploit and then be disciplined.

    An exploit, quite simply, is using a bug in the game to your advantage. Most of them are obvious: attacking NPCs that are stuck, or the ability to duplicate items or money. But we do realize it's not always easy to tell.

    To this end it has been and will continue to be our policy to warn people when they are caught exploiting. It basically works like this:

    1. You are caught exploiting, you receive a warning and the exploit is explained to you.
    2. You are caught exploiting again (this includes either literally being caught or caught and we have evidence you were aware it was an exploit -- in other words, premeditated exploiting) and you are suspended for a period of time.
    3. You are caught exploiting yet again -- you are banned.

    A second issue that's arisen is the question: why do we care if people are exploiting? Are we just upset they're not playing the game the way we wanted them to?

    The answer is honestly "no, that has nothing to do with it." The following is an email and my reply to it which I think more clearly explains why we cannot allow bug exploitation in EverQuest:

    -----Original Message-----
    From: Robert Mayer
    Sent: Tuesday, June 12, 2001 7:30 AM
    To: bmcquaid@verant.com
    Subject: Question

    You guys seem to be holding players responsible for doing things the game allows but you don't intend to happen. You are labeling as "exploits" anything that is possible, but doesn't fit into the game as you designed it.

    Then I guess I failed to communicate the issues very effectively, because that is absolutely NOT what we're doing. We are labeling exploits as we always have, that being using bugs to your advantage in-game. Conquest used spell stacking bugs to their advantage and they used a safe zone (an area where a pathing bug made it such that an NPC wouldn't pursue those it was mad at) to their advantage.


    Now correct me if I'm wrong, but it seems that in effect you're saying "look, we aren't perfect, we can't code a game that is 100% free of loopholes because nobody can, but you should as players be able to infer what we intend, and if you do things that are clearly going against our intent, that's an exploit and you'll be disciplined."

    Yes and no. We're saying that taking advantage of bugs is NOT ok. And we're saying that 1. knowing you're exploiting a bug and 2. where and to what end your exploiting, are both factors in determining how we react to a player or players who are exploiting.


    Whew. I've never heard a game company essentially say that the players are responsible for the developer's programming, design, and implementation errors. No offense, because I realize it's a bitch to run a game like EQ, but it seems absurd to me to expect players to do anything but try their utmost to use every single tool you give them, deliberately or accidentally, to succeed. After all, the only point of EQ, beyond chatting, is to level up, and the only rewards beyond the social come from leveling up and killing things. You put umpteen thousand people into a world and tell them "go forth and kill MOBs," and by gum they're gonna do it. You can't reasonably then expect them to adhere to something like "oh, and only kill MOBs the way we intend for you to kill them!"

    No, they're responsible for not taking advantage of bugs in-between the time they're discovered and when we have a chance to fix them. And we've always had this policy. UO certainly has this policy, and I think AC does to an extent now. And I'd submit the majority of persistent, massively multiplayer community based games in the future will have these policies. Why? Well, because what you do in a community based game like this affects other players. If you take advantage of a dupe bug, you can hurt the economy, and ruin the play experience of hundreds if not thousands of other players. If you take advantage of a pathing bug and kill a monster to obtain loot that you really shouldn't be able to, then the guy who did work harder to obtain that same loot without exploiting is not going to feel very entertained. Rather, he's going to feel frustrated and perhaps even regret that he did it the 'right way'. It's the same as in real life -- society is generally NOT ok with taking short cuts (especially at the expense of others). Insider Trading is not ok. Cutting through a parking lot to avoid traffic is not ok. Embezzling and stealing and cheating to advance oneself at the expense of others is NOT ok. And so why the heck should it be ok in the communities and societies that are being developed in online computer games? Because it's a game? Does my sense of accomplishment and are my feelings about right and wrong somehow less valid inside a virtual reality than they are in the real world?

    No man is an island, and that includes a person in Norrath.


    From reading all of the public info on the Conquest incident, it seems to me that these guys simply took advantage of holes your programmers left in the game. If an NPC can't summon or move for X seconds or due to Y geometry, um, tough titty to you, Kitty--fix it. If you leave it in, you can't ethically blame someone for taking advantage of it. It's your responsibility, and you'rs (Verant's) alone, to make the game unexploitable.

    Agreed to a point, and expanding on that, it's Verant's responsibility to make sure we are offering an entertaining and fair play environment for our customers. And so we fix bugs, and we also crack down on players who would use bugs to cheapen the playing experience of others.

    And I disagree it's unethical to blame a player for taking advantage of a bug. If a person leaves the door open at their place of business, thereby creating a hole in the company's security, yes, it's the person's fault for doing this. But that doesn't mean it's ok for someone to come along and enter the building, taking advantage of the open door. He gets blamed too.


    Now, if players change the code, hack it, or abuse other players, disciplinary action seems warranted. But to even bother suspending players whose only real "crime" seems to be to take advantage of coding problems on your end--well, you're asking way too much of your customers.

    But taking advantage of coding or data problems can in some cases ABUSE OTHER PLAYERS, which is the point I apparently failed miserably to communicate, because it keeps coming up, not just in your email but in others.


    Once you create the world, it isn't yours any more, just like when a novelist writes a book. If someone wants to interpret The Stand as an allegory about French cooking, there's nothing Stephen King can do about it. Yeah, an online game isn't as passive as a book, but it's the same concept. You guys seem to be holding on way too tight. Who cares if they "exploit" the flaws in the programming? Turn off the zone, fix the flaws, and go on. Every single exploit you guys identify should be seen as an opportunity to improve the game or refine it--that's good, and it's your right. But to discipline players for finding your mistakes… man, that's pretty low.

    Again, reading a book is not a shared, community based experience. Misinterpreting a book, and for a better analogy, cheating in a single player game does NOT hurt the play experience for others. But, and this is the key point here, cheating in a shared community based online experience DOES.

    Shark Meat
    Breno wrote:

    [Quote: yea just want to know if VI is going to allow the selling of shark mea back into the game i have a bard that all he does is skill skill skill master baker potter tayler and the other day i found a recipie for smoked shark but when i arrived in hurugan in velious and went to morgans meat where i was told sold the suff to my suprise it had been taken out do to druids and ppl exploiting some ques that they ended up nerfing anyway so why take the shark meat away when he quest was nerfed. some ppl need the stuff for making good food for parties

    The short answer is, no.

    Here's the series of events that lead up to that decision.

    Some time in the far distant past, someone created a quest that required shark meat. At that time the only source of shark meat on Norrath was living sharks with sharp teeth. The quest rewards were balanced based on the facts of the day.

    Recently we added a bunch of new recipes for bakers. Shark meat was used in one (or some) of those recipes. So shark meat was added on a vendor or two to facilitate the use of that recipe.

    This made the original quest extremely easy for people that could transport themselves. People used this fact to gain rewards beyond the intended difficulty of the quest.

    Sure, we should have caught it. But with the huge number of quests and the large increase in baking recipes and items, we missed it. To fix this we had, really, two choices. Remove the quest or remove the meat from vendors.

    We chose to remove the meat. Bakers don't suffer in their ability to make skill, there are other recipes that will get them through those skill ranges. All this does is make shark dishes a rarity, and that's actually something that I think is a good thing. If someone gives you food with shark meat in it, you know that they've gone to some trouble to make that food.
