Brad On The Warrens
- Aurum @ 7:22 PM PST
Brad McQuaid talked about the new upcoming zone "The Warrens" twice today in a thread located on the Gameplay Board:

    I said not this patch, but the next.

    We continue to both battle test it, and we're also experiementing with some different rule-sets that we might make applicable to the zone and future zones.

    -Brad
Here's the follow-up post:
    Grimstaff: Probably not to the degree that would make you happy, no. While we continue to improve internal testing, I don't foresee us ever getting to the point where we can internally test new content added to an already huge post-release game such that it's always launched problem-free.

    I realize some players have a problem acknoledging, understanding, and accepting this, but I'm pretty convinced it's going to be true for MMOGs for quite some time. It's simply not realistic to hire hundreds if not thousands of internal testers, nor to beta test new content for months.

    The more complicated these games become, the easier it is to implement something that has ramifications we didn't think of, or bugs or other issues that were undetectable before hundreds of thousands of people interact with the new content.

    And, as mentioned in another recent post, if adding new content, features, and ideas to a hugely complicated, dynamic, evolving, post-release game means that at times players are both paying customers and 'beta testers', then so be it.

    The alternative (stagnation) is something neither we as developers nor the majority of our customers find acceptable.

    -Brad

  • More Discussion on Breastplate Changes

  • An Open Letter Tto Verant
    EuroEQ wrote:

    [Quote from original post: I think you all have created a great game. I'm sure you have also been well rewarded for your ingenuity and hard work.     [Quote from original post: A little context.]

    I am willing to accept your words at face value. Nobody here ever needs to give me credentials.

I know the guild with whom I play is supporting at least 1/2 dozen "canceled" accounts since they provide good utility in terms of "porting", "resurrections," "mez-on-demand," etc.]

    Again, such things are against the UELA (I think that's the acronym).

I do not disparage them but this subset of your customer-base is largely unaffected by poor customer service. They will argue and complain, but do little else. However, when a suitable alternative to your product is available, they will likely shift their addiction in that direction. What about Ashron's call? Quite frankly, that game, from what I hear, doesn't hold a candle to what EQ was (pre expansions) It just wasn't good enough to peel away your customer base.]

    Fair enough. I'll accept that there are customers (and not just customers of Verant, but just customers in general) that will be satisfied with very little customer service. I will also concede that there are a group of players that have already reached the end of their time with our game and will move on when a suitable alternative comes along. But I would argue that their reactions have little to do with customer service, and everything to do with the kind of customers they are (neither good nor bad, just their nature). Some people will move on to the next best thing regardless of what we do, and others have played themselves out on EverQuest. We do our best to retain these people, obviously.

    [Quote from original post: I think the major issues with the game came when you expanded the characters beyond lvl 50 in an enviornment that was never designed to support it. You have major balance issues between melee and casters because of the wealth of melee items and the relative scarcity of both spells and caster items.]

    I disagree. I think that the environment could support extended levels. Though I readily admit that the implementation of those extended levels wasn't perfect. That does not reflect the ability of the game to support such expansion. It only exposes the fact that we need to work more on making it work. We've already made several strides to reach that goal, and I think we'll have it all fixed up soon enough.

    [Quote from original post: I am starting to delv into the minutia and that is not my intent so let me raise my points up a level.
    I think you are doing a terrible job of communicating with your customers. I am a customer and that is my perception. Many here seem to have the same perception. Perceptions of one's customers is reality...trust me it is. Your customers feel you are adversarial with them. They believe that you think they will destroy "your" game with their unbalanced ideas. Perhaps you are right...but they (we) are the customers so how do you communicate your concerns while answering ours.]

    I'll have to take your word on that. I don't see that myself, but then again I work here. But I talk to customers a lot, and I get a different feeling about it.

    [Quote from original post: I'd like to offer a couple suggestions and then provide a small case-study to illustrate why the implementation of such suggestions (or ones similar) are needed.
    I think Alan's idea for "Talk" threads was a good one. That said, I do not believe Verant Management provided him the support or infrastructure to make that good idea successful. This confuses me. If Verant's data is accurate then you have approximately 300,000 active accounts which equates to about $3 Million dollars in revenue monthly plus revenue from the sale of retail boxes at $16 - $50 each. Surely, even after expenses and R&D, there are enough funds to provide a robust and thoughtful customer support engine.]

    I'm not in a position to argue company revenue with anyone.

    [Quote from original post: I suggest that one member of your current staff be assigned as advocate to each class. Such a person should have as their mission in life to understand the wants and needs of that segment of customer.]

    So you are suggesting that we add another duty to each member of the dev team that they read all the class sites and become experts in the play of a certain class (which, it seems, would also require that they play that class up to 60th level on a live server)? They already do that. Every one of us plays the game. Members of the dev team play as many hours as anyone. And to insist that they spend their work time becoming activists for a certain class is rather silly. I think they should be developing content myself.

    I would argue that there aren't so many 'issues' as you seem to think. To keep up the kind of interaction you seem to want would be a full-time job.

    [Quote from original post: You might say that you already have such advocates as part of your tuning team. Great! Tell us who they are. Have them engage us. You don't know how lucky you are to have a vibrant customer base that WANTS to tell you what they think. Most companies have to pay 3rd parties to find quasi-interested pseudo-customers who then provide their input...for a fee.]

    Heh, yes, I do say that we already have designers playing the game. But to call them advocates would be wrong. You see, and advocate is narrowly focused. His (or her) only desire or job is to push through narrow objectives. We can't do that. We have to look at the entire game. Narrowing your focus to just one class is counterproductive.

    [Quote from original post: These 14 folks would, of course, also have to maintain the objectivity one would expect of a Verant employee as opposed to a purist player. The "good-of-the-game" would temper the natural desire to advance the position of their advocacy group beyond what is reasonable.]

    Ah, sheesh, maybe I should read one paragraph ahead before responding…

    Here's where you'll have to trust me. An advocate that is objective and looks for the good of the game, would be unable to please the players that truly feel that they need an advocate. If a player points out a problem and is logical about the problem, he doesn't need an advocate. That problem is a problem, regardless of the class of the player.

    I've spent a lot of time lately getting to know the desires of the players that identify themselves with a particular class. I've written up those desires, purposefully excluding the ones that did not take into account the good of the game. And yet, as is obvious by your post, I am not considered an 'advocate' for any of them. Why is that? I have done as you have described. I have listened to the players, responded to them (over a longer period of time than I would have if I was only dealing with one class), and listed their concerns. I have been an 'advocate' as you described.

    And yet, you clearly do not see me as such.

    *shrug*

    [Quote from original post: The ideas put forth by the 14 would be managed similar to how Alan has started the "talk" threads, but much more actively so. Part of this open communication would include time lines and thoughts in process. I know companies think of their project plans as some kind of top secret document. "We can't share that with our customers; what if we miss our projected timelines?]

    Perhaps some do. We communicate them as we get them, for the most part. Sure, we keep some things secret because we truly believe that discovery is part of the game. The only thing that your advocate group could do better than one person could do (and I know, I'm that one person), is react more quickly to their… constituents. But the dev team is busy. Their work has to be done. If we added another duty to them, that of advocacy, then you might get faster responses, but they would be responses like, "well, I spent my morning responding to your e-mails and discussing your concerns with you on the message board, so I didn't have time to code in the changes you asked for…"

    I don't see that as a better way to do things.

    [Quote from original post: I would just love to see the look on one of my customer's faces if I told them I would not share my team's project schedule. Communicate with us; tell us what's up. If you miss a date...so what.]

    Wow. You either have never missed a date, or you just don't pay attention to the response from customers when you do. I don't mean to be harsh here, but you're out of touch. Nothing will get people more upset than missing a deadline.

    But that's not the reason that we don't spend our time posting up each and every deadline.

    [Quote from original post: It's a game to us, not a mission critical enterprise application.]

    Er… ok. But if it's just a game to you, then why do you need all this information. Why in the world would you need an advocate of all things, if it's just a game…

    This is where the problem comes in. For us, this is a business. We have to take into account things like workload, priorities and such, for the whole project at hand. An individual player might only have interest in changes that affect him. So that player's desires will almost never match up with our timeline. It can't, no matter what we do.

    [Quote from original post: Tell us why its going to be delayed and give us a new date. Once we look at it, we'll let you know what's next. Nothing hidden here. I imagine that the problem is that we're not moving real fast on the issue that concerns the individual. We can't help that, we have to prioritize over the whole organization, not just one class.

    [Quote from original post: So, to recap.
    1) Verant employed, class specific, advocates.]

    We're not hiring fourteen new people to do this. Our dev team is too busy (as they should be). It's my job to try to be those fourteen people. And I can do it, I just can't do it as fast as fourteen people can.

But, well, I know that you don't trust all of your fellow players to tell the absolute truth, or to look at the situation from a neutral place, for the good of the game. Sure, most do. But we still have to look at each idea and make certain that it is a problem and that the suggested solution works for the game as a whole.

    So once we have taken a look at the list that I have compiled and decided which ones we are going to act on and which we are not, then I will come back to you with a revised list. So what would we have to do next to bring that kind of wonder to the customers?

And when they were done there would be no practical way to keep up that level of interaction, and no real need. Yes, everyone wants to be heard. And we listen as best we can. But we can't have someone go to every customer's house and get their opinions first hand, either…

    It's all about reasonable expectations. We meet those whenever we can. But we can't meet the unreasonable ones, even in the short term because we couldn't keep meeting them, and that would be a disappointment to our customers too.

Handing In Quest Items
- Aurum @ 6:30 PM PST
This is news to note for those attempting to hand in a quest item:

    I wanted to talk to all of you about the way turning in quest items works. We are thinking about making a change here, and I'd like to talk about it.

    Let me set up a fictional example. Let's say that there is a quest NPC that gives out the final item for the quest in exchange for three other items. Currently, if you give the NPC two of the correct items and a third item that you think is the correct item but isn't, the NPC will take all of the items and give you nothing. This seems like a rude thing for the NPC to do.

    Also, if someone has come along and given the NPC one of the three items before you arrive, and you give him the three correct items all at once he'll give you the quest result, but the person that gave the one item previously will get nothing (the NPC is, basically, letting you complete the transaction that he started).

    Both of these situations cause someone to lose out. That's not something that we like.

    We've been thinking about making a change to the way this whole turning in quest items thing works. The most reasonable and eloquent solution to this problem would be to simply have the NPC give the items back if they are not enough to complete the quest. This would mean that if you give him one item, he would give it back to you and tell you that he needs more than just that item to give you what you want. If you give him two correct items and an incorrect item, he would give the quest items back to you and tell you that you made a mistake (he might or might not give you back the mistaken item, depending on the technical ability to get him to do that).

    This would fix a bunch of problems with questing. But, as you all have probably guessed, it would eliminate the ability to 'multi-quest'. We know that the whole concept of multi-questing is a touchy one.

    So I have a question for you. Would eliminating the problems with losing quest items to NPCs be worth the loss of the ability to multi-quest?

    Alan

Another Breastplate Change
- Aurum @ 6:38 PM PST
Alan made this announcement on the Gameplay Board:

    This is regarding the clerical Plane of Growth breastplates, the ones with the ability to cast Celestial Elixer (the Chestplate of Stability, Akkirus' Chestplate of the Risen, the Breastplate of Forbidden Rites and the Templar's Chestplate). For many of the same reasons that we made recent changes to the Donal's Breastplate, we are going to make some changes to these items with the next patch (which will be next week).

    Existing instances of these Plane of Growth breastplates will have the casting time for Celestial Elixer increased to one minute.

    No new instances of the Plane of Growth breastplates will occur (they will stop dropping).

    In places where the Plane of Growth breastplates were being created (off of NPC corpses or as the result of quests) new breastplates will be created.

    Basically, the existing breastplates are having their casting times increased and they won't be dropping anymore, new breastplates will take their place.

    I'm sorry that these items are being changed so long after their introduction. But this is something that we feel needs to be done for the integrity of gameplay in EverQuest.

    Alan
