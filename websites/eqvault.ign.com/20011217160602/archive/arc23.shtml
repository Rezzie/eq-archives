    Dynamic Changes/EverQuest Ingame Story
    [Quote: I certainly would love to play in a world such as you describe. But many people wouldn't. There are a lot of EverQuest players that do not like the idea of allowing a GM to 'smite down' their characters, from on high or from below, with or without reason. <<

    We'll leave the discusion of "Dynamic Changes to the Environment" for another day, as I concede that the task is too large for the current incarnation of EQ...I bring it up mostly as a suggestion for future events.

    The point you make above, however is a very, very valid concern. The same difficulty comes about in every P-N-P (pen and paper :)) RPG that I played. People feel very strongly when they invest time and effort into a character. It's very easy to see any permanent changes to that character as being an attack on the fruits of their labors.

    People generally do not like for situations to get beyond what they consider to be a level of "their control", and some such people refuse to tolerate it. Many people like the security of knowing that the have determined: how they're going to play, that they know the risks, that they accept the risks, and as a result can move forward around the board toward whatever goal they have in mind.

    It is, in fact, one of the things that makes EQ a wonderful game. I know (for example) that there are certain situations where I can advance and be reasonably assured that nothing catastrophic will happen to me. This allows me to spend time doing new, dangerous things, without feeling that I won't ever be able to recover. Dragon raids would be far less common if there was a chance that you could permanently end up in the Dragon's belly. (I believe in this sort of world, too, but it's definitely not EQ's ideology).

    However, when you come to the concept of story, I think it's important to consider the role of a GM as a creator and "prime mover" for his/her world. Whenever someone plays a P-N-P RPG, to a certain extent they are empowering the Game Master to take action against their characters in order to support the storyline and to create a conflict for them to resolve. This can be driven by the GM's ideas for the game, or can come about as a consequence of their own actions. It is that "conflict" (be it a puzzling enigma, a challenging foe, or an adventurous discovery) that drives the story forward. In a sense, they give up some of the security they feel about their characters in exchange for a more dynamic experience, and (hopefully) a free enough hand to spin an entertaining story. If you don't accept the dangers outside of Hobbiton, you'll never experience the wonder of the Lonely Mountain.

    Thus we come to my true point (long winded, aren't I?). I think that the people who would be more interested in a highly "Roleplay" oriented server are also the people who would be very willing to see the Game Masters have such Power. I believe they would (and ultimately MUST) be willing to accept that the GM's have creative control to whatever extents can be given. This allows the GM's the power to not only Roleplay effectively themselves, but to weave a storyline that is unique to their character experiences (with potential permanent consequences). It's accepted that the EQ world can't really be made dynamic, but it seems that many aspects of the Characters themselves COULD be affected (as long as such was within the context of a well-executed storyline).

    Obviously, such a redefinition of "GM Powers" would have to be explained to someone going to such a server right from the start (in much the same way as the PvP servers have explanations associated with them). Each story seed that can be planted is another opportunity to Roleplay, and it seems that more dynamic capabilities (even those capable within the game engine today) would be a natural complement.

    Again, more for you to chew on, as it's a subject that I'm (obviously) keenly interested in.

    -Zekestrom of the Many Names ]

    You sure are long winded! :) But that's a good thing.

    I've had this discussion before, on the old boards, with a player. In the end I think we agreed to disagree (though I also admit that I appear to be in the minority with this issue).     Well, there isn't a lot of mystery about it, but I'll answer your questions as best as I can.

    [Quote: 1. What metrics do you use to determine when or if a split server is created, and what servers it draws from? ]

    We create new servers based on all sorts of factors. Primarily we put one up when their seems to be a need because of overcrowding on existing servers. There's no science to deciding whether a new server will be a 'split' or a new server. We judge that based on the perceived need as much as anything. And when it is a split server, we will split people from servers that are the most crowded. Keep in mind that some servers don't have their busiest times when others do. Recently, for example, we split Karana when it wasn't one of the top two busiest servers during the usual prime time. It was, however, much more crowded during its own prime time than the others were in theirs.

    [Quote: 2. Why are all the split servers delineated from exactly two "older" servers? ]

    Actually, I think one was split from three servers. Splitting from two servers usually gives both the older servers the relief they need, while not flooding the new server with too many people. More than two could easily fill the new server (when you include players that start fresh on that server) so much that the new server would be needing a split… And that sort of defeats the purpose.

    [Quote: 3. Why are there not more opportunities to "split" from one server to another? ]

    Undercrowding is just as much of a problem as overcrowding. We won't split a server based only on a slight or possibly a temporary increase in population. Splitting a server and ending up with an underpopulated server is something we need to avoid. We always approach adding servers with caution, nobody really knows how a server will react to a split, how many people will move and how many will stay.

    [Quote: 4. Once a new server is designated the "split to server", what is the likelihood of it becoming the "split from server" and in what time frame?]

    It's really the other way around. We'll usually identify that we need to split a server or two and then gear up to set up a server to split them to. The timeframe varies, as do all things, based on what's going on here or with our Ops department at the time. But we can move pretty fast.

    [Quote: 5. What happens in the case that you have characters on both of the servers being split from and you /movelog from both servers? ]

    If you have a total of less than eight characters, then they would all be available to you on the new server. If your total, however, is greater than eight, some of those characters will not be available to you. You'll have to delete the ones that you are willing to lose until you have reduced yourself to just eight.

    [Quote: 6. What is the procedure when characters from the two different splitting servers have the same name? Who keeps it, who changes? ]

    When two servers are split to one server, one of the characters with duplicate names will have an 'x' appended to their name at random. The player whose character has had the 'x' appended to its name will be able to have his or her name changed by a GM to a new name. But the character without the 'x' will be the one to keep the original name. If you get the appended 'x' you will have to go by a new name, you won't be getting the old one back.


    But, as was the case recently, if characters are moved to a server by a second movelog and there is an existing character with that name on the server, the existing character will keep the name and the new one will have the 'x' appended. The newly moved character can petition to have his name changed to something new. This is one of the risks of moving to a new server.
