Book List - A through F

Douglas Adams:

His novels are filled with fun and humor.  The Hitchhiker series is his best work.

1)  Hitchhiker's Series:  Very enjoyable, light reading that is technically science fiction, but is almost it's own genre.  This series is just plain funny.   If you like novels that provide a lot of wry humor and don't take themselves too seriously, these are for you.  The "trilogy" runs to five books now.  The first three are better than the last two, but all are worth reading.  In order, the books are:   The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy ;  The Restaurant at the End of the Universe ;  Life, the Universe and Everything ;  So Long, and Thanks for All the Fish ;  Mostly Harmless  .  If you haven't read any of the books yet, try this book, which combines all five books plus a bonus story in one hardback for only $14.99:  The Ultimate Hitchhiker's Guide .

Piers Anthony:

This guy just pumps out the novels.  Many are excellent, while a few are well worth skipping.  In general, the earlier works are all superior to the later ones.  Unfortunately, many of his best novels are out of print.  In my opinion, most of the novels he has written since 1990 are not worth buying.  I'm only including the good ones in this list.

1) The Apprentice Adept Series:  There are seven books in this series, but the last four seem to have been written to exploit the popularity of the original trilogy.  I would only recommend the first three, which are all excellent.  This is a cool concept of two alternate universes, one with a SF theme and one with a fantasy one, which start to juxtapose.  Both universes are well thought out and fun to read about, and these novels are real page turners.  The trilogy is:  Split Infinity ;  Blue Adept ;  Juxtaposition

2) The Magic of Xanth:  This series is hard to review.  There are some truly aweful books in this series, but there are also some really great ones.  For the most part, the novels in this series are funny, light and entertaining.  The first several books are the best ones.  After that, they start to vary significantly in quality.  You won't do bad with any of the followups. Just remember that the name Xanth doesn't guarantee a great book.  The initial books are (I'm not sure of the exact order, but I think this is right): A Spell for Chameleon ; The Source of Magic ;  Castle Roona ;  Centaur Aisle ;  Ogre, Ogre ;  Night Mare

3) Incarnations of Immortality:  The first book in this series is seriously cool:  the story about how a man accidentally becomes the grim reaper.  The rest of the books never quite live up to the first, but as a whole, it is still a fun series to read.  I like the concept.  On a Pale Horse ;  Bearing an Hourglass ;  With a Tangled Skein ;  Wielding a Red Sword ;  Being a Green Mother ;  For Love of Evil ;  And Eternity

Isaac Asimov

1) The Foundation Trilogy: This is an all time classic.  The original three books in this trilogy are as good as science fiction gets.  Asimov resisted revisiting his masterpiece until much later, when he must have decided the money was too good to resist.  He didn't do too bad with the follow-up novels, but the original trilogy was too good to really match, so they were basically disappointing.  If for some odd reason you haven't read these classics, buy them now.  They really live up to the hype. The original trilogy:Foundation ;  Foundation and Empire ;  Second Foundation .

Robert Aspirin

1) Myth Books:  These have a quirky humor similar to the Hitchhiker and Xanth books.  Light and fun reading.  I'm pretty sure the first three I list are the first three in the series. I'm not sure if I have the rest of the order down right, but they don't really have to be read as a series.    Another Fine Myth ;  Myth Conceptions ;  Myth Directions ;  Myth-Ing Persons ;  Myth-Nomers and Im-Pervections ;  Little Myth Marker ;  M.Y.T.H. Inc. Link ;  M.Y.T.H. Inc. in Action ; Sweet Myth-Tery of Life

Ian Banks

1) The Player of Games :  Excellent and fascinating novel about a game player who takes on another culture in a game with the future of humanity at stake.  Really fascinating description of a game that completely dominates a whole culture.  This is a must read for the game players out there.

Ray Bradbury

1)  The Martian Chronicles :  Classic science fiction.  This is really a collection of stories based upon man's supposed colonization of mars that allows the author to explore the dark side of humanity.  This is not easy reading, but it is very satisfying.  The amazing thing is how realistic the portrayal is and how relevant it has stayed over the course of time.

2) Fahrenheit 451 :  Written 40 years ago, this novel is still timely.  The author's style is deliberate and there are definitely some people who will not enjoy this work.  The message however is haunting and the story is absorbing.  Worth making the effort to read.

Terry Brooks

1)  Shanarra:  Typical fantasy world of elves, dwarves, magicians and the little guy who needs to save the world from dark evil.  Nothing wrong with that.  These novels are very well written with a vivid setting and well thought out characters.  I highly recommend the original trilogy.  The follow up novels were not as good.  The original Trilogy is:   Sword of Shannara ;  Elfstones of Shannara ;  The Wishsong of Shannara

Steven Brust

1) Jhereg:  The hero of this series is a very interesting and well thought out character.  He is an assassin and rogue in a world where this is an honorable profession.  The development of his character, as well as that of his witch wife and interesting pet is what makes the series interesting.  Interesting fantasy that is not as dark as it first appears.  The first three books are the best of the series.  Unfortunately, some of the books are listed as out of stock.   Jhereg ;  Yendi ;  Teckla ;  Taltos ;   Phoenix ;  Athyra ;  Orca ;  Dragon

Orson Scott Card

1) Ender Series:  The first book in this series is excellent - one of the best SF books ever written.  The ending of the book is haunting, and you will be thinking about its premise for a long time.  I don't think it was originally conceived as a series, but given the popularity of the first, he went on to write several sequels.  Fortunately, the sequels are almost as good, if not quite up to, the first one, although that's a pretty high standard.  This is a unique science fiction series that is well written and almost visionary.  The novels are incredibly intense and you will have a hard time putting them down until you finish them. Ender's Game ;  Speaker for the Dead ;  Xenocide ;  Children of the Mind   and the newly released in hardcoverEnder's Shadow

Jack L. Chalker

1) The Well of Souls:  Very few series have managed to create such a vivid and memorable lead character as Nathan Brazil.  The well of souls is also a unique and fascinating world.  The original four books are classic science fiction adventure, and highly recommended reading.  The sequels that were done later are not as good, but still worthwhile.  For some odd reason, the second one is listed as out of print, which makes no sense since the other three are available.  The first four are: Midnight at the Well of Souls ;  Exiles at the Well of Souls ;  Quest for Well of Souls ;  Return of Nathan Brazil and  Twilight at the Well of Souls .  Other well world books are:  Echoes of the Well of Souls (Watchers at the Well 1) ;  Shadow of the Well of Souls : (The Watchers at the Well 2) ; Gods of the Well of Souls (The Watchers at the Well 3)

Arthur C. Clark

1) 2001 A Space Odyssey :  You saw the movie and were completely baffled.  Admit it.  It's OK, so was everyone else ... that is until they read the book.  The book is excellent.  Read it and go back and watch the movie again (preferrable in an altered state of mind).  See.  It all makes sense now.  Now go out and get the sequels, which carry the story forward and are also fine novels in their own right.  2010 : Odyssey Two ;  2061 : Odyssey Three ;  3001 : The Final Odyssey

2)  Rendezvous With Rama :  The first book was quite simply a masterful piece of imagination and writing.  I was captivated by it.  I really don't think he ever intended there to be sequels, but a book this popular will not stand alone for long.  I found the sequels to be somewhat disappointing, but I guess they had a hard act to follow.  They are: Rama II ;  Garden of Rama ;  Rama Revealed

Glen Cook

1) The Black Company:  The original trilogy is one of the finer works of dark fantasy out there.  You can almost feel the grit and grime of the world torn by war.  The writing style is also refereshingly different, giving you different points of view throughout the trilogy.  The sequels are also very good, if not quite up to the first three.  The original trilogy:  The Black Company ;  Shadows Linger ;  White Rose . The sequels:   Shadow Games ;  Silver Spike ;  Dreams of Steel ;  She Is the Darkness (Glittering Stones 1) ;  Bleak Seasons (Glittering Stones 2) ;  Water Sleeps (Glittering Stones 3) [hardback]

Rick Cook

1) The Wiz Series:  These are not technical masterpieces, but they are fun and the premise is kind of cool.  Some enjoyable light reading. The Wiz Biz ;  The Wizardry Cursed ;  The Wizardry Consulted ;  The Wizardry Quested

Stephen R. Donaldson

1)   The Chronicles of Thomas Covenant the Unbeliever: Never have I wanted to strangle the hero of a book more than I wanted to strangle Thomas Covenant.  This is a remarkable trilogy in the way that it plays off traditional fantasy and does unexpected things.  It makes these books both aggravating and enjoyable.  Highly recommended. Lord Foul's Bane ;  The Illearth War ;  The Power That Preserves

2)  The Second Chronicles of Thomas Covenant the Unbeliever: A follow-up to the original series.  Not quite as good, but still vastly entertaining.  If you liked the first three, you will like these too.  The Wounded Land ;  The One Tree ;  White Gold Wielder

David Duncan

1)  The Seventh Sword:  A cool series about a modern man whose soul is transferred into the body of a master swordsman in another world and given a mission by the gods to save the world.  The whole world thought up in this series is fun and fascinating, and the characters are very well defined.  The Reluctant Swordsman ;  The Coming of Wisdom ;  The Destiny of the Sword

2) The Man of his Word:  Excellent series.  The premise is standard:  a young stableboy goes on to gain magic undreamed of and win the heart of the princess.  However, the execution is above normal.  Also, the magic system is very unique, and the ending is unpredictable.  Between this and the follow-up series, you get eight great novels.  Highly recommended.  Magic Casement ;  Faery Lands Forlorn ;  Perilous Seas ;  Emperor and Clown

3)  A Handful of Men:  A follow-up to the Man and his Word series. It really is just a continuation of the series, so you need to read those novels first.  If you do, you will be sure to buy these which continue the story a few years down the road.  More excellent work. The Cutting Edge ;  Upland Outlaws ;  The Stricken Field ;  The Living God

Dungeons and Dragons

You love playing Everquest and other computer role playing games?  Well believe me, as fun as they are, they still don't touch the classic AD&D.  This is what started it all and inspired all of the role playing games from there on.  It has gotten a little commercial since Gary Gygax left, but it still is the best there is.  Totally addictive and completely engrossing. this is the best game ever invented.  The level of enjoyment is only limited by your imagination.  If you can get a group together and are willing to take the time to learn to play, this could even drag you away from Everquest.  There are tons of books out there, but you really only need a few, and remember you don't have to stick by the rules layed out by the books.  It is your fun and you can play it however you want.  The essentials to get started are:   Advanced Dungeons and Dragons/Players Handbook ;  Advanced Dungeons & Dragons, Dungeon Master's Guide ;  Monstrous Manual and  AD&D Adventure Dice Set (7 Dice Set) .  You must also get a starting adventure or dungeon module.  I am happy to see that the classic dungeon module,  The Keep on the Borderlands , which came with the original D&D boxed set, is still available.  This will start you with an excellent scenario involving fighting orcs in the mountains near the Keep (Hmmm - sounds familiar doesn't it?). You can probably get away with only getting the players guide and some dice, but you will be missing a lot of the game rules. While it is not essential, I would also recommend you get the following: Dungeon Master Screen and Master Index and  Character Record Sheets/Ref2 .  And once you start to get into it, these books are very good at expanding upon the basic game and opening up more possiblilties for building your own dungeons (though only a true junkie will have them all): Tome of Magic ;  The Bestiary (Dramatic Supplement) ;  Arms and Equipment Guide ;  Encyclopedia Magica (Advanced Dungeons and Dragons), Vol. 1: A-C ;  Encyclopedia Magica (Advanced Dungeons and Dragons), Vol. 2: D-P ;  Encyclopedia Magica (Advanced Dungeons and Dragons Accessory) Vol. 3: P-S ; Encyclopedia Magica, Vol. 4: S-Z & Index Access ;  Book of Artifacts ;  The Inner Planes : Being a Guide to the Planes of the Elements ; Faces of Evil : The Fiends  ;  Faiths & Avatars  and  Player's Option : Spells and Magic . There is also a nice set of handbooks for each class, which are worth getting if you are really into a particular class:   The Complete Bard's Handbook ;  The Complete Fighter's Handbook ;  The Complete Paladin's Handbook ;  The Complete Priest's Handbook ;  The Complete Psionics Handbook ;  The Complete Ranger's Handbook ;  The Complete Thief's Handbook and  The Complete Wizard's Handbook  And another set for certain races: The Complete Book of Elves ;  The Complete Book of Gnomes & Halflings ;  Complete Book of Humanoids . There are plenty of other books you can get to supplement these, but after a while it just reaches overkill.   I may list more later.

David Eddings

If you are one of the few who haven't read any of David Eddings' novels, follow the links and buy them now.  He is definitely one of the best in the business.

1) The Belgariad:  His first series.  It is not original, but it sure is fun.  Pure traditional fantasy at its finest.  I guarantee you will be up nights telling yourself you'll just read one more chapter before going to bed.  There is a reason so many magic users running around games like Everquest are named Belgarath or Polgara.  Very entertaining.  Pawn of Prophecy ;  Queen of Sorcery ;  Magician's Gambit ;  Castle of Wizardry ;  Enchanter's End Game

2)  The Mallorian:  This is basically a sequel to the Belgariad.  It is not quite as good as the first series, but that is a goal which is hard to live up to.  This means instead of being excellent, it is merely great.  If you got hooked on the Belgariad, you will want to continue with these.  Guardians of the West ;  King of the Murgos ;  Demon Lord of Karanda ;  Sorceress of Darshiva

3)  The Elenium:  A different world than that of the Belgariad and Mallorian.  This is more of a romantic fantasy.  Like all of Eddings books, the characters and situations are fascinating. The Diamond Throne ;  The Ruby Knight ;  Sapphire Rose

4)  The Tamuli:  This is a sequel trilogy to The Ellenium with many of the same characters and the same world.  If you enjoyed the previous books, you will want to read these too.   Domes of Fire ;  The Shining Ones ;  The Hidden City

Mark Fabi

1)  Wyrm :  Interesting novel written by someone who obviously plays a lot of online games about an online game going wrong.  Cool concept for those of us who spend too much time in Everquest (which is probably all of us).

Philip Jose Farmer

1) Riverworld:  A truly unique work of science fiction.  He starts with the basic parlor concept of who would you invite to supper if you could invite anyone who ever lived and runs with it to create a world where indeed every person who ever lived on Earth is resurrected and lives together.  The fun of the novels is watching the famous and infamous figures of history interact and try to unravel the secret of their existence.  Just a cool concept. To Your Scattered Bodies Go ;  The Fabulous Riverboat ;  The Dark Design ;  The Magic Labyrinth ;  Gods of Riverworld
 

Raymond E. Feist

The world of Midkemia is a great creation.  You may recognize such cities as Freeport and Highhold.  You certainly will recognize them if you played any of the Krondor games.  This guy knows how to write.  I recommend all of his books, but the original Riftwar series is still the best.

1)The Riftwar:  OK.  I'll admit that I enjoy the basic plot of the little guy growing up to become the most powerful magician in the world. Heck, it's what we all are trying to do in Everquest.  It's been done in various ways, but this is one of the best.  What's fascinating is how he weaves two different and very complex worlds together into one series.  These books are inspired. Magician : Apprentice ;  Magician : Master ;  Silverthorn ;  A Darkness at Sethanon

2)  The Serpentwar:  Very good series of books set about 40 years after the riftwar.  Some of the same characters carry over to these novels, so you should really read the Riftwar Trilogy first.  To really get the full feeling of this series, you should also read the two stand alone books  The King's Buccaneer and  Prince of the Blood .  Both are entertaining, if not quite up to the standards of his other books, and introduce characters who play a large part in the Serpentwar series.  This series is well worth reading, although I do get the feeling it was originally meant to be a trilogy, since the fourth book is more of an afterthought than the climax it should be.  The series is:  Shadow of a Dark Queen ;  Rise of a Merchant Prince ;  Rage of a Demon King ;  Shards of a Broken Crown  .  If you are really looking to fix the Midkemia jones, there is a new hardback out that I haven't read called Krondor, the Betrayal .  I don't know how good it is or what it is about.

The Forgotten Realms

    These novels are grouped together, because it's a lot easier to look as them as a group than as novels written by individual authors.  They are all based in the world of Dungeons and Dragons, so the background is familiar to many of us.  There are some excellent books in this series.  There are also some pretty forgettable ones.  By far the best author is R.A. Slavatore, which is why his books are listed under his own name and not here.  Many of the other authors in this series are interchangeable.  I'm only including the good ones here.

1)   Elfshadow and  Elfsong by Elaine Cunningham. Part of the harpers series.

2)   Shadow of the Avator Trilogy by Ed Greenwood:  This is a good standard fantasy trilogy.  Nothing to rave about, but enjoyable reading.   Shadows of Doom ;  Cloak of Shadows ;  All Shadows Fled

3)  The Avatar Trilogy by Richard Awlinson:  One of the better Forgotton Realms trilogies.  There is some real depth to this one.   Shadowdale ;  Tantras ;  Waterdeep

C.S. Friedman

1) The Coldfire Trilogy:  Very engrossing novels which explore the nature of good and evil through the well constructed character of Gerald Tarrant.  Interesting dark fantasy set in an unusual setting.  Very worthwhile reading.   Black Sun Rising ;  When True Night Falls ;  Crown of Shadows

Alan Dean Foster

1) Flinx:  The adventures of the orphan Flynx and his mini dragon pip make up a fun and fascinating series.  The books were not written linearly, and thus are more of a collection of individual novels than an intertwined series.  This does not take away from them at all.  My only problem with them is that he seemed to abandon the books half way through and there are hints of plot lines which are never carried on.   The Tar-Aiym Krang ;  Orphan Star ;  The End of the Matter ;  Bloodhype ;  Flinx in Flux ;  For Love of Mother Not
