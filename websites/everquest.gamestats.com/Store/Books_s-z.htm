Book List S through Z

Fred Saberhagen

1) The Lost Swords:  Unfortunately the several of these  books are listed as out of stock.  I don't know if they will get them back in, but if you can find them somewhere, the rest are available here.  This is a fascinating science fiction/fantasy series with a pretty cool post apocolyptic premise.  Very unique.  Unfortunately the book "Empire of the East", which is a great book and sets up the premise for this series, is out of print.  You don't need to have read that for this series, but it would let you appreciate some of the finer nuances, so go out to a used bookstore and look for it if you can.  The First Book of Swords ;  The Second Book of Lost Swords : Sightblinder's Story ; The Third Book of Lost Swords : Stonecutter's Story ;  The Fourth Book of Lost Swords : Farslayer's Story ;  The Fifth Book of Lost Swords : Coinspinner's Story ;  Mindsword's Story (6th Book of Lost Swords) ;  The Seventh Book of Lost Swords: Wayfinder's Story  ; The Last Book of Swords : Shieldbreaker's Story

R.A. Salvatore

1)  The Icewind Dale Trilogy:  This is part of the AD&D Forgotten Realms set of books, and is one of the best.  Because it's setting is so familiar, the author is able to concentrate more on the characters and the story.  He succeeds in creating some really memorable characters. The dark elf character Drizzt Du'Orden is really well done (and the main character in the next series).  This is pure escapist fantasy at its best.   The Crystal Shard ;  Streams of Silver ;  The Halfling's Gem

2) The Dark Elf Trilogy:  The story of the dark elf Drizzt and how he discovers the light.  Great fantasy; great characters; great writing.  One of the best fantasy series written.  Running around EQ, I think I've seen 40 variations on the name Drizzt among the dark elf players, so I know others must have liked these as well. Homeland ;  Exile ;  Sojourn  .  Better yet, get the whole trilogy in a single hardbound edition The Dark Elf Trilogy : Homeland, Exile, Sojourn

3) Spearwielder Trilogy:  A break from Salvatore's Forgotten Realm novels, this is written in his normal crisp style and is a very good read. The Woods Out Back ;  The Dragon's Dagger ;  Dragonslayer's Return

4) The Cleric Quintet:  This was just re-released in a single set with all five books in one hardback (and only $17.49).  Another great set of books by Salvatore, who doesn't really write anything bad.  What's interesting is that clerical magic is not really represented in fantasy too much, so this comes from a somewhat different perspective.  If you liked his other works, you will also enjoy this.
 

Dan Simmons

1) Hyperion:  Fabulous science fiction.  A very intense and interesting work that will leave you thinking about its premise for a long time.  The first book in this series is one of the best ever written.  The rest come pretty close.  These are not books you can read off and on over a period of time.  Be prepared to lose yourself for hours.  Hyperion ;  The Fall of Hyperion ;  Endymion ;  The Rise of Endymion

E.E. "Doc" Smith

1) The Lensman Series:  I can't believe that these are still in print!  Written a long time ago, well before I was born, these books inspired a whole generation of science fiction writers.  50 years or so later, they are still as good as when they were first written.  The books seem to only be available as trade paperbacks, so they are a little more expensive than most. One warning.  The first book in the series is basically to provide background for the rest, and is thus pretty tedious at times.  Don't let it discourage you from getting the rest, which are rocking space opera at its finest.  You can even skip the first book without mising a whole lot if you want. Triplanetary : A Tale of Cosmic Adventure ;  First Lensman ;  Galactic Patrol ;  Gray Lensman ;  Second Stage Lensmen ;  Children of the Lens
 

Christopher Stasheff

1)  The Wizard in Rhyme Series:  This is a light fantasy series which uses the standard plot of a modern person finding himself in a fantasy world and able to practice magic.  Still, it is well done and fun to read. Her Majesty's Wizard ;  The Oathbound Wizard ;  The Witch Doctor ;  The Secular Wizard

Sheri S. Tepper:

1) The True Game :  This novel is actually a compilation re-release of three really cool books:  King's Blood Four, Necromancer Nine, and Wizard's Eleven.  I personally think many RPG makers based at least part of their worlds on these books.  I really enjoyed these books.  There were two other series (Jinian Footseer and Mavin Manyshaped) which followed these books and were also pretty good, but unfortunately they are listed as out of print.

J.R.R. Tolkien

1) The Lord of the Rings:  What can I say.  This is the one that started it all.  It is by far the most popular and famous fantasy serious ever written.  Most modern fantasy novels can trace at least part of their roots back to these novels.  I have to say that they are not easy to read.  The writing style back then was different than it is today.  But they are well worth reading, both for the high entertainment value and because every fan of this genre ought to know these books.  If you are going to buy them, you might as well get the nice boxed set of trade books, which is only $19.17 for both the Lord of the Rings and the Hobbit.  It is here: J.R.R. Tolkien : The Hobbit and the Complete Lord of the Rings .  Or if you're feeling extravagant, there is a cool looking hard cover boxed edition Lord of the Rings : The Return of the King/the Two Towers/the Fellowship of the Ring .  Then you might as well get  The Silmarillion even though it is rather dry reading, and some of his later short stories, many of which were gathered together by his son after his death and are actually quite good:  Unfinished Tales ;  The Book of Lost Tales ;  The Book of Lost Tales 2 ;  The Lays of Beleriand (The History of Middle-Earth, 3) ;  Shaping of Middle-Earth : The Quenta, the Ambarkanta and the Annals (The History of Middle-Earth 4) ;  The Lost Road and Other Writings : Language and Legend Before the Lord of the Rings (The History of Middle-Earth , Vol 5)  .

    If you are really looking for a Tolkien fix, this is a great book of illustrations and descriptions of the creatures of Middle Earth:  A Tolkien Beastiary .  Other interesting looking books on Middle Earth are as follows (I haven't read these so you will have to check the amazon.com descriptions for recommendations, but many of them look pretty cool):   Realms of Tolkien : Images of Middle-Earth ;  Tolkien : The Illustrated Encyclopaedia ;  Tolkien's World : Paintings of Middle-Earth ;  The Languages of Tolkien's Middle Earth ;  The Complete Guide to Middle-Earth : From the Hobbit to the Silmarillion ;  The Atlas of Middle-Earth ;  The Map of Tolkien's Middle-Earth ;  There and Back Again : The Map of the Hobbit

John Varley

1)  The Gaea Trilogy:  Highly imaginative science fiction.  The themes are very adult, and you may not agree with all of the author's political views, but you will keep reading to see what happens to his characters.  Certainly not light reading, but intriguing nonetheless. Titan ;  Wizard ;  Demon

T.H. White

1)  The Once and Future King :  T.H. White's telling of the story of King Author is so well done and popular that it is almost accepted as fact.  This is often considered a masterpiece in the field.  I personally wouldn't go that far, but it is still an excellent read.

Kurt Vonnegut

1)  Cat's Cradle :  The author would probably say this isn't science fiction, but I read this in a SF literature class in college, so I think that makes it official.  Considered a classic of American literature, unlike may other novels foisted upon you in school, this one happens to be amazingly entertaining.  There is a wry humor in this that really grows on you as you read it.  Highly recommended.

Tad Williams

1)  The Dragonbone Chair Trilogy:  Another classic story line done perfectly and entertainingly.  Magic and mahem and a fine story line.  It is called a trilogy, but they split the last book into two still very long novels, so there are actually four books to this series.  Good reading. The Dragonbone Chair ;  Stone of Farewell ;  To Green Angel Tower, Part 1 ;  To Green Angel Tower (Part 2)

Gene Wolfe

1) Book of the New Sun:  These books are written in a unique style that at first is difficult to get into.  However, much like iambic pentameter, the style will suddenly click and you will find yourself deeper into them then you have gone into many novels.  They are dark, depressing and ultimately engrossing.  This is not light reading, but is extremely entertaining.  These books have just been re-released as a pair of two-volume sets.   Shadow & Claw : The First Half of the Book of the New Sun : The Shadow of the Torturer/the Claw of the Conciliator ;  Sword & Citadel : The Second Half of the Book of the New Sun : The Sword of the Lictor and the Citadel of the Autarch

Roger Zelazny

1) The Great Book of Amber : The Complete Amber Chronicles, 1-10 :  Zelazny fans can rejoice.  This compilation is supposed to be released in December, 1999.  I know I plan on buying it, since I foolishly lost these books long ago.  One of the most original and interesting science fiction/fanatsy series ever conceived (it really fits both categories).  The original series of 5 books was fabulous.  The followup series was not quite as good, but still very entertaining. Since this says volumes 1 through 10, it must contain both.  If this is out for Christmas, it will make a great gift.
