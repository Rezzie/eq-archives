Recently I posted a preview of ENES 6 with some half-formed rules. However, I realized that I had not made an ENES 5 yet! Obviously this HAD to come first. So here we are, with another not-quite-usual NES.

Those familiar with Neo Nes 7 will recognize the ruleset.

MONEY:
Represented by Production Points. Each turn you are given a certain amount of PP to spend. These may not be banked.

INCREASING PP:
1) You can spend 5PP in order to increase your PP income by 1. Say you had 50 pp one turn, spent them all on increasing, then you would have 60 pp to spend the next turn and after that.
2) At certain tech levels or points in the game, resources will be found/noticed in your nation. These provide a PP boost every turn you have them and can be traded away, since you only get the benefit from even if you have several.
3) If you invade another nation, you may pillage PP from them. These are a one-time bonus, appearing as a plus after your PP to spend (meaning that you will not earn that amount next turn unless you continue pillaging.) This is the least dependably method, but the one with the highest possible returns.

ARMIES AND NAVIES:
An army/navy will be displayed as a number from 1 to 9 on the map. On the first page, it will be referred like this:
1: {3} (1)-(2) [1]
This means that Army 1 has a size of {3}, attack value of (1) and defense value of (2), and a movement rate of [1].
If your army is in your territory, it will be in black. If not, it is in your color.
An army is increased or built by spending 5pp per size level.
Note that armies need tech level 5 to move outside your territory. You need tech level 12 to build navies.
Armies and navies cost 5pp per size level to build (4 if you have the correct trait.)

EXPANSION:
You territory expands each turn. You can specify which direction main expansion should be in.

EDUCATION:
Spend 5 PP for one point of education. Certain levels give you bonuses.
Education at 3: You may choose your people's trait. (see below.)
Education 5: Armies may move outside your territory.
Education 10: Your armies gain 1 offense value.
Education 12: You may build navies.
Education 15: Your armies gain 1 defense value.
Education 20: Despotism government (3 armies, 2 navies)
More will be revealed as technology advances. After all, you cannot be quite sure what the future holds.

TRAITS:
After reaching tech level 3 (rudimentary) you may choose 1 trait.
Scientific: Only need 4 PP per point of education.
Militaristic: Only need 4 PP per point of army.
Seafaring: Only need 4 PP per point of navy, can build navies without needing lvl 12.
Industrious: Only need 4/5ths the amount of PP to build wonders.

WONDERS:
At certain points of technology, you may begin construction of Wonders. These generally take a lot of time, but give very beneficial effects.

GOVERNMENTS:
You start out as a Tribal Chiefdom. You are restricted to 2 armies and 1 navy (which may be as large as you like.) Better education will give better governments which will allow more armies and navies.
New government- Despotism- 3 armies, 2 navies- requires 20 tech.

NOTES AND CLARIFICATIONS

You can only increase army/navy SIZE. Attack, defense and speed need tech, just like in Civ3.

THERE WILL BE NO COMMERCIAL TRAIT.

YOU CANNOT BANK PP.

CARTHAGE

Many, many years ago, the site where Carthage now stood had simply been a minor outpost of the once-mighty Phoenician Empire. Populated mainly by traders, its only business was to exchange Phoenician products for native ones and ship them back to their native land. While many of the commodities were very valuable, little of the wealth ever reached the hands of the people responsible for its acquisition.

All of that changed, however, after the destruction of Tyre and the subjugation of Sidon by the mighty land of Assyria. During this time of trouble, the Phoenicians lost control of most of their colonies. Faced with imminent doom, the people of these two cities launched a fleet of many ships carrying not soldiers, but citizens and freemen. Many ships were sunk in storms or scattered by the fickle seas, but those that weathered the journey brought their people to the place where they would found Carthage, the "New City." Since then, Carthage had been the capital of its own empire, growing and expanding in many possible ways.

Now, they had met the nation of the Saharans, a people who had once dwelled in the unknown southern lands but had been driven out by others. Compared to Carthage the people of this land had less wealth, but they were devoted to understanding the ways and laws that governed the world, and thus had a greater respect for knowledge. Carthage's army was bigger, but the Saharans had the better one. It seemed that these people had good trade potential, despite the fact that they mostly lived in a merciless desert with very few good places to settle. Besides, the fact that they could live in such a harsh environment relatively comfortably indicated that this was a strong and resourceful race.

***

Meanwhile, a villager somewhere out in the sticks was sitting on a fence, resting from his daily chores. He amused himself by hitting rocks with a stick. Each time he hit a rock, he would see how far it would fly. Suddenly, an idea came to mind...

He turned to a friend. "I have an idea," he said. "How about every evening after we're done working in the fields, we all hold a rock-hitting competiton to see how far we can hit rocks with a stick?"

"I dunno," the second guy said. "Just hitting rocks sounds boring. Maybe if we could find a way to make it a little more exciting..."

Silence. Then the second guy spoke up. "How about this: We set up two big smooth stones a certain distance apart. One guy hits a rock with the stick and then runs from one stone to the other and back before another guy can find the rock he hit and tag him. How's that sound?"

"Very interesting," the first guy answered. "And maybe we could make it to where if the guy is standing on one of the stones, he's safe from tagging."

"But then how would he run back?"

"Simple. Another guy would hit the rock again at the first stone. Then, while the second guy runs to the base stone, the first guy can run back 'home' to the starting stone."

"But wouldn't they be in each other's way and get slowed down?" the second guy said. "How would we solve that?"

A third guy chimed in. "Why not add another base stone? That way, when the second guy runs to the first base, the first guy can run to the second base and then home."

"Or better yet," said a fourth, "make three base stones. That would give the runners a challenge."

"How would that be a challenge?" asked a fifth. The conversation was beginning to attract others.

"Yeah," yet another assented, "Wouldn't that just give the other guy a harder time trying to tag 'em all?"

After this point, the whole discussion took on a life of its own, drawing comments like a moth to a flame.

"Well, we could give the rock-pitcher a few helpers close to the bases, and maybe a few more out in the field, just in case the guy hits a good one."

"How would we keep track of who's supposed to be what, with this many people involved?"

"We could divide everyone into two teams. One team hits, while the other team catches and throws. Then they'd alternate."

"How would we decide what time it is to switch?"

"Why not assign a point for every time somebody runs around the bases, then rotate after so many points have been scored?"

"But if guys keep on getting tagged, the game would take forever!"

"I have a solution for that. Each time a guy gets tagged, his team gets a penalty. After three penalties, the teams switch places."

"What if the guy misses the rock with his stick? Wouldn't that hold up the game?"

"Easy. If the guy misses three times, his team takes a penalty and has to put up another guy."

"But what if there's something wrong with the throw, and it goes too high or too low for the batter to hit?"

Silence.

Finally, someone came up with an answer. "Then we give the pitcher a penalty. After so many bad throws, the batter could go ahead to the next base."

And so it began. After minutes of discussion, several details were worked out, such as using a leather ball instead of a rock, a bigger bat, how long the game would last, and so forth. Finally, they were ready to start a test game.

Or rather, they were almost ready.

A guy went out to look for base stones. He could only find three. There wasn't a stone for home base.

"What a shame," one guy said.

"So much for that idea," another added.

"Wait a minute," said the guy who had come up with the idea. "I'll be right back." And so he left them for a moment. He came back with a plate from a china set.

"Where'd you get that?" somebody asked.

"The master's house."

"Are you CRAZY!?! You're going to get us all killed!"

"I'm only borrowing it..."

Anyway, the game began. Many points were scored, there was a lot of running and dodging, and one guy even took a hit in the stomach from a really bad pitch (which brought on a slight rules change). Most importantly, everyone had fun.

"Wow, that was great," one guy said to the inventor of the game. "What'll you call it?"

"I think I'll name it..."

He was interrupted by the master of the farm. "OK everyone, back to work. And which one of you lowlifes stole my good china plate?"

"Uh oh..."
