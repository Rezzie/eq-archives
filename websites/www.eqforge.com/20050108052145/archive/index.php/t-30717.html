Unfortuneatly, the theme that 1984 once professed has finally come to fruition. It has finally come to the point where technology can do what Orsen Wells once warned. And the fools will welcome it.

Gather round children and let me tell you a story.

In the far far future, beyond the centuries for which even your great grandchildren will see, was born a man. He was a great man by every measure that one can place on a man. It was he who built man's first faster than light craft, and it was he who went with it on it's first great voyages to the worlds beyond our own.

On his great journey he learned that language was a uniquely Earth invention, and that every other race in the universe used telepathy to communicate. But as it turned out, the Earthly invention, language, was of magnificent value to all the creature he met. Everywhere he went he taught Earthling language to the creatures and they paid him vast measures of wealth. He returned to Earth as the richest man who ever had lived.

One day he was in at a social club in New New York discussing his great journey with a team of wide eyed women and jittery journalists. One particularly inquisitive woman asked him, "Why do they want to learn language? Isn't telepathy a better form of communication?"

"No," he said. "Telepathic communication kills the idea of self for all creatures. Imagine if every day everyone within a 100 feet of you knew exactly what you were thinking, exactly what you were going to say, or exactly what you thought of them. There could be no privacy, no modesty, and no gracious lying. On all these planets, marriages don't last because one partner knows that the other thinks about someone else. Similarly, jobs don't last becuase the boss always intercepts some random thought about an employee taking vengeance on him. These are the things that we think about every day -what Jung called our 'shadow'- but can hide because of language. Language creates solace, language creates self."


Privacy has always been paramount to American civilization. We don't give the police the power to search anything and everything on a whim because we accept privacy as an inalienable right. Imagine if we did allow that kind of invasion.

For instance, there have been and always will be things that may be wrong, but not evil. There are levels of sin. Like...stealing towels from hotels. Sure it's wrong, it can even be called theft, but it's not something that a police force is going to throw up a road block for. Admittedly, that's a lite example. But imagine some of these other things that are so called wrong. For example, in Alabama there is a law on the books that says blacks cannot intermarry with whites. There are these kind of laws all over this country. Things like no oral sex, or no sex on sunday, or it's an arrestable offense to cry in public. These are things that are not only on the books, but also legally enforceable.

We give people privacy because there are always things that are not necessary for other people to know about. Without this notion of privacy it opens the door to all kinds of enforcement. If I got arrested for making love on the sabbath, I'd throw a fit. I'd start firebombing over that. Most people -normal people- accept certain things as open to interpretation by the individual. I say it's wrong to have sex on Sunday, you say it's OK, let's just leave it. You do it in your home, it's not my problem, and if it really is wrong you'll pay for it in the afterlife.

The quote, "People who have nothing to hide have nothing to fear [from surveilance]," has long been the mantra of the fascist. It's utter non-sense in it's purest form. Everyone has something to hide: whether it's sex on Sunday, or that you spank your kid, or that you installed Windows 95 on two computers. These are small things, and of no importance to anyone but the individual and the almighty.

Anything that compromises this veil of privacy, no matter how smoke and mirrored up it is, is a crime unto itself. People have secrets, and 90 percent of them are of no consequence to anyone. Stay out of people's lives, Emporer Bill. And stay out of our computers. What we do is none of your business, no matter how rich you want to be or how much of a tool you are.

I'm not trying to say piracy is a small thing. It certainly is not, but the ability for anyone to say what you can and can't do with your computer is a crime against the human right of self.
