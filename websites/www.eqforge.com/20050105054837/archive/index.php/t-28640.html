This is what I recommend:

1. If everything is running fine, just hold on to them. Don't install if you have no problems.

2. If/when you do finally install, follow the golden rule: be prepared to lose all files and re-install everything from scratch.



Hopefully, you will never have such a huge mess that you will ahve to reinstall from a clean (reformatted) drive. But it can... and does happen. Better to be prepared, than cursing irreplaceable data when it occurs.

Here is the problems... M$ is the richest, largest software company in the world. This renders them incapable of delivering software which is bug free. There are over 6,000 known bugs in Windoze XP, for instance. Usually, only a handful affect anyone at one time. But if a particularly nasty bug rears its ugly head at the wrong time (esp. during upgrades of the OS and/or drivers), the machine can be in such a state that an average user cannot recover, or at least it is much faster just to reformat and re-install from scratch.

So the trick is either to back up everything to another drive (HD or CD), or at least back up irreplaceable data (stuff that you download, create, edit, etc.).


Do not take driver installation lightly... to be unprepared for disaster seems to just attract it more ;).

It always helps when people give too much than too little.... America's Army is being programmed by some people who know what they are doing, and are puching even the current white paper specs on 3D accelerations to achieve the realism they want.

Here is what that means to you... the T&L (Transform & Lighting) calls that are made by the software are required to be able to run properly. These sections of the nVidia drivers are being tweaked on an almost daily basis to get better efficiency and speed... but sometimes (actually, pretty often) something will slip thru, which is no big deal for HalfLife and most 3D games. But AA and a few others are exercising parts of the code that are apt to expose a weakenss in nVidia's driver, and so they rush to fix it. This means you are one of the people that will be needing to regularly update your nVidia driver. It also means that you are well advised to keep your machine backed up.

So yes, in you case, I recommend you not only apply the driver, but visit nVidia and use their reference drivers on a regular basis. I have been with nVidia myself, since the first week of the TNT release, and been there, done that. When I get a GEForce 4 and run programs like AA, I'll be there again ;).

PS, Don't bother with your video card's drivers, unless your video card has a special 3rd-party function, like maybe Video Out, that you really use. All nVidia-based cards have drivers that their manufacturers take the nVidia ref drivers, and tweak/modify. Typically, the manufactures (like Diamond & Asus & Hercules, etc.) are quite a bit behind the latest and best ref drivers at nVidia. I quite using my Diamond drivers in 1998, about 2 weeks after my first TNT card. ;)

AMD 751 drivers are chipset drivers, not processor drivers. Processors require no drivers. What motherboard do you have? Just because your processor is an AMD, does not mean your chipset is. It could be a VIA chipset, an AMD chipset, or even (!) a combination of the 2. This is very important...you absolutely must have the correct drivers, for your chipset, for your computer to work properly.
